### 46.1 What is a Value Chain?

> **Core insight:** Every company has a value chain, and this value chain determines the levers a Business Development executive can use. It is applicable for an end-product, a component, or even a raw material.

**The Primary Value Chain:**

```
R&D → Procurement & Inbound Logistics → Manufacturing → 
Warehousing & Outbound Logistics → Sales & Marketing → After-Sales Service
```

This framework is universally applicable — whether the company makes automobiles, electronics, white goods, or is even a mining company.

**Cascaded Sub-Value Chains:**

| Function | Sub-Elements |
|---|---|
| **R&D** | Idea Design → Initial Prototyping & Testing → Specification Setting → Final Prototyping & Testing → Field Optimisation → Final Release |
| **Manufacturing** | Facility Planning → Materials Planning → Production Planning → Production Quality → Environment, Safety & Health |
| **Sales & Marketing** | Market Research → Demand Planning → Product Management → Sales Promotion → Sales → Parts & Service (+ Distribution) |

> **Key insight for BD professionals:** You must understand the value chain in primary as well as sub-elements to project the product in the right manner for customers. Understanding where the company is strong enables effective product positioning — and the value chain itself can be offered as a service, creating a new business model.

These ESG standards, combined with functional specializations, allow the company to be uniquely positioned by the BD team. They are not merely compliance functions — they are **sources and levers of business development**.

> These services — often neglected — have emerged as important areas of national and global outsourcing.

### 46.3 Outsourcing and Offshoring of Value Chains

**Evolution of Value Chain Strategy:**

```
Maximum In-house Manufacture (Captive) 
    → Make-or-Buy (National Outsourcing) 
        → Global Offshoring (Asset-light and Agile)
```

**BD in R&D — Risk-Reward Matrix:**

| Type | Risk | Control | Reward |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exploratory Research (National Outsourcing) | Low | High | Low |
| Applied Research (National Outsourcing) | Low | High | Medium |
| Integrated Research (Global Outsourcing) | High | Low | High |
| Global Outsourcing | High | Low | Medium |

**Entities in the R&D Value Chain:**
- Universities → Fundamental Research
- Start-ups → Applied & Futuristic Research
- Contract Developers → Applied & Integrated Research

> **Start-ups have a critical role** in the development of the R&D value chain across entities.

**Outsourcing for Operations:**

| Mode | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Onshore Production | Local market competitiveness, inhouse |
| Onshore Outsourcing | Strategic sourcing for domestic needs |
| Offshore Production | Manufacturing in optimal global locations |
| Offshore Outsourcing | Global competitiveness through outsourced production |

**Procurement Structures:**

| Mode | Description |

| Dispersed Internal Procurement | Local subsidiaries procuring as per global specs |
| Outsourced Central Procurement | Fixed cost-light global procurement |

### 46.5 Strategic Effects of Forex Variations

| Scenario | Effect on Domestic Firms | Effect on Global Firms |
|---|---|---|
| **Devaluation** | Increases competitiveness of domestically produced products against imports; Decreases competitiveness of products with high import content | Increases competitiveness in domestic markets; No effects on global market |
| **Revaluation** | Decreases domestic competitiveness; Increases competitiveness of products with high import content | No direct effects domestically; Increases global competitiveness |

> Central bankers face a dilemma in allowing currency to move freely versus intervening. BD professionals must factor in forex dynamics in their global strategies.

|---|---|---|
| Talent Movement | Within each subsidiary | Across all subsidiaries globally |
| Talent Pool | Local talent for each subsidiary | A global management talent pool |

### 47.1 Purpose and Hierarchy of R&D

> **Definition:** R&D (also called Product Development/Design & Development) is the engine of growth for companies, and especially for firms seeking self-reliance.

**R&D Functions:**
- Develops new products and services to fulfil customer needs
- Sometimes develops products *ahead* of customer needs
- Sets product standards and supports manufacture
- Spans the full hierarchy: End-product → Systems → Components → Materials

**Hierarchy Illustration (Commercial Vehicle):**

```
Truck
├── Engine
│   ├── Cylinder Block (Ferrous Metals)
│   ├── Fuel Injection Pump (Electronics, Components)
│   └── Various Components
├── Gearbox
│   ├── Gears (Gear Forgings)
│   └── Transmission Casing
├── Chassis, Axles
└── Various Others
```

> R&D is as deep and as wide as the Bill of Material (BoM) of a product. Any change in material, component, or manufacturing process requires R&D re-engagement.

---

### 47.2 Types of R&D

| Factor | Basic Research | Applied Research | Development | Manufacturing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Objective** | Knowledge acquisition only | Knowledge for principle/model development | Apply research for new products | Manufacture per designs |
| **Personnel** | High knowledge workers | Knowledge workers | Knowledge workers | Mix of knowledge workers and doers |
| **Facilities** | Complex dedicated facilities | Regular lab facilities | Regular lab facilities | Standard production facilities |
| **Time to Output** | 5–10 years, uncertain | 3–5 years, certain | 1–3 years, certain | Daily and certain |
| **Cost** | Highest | High | High | High |
| **Financing** | Grants; Risk capital | Mix of equity and debt | Equity and debt | Internal generations |
| **Management** | Empowered; Self-directed | Empowered; Project-managed | Monitored; Project-managed | Intensely monitored hierarchically |
| **Contribution** | Phenomenal but risky | Very useful | Very useful | Essential |

**Integrated R&D Value Chain — Pharma Example:**

```
Fundamental Research (Universities/Start-ups)
    ↓
Applied Research (Innovate — new molecules like Adalimumab)
    ↓
Differentiation Research (Biosimilars, e.g., Amjevita)
    ↓
Generic Development (Cost-competitive alternatives)
```

> *The race for COVID-19 vaccines (Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna, AstraZeneca) compressed years of development into months — underscoring integrated R&D value chains.*

### 47.4 Marketing Management

> **Purpose:** A set of activities connecting the firm and the customer for mutual value creation — far more than just selling products; it serves customers, society and the firm based on aligned value propositions.

**The 4 Ps of Marketing (Physical-Digital Era):**

| P | Elements |
|---|---|
| **Product** | Design, Specifications, Styling, Quality, Warranty, Brand, Service |
| **Price** | Gross/Net price, Discounts, Payment terms, Credit period |
| **Promotion** | Sales force, Sales promotion, Offers, Advertising, Brand building, E-marketing |

**Granularity of Sales — Market Analysis Framework:**

```
Operating Market for the Firm (what we are actually doing)
    ↑ expand to
Available Market for the Firm
    ↑ expand to
Available Market for the Industry Segment
    ↑ expand to
Total Available Market for the Industry
```

> Analyse across Product Clusters (P1, P2...) × Market Groupings (M1, M2...) over a strategic time horizon.

**Product-Market Growth Grid:**

| | Existing Products | New Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | Market penetration improvement | New product launch in current markets |
| **New Markets** | Market development (seek new markets for existing products) | Diversification (new products in new markets) |

### 47.5 Financial Leadership

**Financial Strategy** is developed pursuant to business strategy and addresses the SWOT from strategic, operational, and financial perspectives. It typically covers a 5-year horizon; Year 1 serves as the annual budget.

**Key Financial Goals:**

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Liquidity Maximization** | Cash flows, EBITDA |
| **Level Maximization** | Profits, Sales, Total assets |
| **Ratio Maximization** | Net profit margin, Return on investment, Net profit to net worth |
| **Growth Maximization** | EPS, Market rate of return, Market cap |

> **Cash is king.** Sustainable operating profitability should be the primary goal.

**Financial Strategy — Important Caveats:**
- A company cannot be governed only by profit/shareholder wealth maximization
- Financial priorities change with economic volatility
- Strategic enterprise value includes intangibles beyond financial value
- Financial strategy, divorced from business realities, can destroy long-term investor wealth

**Integrated Financial Oversight:**
- Corporate Governance — run company on prudential lines
- Ethics and Compliance — honest corporate citizenship
- Business Continuity and Risk Management — robustness against disruptions

### 48.1 What is Business Negotiation?

> **Definition:** Negotiation is the process of conferring with someone to come to terms or reach an agreement. It is an essential plank of business development.

**Stages of High-Level Business Negotiation:**
1. Agreeing to meet and discuss potential collaboration
2. Agreeing for mutual due diligence of the parties
3. Agreeing on the key terms of a possible contract
4. Agreeing on the final terms of the contract

> *Real example from the professor:* When seeking a Toyota partnership for an auto company, the professor attended a conference knowing a Toyota senior executive would be there, introduced himself, and convinced Toyota to add the company to their India consideration list. *Even agreeing to meet is itself a negotiation.*

---

### 48.2 Key Business Terms in Negotiation

In a typical licensor-licensee or developer-marketer arrangement, the following must be negotiated:

| Parameter | Key Questions |
|---|---|
| Products | Which products to make? |
| Markets | Which markets to serve? |
| Volumes | What volumes to supply? Minimum guaranteed volumes? |
| Cost & Price | At what costs and prices? |
| Market Share | What should be the market share? |
| Fees | Development fees — lump sum or installments? |
| Remuneration | How remunerated upon commercialization? |
| Warranties | What warranties are explicit/implicit? |
| Term | Duration of agreement? |
| Renewal/Termination | Conditions for renewal or termination? |
| Intellectual Property | How will IP be protected? |

---

### 48.3 Interpersonal Dynamics of Negotiation

| Factor | Nature | Each Party's Objective |
|---|---|---|
| Assumptions | Not explicit | Make one's assumptions dominant for the other party |
| Eventualities | Not predictable | Own required eventuality is pre-set |
| Alternatives | Many options exist | Believes in its own alternative |
| Relationship | Short/medium/long term? Transactional/Emotional? | Each party has its own expectation |
| End Stage | Like a needle in a haystack | Clear to each party, unclear to the other |
| Communication | Multiple modes | Each finds its unique mode for advantage |
| Commitment | Give and take or who blinks first? | Toughest call of negotiation |
| Legitimacy | Not openly stated | Depends on personality |
| Interests | Not disclosed | Must be interpreted |

> **The fundamental challenge of negotiation:** Each day the negotiator wakes up to agree on something which the other party is not willing to agree on, except on the other party's terms.

**Four Negotiator Personality Types:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Competitor** | Confrontational; tries to win at all costs |
| **Collaborator** | Win-win oriented; cooperative |
| **Confronter** | Aggressive browbeating style |
| **Acceptor** | Yielding/accommodative |

---

### 48.4 Negotiating Models and Styles

**The Dual Concerns Negotiating Model:**

|  | Low Concern for Own Outcomes | High Concern for Own Outcomes |
|---|---|---|
| **High Concern for Others** | Yielding (Agreeing in haste) | Problem Solving (Win-Win) |
| **Low Concern for Others** | Inaction (Diffident) | Contending (I Win – You Lose) |

> A win-win approach stems from a **balanced interest** in both own and others' outcomes.

**Negotiating Styles:**

| Style | Description |
|---|---|
| Competitive | Try to get everything |
| Collaborative | Maximum possible for both |
| Accommodative | Yield entirely |
| Confrontation | Aggressive browbeating |
| Avoiding | Stay out of negotiation |
| Wear-out | Wait for desperation of other party |
| Compromising | Split the difference without logic |
| Retaliative | Threaten/accuse the other party |

---

### 48.5 Positive vs. Negative Negotiation Constructs

**Positive Negotiation Construct:**
- Explore mutual interests
- Quantify assets and restraints
- Table mutual propositions
- Identify gains for both parties
- Obtain maximum value by enlarging the pie
- Backed by: Business Competence + Business Conviction + Openness + Empathy

**Negative Negotiation Construct:**
- Revengeful Style: Injure the other party
- Self-injurious Style: Self-harm to spite the other
- Both styles: Lack of conviction, competence, openness, and empathy

---

### 48.6 Negotiation Stances

| Aspect | Soft Stance | Hard Stance | Balanced Stance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Relationship | Participants are friends | Participants are adversaries | Participants are problem solvers |
| Goal | Agreement | Victory | Win-win wise outcome |
| People | Soft on people and problem | Hard on people and problem | Soft on people, hard on problem |
| Trust | Trust others | Distrust others | Proceed objectively |
| Position | Change position easily | Dig in | Focus on interests, not positions |
| Commitment | Accept one-sided losses | Demand one-sided gains | Invest in options for mutual gain |
| Pressure | Yield to pressure | Apply pressure | Yield to principle, not pressure |

> **Best practice:** Balanced stance — separate people from the problem and apply objective criteria.

---

### 48.7 Perils of Self-Negotiation (Negotiating Against Yourself)

- Working to unrealistic timelines
- Imagining process collapse if other's point is not accepted
- Unwilling to put forth a point of view for fear of offending
- Unable to reach internal consensus before starting
- Wanting to finalise at any cost on any terms

---

### 48.8 Negotiation Tactics

| Tactic | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| Bidding | Various alternatives get known | Credibility impacted by multi-party negotiations |
| Brinkmanship | Nudges stalling partner to be realistic | Not to be deployed regularly |
| Good Guy/Bad Guy | Simultaneously soft and hard | Pattern becomes predictable and ineffective |
| First Concession | Develops 'good intentions' image | May be seen as weakness if too generous |
| Distant Carrot | Signals intent for strategic relationship | May be seen as a non-serious bargain |
| Information Overload | Canvas for considering all options | Can be seen as deliberate stalling |
| Deadline | Creates task and result orientation | Sanctity questioned if unrealistic/repetitive |
| Negotiator Change | Fresh approach | Process may turn volatile |
| Surprise Element | Can accelerate agreement | Non-event or wasted if off-mark |

---

### 48.9 Pricing in Negotiation

**Three Pricing Approaches:**
1. **Cost Plus** — Price based on cost + margin
2. **Market-bearing Price** — What the market will accept
3. **Future Value** — NPV of future net cash flows from the transaction

**Zone of Possible Negotiation (ZOPAN):**

```
Seller's Opening Point (Maximum Desired Price)
    ↓ Seller's Target Point
    ↓ Seller's Resistant Point (Lowest Willing Price)
──────── Zone of Possible Negotiation ────────
    ↑ Buyer's Resistant Point (Highest Willing Price)
    ↑ Buyer's Target Point
Buyer's Opening Point (Minimum Desired Price)
```

---

### 48.10 Power Play in Negotiation

**Sources of Negotiating Power:**
- Positional Power (Legitimate Authority)
- Rewards and Risks (Sanctions and Incentives)
- Force and Persuasion
- Information and Expertise/Wisdom
- Charisma (Referent Authority)
- Moral Authority
- Instinct and Intuition
- Standard or Unique Solution

**Negotiation Process Flow:**
```
Understand Mutual Interests
    ↓
Identify Goals, Stakes, Chemistry
    ↓
Exchange Information (SWOT)
    ↓
Agree on Scope and Price
    ↓
Agree on Terms and Conditions
    ↓
Establish the Relationship
    ↓
Close the Transaction
    ↓
Execute the Contract
```

### 49.1 Why Culture Matters for Business Development

> **Key tension:** Business is global while culture is local. BD requires partnering with multiple entities of different nationalities, building long-term partnerships, and institutionalizing collaboration to withstand environmental vicissitudes.

**What is Culture?**
Culture is the characteristics and knowledge of a particular group of people, encompassing language, way of life, religion, cuisine, social habits, music, and arts.

**Cultural Cascade:**

```
National Culture
    ↓ impacts
Regional Culture ←→ Political Culture
    ↓ impacts
Business Culture ←→ Social Culture
    ↓ impacts
Organisational Culture
    ↓
Collaboration / Competition / Negotiation dynamics
```

### 49.7 International Culture Challenges

**Multicultural Teams:**
- Some cultures are overawed by expatriates; others are hostile
- Communication etiquette differences
- 'Go with the flow' vs. 'shape paths' cultures
- Conflict resolution approaches vary

**Managerial Interactions:**
- Compliance vs. independence cultures (Boss/Colleagues/Subordinates dynamics)
- Contract negotiations: partnership vs. individualistic cultures

**Major Obstacles:**
- Discussion of differences seen as uncomfortable or threatening
- Assumption of similarity (homogeneity) or needless dissimilarity
- Cultural diversity lost as a resource; transformed into a handicap
- Cautious behaviour and unproductive politeness as coping mechanisms
- Low risk-taking; avoidance of confrontation; achieving smallest common denominator

| **Role in Negotiations** | None | None | Big role | Go with the flow | Big role | Big role |

### 49.9 Inclusive vs. Authoritative Cultures

| Dimension | Inclusive/Facilitative | Authoritative/Hierarchical |
|---|---|---|
| Disagreement | Encouraged based on facts; better quality decisions | Boss's opinion is final; quality of discussions poor |

### 50.1 The Competence-Outcome Approach

> **Framework:** Leader Attributes → Leader Competence → Leadership Outcomes

**Effectiveness** = The ability of a leader to accomplish the desired outcome efficiently.

| Component | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Attribute** | A quality or feature (e.g., patience in a teacher; studious nature in a student) |
| **Competence** | The ability to do something well; the knowledge/skill to accomplish a particular thing |
| **Leadership Competency** | A holistic set of capabilities needed to accomplish leadership tasks |

---

### 50.2 Four Factors of Leader Competence

| Factor | Description |
|---|---|
| **Trait** | A particular quality in personality; ingrained behaviours that up- or down-regulate knowledge/skill acquisition and interpersonal interactions |
| **Knowledge** | Gained through acquisition or experience; the underlying understanding that enables tasks and outcomes |
| **Skill** | Ability to do something well — comes from knowledge and its practical application; improves with practice |
| **Character** | The set of values and qualities that differentiate one person from another |

> Competence is the **cumulative result** of all four. While traits and character are behaviourally ingrained, knowledge and skill are continuously updated. Critically, traits and character can and should be modified to enhance effectiveness.

**Trait — Two Dimensions:**
- Personal Behaviour (enhances knowledge)
- Social Behaviour (connects with people)

**Knowledge — Two Dimensions:**
- Educational Knowledge (gain and apply)
- Experiential Knowledge (apply and gain)

**Skill — Two Dimensions:**
- Interpersonal Skill (aligns people)
- Technical Skill (delivers on tasks)

**Character — Two Dimensions:**
- Accountability to Values (personal accountability)
- Advocacy of Values (organisational accountability)

---

### 50.3 Vision-Strategy-Execution Trilogy

```
5-Year Vision
    ↓ (Annual Strategy Process)
Strategy
    ↓ (Continuous Execution)
Execution
    ↓ (Feedback loop)
Back to Vision (reviewed every ~2 years in dynamic environments)
```

> In the current dynamic environment: Vision reviewed every 2 years; Strategy analysed even within a year; Execution elevated through benchmarks and metrics with integrated performance management.

---

### 50.4 Leadership Attributes — Full Framework

Leadership attributes are of **two types**:

**A. Developmental Attributes** (Qualitative — intrinsic, shaped by family/education/socio-cultural journey):

| Attribute | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Trust** | Firm belief in reliability/truth/ability; leader should be trusting AND trustworthy |
| **Empathy** | Ability to understand and share the feelings of another — must be genuinely felt |
| **Passion** | Strong, unflinching desire to accomplish goals |
| **Courage** | Bravery and risk-taking ability — doing something without fear |
| **Integrity** | Honest with strong moral principles — the most important attribute |
| **Mentorship** | Guiding others based on experience and expertise |
| **Empowerment** | Sharing one's power to enable the team |
| **Reflection** | Contemplative thinking leading to considered decisions |
| **Aspiration** | Ambition to achieve something big and desirable |
| **Commitment** | Dedication to a cause or activity — staying committed to all other 9 attributes |

**B. Performance Attributes** (Quantitative — acquired through educational and experiential journey):

| Attribute | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Knowledge** | Facts, information, and skills from experience or education |
| **Vision** | Ability to plan the future with imagination or wisdom |
| **Strategy** | Plan of action to achieve long-term goals |
| **Execution** | Carrying out the plan; effectiveness judged by this ultimately |
| **Conceptual** | Ability to imagine and visualise issues; distil to core relevant ones |
| **Analytical** | Using logic, data and analysis to define problems and prioritize solutions |
| **Decisiveness** | Making decisions quickly and effectively — from vision to execution |
| **Communication** | Impactful speaking and perceptive listening; builds trust and rapport |
| **Collaboration** | Working with stakeholders — internal and external — for aligned purpose |
| **Negotiation** | Developing and finalizing terms of engagement between parties |

---

### 50.5 Communication–Collaboration–Negotiation Triad

These three skills are interrelated and mutually reinforcing:

```
Communication (foundational)
    ↓ enables
Collaboration (internal + external alignment)
    ↓ enables
Negotiation (finalizing terms of joint engagement)
```

**Communication:** Art and science of insightful speaking and perceptive listening. More than language skill — ability to process thoughts and constructs in real-time is key.

**Collaboration:** As important as competitiveness. Authentic communication builds credibility and rapport, leading to collaboration with greater alignment of purpose. Partners may be value chain participants or even competitors.

**Negotiation:** Not merely bargaining — it is the skill of developing and finalizing terms of engagement between parties in a common endeavour. Success depends on communication and collaboration.

> These three are also skills of efficiency and effectiveness — the impact must be felt quickly and the positive benefits must last long.

---

### 50.6 Conceptual–Analytical–Decisive Triad

```
Conceptualization
    → naturally occurs to leaders who grasp issues in entirety
    → distil issues to the core relevant ones

Analytics
    → worthwhile when reliable data sets are available
    → algorithmic, heuristic, AI/ML capabilities increasingly available
    → real-time observations and logic are preferred

Decision Making
    → digital tools: AI, machine learning, deep learning
    → integrates intuitive conceptualization with newer AI technologies
```

> **Decisiveness is facilitated** when issues are conceptually distilled and analytically dissected. A leader cannot delegate conceptual and analytical tasks — must be hands-on.

---

### 50.7 Soft Skills vs. Hard Skills

| Dimension | Soft Skills | Hard Skills |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | Personality driven; individual-specific; conditioned by ecosystem; influenced by core values | Technical and scientific; domain-specific |
| **Acquisition** | Difficult to acquire; easy to lose | Difficult to acquire; hard to lose |
| **Application** | Related to interpersonal and leadership areas | Related to design, manufacturing, quality, marketing |
| **Relationship** | Supplement and reinforce hard skills | Supplement and reinforce soft skills |

> *In people and technology-driven quality consulting, for example, the essentiality of soft and hard skills being together cannot be overemphasized.*

### 50.8 Passion and Individual Behaviour

A passionate and effective leader integrates all of the following:

```
Purpose and Focus
    ↔ Aptitude and Attitude
        ↔ Logic and Perspective
            ↔ Goals and Results
                ↔ Competency and Competitiveness
```

The CHRO and leadership team together must create an organisational ecosystem that sparks and sustains this passion in employees.
