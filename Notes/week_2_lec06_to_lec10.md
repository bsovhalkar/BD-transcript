# Lecture 6: Successful Businesses

## Overview
This lecture explores the fundamental drivers of business success through global and Indian examples, establishing that business development is both an art and a science.

---

## 1. Fundamental Drivers of Business Success

Sustainable business growth is derived from **three interconnected pillars**:

| Pillar | Description |
|---|---|
| **Product Innovation** | Developing differentiated, market-relevant products |
| **Operational Excellence** | Efficient, high-quality delivery of goods/services |
| **Customer Fulfilment** | Consistently meeting and exceeding customer expectations |

**Key insight:** Different companies emphasise different combinations of these drivers:
- **Apple** — balanced mix of all three
- **Tata Motors** — product and manufacturing-driven
- **Amazon** — supply chain / customer fulfilment-driven
- **L&T** — integration of skills across product, execution, and customer satisfaction
- **Hyundai** — tech-ops driven
- **Reliance Industries** — operational excellence-driven
- **Disney** — unmatched experience-driven
- **Microsoft** — leading-edge market position

# Lecture 7: Industry and Market

## Overview
This lecture introduces the theoretical framework of business development through the lens of industry scope, market types, segmentation, product concepts, and demand fulfilment strategies.

---

## 1. Scope of a Firm

The strategic scope for a firm is defined across two axes:

```
Industry                      Markets
  └── Segments                  └── Segments
       └── Products                   └── Customers
```

**Key insight:** The combination of industry segments × product lines × market segments × customer groups creates a canvas for business development through permutations and combinations.

**Decision challenge:** Breadth of product range for market maximisation — a moving puzzle where each decision (e.g., making a product break-even) introduces new constraints requiring iteration.

---

## 2. Macro-Markets

A **macro-market** is a business entity or customer agglomeration that provides a sustainable growth opportunity.

### Two Types of Macro-Markets

| Type | Nature | Modality |
|---|---|---|
| **Business Entities** | The primary markets; produce and consume goods | Modalities: exclusivity, stability, but with buyer power |
| **Marketplaces** | Agglomerations of individual customers | Reached individually or through intermediaries |

### Business Entities vs. Marketplaces

| Dimension | Business Entities | Marketplaces |
|---|---|---|
| Demand pattern | Standardised, predictable | Hard to predict; more volatile |
| Switching behaviour | Lower; long-term vendor loyalty | Higher; prone to brand switching |
| Buyer power | Strong | Moderate; manageable through advertising |
| Relationship nature | Exclusive, process-driven | Mass; relationship through communication |

---

## 3. Considerations in Choosing Macro-Markets

Four key considerations:

1. **Strong demand base** around felt or latent needs (e.g., pharmaceuticals — healthcare demand only grows)
2. **Homogeneous but with variations** — too homogeneous means no competitive differentiation possible; variations allow segmentation
3. **Supported by strong demographics** — population growth, income levels, urbanisation
4. **Amenable to partnerships and relationships** — particularly important for B2B markets

**Important note on technology shifts:** Demand base can shift — e.g., from small-molecule pharmaceuticals to biologics, or from petroleum to battery/hydrogen vehicles.

---

## 4. Product Concept

A product concept is an important foundation in strategic management. It helps position pioneering ideas before they become full products.

- Companies with multiple products experiment with raw product concepts to gather feedback
- A concept is evaluated for design, manufacturing, and commercialisation feasibility
- Early feedback shapes the market definition around the concept

---

## 5. Market Definition

**A product concept is viable only when a market gets defined around it.**

Example (Flip phone concept):
- Female users
- Millennials
- Multi-taskers
- Fashion seekers
- Discrete users

Early anchors and influencers form the core market for any new concept.

---

## 6. Market Segmentation

Not all interested customers are potential customers. Market segmentation allows targeted positioning.

### Four Types of Segmentation

| Type | Key Variables |
|---|---|
| **Demographic** | Population, income, language, gender, education, religion, family size, household nature |
| **Behavioural** | Instinctive, impulsive, cautious, conservative, adventurous, seasonal, loyal, essential |
| **Geographic** | International, continental, regional, national, zonal, state, city, town, village |
| **Psychographic** | Personality, lifestyle, values, associations, activism, opinions |

**Application:** Companies must always be willing to experiment with raw product concepts and segment them appropriately to broaden and deepen product appeal.

---

## 7. Micro-Markets

A **micro-market** is a specific subset of a macro-market, defined by granular characteristics. Micro-market development involves:

- Targeted product variants for specific sub-segments
- Localised promotions and distribution
- Building relationships one customer at a time within a defined geography or demographic

---

## 8. Types of Sales and Marketing Activities

### Demand Generation Activities

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Advertising** | TV, print, digital, outdoor, in-store |
| **Sales Promotion** | Discounts, buy-one-get-one, loyalty points |
| **Direct Sales** | Field sales force, telesales, direct mail |
| **Digital Marketing** | SEO, SEM, social media, email campaigns |
| **Event Marketing** | Trade shows, product launches, sponsorships |
| **Public Relations** | Press releases, media coverage, brand stories |

---

## 9. Market Research Approaches

| Approach | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Primary Research** | Direct data collection (surveys, interviews, focus groups) |
| **Secondary Research** | Analysis of existing data (industry reports, census data) |
| **Ethnographic Research** | Observation of customers in their natural environment |
| **A/B Testing** | Comparing two variants to determine which performs better |

---

## 10. Fulfilment Plan

**Order-to-Cash Cycle:**
1. Receive order from customer
2. Schedule production
3. Receive/produce the product
4. Deliver to customer
5. Receive payment
6. Monitor customer satisfaction
7. Generate next order (virtuous cycle)

**In practice:**
- Production is scheduled in **anticipation** of demand
- Products are made available at retail points before actual purchase
- Credit support mechanisms are extended to wholesalers and retailers
- Promotional activities influence both retailers and end-customers

**Tight working capital management** from a swift order-to-cash cycle reduces financial burden and improves business health.

---

## 11. Key Takeaways from Lecture 7

1. **Industry scope and market scope are distinct** — understanding both is fundamental to business development strategy.
2. **Macro-market selection is a strategic entry decision** — it deserves as much rigour as any major business decision.
3. **Segmentation unlocks latent demand** — breaking markets into demographic, behavioural, geographic, and psychographic segments enables precise targeting.
4. **The fulfilment cycle must be proactive** — waiting for orders before scheduling production leads to missed opportunities; anticipatory fulfilment is the gold standard.
5. **Bundling across product and customer profitability** — companies must bundle high-margin and low-margin products/customers strategically, not purely arithmetically.

# Lecture 8: Vision, Mission and Strategy

## Overview
This lecture explains the cascade from vision to mission to strategy, examines how key functions influence business strategy, and illustrates strategic marketing-orientation through Nike, Google, and Electrolux.

---

## 1. The Vision-Mission-Strategy Cascade

### Illustrated with a Global Pharmaceutical Company

| Level | Statement | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Vision** | "Contribute to a world free of infectious diseases with the therapeutics we develop, manufacture and provide" | Very broad; long-term; aspirational |
| **Mission** | "Become a 'One Stop Shop' for medicines and vaccines that cure and prevent any infectious disease" | More specific; narrows scope to medicines and vaccines |
| **Strategy** | "Focus on development, manufacturing and marketing of medicines and vaccines — from APIs to Finished Dosage Forms" | Actionable; defines what the company will do and how |

### Functional Strategies (Cascaded from Strategy)

| Function | Strategy Statement |
|---|---|
| **R&D** | Green chemistry for APIs; nanotechnology and pre-filled syringes for FDFs; focus on biologics and vaccines driven by genomics |
| **Manufacturing** | Stay technologically modern but asset-light; global network of in-house and outsourced facilities (offshored and onshored) |
| **Marketing** | Clinical integration of all stakeholders; genomic profiling for personalised medicine; collaborate on individual Genomic ID initiative |

**Key insight:** Vision is broad, mission is specific, and strategy is actionable. Each functional strategy provides colour and content to the overall business strategy.

---

## 2. Influence of Key Functions on Business Strategy

| Decision Component | Marketing | R&D | Operations | Finance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic business direction | High | High | Low | Medium |
| Strategic business scope | High | High | Low | Medium |
| New product development | High | High | Medium | Medium |
| New market development | High | Low | Low | Medium |
| Choice of strategic partners | Medium | Low | Low | Medium |
| Choice of facility locations | Low | Low | High | Medium |
| Capital investments | Low | Low | Medium | High |
| Pricing | High | Low | Low | High |
| Product lifecycle | High | High | Low | Low |
| Divestments/Exits | High | Low | Medium | High |

**Insight:** Marketing and R&D dominate strategic decisions related to products and markets; Finance dominates capital and exit decisions; Operations is most critical in facility and execution decisions.

---

## 3. Market-Oriented Strategic Approach

Companies with strong market and marketing orientation exhibit these characteristics:

| Characteristic | Description |
|---|---|
| Market-driven business development | Strategy is pulled by market intelligence, not pushed by production |
| Manufacturing that is asset-light | Combine internal and external, organic and outsourced manufacture |
| Robust investments in sales and aftersales | Customer service is viewed as an investment, not a cost |
| Strong marketing organisation | Dedicated marketing function with market and customer interface |
| Customer-centric culture | Customer experience metrics are embedded at all levels |
| Focus on customer experience | End-to-end experience design from discovery to purchase to loyalty |
| Business effectiveness measured through marketing metrics | Revenue, market share, NPS, CSAT as primary business KPIs |

**Outcome:** Market-oriented companies are environmentally **proactive**, **responsive**, or **agile** as required by context.

## 5. Case Study: Google — Growth Through Others' Marketing

Google was founded in 1998 as a search engine offering its product **for free**. This zero-pricing policy was a marketing coup that built boundless value.

### Google's Revenue Model (Without Charging Users)

| Product | Mechanism | Revenue |
|---|---|---|
| **AdWords** | Pay-per-click ads based on keyword searches | Advertisers pay per click |
| **AdSense** | Contextual display ads on third-party websites | Website publishers earn per click; Google takes a cut |
| **Marketing Asset Management** | Data analytics optimising web marketing for companies | Consulting/tool fees |
| **Android & YouTube** | Platforms for broad range of generic and customised products | Advertising + App revenue |

**Google's four strategic pillars:**
1. Create and distribute products for **free**
2. Continuous creation of **online advertising space** for targeted advertisers
3. **Marketing asset management** for companies through data analytics
4. Leverage **Android and YouTube** for reach and revenue

**Key insight:** Google never charged users but monetised their attention and data, creating a $150 billion+ advertising business. The organisational ecosystem of Alphabet enables boundary-pushing innovation.

## 6. Case Study: Electrolux — Driven by Holistic Management

Electrolux is a global leader in home and professional appliances.

### Demand Drivers

| Driver | Description |
|---|---|
| Per Capita Income | Rising incomes → higher appliance adoption |
| Lifestyles | Changing lifestyles → demand for premium appliances |
| Consumer Spending | Urbanisation drives white goods demand |
| Urbanisation | Growing cities → growing appliance markets |

### Competitive Advantage — Four Pillars

| Pillar | Description |
|---|---|
| **Design Legacy** | Scandinavian heritage of elegant, minimalist design |
| **Innovation** | Nanotechnology, digitalization, automation, modularization |
| **Operational Excellence** | Focus on operating margin, RONA, and capital turnover |
| **Profitable Growth** | Wide product range, dedicated R&D, people and culture |

**Electrolux's key strategic insight:** It does not see itself as a refrigerator or washing machine manufacturer. It positions itself as a **food and clothes experience provider**, which fundamentally shifts the innovation agenda and customer engagement.

---

## 7. Strategic Marketing — Product and Customer Profitability Bundling

### Bundling Approach for Macro-Markets

When managing a macro-market, companies should NOT take a purely arithmetic view of product or customer profitability. Instead:

- **Premium products** should cross-subsidise low-margin products
- **Low-margin products** should ensure that all types of customers remain loyal to the brand

**Example:** A hospital pharmacy cannot refuse to supply low-cost generic medicines just because they are unprofitable. The full bundle (ICU medicines + generics) ensures complete customer trust.

**Rule:** Bundle products across the profitability spectrum; bundle customers across the loyalty spectrum. Strategic (business) view of profitability > Arithmetic view.

---

## 8. Key Takeaways from Lecture 8

1. **Vision → Mission → Strategy** is a cascade from broad aspiration to specific action — each level must be internally consistent.
2. **Functional strategies must align** with the overall business strategy; marketing and R&D have the highest strategic influence in most industries.
3. **Market orientation is a culture, not just a function** — it must permeate all levels of the organisation.
4. **Nike, Google, and Electrolux** demonstrate three distinct approaches to strategic marketing: athlete endorsement, zero-cost user acquisition, and experience repositioning.
5. **Product/customer bundling** requires a strategic lens — pure profitability arithmetic can destroy long-term relationships and brand trust.

# Lecture 9: Goals

## Overview
This lecture covers the four key types of business goals, the balanced scorecard framework, goal hierarchy across organisations, SMART goal-setting, functional goal cascading, and their application to business development.

---

## 1. The Four Business Goals

Every business pursues four interconnected goals:

| Goal | Driver | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue** | Scale and scope of operations | Grows with market expansion |
| **Profit** | Cost of production vs. price realised | Function of efficiency + pricing power |
| **Market Share** | Strategy and marketing effectiveness | Reflects competitive standing |
| **Market Capitalisation** | Real (revenue/profit/share) + Perceived (macro/geopolitical factors) | Partially within and partially beyond management control |

**Key insight:** Business strategy seeks **year-over-year growth** on macro parameters; operational strategies pursue **monthly and daily** performance metrics.

---

## 2. The Balanced Scorecard (BSC) of Goals

Proposed by **Prof. Robert S. Kaplan and David P. Norton** in their *1992 Harvard Business Review* article, the BSC remains relevant today.

### Four Perspectives

| Perspective | Focus | Key Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Financial** | Revenue, profit, ROI, EVA | "How do we look to shareholders?" |
| **Customer** | Market share, satisfaction, loyalty, NPS | "How do customers see us?" |
| **Internal Business** | Process efficiency, quality, innovation | "What must we excel at internally?" |
| **Innovation & Learning** | Employee capability, culture, learning, R&D | "Can we continue to improve and create value?" |

**Why "balanced"?** The BSC prevents over-emphasis on financial metrics by simultaneously tracking customer, process, and learning perspectives — all of which are required for sustainable growth.

---

## 3. Hierarchy of Goals

Goals cascade from the highest organisational level to the most granular:

```
Conglomerate Goals
    └── Company 1 Goals → Company 2 Goals → Company 3 Goals
          └── Business 1 Goals → Business 2 Goals → ...
                └── Market 1.1 Goals → Market 1.2 Goals → ...
```

### Pharma Example (Rs. 1000 Crore Company Goal)

```
Company Goal: Rs 1000 Crore
├── Pharma Goal: Rs 700 Crore
│   ├── Retail: Rs 100 Cr
│   ├── Clinic: Rs 200 Cr
│   └── Hospital: Rs 400 Cr
│       ├── Metro: Rs 250 Cr
│       ├── Tier 1&2: Rs 100 Cr
│       └── Rural: Rs 50 Cr
└── Device Goal: Rs 300 Crore
    ├── Retail: Rs 50 Cr
    └── Institutional: Rs 250 Cr
```

**Insight:** Goal optimisation becomes exponentially more complex as companies become multi-business and businesses become multi-market. Yet this complexity must be managed for alignment.

---

## 4. Conglomerate Grid of Goals

For a conglomerate with 3 companies × 6 businesses × 5 markets = **90 market-level goal nodes** to manage. The challenges include:
- Overlapping goals across companies/businesses
- Conflicting priorities between divisions
- Synergies that can be leveraged if goals are aligned

---

## 5. Goals vs. Objectives

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Goals** | Metrics of performance; emanate from mission | "Become Top-5 Speciality Chemicals Manufacturer Globally" |
| **Objectives** | Goals + strategic purpose/business model flair | "Become Top-5 Speciality Chemicals Manufacturer Globally of Indian Origin" |

Objectives integrate **direction** (goals) with **identity** (purpose and model).

---

## 6. SMART Goals

The SMART acronym ensures goals lead to clarity and organisational alignment:

| Letter | Meaning | Nuances |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Specific | Simple, Sensible, Significant |
| **M** | Measurable | Meaningful, Motivating |
| **A** | Achievable | Agreed, Attainable |
| **R** | Relevant | Reasonable, Realistic, Resourced, Results-based |
| **T** | Time-bound | Time-based, Time-sensitive, Time-efficient, Timely |

**Outcome:** SMART goals drive clarity, alignment, and effective execution across the organisation.

---

## 7. Functional Goal Cascading

Corporate goals must be functionally cascaded for effective execution.

### Example: Speciality Chemicals Company

**Corporate Goal:** Become Top-5 Speciality Chemicals Company Globally

| Function | Functional Goal |
|---|---|
| **R&D** | Innovate end-chemicals and intermediates/starting materials |
| **Operations** | Establish an end-to-end integrated value chain for globally competitive manufacturing |
| **Finance** | Provide resources required for development, manufacturing, and marketing |
| **Marketing/Sales** | Establish a global sales, marketing, and distribution network |
| **Quality** | Ensure highest quality products at the lowest possible costs |

---

## 8. Case Example: Business Goals in FMCG (Gummies Innovation)

An FMCG company decides to enter the over-the-counter wellness space with nutritional gummies:

- **Strategic growth goal** → Establish a product-driven **Strategic Business Unit (SBU)** for gummies
- **SBU business goals** → Revenue target for gummies category
- **Sales and marketing goals** → Channel penetration, consumer trial, repeat purchase metrics

**Product range introduced:**
- Anti-glycemic gummies for diabetics
- Nutritional gummies for kids
- Multivitamin gummies for children

**Insight:** The evolution of FMCG business development — from advertising-led "marketing magic" to deeply matching consumer needs with products — represents a higher order of business development. Packaging design, communication strategy, and consumer experience all converge.

---

## 9. Key Takeaways from Lecture 9

1. **Goals are the foundation of business strategy** — revenue, profit, market share, and market capitalisation are interconnected, not independent.
2. **The BSC provides a balanced view** — financial metrics alone are insufficient; customer, process, and learning perspectives are equally critical.
3. **Goal hierarchy ensures alignment** — corporate goals must cascade to the lowest operational level to ensure everyone pulls in the same direction.
4. **SMART goals prevent ambiguity** — vague goals lead to vague execution; specificity and time-binding drive accountability.
5. **Functional goal cascading is non-negotiable** — without function-level goals, corporate goals remain wishes, not commitments.

## 5. The HUL Marketing War — Adversity #1

### Background
HUL (Hindustan Unilever Limited), the dominant FMCG multinational in India, launched a full-scale **six-year marketing war** targeting Marico's core business — Parachute Coconut Oil.

### HUL's Strategy
- Leveraged its massive distribution muscle and brand equity
- Aggressive price competition
- Heavy advertising and promotional spends
- Distribution incentives to retailers to switch

### Marico's Response
- **Held their ground** — did not panic or capitulate
- **Strengthened its product quality** and brand positioning
- **Doubled down on customer relationships** and distribution partnerships
- **Invested in innovation** — new product variants and category extensions
- **Leadership conviction** — Harsh Mariwala rallied the entire organization behind the Parachute brand

**Outcome:** Marico not only survived the HUL war but emerged **stronger and more innovative**, retaining its coconut oil leadership while growing new businesses.

---

## 6. Adversity #2: Family Separation

### Background
Marico also went through a **family separation** — a common challenge in Indian family-owned businesses when growth demands professionalisation and governance reforms.

### How Marico Navigated It
- **Clarity of purpose** — the business mission remained undiluted
- **Professional management** — the company had already heavily invested in professional leadership, reducing dependence on any single family member
- **Organisational culture** — collaborative and entrepreneurial culture absorbed the disruption
- **Strategic continuity** — growth strategy remained on track through the transition

## 7. Lessons from the Marico Case Study

### 7.1 Comprehensive Business Development Framework

Marico's success can be attributed to a **360-degree strategy** covering:

| Dimension | Marico's Approach |
|---|---|
| **Product Development** | Continuous innovation from coconut oil to premium hair care, Saffola, gummies |
| **Manufacturing** | Integrated manufacturing with efficiency focus |
| **Distribution** | National distribution system from day one; reaching retail |
| **Marketing** | Brand-building through emotional connect and product quality |
| **Organisation** | Early and aggressive professionalisation; TQM, 3-Ps, 5-Es programmes |
| **Culture** | Collaborative and entrepreneurial; rally-around-the-leader in adversity |
| **Internationalisation** | Bangladesh, Dubai, US — phased international expansion |

### 7.2 Principles of Business Development Excellence (from Marico)

1. **Professional leadership at the right time** — Harsh Mariwala's decision to induct top managers from MNCs was decisive
2. **Brand as fortress** — Parachute's 50% market share was defended because the brand stood for genuine quality, not just advertising
3. **Adversity as accelerant** — the HUL war accelerated Marico's innovation, distribution depth, and brand strengthening
4. **Culture over strategy** — Marico's collaborative culture is what enabled the team to withstand the marketing war
5. **Portfolio diversification alongside core strength** — Saffola, Hair & Care, Revive, and global Ayurvedic lines were built without diluting Parachute

### 7.3 The Broader Lesson for Entrepreneurs

> *"Adversity should bring in the best of us — as individuals, as organisations, and as companies."*
> — Prof. C Bhaktavatsala Rao

Success *despite* adversity requires:
- Continuous addition of new products and businesses for future growth
- Defending the current leadership position vigorously
- Enhancing wherever possible through innovation
- A 360-degree strategy executed across all functions

## 8. Key Takeaways from Lecture 10

1. **Business development excellence is built over decades** — Marico's journey from 1887 to 2022 shows that great businesses are constructed over generations.
2. **Professionalisation is non-negotiable for scale** — family businesses that resist professionalisation hit a ceiling; Marico broke through by inducting talent aggressively.
3. **Leadership character determines organisational character** — Harsh Mariwala's hunger for growth, openness to talent, and courage under fire defined Marico's culture.
4. **Marketing wars can be won by incumbents** — with strong product quality, deep distribution, and brand conviction, Marico defeated a far larger HUL in the coconut oil category.
5. **A diversified portfolio with a strong core** is the optimal business model — the Parachute core funded Marico's diversification, while diversification reduced dependence on the core.
