## 2. Key Explanations

### 2.1 Customers vs. Consumers

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Customer** | An individual or entity that *purchases* a product or service | An administrative manager buying software for the firm |
| **Consumer** | An individual or entity that *uses* the product or service for personal needs | The employees who actually use the software |

- A **dealer** is a customer for an automobile firm; the dealer's end-buyers are the consumers.
- The company must understand both — the dealer (customer) and the end-user (consumer) — because **demand at the consumer level drives the entire marketing value chain**.
- In practice, the two terms are used interchangeably in strategic and business development courses.

---

### 2.2 Individual Consumer Markets vs. Business Consumer Markets

| Factor | Consumer Markets | Business Markets |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Buyers** | Huge in number (lakhs to millions) | Relatively fewer |
| **Products/Services** | Common | Common and specific |
| **Access** | Relatively easy | Relatively harder |
| **Branding** | Strongly influences perception | Low branding impact |
| **Packaging** | Designed to attract | Primarily for protection |
| **Promotion** | Influences customers | Sometimes even prohibited |
| **Demand** | Relatively elastic | Relatively inelastic |
| **Buying Process** | Direct, in-store or online | Procedure-bound, through intermediaries |
| **Decision Making** | Individual (at times influenced by others) | Multi-tier with levels of authority |
| **Code of Conduct** | General buying-selling principles | Ethical code with legal consequences |
| **Extent of Contract** | Limited to individual purchase; warranty expected | Long-term, project-specific; strong AMC requirements |

> **Strategic Question:** Should the company focus on consumer markets, business markets, or both? (e.g., edible oil companies serve both individual consumer packs and bulk tins for HoReCa businesses)

---

### 2.3 Customer-Oriented Organisation

**Traditional Organisation:** A pyramid — top management at apex, frontline at the base. Only the frontline has strong customer connect.

**Contemporary Concept:** A circular organisation where **every function** (R&D, Production, Finance, Marketing, IT, Others) is encircled by and directly connects with customers.

**Leadership Examples:**
- **Azim Premji (Wipro):** Personally visited clients — large and small — and sought feedback during his tenure as full-time CEO.
- **Mike Ball (Hospira):** On taking over as CEO, his first action was a "listening tour" to understand how the company serves customers — laying the foundation for meaningful change.

> **Key Insight:** Chief Marketing Officers must set the tone at the top in customer interface by frequently meeting customers. Customer connect must not be left only to the sales team.

---

### 2.4 Types of Customer Needs

Customers have diverse and layered needs:

| Need Type | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Individual Needs** | Personal daily requirements |
| **Family Needs** | Needs of the family unit |
| **Social Needs** | Needs driven by relationships and community |
| **Business Needs** | Professional and organisational requirements |
| **Functional Needs** | Utility-based requirements |
| **Seasonal Needs** | Time-bound or seasonal requirements |
| **Esteem Needs** | Status and image-related needs |
| **Replacement Needs** | Needs arising from product obsolescence or wear |
| **Augmentation Needs** | Needs for upgrading or enhancing existing products/services |

---

### 2.5 Maslow Need Hierarchy and Its Limitations

**Maslow's Hierarchy (bottom to top):**
1. Physiological Needs (food, water, shelter)
2. Safety Needs (security, protection)
3. Social Needs (belonging, love)
4. Esteem Needs (recognition, status)
5. Self-Actualisation (self-development and realisation)

**Limitation in Practice:** Maslow postulates that once a lower-order need is fulfilled, the individual moves to the next. However, different products in the *same category* fulfil multiple need levels depending on the customer's income and background:

| Income Group | Product |
|---|---|
| Super High Income | Lamborghini |
| High Income | Audi Q7 |
| Upper Middle Income | Maruti Ciaz |
| Lower Middle Income | i20 |
| Low Income | Scooter |

> **Key Insight:** Customers of different income groups have their own specific need hierarchies *regardless* of what was fulfilled at the previous income level. **Demographic and socio-economic factors make the hierarchy non-linear.**

---

### 2.6 Key Customer Characteristics

Customers come with different attributes, backgrounds, and influences:

| Influencer | Key Factors |
|-----------|------------|
| **Demographic** | Age profile, income patterns, gender diversity, ethnicity |
| **Socio-Cultural** | Social values, mores, relationships, historical and religious influences |
| **Economic** | GDP, per capita income, savings, price indices, consumer spending, tax levels |
| **Lifestyle** | Quality of life, comparators and benchmarks |
| **Technological** | Use of technology for productivity, convenience, esteem; technology adoption in professional and personal life |
| **Natural** | Resource endowments, climatic conditions, nature's vagaries, environmental concerns |

> **Key Insight:** These factors have **overlapping influences**. Two customers with the same income level can have different need profiles based on socio-cultural background or technology-friendliness. Customer characteristics resist straight-jacketed classification.

---

### 2.7 Vertical vs. Horizontal Customer Engagement

| Type | Description | Risk/Reward |
|------|------------|------------|
| **Vertical Customer Engagement** | All products required by a single cluster of customers; meets multiple needs of a single customer across income/life levels | Requires a full product range from low-end to high-end. Placing *scope ahead of scale* can lead to a serious strategic trap (manufacturing complexity) |
| **Horizontal Customer Engagement** | Single class of products to multiple customer clusters across geographies; singular product need for a large customer cross-section | Requires product specialisation + geographic diversification = **scale efficiency** |

**Strategic Recommendation:** A firm should first build *scale* by meeting common customer needs globally (horizontal), and then move into vertical engagement with diverse needs of specialised groups. Timely vertical migration provides scope advantage.

---

### 2.8 Customer Value Perception

Customer value is a **perceived** value — the customer calculates it mentally based on:

**Total Customer Benefit:**
- Usage Benefit (functional value during use)
- Ownership Benefit (pride, practicality of ownership)
- Image Benefit (brand/esteem image)
- Resale Value (value at end of life cycle)

**Total Customer Cost:**
- Purchase Cost
- Operating Cost
- Service and Parts Cost
- Image and Esteem Loss (technological obsolescence eroding status, e.g., an old-generation smartphone in an evolved office environment)

**Three Routes to Value:**
1. **Product** — Designed for a price-performance point relevant to the customer (e.g., Cadbury from candy bar to family pack)
2. **Service** — Uptime during use, parts support, robust warranty, nationwide service network, interoperability
3. **Optimisation** — Continuous performance monitoring and ongoing upgrades (e.g., mobile OS updates)

> **Key Insight:** Companies are placing increasing efforts on service and optimisation verticals. Software has become a key tool for sustaining higher value perception **after** the product has been sold.

---

### 2.9 Traditional Customer Model vs. New Internet Economy

**Traditional Model (Transaction Economy):**
- Firm creates value proposition → Products/services provide value → Consumers represent demand
- Firm is the provider; customer is the receiver. Interaction is purely transactional.
- Focus on tangible attributes: look & feel, functionality, utility, lifecycle cost

**New Internet Economy:**
- Universal digital market → Instant filtered search → Product description → Multiple options → Purchase transaction → Product delivery → Customer support & service → Product return/trade-in
- Customer can now: **View Physical, Buy Physical** | **View Physical, Buy Digital** | **View Digital, Buy Physical** | **View Digital, Buy Digital**

> **Key Insight:** The digital marketplace has become an integral part of the customer search and fulfilment experience. The customer is no longer merely reactive — they arrive informed, compare across platforms, share reviews, and co-create brand reputation.

---

### 2.10 The DAWN Framework — A New Need Spectrum

Beyond Maslow, this course introduces an original practical framework: **DAWNs (Desires, Aspirations, Wants, Needs)**

| Level | Definition | Strategic Implication for Firms |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------------|
| **Need** | A requirement for something essential or very important; the prime mover for most economic activity | Firms must fulfil all consumer needs efficiently and economically |
| **Want** | A requirement as a matter of routine | Firms must fulfil wants along with needs to stay relevant |
| **Desire** | A strong wish to have or do something | Economic development should convert desires into needs; firms must be prepared to cater to desires |
| **Aspiration** | A strong requirement for something not easily achievable; gives the economy a "developed" qualification | Economic aspirations prime society to focus on technological innovation |

> **Key Insight:** Companies can *move customers up the DAWN ladder* — from need to want to desire to aspiration — through thoughtful products, upgrade offers, and trade-in programs (e.g., NPTEL self-paced programs fulfilling learning aspirations; real estate companies promising apartment upgrades along with career growth).

---

### 2.11 Customer Multiple Personalities and the Customer Ecosystem

Customers are influenced by multiple overlapping ecosystems:

| Layer | Intrinsic Motivation |
|-------|---------------------|
| **Individual** | Personal DAWNs and purchasing capability |
| **Family** | Family needs may diverge from individual needs (e.g., save vs. spend) |
| **Friends** | Peer influence on lifestyle and purchase decisions |
| **Organisation** | Professional needs governed by business goals and budgets |

**Well-aligned Customer Ecosystem (Individual example — Asian Paints):**
- Core need: Have a home → Furnish a home → Lifestyle in a home → Social and professional recognition
- Asian Paints moved from surface protection → surface attraction → interior design → interior fitments → home furnishings → holistic home design

**Business Customer Ecosystem (Hierarchy of Business Needs):**
1. Basic business operations
2. Viable business operations (cost-efficient)
3. Competitive business operations
4. Growing business operations (innovative)
5. Sustainable business operations

> **Key Insight:** Business development must align itself to the **phase of growth** of the business customer. The offering must evolve as the customer's business evolves.

---

### 2.12 Business Development and New Business Models

Using the food industry as an illustrative canvas:

| Model | Description | Customer Value |
|-------|------------|---------------|
| **One Restaurant, One Food Type** | Traditional, specialised, pre-decided choice | Limited to one cuisine |
| **Food Delivery** | Multiple cuisines from multiple restaurants to doorstep | Choice without ambience |
| **Foodplex** | Restaurants collaborate; customer can walk into any and order from any | Choice with ambience |

> **Key Insight:** Business development is about providing **personalised experiences to a wide spectrum of customers**. Companies must move beyond the transactional "provider-receiver" paradigm to understand the full DAWN hierarchies of individual customers, groups of customers, and businesses — and then design innovative models to fulfil them.

## 3. Important Insights (Lecture 21 Summary)

1. **Customers ≠ Consumers, but both matter.** A company must understand the full demand chain — from the purchasing customer (e.g., a dealer) to the end-consuming customer — to drive effective business development.
2. **Customer-orientation must be enterprise-wide.** The traditional pyramid limits customer insight to frontline teams. Circular, customer-encircled organisations — exemplified by leaders like Azim Premji and Mike Ball — create structural advantage.
3. **Maslow's hierarchy has real-world limitations.** Income alone does not determine need hierarchy; socio-cultural, technological, and lifestyle factors create non-linear customer journeys.
4. **The DAWN Framework is a richer toolkit than Maslow.** By distinguishing needs, wants, desires, and aspirations, firms can design offerings that *evolve with the customer's ambition*, not just current purchasing power.
5. **Vertical vs. Horizontal engagement is a key strategic decision.** Scale first (horizontal) → then scope (vertical) is the recommended sequence to avoid strategic traps.
6. **Customer value is perceived, not absolute.** The total value equation (benefits minus costs, including image and esteem) must be actively managed by the firm across the product, service, and optimisation dimensions.
7. **The internet economy has shifted power to the customer.** Firms can no longer be purely product-centric. The new paradigm demands customer co-creation, real-time feedback, and flexible multi-channel purchase journeys.
8. **Business models must innovate around customer need ecosystems.** As Asian Paints demonstrated, understanding the full customer ecosystem (from home ownership to lifestyle to recognition) unlocks entirely new revenue streams and deepens competitive differentiation.

## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. Customers and Consumers — The Key Distinction

In business parlance, *customer* and *consumer* are used interchangeably, but a strategic distinction exists:

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Customer** | Individual or entity that **purchases** a product/service | Administrative manager buying office supplies |
| **Consumer** | Individual or entity that **uses** a product/service for personal need | Employees using those supplies |

> "Trying to segregate customers and consumers in a vast market is like searching for needles in a haystack."

**Why it matters strategically**: The *consumer* (end-user) shapes demand. A dealer is the customer of an automobile firm, but it is the ultimate consumer's preferences that determine what models sell. Firms must understand the consumer to decode the true demand signal.

---

### 2. Individual Consumer Markets vs. Business Markets

Strategic marketing begins with choosing whether to operate in consumer markets, business markets, or both.

| Factor | **Consumer Markets** | **Business Markets** |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Buyers** | Huge in numbers (lakhs to millions) | Relatively fewer |
| **Products/Services** | Common, standardised | Common and specific (e.g., adhesives for different joints) |
| **Access** | Relatively easy (kirana to supermarkets) | Hard; needs appointments, negotiations |
| **Branding** | Strong influence on perception | Low impact; buyers test independently |
| **Packaging** | Designed to attract and display | Primarily for product protection |
| **Promotion** | Influences purchase decisions | May be prohibited (ethical restrictions) |
| **Demand** | Relatively **elastic** (price-sensitive) | Relatively **inelastic** (capability-driven) |
| **Buying Process** | Direct — in-store or online | Procedure-bound; may use intermediaries |
| **Decision-Making** | Individual; sometimes influenced by family | **Multi-tier** with levels of authority |
| **Code of Conduct** | General buyer-seller principles | Formal ethical code; consequences for breach |
| **Extent of Contract** | Limited to individual purchase; manufacturer warranty | Project-specific, long-term; strong warranty + AMC |

**Example**: Edible oil companies sell consumer packs (pouches, pet bottles) AND tins for the hotel-restaurant-café (HoReCa) business market. The same product but different packaging, pricing, and relationship models.

### 3. Customer-Oriented Organisation

#### 3.1 The Traditional Pyramid Model

```
         ┌──────────────┐
         │  Top Mgmt    │   ← Least customer contact
         ├──────────────┤
         │ Middle Mgmt  │   ← Moderate customer contact
         ├──────────────┤
         │  Frontline   │   ← Primary customer contact
         └──────────────┘
```

The weakness: Customer connect *decreases* as seniority increases. The real pulse of the market is siloed at the bottom.

#### 3.2 The Contemporary Circular Model

The Prof. proposes a **circular (ring) model** where customers encircle the organisation, with every function — R&D, Production, Marketing, Finance, IT, Others — in continuous, equal contact with customers.

> The IT leader, for example, should not merely build architecture; they should visit distributors, retailers, and consumers to understand how real-time data can optimise the supply chain for customer fulfilment.

**This is the true definition of a customer-centric organisation.**

#### 3.3 Leadership Examples in Customer Connect

| Leader | Company | Action | Outcome |
|--------|---------|--------|---------|
| **Azim Premji** | Wipro | Personally visited all clients (small and large), sought feedback, shared views on technology | Positioned Wipro as a reliable, customer-centric partner; strengthened customer bonding |
| **Mike Ball** | Hospira | Conducted a **"Listening Tour"** as his first act as CEO — visited every geography where Hospira operated | Laid the foundation for company transformation based on direct customer insight |

> "CMOs must set the tone at the top — meetings should be spontaneous, genuine, and goal-directed, not arranged and performative."

### 4. Who are Customers? — Types of Needs

A customer judges every purchase through two filters:
1. **Need fulfilment** — Does the product solve the problem?
2. **Price affordability** — Is the value worth the cost?

**Types of Customer Needs:**

| Need Type | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Functional** | Utility for undertaking an activity | Buying a car to commute |
| **Esteem** | Social acceptability and recognition | Buying a premium brand pen |
| **Augmentation** | Enhancing existing capability | Buying a second laptop for redundancy |
| **Replacement** | Product has exhausted its utility | Replacing an old refrigerator |
| **Seasonal** | Festival or weather-driven needs | Winter woollens, Diwali sweets |
| **Individual** | Personal requirements | A hobby kit |
| **Family** | Household requirements | A washing machine |
| **Social** | Community or peer-group needs | Social media subscriptions |
| **Business** | Organisational requirements | Office ERP software |

> Even a "one-time" product like a lift creates recurring needs — daily cleaning, periodic maintenance, annual service contracts.

### 5. Maslow's Need Hierarchy — Application in Marketing

| Level | Need Type | Description | Marketing Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **5** | Self-Actualisation | Self-development, realisation | Premium education, spiritual retreats |
| **4** | Esteem | Recognition, status, self-worth | Luxury cars, branded watches |
| **3** | Social | Belonging, love, peer acceptance | Social platforms, family cars |
| **2** | Safety | Security, protection, stability | Insurance, home security systems |
| **1** | Physiological | Food, water, shelter | FMCG, real estate, utilities |

**Key Insight**: A single product can satisfy multiple Maslow levels. An automobile satisfies Level 1 (transportation/shelter from elements), Level 2 (safety features), and Level 4 (brand prestige).

#### 5.1 Limitations of Maslow in Consumer Marketing

Maslow assumes sequential fulfilment — satisfy Level 1 before Level 2, and so on. **This does not hold in practice**:

| Income Group | Product Preferred | Maslow Level Satisfied |
|-------------|------------------|----------------------|
| Low Income | Scooter | Level 1–2 |
| Lower Middle | i20 Car | Level 1–3 |
| Upper Middle | Maruti Ciaz | Level 2–4 |
| High Income | Audi Q7 | Level 3–5 |
| Super High | Lamborghini | Level 4–5 |

> A higher-income consumer does **not** stop using lower-category products. They develop **parallel need hierarchies**, not strictly sequential ones. A person with an Audi can still ride a scooter in certain contexts.

### 6. Key Customer Characteristics (Six Influencer Factors)

Customers bring diverse attributes shaped by multiple, overlapping factors:

| Influencer | Components | Marketing Implication |
|------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Demographic** | Age, income, gender, ethnicity | Different price points, product features |
| **Socio-Cultural** | Social values, religion, relationships, history | Cultural marketing, festival campaigns |
| **Economic** | GDP, per capita income, savings, price indices, taxes | Premium vs. value positioning |
| **Lifestyle** | Quality-of-life aspirations, benchmarks, comparators | Aspirational branding |
| **Technological** | Tech-savviness, productivity tools, digital adoption | Digital-first products, app ecosystems |
| **Natural** | Climate, seasonal availability, environmental concerns | Seasonal product portfolios |

> **Overlap is the norm**: Two customers at the same income level can have completely different need profiles based on their socio-cultural background. An upper-middle-income professional might prefer a roadside dhaba over a five-star restaurant.

### 7. Vertical vs. Horizontal Customer Engagement

#### 7.1 The Strategic Framework

| Dimension | **Vertical Engagement** | **Horizontal Engagement** |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Scope** | All products for one cluster of customers | One class of products for multiple customer clusters |
| **Brand** | Corporate brand dominates | Product brand dominates |
| **Focus** | Multiple needs of a single customer | Singular product need of a large cross-section |
| **Risk** | Scope before scale → strategic trap | Scale-first → efficiency and reach |
| **Advantage** | Scope advantage when done at the right time | Scale efficiency and geographic diversification |

#### 7.2 The Strategic Prescription

```
Step 1: Build HORIZONTAL scale first
         → Product specialisation + customer & geographic diversification
         → Achieve scale efficiency

Step 2: Then migrate VERTICALLY
         → Meet diverse needs of specific customer groups
         → Gain scope advantage
```

> "It would be somewhat foolhardy for a company at the very initial stages to aim at providing a product for every customer in the total customer universe."

**Vertical engagement too early** = scope placed ahead of scale = manufacturing complexity + cost burden.

### 8. Customer Value Perception

Customer perceived value is **not objective** — it is a mental calculation influenced by communication, experience, and comparison.

#### 8.1 The Value Formula

$$\text{Customer Perceived Value} = \text{Total Customer Benefit} - \text{Total Customer Cost}$$

| **Total Customer Benefit** | **Total Customer Cost** |
|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Usage Benefit | Purchase Cost |
| Ownership Benefit | Operating Cost |
| Image Benefit | Service & Parts Cost |
| Resale Value | Image & Esteem Loss |

#### 8.2 Three Dimensions of Value Delivery

| Dimension | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Product** | Designed for a specific price-performance point; product mix suits a customer mix | Cadbury from single candy bar to family pack |
| **Service** | Uptime support, parts availability, warranty, nationwide service network | Kendriya Vidyalaya's portability model |
| **Optimisation** | Post-sale improvements via software/OS updates | Smartphone OS updates improving performance over time |

**Image & Esteem Loss** is a real cost: an outdated smartphone in a technology-progressive workplace imposes a social penalty. Firms must manage **technological obsolescence** as part of value proposition.

### 9. The Traditional Reactive Customer Model

In the **transaction economy** model:
- Firm creates value proposition → Products provide value → Consumers represent demand
- Firm researches needs → Firm innovates → Firm builds value and supply chains
- **Customer = passive receiver; Firm = sole provider**

The accent is on tangible attributes: **Look & Feel → Functionality → Utility → Lifecycle Cost**

> This is the foundational model of most 20th-century businesses — adequate in a supply-constrained world, but insufficient in the information-abundant digital economy.

### 10. Customer Experience in the New Internet Economy

The digital revolution has made customers **active, informed, and multi-channel**:

```
Universal Digital Market
        ↓
Instant Filtered Search → Product Description
        ↓
Multiple Options → Product Stores (Brand Sites)
        ↓
Mutual Experiences & Insights (Reviews)
        ↓
Purchase Transaction → Product Delivery
        ↓
Customer Support (Manufacturer) or Return/Trade-in (Platform)
```

**Purchase modalities (2×2 matrix):**

| | **Buy Physical** | **Buy Digital** |
|--|----------------|----------------|
| **View Physical** | Traditional retail | E-commerce |
| **View Digital** | Showrooming | Pure digital commerce |

> The digital marketplace is no longer a channel — it is the *primary* arena for customer search and fulfilment experience.

### 11. The DAWN Framework (Original Course Framework)

Prof. Rao introduces DAWN as a **practical, philosophical extension of Maslow** — a framework that better explains what drives modern customers.

| Component | Definition | Strategic Meaning for Firms |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------------|
| **D — Desires** | A strong wish to have or do something | Drive higher-end product categories; upgrade paths |
| **A — Aspirations** | A strong requirement for something not yet easily achievable | Fuel for technological innovation; long-term product roadmaps |
| **W — Wants** | Routine requirements; regular diversification needs | Core portfolio; subscription and recurring revenue models |
| **N — Needs** | Essential, non-negotiable requirements | Base business; must be fulfilled efficiently and economically |

**Illustrative Application**:
- **Need**: Study to stay professionally relevant (e.g., taking one NPTEL course)
- **Want**: Regularly upgrading knowledge across domains
- **Desire**: Becoming top scorer in NPTEL; pursuing multiple certifications
- **Aspiration**: Linking all learning to become a serial entrepreneur — a long-range, systemically difficult goal

#### 11.1 DAWN and Economic Development

| DAWN Level | Economic Significance | Firm's Role |
|-----------|----------------------|------------|
| **Need** | Prime mover of all economic activity | Fulfil efficiently and economically |
| **Want** | Driver of economic diversification | Expand portfolio; offer variants |
| **Desire** | Catalyst for higher-end economic activity | Convert desires into accessible products |
| **Aspiration** | Benchmark of developed-economy status | Invest in innovation to realise aspirations |

> "As an economy grows, new needs and wants get developed even as the aspirations of the past get fulfilled." — Smartphones in India are a perfect example: once an aspiration, now a universal need.

### 12. Customers Have Multiple Personalities

Customers do not act in isolation. They occupy multiple roles simultaneously:

```
        Individual
           ↕
        Family ←→ Organisation
           ↕
         Friends
```

- **Individual**: Personal motivations — may want to spend
- **Family**: Shared goals — may prioritise saving
- **Friends**: Social benchmarking — may drive upward consumption
- **Organisation**: Professional identity — may require status-consistent purchases

Each group **pursues its own DAWNs**, is **constrained by its purchasing capability**, and is **influenced by others' purchasing capability**.

> "Customers buy not only for themselves but also for the related ecosystem influencers."

### 13. The Well-Aligned Customer Ecosystem

**Asian Paints** is the canonical example of building a customer ecosystem:

```
Customer Goal Hierarchy:
  Have a Home → Furnish a Home → Lifestyle in a Home → Social & Professional Recognition

Asian Paints' Business Evolution:
  Core Painting → Interior Design → Interior Fitments → Home Furnishing → Design Architecture
```

This is **ecosystem expansion in action** — the company moves from a product to a solution to a lifestyle partner. Even if a customer later chooses a competitor's paint, the ecosystem relationship endures.

> Companies are opening new business frontiers to meet customer needs **holistically**, not just transactionally.

### 14. Business Customer Ecosystem

Business customers have their own need hierarchy — mirroring Maslow but for organisational health:

| Level | Business State | BD Strategy Required |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|
| **1** | Basic Operations | Supply components; ensure reliability |
| **2** | Growing Operations | Scale-up support; capacity solutions |
| **3** | Viable Operations | Cost-efficient solutions |
| **4** | Competitive Operations | Differentiated, competitive products |
| **5** | Sustainable Operations | Long-term partnerships; AMC |
| **6** | Innovative Operations | Co-development; R&D collaboration |

### 16. Business Development Creating New Models — The Foodplex

Three models of food delivery evolution, illustrating BD innovation:

| Model | Description | Customer Experience |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **One Restaurant, One Food Type** | A restaurant serves only its cuisine | Customer must pre-decide restaurant AND food type |
| **Food Delivery** | Aggregator delivers multiple cuisines to doorstep | Choice of food; **no ambience** |
| **Foodplex** | Restaurants collaborate; any cuisine available at any outlet | Choice of food **AND** ambience; maximum customer freedom |

**Foodplex in practice** (Taj Coromandel example):
- Southern Spice (South Indian) + Anise (Multi-cuisine) + Golden Dragon (Chinese) + Italian Restaurant
- Customer enters any restaurant but can order food from all four

> The **network effect** in a Foodplex can fulfil customer needs far better than any single-restaurant model — and this principle applies to any product or service ecosystem.

## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. The Concept of Strategic Customer Mix (SCM)

One of management's key challenges is **reducing complexity to simplicity**. In marketing, where millions of heterogeneous customers exist, the solution lies in grouping customers into meaningful, manageable segments. The **Strategic Customer Mix (SCM)** is the framework that does exactly this.

> *SCM reflects a price-feature mix that customers support — the company first identifies what customer groups it wants, then designs the Strategic Product Mix (SPM) to serve them.*

The SCM is not about forcing customers into boxes, but about *stimulating customer thinking* to opt for one or another value proposition.

---

### 2. Customer Typology 1 — The Price-Feature Matrix

The first typology maps customers on two axes: **Price** (low to high) and **Features** (low to high), yielding four quadrants:

| Quadrant | Customer Group | When It Arises | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High Price – High Features** | Premium Seekers | Developed economies; customers willing to pay for differentiation | iPhone Pro, BMW |
| **Low Price – Low Features** | Bottom of Pyramid | Emerging economies; basic needs; social equity | Entry-level phones, budget transport |
| **Low Price – High Features** | Demanding Buyers | **Buyer's market** — supply > demand; customers leverage competition | ICE cars vs. EVs: buyers demand better features from ICE makers |
| **High Price – Low Features** | Constrained Buyers | **Seller's market** — demand > supply; capacity shortfall | Scarce goods during a shortage |

**Key insight from the transcript:** When EVs arrive in the market, a customer may approach an ICE vehicle manufacturer and say, *"I will buy from you, but only if you give me better features"* — this is the Low Price–High Features dynamic driven by competitive market forces.

As an economy evolves from emerging to developed, the **High Price–High Features** customer group grows in share. The **Low Price–Low Features** group remains essential for social equity and, as C. K. Prahalad argued, represents *partners in progress* — both for the company and the broader economy.

---

### 3. Customer Typology 2 — The Income-Feature Pyramid

This typology overlays the **income pyramid** with the **feature pyramid** of products:

| Income Level | Typical Feature Preference |
|---|---|
| **High Income** | High+ Features |
| **Upper Middle** | High− Features |
| **Lower Middle** | Basic+ Features |
| **Low Income** | Basic Features |

**Critical nuance:** The pairing is *not always diagonal*. A high-income customer may deliberately choose a basic product (e.g., a minimalist lifestyle), and a lower-income customer may aspire to and acquire a high-feature product (e.g., a flagship phone bought on EMI). The matrix of combinations is wide, and identifying the **viable, commercially plausible combinations** is where true marketing skill lies. Market research plays a crucial role here.

---

### 4. Case Study: Samsung Galaxy — SCM in Mobile Phones

Samsung's Galaxy product line demonstrates SCM applied to consumer electronics:

| Product Series | Price | Features | Customer Group |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S Series** | High | High | Premium buyers — fully differentiated flagship experience |
| **SE Series** | High | Low/Medium | Aspirational buyers — S-series image at reduced specs |
| **M/A Series** | Low | High | Budget-conscious buyers seeking superior experience |
| **M/F Series** | Low | Low | Online-first, entry-level, value shoppers |

> *"Not every CEO can be intuitively and technologically futuristic on what can inspire customers at different value and price points. Most companies will need to follow structured methods."*

### 5. Case Study: Taj Group of Hotels — SCM in Hospitality

Taj Group maps its hotel brands across the same price-feature (price-service) matrix:

| Hotel Brand | Service Level | Price Level | Customer Group |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lake Palace** | Low (vast palatial — cannot have 1:1 service) | High | Palatial ambience seekers; heritage experience |
| **Vivanta** | High | High | Luxury travelers (business + leisure) |
| **Gateway** | High | Medium-Low | Business travelers seeking features at value price |

### 6. Three Ways to Understand Customers

| Method | Nature | Who Uses It |
|---|---|---|
| **Experiential & Intuitive** | Seasoned, visionary process; born of long customer interaction | Trade channel players, experienced marketers |
| **Market Research** | Established structured process; data-driven; repeatable | Professional market research firms, FMCG companies |
| **Design Thinking** | New, empathy-first approach to discovering latent customer needs | Start-ups, innovation-led companies |
| **Apple's Way** *(Bonus 4th)* | Seasoned visionary intuition at the CEO/CMO level; near-impossible to replicate | Apple under Steve Jobs |

Apple's approach is categorised separately because it is an **intrinsic natural competency** embedded in the personality of the CEO and CMO — it cannot be institutionalised into a process like market research can.

### 7. Steve Jobs — Life, Soul, and Philosophy of Technology

#### 7a. Who Was Steve Jobs?
- CEO of Apple, Inc. (1995–2011)
- Led Apple to become the **first company to cross USD 2 trillion in market capitalisation**
- Defined in practical terms what *creativity, innovation, and connectivity* mean
- Introduced the **first iPhone** on January 9, 2007, at the Macworld Conference & Expo, San Francisco
  - Described it as: *"A widescreen iPod with touch controls + a revolutionary mobile phone + a breakthrough internet communicator"*

#### 7b. Device Socialism

| Conventional Wisdom (Pre-Steve) | Steve's Paradigm |
|---|---|
| Offer utilitarian (low-level) products to lower strata | One standardised, high-technology, high-value product for **all** sections of society |
| Offer luxury (high-level) products to upper strata | No income-based fragmentation |
| Market skimming with hyped value and high pricing OR fragmentation with broken tech and flexible pricing | Universal appeal — same product for billionaires and commoners |

**Device Socialism** = the socialistic philosophy that product aspirations are common across income segments. In the first years of iPod, iPhone, and iPad, Apple offered **only one model per product** for all customer segments. It was common to see a cab driver and the MD of a company using the identical iPhone.

> *"Segmentation, as defined for decades by marketing gurus, had no relevance for Apple products and customers."*

#### 7c. Digital Ecosystem
Steve Jobs extended the product experience beyond the device:
- **Apple Stores**: Not mere retail, but *customer experience hubs* — challenging the norm that manufacturing and retailing are separate. Within 3 years: 320 stores globally (237 in USA).
- **iCloud (2011)**: Seamlessly stores music, photos, movies, documents, apps, mails, calendars — and wirelessly pushes to all Apple devices. The cloud became a **digital home** for Apple customers.
- **Strategies of integration, outsourcing, and collaboration** converged under a single vision: the Apple digital ecosystem.

### 13. Basic Customer Types

| Type | Description | BD/Marketing Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Neutral** | Unaffected by brand, ads, or past associations; buys only on genuine need | Communicate genuine utility; build awareness |
| **Questioning** | Never fully satisfied; challenges product utility; switches frequently | Provide comprehensive answers; address every objection proactively |
| **New** | Exploring a product/category for the first time | Encourage trial; build attachment and positive experience |
| **Loyal** | Deeply attached to brand; resistant to switching | Reward loyalty through exclusive programs, customised features, personalised service |

### 14. Business Customer (B2B) Typologies

Business customers have personalities distinct from individual consumers. Six primary B2B personality types:

| Type | Characteristics | BD Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Visionary** | Shares the BD vision; focused on transformative outcomes | Ideal for *mega relationships*; long-term strategic alliances |
| **Explorer** | Constantly seeks new development vistas; multi-option thinker | Can get bogged down by too many choices; needs focused facilitation |
| **Adventurer** | Bold risk-taker; anticipates high rewards | Impatient and non-persistent; suited for high-risk, high-reward ventures |
| **Seeker** | Ever-hungry for new options; takes intuitive optimal risk-reward bets | Good for steady, systematic partnerships with innovative products |
| **Analyser** | Data-hungry; rigorous decision-making | Can only do limited partnerships; analysis can become paralysis |
| **Protector** | Risk-averse; seeks multiple assurances | Likely to miss great opportunities; slowest to commit |

**GE's entry into financial services** is cited as an example of Adventurer-type behaviour — bold, initially successful, but ultimately divested to private equity.

### 15. Desirable Customer Typology (Composite Persona)

A customer's persona is **never monolithic** — it is a blend of multiple typology attributes. The ideal (desired) business customer persona for a BD executive to target would lean towards:

- **Dominant**: Visionary + Explorer + Seeker
- **Secondary**: Adventurer
- **Minimal**: Analyser + Protector

This is not a standardised prescription — the ideal mix varies by industry, product, and context. The value lies in **knowing the mix** of your actual customer base and calibrating your BD strategy accordingly.

### 16. BD–Customer Interface: The Paradox of Roles

Using a **Licensor–Licensee** deal as the model:

| Party | What They Have | What They Need |
|---|---|---|
| **Licensor** | The product/technology | Money and market reach (monetisation) |
| **Licensee** | Money and market access | The product/technology |

**Paradox:** The licensor is doing BD (monetising the product), but *from the licensor's perspective, the licensee is a customer*. Simultaneously, the licensee is a customer acquiring a product, but *from the licensee's perspective, they are developing their business*. Hence:

> *"Every business developer is a customer. Every customer is a business developer."*

This duality means **self-awareness** (knowing your own BD personality) is as critical as awareness of the customer's personality. Convergence or divergence in personalities determines the *nature, quality, and sustainability* of business deals.

### 17. BD Typology Interaction Matrix

The matrix maps **Customer Personality** (rows) against **BD Executive Personality** (columns) to predict deal outcomes:

| Customer ↓ / BD → | **Visionary** | **Explorer** | **Adventurer** | **Seeker** | **Analyser** | **Protector** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Visionary** | Mega Futuristic Ventures | All Over with Little Results | Hi-Risk Futuristic Bets | Calibrated Futuristic Bets | Well-balanced Futuristic Ventures | Vision sub-optimised by conservatism |
| **Explorer** | All Over with Little Results | Hi-Potential Low Result | Moderation of high-risk bets | Conflict with Some Resolution | — | — |
| **Adventurer** | Hi-Risk Big Bets | Moderation of high-risk bets | Conflict with Some Resolution | Conflict with No Resolution | — | — |
| **Seeker** | Calibrated Futuristic Bets | Aggressive Outreach with Optimal Results | Conflict with Some Resolution | Steady, Systematic Bets | Eliminating Many Options | — |
| **Analyser** | Well-balanced Futuristic Ventures | Conflict with Low Results | Conflict with No Resolution | Joint Work with Good Result | High Analysis, Low Result | Eliminating Many Options |
| **Protector** | Vision sub-optimised by conservatism | — | — | — | Eliminating Many Options | Risk-Averse, Poor Result |

**Key insights from the matrix:**
- **Best outcomes**: Visionary × Visionary → Mega Futuristic Ventures; Seeker × Explorer → Aggressive Outreach with Optimal Results
- **Worst outcomes**: Protector × Protector → Risk-Averse, Poor Result; Analyser × Analyser → High Analysis, Low Result
- **Same type ≠ always best**: Explorer × Explorer leads to Hi-Potential but Low Result (both exploring, neither committing)
- **Cross-type can be optimal**: Seeker (BD) with Analyser (Customer) → Joint Work with Good Result

## 📊 Summary

Lecture 22 opens by establishing the central challenge of marketing: reducing the complexity of heterogeneous customer populations into manageable, strategically useful typologies. The **Strategic Customer Mix (SCM)** framework is introduced as the starting point — companies should first define the customer mix they wish to serve and then develop the product mix accordingly. The **Price-Feature Matrix** yields four customer quadrants (High-High, Low-Low, Low Price–High Features, High Price–Low Features), each arising under distinct market and economic conditions. The **Income-Feature Pyramid** adds a second dimension, showing that income level and feature aspiration do not always move together, creating a rich variety of viable customer-product combinations.

Two detailed case studies — **Samsung Galaxy** and **Taj Group of Hotels** — illustrate how SCM plays out in practice, with each brand creating a portfolio of sub-brands or product tiers to serve distinct customer groups simultaneously. Three formal methods of understanding customers are discussed — Experiential/Intuitive, Market Research, and Design Thinking — with Apple's approach recognised as a rare fourth: Seasoned Visionary Intuition. The lecture then takes a deliberate detour through **Steve Jobs' philosophy**, demonstrating that the highest form of customer understanding is achieving a **universal product** that transcends income-based segmentation — what Jobs called *device socialism* and the *digital ecosystem*. 

The lecture then maps customer typologies across five dimensions: **Demographics, Geography, Behaviour, Culture, and Psychography**, each with a four-sub-category framework and illustrative examples. **Four basic customer types** (Neutral, Questioning, New, Loyal) are explained with distinct BD/marketing implications. Shifting to B2B, **six business customer personality types** (Visionary, Explorer, Adventurer, Seeker, Analyser, Protector) are introduced, each with distinct risk orientations and partnership styles. The critical insight is that a customer's persona is a **composite mix** of these types, not a single label. Finally, the **BD Typology Interaction Matrix** maps all possible pairings of customer and BD personalities to predict deal outcomes, from Mega Futuristic Ventures to Risk-Averse Poor Results — establishing that **self-awareness in personality** is as essential as customer understanding for successful business development.

## 🧠 Main Points

## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. The Market Research Process

Market research links the **consumer, customer, and public** with the firm. It generates insights about marketplace stakeholders and helps the firm offer what customers truly need.

**Definition:** Market research is a function that helps marketers take appropriate **tactical decisions (short run)** and **strategic decisions (long run)**. It makes available **timely, accurate, and actionable** information about consumers, customers, competitors, and brands.

**Institutional characteristics:**
- It is a **continuous** institutional process linked to the strategic planning process.
- It requires a **central department** excelling in data collection, analytics, experimental design, and interpretation.
- It must be both **organic** (internal expertise) and **inorganic** (external expertise) — the market is too vast for an internal team alone.

**The 6-Step Market Research Process:**

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1. Define problem & objectives | Identify what needs to be researched |
| 2. Develop research plan | Decide the methodology, tools, and scope |
| 3. Collect information | Gather data from respondents/sources |
| 4. Analyse information | Use statistical and qualitative methods |
| 5. Present findings | Report insights to decision-makers |
| 6. Make the decision | Apply insights to marketing/business strategy |

**Sample questions MR can answer:**
- Why is my product not selling as well as competitors'?
- What features do consumers prioritise?
- How price-elastic is my product?
- What incentive schemes help retailers?
- What is the role of loyalty/brand connect?
- How can we reposition the brand for better profitability?

### 2. Types of Enquiries

#### 2A. Open-Ended Questions
Used in **exploratory research** with relatively open boundaries. No pre-set answer choices — respondents can answer in unlimited ways.

| Technique | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Completely Unstructured** | No pre-set questions; answer in any way | Open conversation with customer |
| **Word Association** | A word is presented; respondent says the first word that comes to mind | "Gold Winner" → "Pure" / "Healthy" |
| **Sentence Completion** | Incomplete sentence to complete | "I like Brand X because ___" |
| **Story Completion** | Incomplete story presented; respondent completes it | "When I opened my ice cream parlour, customers came all day — then only daytime customers came. Why?" |
| **Picture (Cartoon)** | Two characters shown; respondent fills in the empty speech balloon | Identify with one character; fill the dialogue |
| **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)** | A picture presented; respondent makes up a full story | Picture of an ice cream parlour → predict footfall patterns |

#### 2B. Closed-Ended Questions
All possible answers are specified. Easier to interpret and tabulate. Still require careful structuring with statistical tools for weighting and ranking.

| Scale / Type | Description | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| **Dichotomous** | Two possible answers | Yes / No |
| **Multiple Choice** | Three or more options | A / B / C / D |
| **Likert Scale** | Degree of agreement/disagreement (5 points) | "My company treats employees respectfully" → Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree |
| **Semantic Differential** | Two bipolar adjectives; respondent marks a point between them | Modern ←————→ Old-fashioned |
| **Importance Scale** | Rates the importance of an attribute | "Creaminess in dairy is: Very Important / Somewhat / Not Important" |
| **Rating Scale** | Rates attribute from poor to excellent | Service quality: 1 (Poor) to 5 (Excellent) |
| **Intention-to-Buy Scale** | Measures likelihood of purchase | "Definitely will buy / Probably / Might / Probably not / Definitely not" |

> **Note:** Besides quantitative analysis, **qualitative research** using word association, projective techniques, and visualisation is also deployed. Food delivery apps use minimal surveys and comment solicitation as real-world examples.

### 3. Seven Characteristics of Good Marketing Research

| # | Characteristic | Description |
|---|----------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Scientific Method** | Careful observation, proper hypothesis formulation, iterative testing |
| 2 | **Research Creativity** | Use of creative prompts and comparators to read the customer mind |
| 3 | **Multiple Methods** | Using more than one technique (quantitative + qualitative; open + closed) |
| 4 | **Models and Data** | Realistic data and statistically meaningful models |
| 5 | **Value and Cost** | Value of information must justify the cost of collection and analysis |
| 6 | **Evaluative and Objective** | Overcome legacy biases; data-based but contextualised |
| 7 | **Ethical Marketing** | Respect privacy, make personal details optional, avoid disparaging competitors |

> Market research is also called **marketing research** — the terms are used interchangeably.

### 4. Creativity in Marketing Research

Marketing research must not be mechanical or routine. It should be **exciting and inclusive** to respondents.

**Shift from:** Question Pad and Ticking → **Shift to:** Digital Inclusivity

| Creative Method | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Visual Explainers & Comparators** | Show images of two products; ask which form factor / colour scheme / design the respondent prefers |
| **Participative Thinking** | Treat the process as collaborative; let customers think and engage, not just respond |
| **Emotional Pivots** | Ask questions that evoke emotional connections: "What makes this product connect with your family?" |
| **Digital Simulation** | Use AR, VR, MR, interactive digital gamification to simulate real usage scenarios |
| **Reflection** | Design questions that prompt thoughtful reflection, not knee-jerk reactions |
| **Self-evaluation** | Both the questioner and respondent reflect on their own biases and responses |

By using digital technologies, market research can be **significantly stimulating and holistically comprehending**.

### 5. Design Thinking — Overview

**Definition:** Design thinking is a **human-centred, 5-step iterative process** that designers, marketers, and problem solvers use to discover true customer needs and issues, and find solutions collaboratively.

**The Five Stages:**

| Stage | Done With/For | Key Activity |
|-------|---------------|--------------|
| **Empathise** | Users | Deep immersion in user experience; observe, listen, engage |
| **Define** | Users' needs, problems, solutions | Synthesise empathy data; define the core problem statement |
| **Ideate** | Integrating Define-phase inputs | Challenge assumptions; seek divergent ideas and creative solutions |
| **Prototype** | Chosen ideas | Convert ideas into workable, tangible prototypes |
| **Test** | Prototypes | Validate solutions against specifications; assess viability |

> The five stages are **NOT necessarily sequential** — they are **highly iterative**; teams may loop back to any earlier stage based on findings.

---

### 6. Unique Advantages of Design Thinking (Jeanne Liedtka Framework)

Source: *"Why Design Thinking Works"* — Prof. Jeanne Liedtka, HBR, September–October 2018.

Design thinking offers a **seamless flow of ideas into actionable work — from research to rollout**.

**Three outcomes successful innovation requires:**
1. **Customer Discovery** → Immersion, Sense Making, Alignment
2. **Idea Generation** → Emergence, Articulation, Prioritisation
3. **Testing Experience** → Pre-experience, Learning in Action, Validation

### 7. Design Thinking as a Tool of Customer Engagement

Immersion in customer experience produces **data → insights → design criteria → brainstormed solutions**.

Five key dimensions of design thinking as customer engagement:

| Dimension | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Counteracting Human Biases** | DT processes are open-ended; biases cancel each other out; creativity is unleashed; superior solutions emerge at lower cost and risk |
| **Emphasising the Human Element** | Recognises customers and organisations as human; emphasises engagement, dialogue, and learning |
| **Commitment to Change** | Customers become stakeholders in problem definition and solution development; broad commitment to change is secured |
| **Structure for Innovation** | Provides a structural process for innovators (whether customers or company reps) to collaborate and agree on essentials at every phase — a Gemba innovation |
| **Social Technology** | Company co-experiences the problem and solution with the customer; representatives are open, transparent, and socially networked — this is social technology at work |

> **Contrast with traditional approach:** When a customer reports a product problem, a company typically first checks warranty status and investigates misuse. Design thinking removes this investigative posture entirely — representatives experience the problem alongside the customer.

### 8. Mistakes in Empathising

Empathising is the "foremost interpersonal and emotional skill" — it requires special training. Common mistakes:

1. **Viewing customer experience through design/functional filters** — judging how the product *should* be used instead of how it *is* used.
2. **Asking incorrect questions** — leading to incorrect answers; also interpreting even correct answers through biases.
3. **Inability to interpret words into feelings** — language and expression vary; body language is often ignored.
4. **Inadequate observation of engagement and listening** — if only one customer is articulate in a group of five, the activity is failing.
5. **Inability to differentiate core from peripheral insights** — in an open discussion, many ideas emerge; the key is identifying which insight drives the primary product development imperative.

**Remedy:** Acquire skills to be an empathetic **observer, engager, listener, and analyser**.

### 9. MR Space vs DT Space

A consumer's thought process can be mapped across four layers:

| Consumer Thought Layer | Description | Research Space |
|------------------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Unknown Thoughts** | Desires about potential needs/wants the consumer is not even aware of | **DT Space** |
| **Unexpressed Thoughts** | Thoughts the consumer has but is unwilling to express (personal care, symbolic products) | **DT Space** |
| **Expressed Thoughts** | Thoughts the consumer is willing to share | **MR Space** |
| **Visible Behaviour** | Behavioural actions the consumer is willing to reveal externally | **MR Space** |

> **Key insight:** DT can extend into the MR space, but MR typically cannot reach the DT space. DT is **entrepreneurial and start-up oriented**; MR is **structured and systematic**. Today, both established companies and startups are recognising DT's importance for every new product development.

### 10. Holistic Engagements in Design Thinking

Design thinking engagements must produce holistic solutions through an iterative cycle:

**Create → Refine → Repeat**

Components of the holistic engagement:

| Component | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Consumer Research | Understand the problem |
| Technology Forecasting | Identify solution platforms |
| Gemba Walks | Be in the field; observe actual usage |
| Solution Sketching | Draw initial solution ideas |
| Challenging / Pressure Testing | Stress-test assumptions |
| Freezing Design Solution | Finalise a design |
| Initial Manufacturing Solution | Translate design to producible form |
| Viability Analysis | Assess feasibility for company and customer |
| Cost-Price Analysis | Ensure economic viability |
| Pathway to Prototyping (Product Plan) | Bridge to commercial prototype |

> Customer involvement is greatest during **ideation, prototyping, and testing**; less so in manufacturing viability analysis.

#### Spectacles

| Aspect | Market Research | Design Thinking |

| Factor | Market Research (MR) | Design Thinking (DT) |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|

## 📊 Summary

Lecture 23 covers two major, complementary tools for understanding customers: **Market Research (MR)** and **Design Thinking (DT)**. ### 1. Customers' Multiple Filters — The Purchase Decision Funnel

Before arriving at a final purchase decision, customers pass through a **sequential set of filters**. Companies must understand and support each stage.

| Stage | Filter | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Target Product Search** | Awareness of need + awareness of the target product category |
| 2 | **Awareness of Products and Brands** | Knowledge of alternatives; experience-based or store/digital visit |
| 3 | **Open to Trying Out** | Physical feel, reviews, exploration interest (e.g., test-driving a car) |
| 4 | **Switching Cost Analysis** | Benefits must outweigh the cost of switching from a current brand |
| 5 | **Use Experience** | Post-purchase satisfaction/dissatisfaction governs future patronage |

> **Key Insight:** At any of these stages, a customer can switch brands. Companies must actively support migration and retention throughout this funnel.

### 2. Forming Strong Customer Bonds

Bond formation is a **complex, cross-departmental process** — not just a marketing function. Six foundational practices:

1. **Create superior products, services, and experiences** — Packaging itself signals quality; poor labelling or leakage damages bonds even for great products.
2. **Cross-departmental participation** in customer satisfaction and retention — from R&D to labelling to logistics.
3. **Voice of the Customer (VoC)** — Capture both stated and unstated needs; communicate them to top management, not just internal metrics.
4. **Customer database** — Maintain accessible records of individual needs, preferences, purchase frequency, preferred retailers, and interactions.
5. **Customer-company interaction forums** — Enabling customers to express needs, perceptions, and complaints builds mutual understanding.
6. **Loyalty and club marketing programmes** — Reward long-standing customers, outstanding employees, distributors, and retailers.

> "Nobody takes that product loyalty, once established, will remain on its own forever — everybody strives to keep this loyalty intact."

### 4. Value Creation Practices — The SERI Framework

#### 4.1 SOCIALISING

### 7. Customer Performance Scorecard

### 10. Consumer Search Dynamics — The Funnel Filtration

When buying a laptop, a consumer narrows down from:

```
Total Set               Awareness Set          Consideration Set      Choice Set         Decision
─────────────────────   ─────────────────────  ─────────────────────  ─────────────────  ──────────
Apple, Dell, HP,        Apple, Dell, HP,       Apple, Dell, HP,       Apple, HP,         ?
Toshiba, Lenovo,        Lenovo, Acer,          Microsoft              Microsoft
Asus, Acer, NEC,        Microsoft
Samsung, Microsoft
```

- **Total Set**: All brands that exist in the market
- **Awareness Set**: Brands the consumer has heard of / seen (well-advertised, present in working environment)
- **Consideration Set**: Brands shortlisted based on features, image, and price
- **Choice Set**: Final shortlist after deeper evaluation
- **Decision**: Triggered by store visit, digital review, or peer recommendation

**Filtration levels:**
1. **Corporate level** — "Apple is a great company"
2. **Product brand level** — "MacBook is well known; HP Envy has a strong reputation"
3. **Product level** — RAM, display quality, weight, battery life

> The transparent company that provides complete standalone and comparative information gains in the long term.

|---|---|---|---|---|

### 12. Steps Between Evaluation of Alternatives and Purchase Decision

Even after evaluating alternatives, the decision is not final:

```
Evaluation of Alternatives
         ↓
   Purchase Intention
         ↓
   Two intervening factors:
   ① Attitudes of Self (how greeted in store, product display, availability)
   ② Ecosystem Factors (purchasing power that day, competitive promotions, urgency)
         ↓
   Final Purchase Decision
```

- **Attitudes of Self**: Salesperson behaviour, product display, availability
- **Ecosystem Factors**: Purchasing power, competitive promotions, urgency of need, psychographic state on that day
- Understanding consumer **rigidities and sensitivities** helps companies predict purchase conversion rates

### 13. Use-and-Reuse Consumer Mindset (Circular Economy)

The rapid pace of technological change creates **large inventories of old products**. The circular economy requires marketers to adopt a new mindset:

**Options when retaining a product:**
- Use it for original purpose (old smartphone for basic calling)
- Convert to new purpose (add external HDD to old laptop)
- Store it idly (suboptimal but common)

**Options when disposing of a product:**
- Monetise the ownership (rent out)
- Lease or lend
- Trade-in for a new product
- Sell (direct-to-consumer via OLX/eBay, through middleman, or to middleman)
- Give away / donate
- Throw away (least preferred)

> **Critical Shift:** Earlier disparaging views about second-hand markets must be replaced by responsible consumption and trade-in frameworks. **Producer responsibility** (extended producer responsibility/EPR) includes taking back products, reclaiming packages, and recycling.

### 14. The Mutual Awareness Framework (2×2 Grid)

A powerful framework for understanding where companies and consumers stand in terms of awareness:

|  | **Customer Aware** | **Customer Unaware** |
|---|---|---|
| **Company Aware** | **Traditional Product-Market Play** (Specialised Way) — Comfort zone; both know the need | **Developmental Product-Market Play** (Diversity Way) — Company knows the need, customer doesn't; company must educate |
| **Company Unaware** | **Blinkered Product-Market Play** (Blind Alley) — Customer knows the need, company misses it; leads to disruption | **Open Space for Start-up Play** (Apple Way) — Neither knows; driven by intuition, innovation, creativity |

- **The Blind Alley**: Company overconfident in current products; eventual disruption
- **The Open Space**: Where truly innovative start-ups shine — requires intuition + design thinking
- **Apple Way**: Rarer — where the company discovers needs that neither the company nor the consumer was explicitly aware of
- Companies should move from traditional play → developmental play → open space play

### 15. Consumer Neuroscience and Neuromarketing

| Dimension | Details |
|---|---|
| **Definition** | Measurement of physiological and neural signals to gain insight into consumer motivations, preferences, and decisions |
| **Common Methods** | Brain scanning (neural activity); physiological tracking (eye movement, face tracking, proxy sensors); AI-based analysis |
| **Insights Possible** | Consumer motivations, preferences, decisions, engagement levels, openness to change, emotional responses, brand recall |
| **Potential Uses** | Price setting, brand improvement, ad campaign design, hypothesis generation |
| **Pros** | Scientifically links behavioural responses to internal information processing; systematises marketing mix to predictable influences |
| **Cons** | Expensive equipment; controlled conditions needed (not scalable to Kirana stores); **intrudes on consumer privacy**; risks thought and behaviour manipulation |
| **Recommended Path** | Limited use of scanning for hypothesis development on target groups; extensive use of sensing/tracking/AI to improve consumer experience, not just sales |

> "Consumer neuroscience and neuromarketing are scientifically feasible but **dystopian** in the face of consumer privacy."

### 20. The Customer Loyalty Ladder

A loyal customer progressively demonstrates these behaviours (bottom to top):

```
Level 5: COLLABORATES — co-creates new products and experiences with the firm
Level 4: PREMIUM PAYER — willing to wait or pay premiums for loyal brands
Level 3: RESISTANT — resists competitors' inducements to switch
Level 2: FEEDBACK PROVIDER — wants to grow the relationship through feedback
Level 1: ENDORSER — remembers the brand and endorses products to others
```

> **Example:** Apple customers wait months for Apple's annual launch cycle even when Samsung releases phones in February — this is loyalty embedded in an ecosystem.

> Customer loyalty is the **biggest defence in recession** and the **biggest driver in fast-growth markets**.

### 21. Business Buyers Matrix — 2×2 Classification

**Axes:** Firm's investment in customers (Low/High) × Customer's position on loyalty ladder (Low/High)

| | **Low Customer Loyalty** | **High Customer Loyalty** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Firm Investment** | **Underperformers** — Over-invested but cannot move customers to high loyalty | **Valued Partners** — Highest correlation; pinnacle of B2B relationship |
| **Low Firm Investment** | **Commodity Buyers** — Pure price-based purchases; no differentiation (e.g., sand, clinker, pigments) | **Valued Customers** — Customer remains loyal despite low firm investment; positive brand perception |

**Progressive Journey:** Commodity Buyers → Valued Customers → Valued Partners

> The correlation between firm's investments and customer's loyalty is **most profound in Valued Partnership**.

The SERI framework (Socialising, Engaging, Recognising, Integrating) offers a comprehensive model for systematically deepening customer engagement. Real-world loyalty programmes — from Lufthansa's Miles and More to Britannia's Homepreneur initiative — demonstrate the spectrum of financial and emotional bonding tools available to companies of all sizes. Customer performance must be measured through a full scorecard (not just gross sales) and tracked across four economic verticals: Customer Metrics, Unit Metrics, Cash-Flow Metrics, and Brand Metrics. The Consumer Behaviour Model contextualises purchasing within both company-controlled inputs and macro-environmental factors, while the Expectancy Model and Consumer Search Dynamics funnel reveal how consumers cognitively evaluate alternatives. The Mutual Awareness Framework provides a powerful 2×2 lens for strategic positioning — companies that play in the "Open Space" (Apple Way) or "Developmental" zones are best positioned for long-term growth. Individual bonds are transactional and emotional; business bonds are strategic and techno-commercial — each requiring tailored investment. The Air India case shows that rebuilding damaged bonds is even harder than building them, but yields high returns. Emotional connectivity — backed by HBR research showing it delivers more than twice the value of mere satisfaction — and the Customer Loyalty Ladder together make the case that loyalty is not just an outcome but a strategic vertical that companies must invest in patiently and continuously.

## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. What is Customer Relationship Management?

**CRM** is defined as a set of approaches and tools for a company to:
- Stay engaged with its customers
- Enhance customer experiences continuously

It is fundamentally a **philosophy** and a **cultural facet** of progressive companies, not merely a software or sales tool. CRM is applicable to every type of organisation — commercial, non-profit, B2B, B2C, etc.

> **Key insight:** CRM is not a department; it is the DNA of a customer-centric organisation.

---

### 2. Hotel Touchpoints — CRM in Hospitality

A hotel represents one of the richest examples of CRM in action. Every service offered is a **touchpoint** — an opportunity to demonstrate customer-centricity.

**Touchpoints for a Hotel:**

| Category | Touchpoints |
|---|---|
| Stay & Access | Reservation, Check-in, Check-out |
| In-Room | Room Service |
| Food & Beverage | Restaurant & Bar Service, Takeaway Services |
| Wellness | Fitness and Health Services |
| Lifestyle | Boutique & Salon Services, Shopping Services |
| Support | Laundry Services, Business Services, Travel Services |
| Loyalty | Frequent Stay Programmes |

**Taj Group** is the gold standard — by making every interface touchpoint enjoyable, the Taj has embedded CRM in organisational culture through policies, values, and conduct principles. This customer value compounds over time.

### 3. Defining Customer Preferences — The Apple Model

Continuous data collection and analysis at customer touchpoints improves fulfilment and satisfaction. **Apple** exemplifies this comprehensively:

| CRM Pillar | Activities |
|---|---|
| **Launch Engagement** | Public events, product detailing, leader engagements, developer conferences |
| **In-Store Engagement** | Full product range display (multiple SKUs), openness to use & feel, transparent trade-in and supply procedures |
| **Online Store** | Smooth, suave shopping portal; significant popularity gained |
| **Company Service** | High customer confidence, genuine parts & technical support, authorised service outlets |

**Supporting Pillars:**
- Well-defined product cadence
- Strong, dedicated Apple ecosystem
- Applicability of operating system across devices
- Showcasing sustainability in design and operations

**Landmark Example:** The **fall detection feature** in Apple Watch made a deep impression on a *wider* set of users beyond just the elderly or vulnerable — demonstrating how a single innovation can expand CRM reach.

### 4. Marketing Evolution — The Four Phases

CRM maturity is aligned with marketing's historical evolution:

| Era | Phase | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **1950s – 1970s** | Mass Marketing | One product, all customers; few SKUs, vast coverage |
| **1980s – 1990s** | Mass Customisation | Curated products; customer groups; internet begins enabling customisation |
| **2010s – 2020s** | Personal Touch in Marketing | Internet communication, behavioural data analytics, digital touchpoints |
| **Future** | Personalised Marketing | Product/process customisation per individual; data-driven personalisation |

**The Medicine Analogy:** When medicine can be personalised (genetic profiling of patients matched to therapeutic drug profiles), marketing — which governs less complex interactions — certainly can and must be personalised. *No two customers are the same.*

### 5. The Customer 'Switch-Retain' Paradox

Marketers seek a **paradoxical** (self-serving) behaviour from customers:
- **Want customers to SWITCH TO their brand easily**
- **Want customers to REFUSE to SWITCH AWAY once acquired**

This creates a 2×2 matrix:

|  | **Easy to Retain** | **Hard to Retain** |
|---|---|---|
| **Inclined to Switch** | Impulsive Buyers | Dynamic Buyers |
| **Disinclined to Switch** | **Loyal Buyers** *(ideal)* | Demanding Buyers |

- **Loyal Buyers:** Every company's ideal — disinclined to switch AND easy to retain
- **Dynamic Buyers:** Always seeking novelty; switch at first opportunity
- **Demanding Buyers:** Highest standards; require maximum marketing effort
- **Impulsive Buyers:** Susceptible to persuasion; inclined to switch but can be retained with appeals

### 6. Six Types of Customer Personas

Even within the same demographic and socio-economic segment, customers exhibit different psychological profiles:

| Customer Type | Decision Style | Marketing Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Analytical** | Logical, data-based; evaluates, validates; reads all product literature | Must use technical detailing; specifications-heavy communication |
| **Experimental** | Tuned to new and innovative; high expectations; among first adopters | Can co-work as early testers; preview launches |
| **Impulsive** | Driven by instincts and appeals; style > substance; celebrity endorsements work | Huge latitude for promotion-led sales |
| **Dynamic** | Continuously seeking new things; quick to switch at first alternative; NOT the same as experimental | Create a product range to hook them; prevent competitor switching |
| **Demanding** | Seeks high quality + low price (cost leadership + product differentiation simultaneously) | Mastering this segment (as global giants do in India) builds resilience |
| **Loyal** | Conformist; goes by past association; passive decision-making | Do not ossify strategy by over-relying on their loyalty; may resent variation |

> **Key distinction:** *Experimental* customers will stay loyal **as long as the product stays innovative**. *Dynamic* customers will leave **regardless**, always chasing the next thing.

| Customer Type | Product Preference Style | Marketing Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Analytical | Deep specifications; technically oriented | Technical detailing and data sheets |
| Experimental | First-to-try; co-development interest | Beta programmes; early access |

### 8. The Six Customer Principles

These six principles form the core of **Strategic Marketing / Business Development**:

#### Principle 1: Expectation Discovery
The starting point of CRM — understanding or **shaping** customer expectations.

**Virtuous Cycle:**
> Discovering Expectations → Providing Right Solutions → High Customer Satisfaction → High Customer Loyalty → Repeat Purchases → (back to) Discovering Expectations

- Market research and design thinking are the tools
- Companies must **discover AND deliver** — discovery without delivery leads to frustration, not satisfaction
- The right model = **Expectation–Reality Match**

#### Principle 2: Performance Fulfilment
Performance fulfilment is both a product development AND a marketing responsibility.

- Customers **build** expectations based on product appearance and material → they **seek** to fulfil expected performance in use
- Buyers choose firms offering **high performance balanced across price points**
- **Operational Value** = Total Customer Benefits − Total Customer Costs
- **Goal:** Maximise net operating benefit for the customer (the foundational principle of strategic marketing)
- High performance ≠ High lifetime value automatically; both must be achieved together

#### Principle 3: Portfolio Mix
Companies must optimise **customer-product profitability**, not just individual product profitability.

- **Obvious:** Losing a profitable customer deeply impacts profitability
- **Less obvious:** Losing a marginal customer is a strategic disadvantage — relationship loss is more lasting than the immediate revenue loss
- **Hospital analogy:** Doctors need the full pharmaceutical product suite; if a pharma company refuses to supply commodity generics (low margin), the doctor switches the entire relationship (including premium injectables) to another company
- **Cost of acquiring a new customer** can be many times the cost of retaining an existing one — especially in B2B
- **The 20-80 rule is inappropriate for strategic marketing decisions** — marginal customers must not be dismissed

#### Principle 4: The Quality Pinnacle
Quality is the most important influencer of customer loyalty, and marketers must **actively drive** quality standards upward.

**Quality is defined as:** *The totality of features and attributes of a product or service that establish its ability to fulfil stated or implicit needs — including the quality of customer experience.*

**Three Quality Stalwarts cited:**

| Pioneer | Organisation | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Taiichi Ohno** | Toyota | Toyota Production System (TPS); zero-defect manufacturing |
| **Joseph M. Juran** | — | Quality Management; Juran's Trilogy (Planning, Control, Improvement) |
| **W. Edwards Deming** | — | 14 Points for Management; statistical quality control |

- Quality cannot be measured on a single dimension — JD Power evaluates automobiles on multiple parameters (design, development, after-sales service, on-road performance across conditions)
- Marketers should engage **external independent consultants** for quality ratings

#### Principle 5: Lifetime Value (LTV)

**LTV is conceptually elegant but practically difficult to assure.** Past customer databases provide benchmarks for future propositions.

**Three Components:**

| Component | Elements |
|---|---|
| **Acquisition Cost** | Net cash outflow on the buy + Cost of capital |
| **Operating Cost over Lifespan** | Running & maintenance costs + Useful life for intended application |
| **Resale Value at End-of-Life** | Trade-in facility availability + Net value after refurbishment |

**Formula:**
> **LTV = Acquisition Cost (Day 1) − [Discounted Operating Cost over Lifetime] + [Discounted Resale Value]**

All future costs/values discounted back to **Day 1** for comparability.

- Products with high quality and brand reputation enjoy **higher resale value** — customers factor this into their original purchase decision
- Having a captive financing unit allows a company to offer a better cost of capital to buyers, improving LTV attractiveness

#### Principle 6: Customer Relationship Management
CRM knits together all five principles above and provides the execution infrastructure for strategic marketing.

### 9. CRM Evolution Timeline

| Era | Development |
|---|---|
| **1950s** | Invention of the **Rolodex** — organised customer information manually in flip-through mode |
| **1960s** | **Mainframe computers** — first digitisation of customer information |
| **1980s** | **Database marketing** — strong wave of customer-centric, information-based push-marketing |
| **Late 1980s** | **Contact Management Software** — born from Conductor Software (1987) |
| **Early 1990s** | **Sales Force Automation (SFA)** — pioneering initiative from **Siebel** |
| **1995** | **"CRM" formally coined** — by Tom Siebel, Jagdish N. Seth, and the Gartner Group |
| **Late 1990s** | **Mobile CRM** — Salesforce Company takes the lead |
| **Early 2000s** | **Cloud-based CRM** — Salesforce pushes cloud IT solutions |
| **2004** | **Open-Source CRM** — SugarCRM becomes the first open-source CRM |
| **Late 2000s** | **Social CRM** — Twitter, Facebook, LinkedIn, Instagram integrated |
| **2010s** | **Explosive growth** in CRM software; enhanced internet protocols |
| **2020s** | **Integrated CRM** — from design to delivery, pre-market to after-market, with **personalisation as the core** |

> **CRM's greatest challenge and potential:** Deploying digital tools while ensuring personalisation. Digital data can make humans faceless — CRM must fight this tendency.

### 10. Kimberly-Clark CRM Case Study (HBR, 2004)

**Source:** "CRM Done Right" — Dianne Ledingham and Darrell Rigby, *Harvard Business Review*, November 2004

**Company:** Kimberly-Clark — global FMCG company with hundreds of product categories

**Problem:** Kimberly-Clark did not know which promotions were producing retailer loyalty, shelf space, and sales — and which were wasted spending.

**Solution:** Created a system to measure the **real-time impact of each promotion** on sales and profits — for both Kimberly-Clark AND its retailer customers.

**Outcome:**
- Sales reps shifted mindset from *"managing sales"* to *"managing the business"*
- Consistent data and business rules enabled broader strategic thinking

### 11. Making CRM Sustainable — Routine Issues vs. Strategic Signals

The vast data generated by CRM systems must be filtered to distinguish **operational noise** from **strategic signals**:

| Dimension | Routine Issue | Strategic Signal | Example of Strategic Signal + Solution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Who solves it** | Field salesforce with empowerment | Symptomatic of deeper malaise, impacts loyalty | Retailers not placing orders despite demand → solving boosts loyalty & sales |
| **Competitive value** | Solution easily copied | Solution creates substantial, sustainable competitive advantage | Supplying small-volume SKUs in rural interiors → boosts rural penetration |
| **CRM need** | Does not require CRM to solve | Recurring unless solved with CRM customisation | Seasonal supply mismatch → solve for high all-season sales |
| **Origin** | External/environmental | Seemingly external but can be a rallying point when solved | Lack of bank finance → strategic tie-ups with banks create competitive edge |
| **Cultural impact** | Not vital to organisation | Solving it creates a new cultural anchor | Regular sales beats at PoS → embeds customer-centricity in culture |
| **New capabilities** | Merely fixes the immediate problem | Solving creates new capabilities for additional opportunities | ERP not taking late-night orders → making it 24×7 creates capability for continuous industries |
| **Benefit duration** | Soft or transient benefits | Tangible, recurring benefits | Customer switching → build product relevance & brand loyalty for sustainable sales |
| **Visibility** | Goes unnoticed | Becomes a success story, role model inside and outside the org | Brand clutter → create mini umbrella brands for unity + diversity |

### 13. Humanising CRM

|---|---|

| Science | Relevance to CRM |
|---|---|

**Goal:** Design customer relationship journeys that elicit:
- **Short-term:** The desired behavioural response (purchase, repeat visit, referral)
- **Long-term:** Emotional embodiment — the customer feels an identity connection to the brand

## 📊 Summary

Lecture 25 opens with a fundamental definition: CRM is a philosophy and cultural orientation, not merely a software tool, exemplified by the Taj Group's approach to hotel touchpoints and Apple's systematic customer engagement across launch, in-store, online, and service phases. The customer switch-retain paradox reveals that marketers paradoxically want customers to switch to them easily but be impossible to switch away, producing four buyer archetypes. Six distinct customer psychological personas — Analytical, Experimental, Impulsive, Dynamic, Demanding, and Loyal — require fundamentally different marketing strategies. The lecture then details the Six Customer Principles that underpin strategic marketing: Expectation Discovery, Performance Fulfilment, Portfolio Mix (emphasising that the 20-80 rule is inappropriate for strategic decisions), Quality Pinnacle (citing Ohno, Juran, and Deming), Lifetime Value (with its three-component formula), and CRM as the integrating sixth principle. CRM's own 70-year evolution is traced from the Rolodex (1950s) to integrated, personalised digital CRM (2020s), with the formal coinage of the term by Tom Siebel and Jagdish N. Seth in 1995. The Kimberly-Clark HBR case illustrates a five-stage CRM cycle and how real-time promotion analytics transformed sales rep mindsets. A structured eight-dimension framework distinguishes routine operational issues from strategic signals — the latter requiring CRM investment for sustainable advantage. CRM tools are reviewed, with the critical caveat that no metric substitutes human relationship development; humanising CRM requires empathetic analytics informed by psychology, behavioural economics, and neuroscience. HUL's Horlicks demonstrates personal-scale CRM for a mass brand, while B2C and B2B connect platforms (motor shows, CES, CPHI, JP Morgan Healthcare) are identified as organised CRM touchpoints at scale.
