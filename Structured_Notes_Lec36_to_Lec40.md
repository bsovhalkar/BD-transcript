### 36.1 Corporate Brand Building

Before examining the case, the lecture establishes the framework of **corporate brand equity** — essential to corporate development.

#### Core Activities (6 Factors)

| Factor | Description |
|---|---|
| Strong product portfolio | Known for quality, features, and customer acceptance |
| Strong product brands | Portfolio of brands that reflect product reputation (e.g., Lux, Colgate) |
| Physical and financial performance | Robust business results signal credibility |
| Culture, values, and ethics | Impeccable reputation internally and externally |
| Higher-order business responsibility | ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) commitment |
| Stakeholder reputation | Fair dealing with all stakeholders |

> **Key insight:** Corporate brand equity is not built in a day — it is a decade's journey of assiduous effort.

#### Ancillary Activities

| Activity | Relevance |
|---|---|
| CSR execution and communication | Reinforces social responsibility positioning |
| Collaborative government campaigns | Demonstrates national alignment |
| Campus recruitment and educational sponsorships | Builds talent pipeline and institutional goodwill |
| Awards for public causes (e.g., entrepreneurship) | Elevates corporate stature |
| Active membership in CII, FICCI, ASSOCHAM | Industry association standing |

#### Growth Through Brand Building
Companies grow their brand by building:
1. **Core product and core business** — foundation
2. **Market niche and market share** — competitive standing
3. **Globalization** — international reach
4. **Competitive growth ranking** — sustained industry leadership

## Lecture 37: An FMCG Start-up — Nirma

### Key Topics Covered
- Nirma's origin: frugal product, cost leadership, and market dominance
- Three innovations: product, distribution, and promotional
- Competitive strategy against HLL (Hindustan Lever Limited)
- Backward integration and industrial scale-up
- Diversification: chemicals, pharmaceuticals, education, cement
- Rise and fall of Nirma FMCG market share
- Logic of business development from Nirma

---

### 37.1 Nirma's Origin Story

**Founded:** 1969 by **Karsanbhai Patel** (Government chemist from a farmer's family)

> **Founding insight:** There was a large market of lower and middle-class Indians for an affordable, indigenously developed detergent that could compete against premium MNC products.

**Key facts:**
- Started making detergent by hand in a 100 sq. ft. backyard
- Packed in polythene bags and sold door-to-door at **₹3.50/kg** vs. MNC price of **₹15/kg**
- Named after his daughter, **Nirupama**
- Incorporated as private limited (1984) → public limited (1993) → listed (1994)

**Iconic marketing jingle (35+ years):**  
*"Washing powder Nirma, Dudh si safedi Nirma se aaye, rangeen kapda bhi khil khil jaye"*

> At peak, Nirma became the **largest-selling single detergent brand in the world**.

---

### 37.2 Competitive Strategy — Nirma vs. MNCs

| Dimension | Nirma | HLL (Surf) |
|---|---|---|
| Price | ₹3.50/kg | ₹15/kg |
| Scale | Small Indian startup | Global MNC |
| Innovation | Frugal product; local sourcing | Premium product |
| Marketing | Jingles, wall paintings, BTL | Traditional ATL |
| Distribution | Flat, direct to distributor | Multi-tier |

**First winning formula:**
> **Indigenous innovation + Cost leadership + Advertising punch = Rapid market dominance**

**Nirma's strategy had three innovation pillars:**

| Innovation | Description |
|---|---|
| **Frugal product innovation** | Sustainable competitive advantage; MNCs initially dismissed it |
| **Distribution innovation** | Smooth product flow without inventory buffer disputes |
| **Promotional innovation** | Campaigns that made consumers seek Nirma, not competitor products |

**Result:** Market share grew from **12% (1977)** to **62% (1987)**, while Surf fell from 31% to 7%.

---

### 37.3 Cost Leadership Structure

Nirma's overall low-cost structure rested on:
- **a.** Low overheads
- **b.** Indigenous plant and machinery
- **c.** Lean management
- **d.** Flat distribution (factory → distributor, no intermediates)
- **e.** Cost control at every point of the value chain

**Packaging improvement:** Acquired Kisan Industries for in-house printing and packaging.

---

### 37.4 HLL's Counter-Strategy — Wheel

When Nirma captured the market, **HLL responded in 1995** with:
- **Wheel** — a low-price detergent with equivalent positioning and "better quality" claim
- Drastically altered its business model; created channels through thousands of small outlets
- Reduced pricing to match Nirma

*Result:* Nirma ₹1,500M sales, Wheel ₹1,000M sales, both at ~18% gross margin — HLL partially regained ground.

> **Key lesson:** An indigenous, humble start-up forced a global MNC to overhaul its entire business model.

---

### 37.5 Market Segmentation — Detergents

| Segment | Brands | Market Share |
|---|---|---|
| Premium | Surf, Ariel | 15% |
| Mid-price | Rin, Henko, Tide | 40% |
| Popular | Nirma, Wheel | 45% |

**Nirma's strategic gap:** Dominated the popular segment but left **55% of the market (premium + mid-price) untouched** — a vulnerability that competitors exploited.

---

### 37.6 Backward Integration — Manufacturing Scale

To maintain margins as volumes declined, Nirma invested heavily in backward integration:

| Plant | Investment | Capacity | Technology |
|---|---|---|---|
| Soda Ash | ₹1,140 Cr | 6.5 lakh TPA | AKZO Nobel (Netherlands); Full DCS control |
| Linear Alkyl Benzene (LAB) | ₹630 Cr | 75,000 TPA | Non-HF eco-friendly (UOP, USA); 2nd in Asia |
| Edible Salt | — | 2.88 lakh TPA | Asia's largest salt works (30,000 acres) |
| Soap | ₹200 Cr | 30 lakh pieces/day | 2nd largest soap maker in India |

> Soda ash + LAB account for **~70% of detergent manufacturing cost** — controlling these gave Nirma strong EBITDA margins.

**Capex 1997–2003:** ₹2,000 Cr (≈90% of average annual revenue) — demonstrating extraordinary conviction in backward integration.

---

### 37.7 Distribution Model

| Channel | Reach | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Principal Channel** (Nirma Consumer Care) | 450 exclusive distributors | Lowest-cost system; speed and agility |
| **Parallel Channel** (Nima brand) | 2,000 distributors | Wider reach; market intelligence; multi-brand |

**Combined retail reach:** 2+ million outlets; 40 million loyal consumers.

**Regional distribution:** West 44% | North 24% | South 20% | East 12%

---

### 37.8 Diversification Journey

Nirma evolved from a detergent company into a **diversified conglomerate**:

| Year | Diversification |
|---|---|
| 1969 | Detergents |
| 1992 | Toiletries (soaps, Nirma Bath) |
| 1994 | Education (Nirma University, Gujarat) |
| 1999 | Chemicals (Soda ash) + Edible salt |
| 2006 | Healthcare (Core Healthcare acquired → Aculife) |
| 2007 | Global acquisition: Searless Valley Minerals (California) |
| 2014 | Cement (Nuvoco Vistas Corporation) |
| 2017 | Tronox & Alkali (4th largest soda ash maker globally) |

**De-listing (March 2012):** Promoters bought out minority at ₹260/share (stake → 95.32%) to allow risk profile changes and conglomerate expansion without public market scrutiny.

---

### 37.9 Nirma's SWOT Analysis

| | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|
| | Strong brand equity built over decades | Limited export market |
| | Wide distribution network | Unable to penetrate premium segment |
| | Market leadership in popular segment | High-interest burden post backward integration |
| | Successful advertising campaigns | Lack of innovation in core detergent |

| | Opportunities | Threats |
|---|---|---|
| | Global acquisitions for distribution | MNC dominance in premium segments |
| | Entry into new FMCG categories | Regional players (e.g., Ghadi) |
| | Low per capita detergent consumption in India | Rising incomes → premiumization |

---

### 37.10 Rise and Fall of Nirma FMCG

| Period | Event |
|---|---|
| 1977–1987 | Rose from 12% to 62% market share |
| 1990s | Cluster of companies; then merged in 1997 for economies of scale |
| 2000 | Peak market share ~38% |
| 2006 | Decline begins; lack of product innovation; Ghadi captures share |
| 2012 | De-listed from stock exchanges |
| 2022 | Market share ≈10.4% |

**Current ranking:** Ghadi (17%) → Wheel (16.9%) → Tide (13.5%) → Nirma (4th)

> **Critical mistake:** Nirma failed to feel the pulse of premiumization; used only 3–4% of revenue on ads vs. 6% by MNCs; widened portfolio without core innovation.

---

### 37.11 Logic of Business Development — Nirma Model

```
1. Find a product niche
2. Develop a business strategy and business model
3. Create infrastructure; produce the product
4. Achieve volumes, revenues, and profits
5. Grow product and corporate brands
6. Develop cash reserves and a strong balance sheet
7. Enter a new business (organically or inorganically)
8. Develop a war chest (VC/PE investments)
9. Execute more organic and inorganic projects
10. Repeat steps 5–9
11. Groom new leaders
12. Become a multi-business conglomerate
```

---

### 37.12 Key Takeaways — Lecture 37

- **Frugal innovation + cost leadership + distribution** can dismantle even MNC market dominance
- A company that disrupts the market must continually innovate to stay ahead — stagnation invites imitation
- Backward integration protects margins but must be matched by product-level innovation
- Diversification into unrelated fields alienates investors but can create long-term conglomerate value
- The founder's vision and passion are the foundational engines of business development

## Lecture 38: A Logistics Start-up — Delhivery

### 38.1 Background: Indian Logistics Before Delhivery

India's road transport sector was considered impossible to modernize:
- **Single truck operators** dominated — no business systems or tech upgrades
- Routes and loads were **suboptimal** (trucks often returned empty)
- Clients had **no direct booking** capability
- No digital mediation, all manual

> Delhivery completely disrupted this sector through technology-led, data-driven logistics.

### 38.7 Mission and Corporate Strategy

> **Mission:** Enable customers to operate flexible, reliable, and resilient supply chains at the lowest cost.

**Two-Wheel Model:**
1. **Logistics Wheel** — Superior CX → stickiness → scale → lower cost
2. **Technology + Data Wheel** — Data science → precision → insights for customer profitability

**Six Pillars of Corporate Strategy:**
1. Expand infrastructure and network continuously
2. Build scale in existing business lines
3. Deepen customer relationships
4. Enhance software + hardware capabilities
5. Externalize the Logistics OS
6. Pursue strategic alliances, select acquisitions, and international expansion

### 38.9 ONDC Integration (December 2022)

- **ONDC** = Open Network for Digital Commerce (India's "UPI for e-commerce")
- Delhivery went live on ONDC, enabling SMEs and small sellers to access Delhivery's 18,000+ pin-code network
- Democratizes last-mile delivery for the remotest corners of India

---

### 38.10 Industry Outlook

| Parameter | FY20 | FY26 (Projected) | CAGR |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total Indian Logistics Market | $216B | $365B | ~9% |
| Organized Logistics Players | $6–7B | $38–40B | **34–36%** |

> Organized logistics will grow **5x** faster than overall logistics — a structural opportunity for Delhivery.

---

### 38.11 BD Lessons from Delhivery

1. Be an **early digital disruptor** in a traditional industry
2. **Raise ample capital** for an investment-intensive disruption journey
3. Invest in **proprietary technology** (mesh network, LogOS) — not off-the-shelf
4. Build a **vast all-India partner network** to maintain asset-light model at scale
5. Prioritize **data intelligence and AI** for operational excellence
6. Enable **D2C and SME customers** to unlock new revenue streams
7. Integrate with government platforms (ONDC) to future-proof the business

---

### 38.12 Key Takeaways — Lecture 38

- Digital disruption in logistics requires deep technology investment, not just an app
- The mesh network (vs. hub-and-spoke) is a structural competitive moat
- Losses in early years are intentional if market capture and operational scale are the goals
- Customer-level data science capabilities are as valuable as physical logistics infrastructure

### 39.1 Zydus Wellness — Origin and Foundation

**Established:** 1988 | **Headquarters:** Ahmedabad, Gujarat  
**Promoter:** Zydus Cadila Limited (now Zydus Life — Top 5 pharma company in India)

> **Founding insight:** Before health-consciousness and calorie concerns became mainstream, Zydus identified **sugar substitution** as an untapped Indian niche.

**Mission:** "We approach health and wellness in a holistic manner. We nourish, nurture, and energize millions of lives by offering a variety of innovative industry-leading products."

**Four Pillars of Zydus Wellness:**
1. **Financial strength** — backed by the Zydus Cadila parent group
2. **Product innovation** — continuous new product development
3. **Manufacturing integrity** — quality-first manufacturing
4. **Supply chain efficiency** — strong distribution backbone

### 39.4 STP for Sugar-Free

**Segmenting:** Diabetics, health-conscious urban consumers, fitness-oriented individuals

**Targeting Evolution:**
- Phase 1 (1988): Prescription product for diabetics only
- Phase 2 (1991+): OTC — expanded to all health-conscious consumers
- Phase 3: Digital targeting, lifestyle positioning, cooking/recipe integration

**Positioning:** Not just a medicinal product — a daily lifestyle companion  
**Market share:** **94%** in the Indian sugar substitute category

**4 Ps of Sugar-Free:**

| P | Strategy |
|---|---|
| **Product** | Gold, Natura, Green, Sugarlite; multiple SKUs (tablets, sachet, liquid, drops) |
| **Price** | Premium vs. sugar (accepts health premium); competitive in category |
| **Place** | Medical stores + grocery stores + e-commerce (Amazon); widest dual-channel reach |
| **Promotion** | Celebrity chefs (Sanjeev Kapoor); fitness celebrities (Katrina Kaif, Parineeti Chopra); recipe campaigns |

### 39.10 Logic of Business Development — Zydus Wellness Model

```

5. Theme-based celebrity brand building
6. Successful IPO for greater public stature
7. Aggressive acquisitions to expand product portfolio
```
