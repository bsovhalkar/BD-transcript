## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. What is Customer Relationship Management?

**CRM** is defined as a set of approaches and tools for a company to:
- Stay engaged with its customers
- Enhance customer experiences continuously

It is fundamentally a **philosophy** and a **cultural facet** of progressive companies, not merely a software or sales tool. CRM is applicable to every type of organisation — commercial, non-profit, B2B, B2C, etc.

> **Key insight:** CRM is not a department; it is the DNA of a customer-centric organisation.

A hotel represents one of the richest examples of CRM in action. Every service offered is a **touchpoint** — an opportunity to demonstrate customer-centricity.

**Touchpoints for a Hotel:**

| Stay & Access | Reservation, Check-in, Check-out |
| In-Room | Room Service |

| CRM Pillar | Activities |

### 4. Marketing Evolution — The Four Phases

CRM maturity is aligned with marketing's historical evolution:

| Era | Phase | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **1950s – 1970s** | Mass Marketing | One product, all customers; few SKUs, vast coverage |
| **1980s – 1990s** | Mass Customisation | Curated products; customer groups; internet begins enabling customisation |
| **2010s – 2020s** | Personal Touch in Marketing | Internet communication, behavioural data analytics, digital touchpoints |
| **Future** | Personalised Marketing | Product/process customisation per individual; data-driven personalisation |

**The Medicine Analogy:** When medicine can be personalised (genetic profiling of patients matched to therapeutic drug profiles), marketing — which governs less complex interactions — certainly can and must be personalised. *No two customers are the same.*

### 5. The Customer 'Switch-Retain' Paradox

Marketers seek a **paradoxical** (self-serving) behaviour from customers:
- **Want customers to SWITCH TO their brand easily**
- **Want customers to REFUSE to SWITCH AWAY once acquired**

This creates a 2×2 matrix:

|  | **Easy to Retain** | **Hard to Retain** |
|---|---|---|
| **Inclined to Switch** | Impulsive Buyers | Dynamic Buyers |
| **Disinclined to Switch** | **Loyal Buyers** *(ideal)* | Demanding Buyers |

- **Loyal Buyers:** Every company's ideal — disinclined to switch AND easy to retain
- **Dynamic Buyers:** Always seeking novelty; switch at first opportunity
- **Demanding Buyers:** Highest standards; require maximum marketing effort
- **Impulsive Buyers:** Susceptible to persuasion; inclined to switch but can be retained with appeals

### 6. Six Types of Customer Personas

Even within the same demographic and socio-economic segment, customers exhibit different psychological profiles:

| Customer Type | Decision Style | Marketing Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Analytical** | Logical, data-based; evaluates, validates; reads all product literature | Must use technical detailing; specifications-heavy communication |
| **Experimental** | Tuned to new and innovative; high expectations; among first adopters | Can co-work as early testers; preview launches |
| **Impulsive** | Driven by instincts and appeals; style > substance; celebrity endorsements work | Huge latitude for promotion-led sales |
| **Dynamic** | Continuously seeking new things; quick to switch at first alternative; NOT the same as experimental | Create a product range to hook them; prevent competitor switching |
| **Demanding** | Seeks high quality + low price (cost leadership + product differentiation simultaneously) | Mastering this segment (as global giants do in India) builds resilience |
| **Loyal** | Conformist; goes by past association; passive decision-making | Do not ossify strategy by over-relying on their loyalty; may resent variation |

> **Key distinction:** *Experimental* customers will stay loyal **as long as the product stays innovative**. *Dynamic* customers will leave **regardless**, always chasing the next thing.

### 8. The Six Customer Principles

These six principles form the core of **Strategic Marketing / Business Development**:

#### Principle 1: Expectation Discovery
The starting point of CRM — understanding or **shaping** customer expectations.

**Virtuous Cycle:**
> Discovering Expectations → Providing Right Solutions → High Customer Satisfaction → High Customer Loyalty → Repeat Purchases → (back to) Discovering Expectations

- Market research and design thinking are the tools
- Companies must **discover AND deliver** — discovery without delivery leads to frustration, not satisfaction
- The right model = **Expectation–Reality Match**

#### Principle 2: Performance Fulfilment
Performance fulfilment is both a product development AND a marketing responsibility.

- Customers **build** expectations based on product appearance and material → they **seek** to fulfil expected performance in use
- Buyers choose firms offering **high performance balanced across price points**
- **Operational Value** = Total Customer Benefits − Total Customer Costs
- **Goal:** Maximise net operating benefit for the customer (the foundational principle of strategic marketing)
- High performance ≠ High lifetime value automatically; both must be achieved together

#### Principle 3: Portfolio Mix
Companies must optimise **customer-product profitability**, not just individual product profitability.

- **Obvious:** Losing a profitable customer deeply impacts profitability
- **Less obvious:** Losing a marginal customer is a strategic disadvantage — relationship loss is more lasting than the immediate revenue loss
- **Hospital analogy:** Doctors need the full pharmaceutical product suite; if a pharma company refuses to supply commodity generics (low margin), the doctor switches the entire relationship (including premium injectables) to another company
- **Cost of acquiring a new customer** can be many times the cost of retaining an existing one — especially in B2B
- **The 20-80 rule is inappropriate for strategic marketing decisions** — marginal customers must not be dismissed

#### Principle 4: The Quality Pinnacle
Quality is the most important influencer of customer loyalty, and marketers must **actively drive** quality standards upward.

**Quality is defined as:** *The totality of features and attributes of a product or service that establish its ability to fulfil stated or implicit needs — including the quality of customer experience.*

**Three Quality Stalwarts cited:**

| Pioneer | Organisation | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Taiichi Ohno** | Toyota | Toyota Production System (TPS); zero-defect manufacturing |
| **Joseph M. Juran** | — | Quality Management; Juran's Trilogy (Planning, Control, Improvement) |
| **W. Edwards Deming** | — | 14 Points for Management; statistical quality control |

- Quality cannot be measured on a single dimension — JD Power evaluates automobiles on multiple parameters (design, development, after-sales service, on-road performance across conditions)
- Marketers should engage **external independent consultants** for quality ratings

#### Principle 5: Lifetime Value (LTV)

**LTV is conceptually elegant but practically difficult to assure.** Past customer databases provide benchmarks for future propositions.

**Three Components:**

| Component | Elements |
|---|---|
| **Acquisition Cost** | Net cash outflow on the buy + Cost of capital |
| **Operating Cost over Lifespan** | Running & maintenance costs + Useful life for intended application |
| **Resale Value at End-of-Life** | Trade-in facility availability + Net value after refurbishment |

**Formula:**
> **LTV = Acquisition Cost (Day 1) − [Discounted Operating Cost over Lifetime] + [Discounted Resale Value]**

All future costs/values discounted back to **Day 1** for comparability.

- Products with high quality and brand reputation enjoy **higher resale value** — customers factor this into their original purchase decision
- Having a captive financing unit allows a company to offer a better cost of capital to buyers, improving LTV attractiveness

#### Principle 6: Customer Relationship Management
CRM knits together all five principles above and provides the execution infrastructure for strategic marketing.

### 9. CRM Evolution Timeline

| Era | Development |
|---|---|
| **1950s** | Invention of the **Rolodex** — organised customer information manually in flip-through mode |
| **1960s** | **Mainframe computers** — first digitisation of customer information |
| **1980s** | **Database marketing** — strong wave of customer-centric, information-based push-marketing |
| **Late 1980s** | **Contact Management Software** — born from Conductor Software (1987) |
| **Early 1990s** | **Sales Force Automation (SFA)** — pioneering initiative from **Siebel** |
| **1995** | **"CRM" formally coined** — by Tom Siebel, Jagdish N. Seth, and the Gartner Group |
| **Late 1990s** | **Mobile CRM** — Salesforce Company takes the lead |
| **Early 2000s** | **Cloud-based CRM** — Salesforce pushes cloud IT solutions |
| **2004** | **Open-Source CRM** — SugarCRM becomes the first open-source CRM |
| **Late 2000s** | **Social CRM** — Twitter, Facebook, LinkedIn, Instagram integrated |
| **2010s** | **Explosive growth** in CRM software; enhanced internet protocols |
| **2020s** | **Integrated CRM** — from design to delivery, pre-market to after-market, with **personalisation as the core** |

> **CRM's greatest challenge and potential:** Deploying digital tools while ensuring personalisation. Digital data can make humans faceless — CRM must fight this tendency.

### 10. Kimberly-Clark CRM Case Study (HBR, 2004)

**Source:** "CRM Done Right" — Dianne Ledingham and Darrell Rigby, *Harvard Business Review*, November 2004

**Company:** Kimberly-Clark — global FMCG company with hundreds of product categories

**Problem:** Kimberly-Clark did not know which promotions were producing retailer loyalty, shelf space, and sales — and which were wasted spending.

**Solution:** Created a system to measure the **real-time impact of each promotion** on sales and profits — for both Kimberly-Clark AND its retailer customers.

**Outcome:**
- Sales reps shifted mindset from *"managing sales"* to *"managing the business"*
- Consistent data and business rules enabled broader strategic thinking

**The Kimberly-Clark CRM Cycle:**

| CRM Stage | Key Activities |
|---|---|
| **Development of Offering** | Concept development; features & function prioritisation; customer panel management; cost-position requirements; competitive intelligence & research |
| **Sales** | Sales forecasting; lead management; bid & quote management; pipeline management; cross-selling; personalisation; cluster management |
| **Superior Experience** | Pre-sales experience; offering delivery; interaction management; helpdesk queue management; escalation; service broadcasting |
| **Retention & Win Back** | Share-of-wallet analysis; loyalty programme management; retention management; win-back campaign management; collaborative innovation |
| **Targeting & Marketing** | Market segmentation; customer group behaviour analytics; campaign management; pricing promotion; win-loss analysis |

The company benchmarked its CRM path against competitors (Brother and Molex) to identify capability gaps and competitive advantages.

### 11. Making CRM Sustainable — Routine Issues vs. Strategic Signals

The vast data generated by CRM systems must be filtered to distinguish **operational noise** from **strategic signals**:

| Dimension | Routine Issue | Strategic Signal | Example of Strategic Signal + Solution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Who solves it** | Field salesforce with empowerment | Symptomatic of deeper malaise, impacts loyalty | Retailers not placing orders despite demand → solving boosts loyalty & sales |
| **Competitive value** | Solution easily copied | Solution creates substantial, sustainable competitive advantage | Supplying small-volume SKUs in rural interiors → boosts rural penetration |
| **CRM need** | Does not require CRM to solve | Recurring unless solved with CRM customisation | Seasonal supply mismatch → solve for high all-season sales |
| **Origin** | External/environmental | Seemingly external but can be a rallying point when solved | Lack of bank finance → strategic tie-ups with banks create competitive edge |
| **Cultural impact** | Not vital to organisation | Solving it creates a new cultural anchor | Regular sales beats at PoS → embeds customer-centricity in culture |
| **New capabilities** | Merely fixes the immediate problem | Solving creates new capabilities for additional opportunities | ERP not taking late-night orders → making it 24×7 creates capability for continuous industries |
| **Benefit duration** | Soft or transient benefits | Tangible, recurring benefits | Customer switching → build product relevance & brand loyalty for sustainable sales |
| **Visibility** | Goes unnoticed | Becomes a success story, role model inside and outside the org | Brand clutter → create mini umbrella brands for unity + diversity |

### 13. Humanising CRM

Pure data-driven CRM risks making customers faceless. Humanisation is the antidote.

**Empathetic CRM Analytics requires three capabilities:**

| Capability | Description |
|---|---|
| **Intelligent Data Analytics** | Drawing actionable patterns from vast data |
| **Creative Ingenuity** | Translating data into inventive customer experiences |
| **Human Insight** | Interpreting data through the lens of human behaviour and emotion |

**The Sciences of Relationship — Three domains underpinning CRM humanity:**

| Science | Relevance to CRM |
|---|---|
| **Psychology** | Understanding customer motivations, emotions, and decision-making biases |
| **Behavioural Economics** | How customers actually make choices (vs. rational models) |
| **Neuroscience** | How brands and experiences are encoded in memory and emotion |

**Goal:** Design customer relationship journeys that elicit:
- **Short-term:** The desired behavioural response (purchase, repeat visit, referral)
- **Long-term:** Emotional embodiment — the customer feels an identity connection to the brand

### 14. HUL / Horlicks CRM — A Case Study in Personalisation

**Context:** After acquiring Horlicks from GSK, Hindustan Unilever (HUL) launched three new health products through Horlicks digital stores.

**Three New Horlicks Products:**
1. Mulberry Leaf Gummies — for Diabetes Control
2. Nutrition Gummies — for Children
3. High Fibre Powder — for Better Metabolism

**CRM Practice:** HUL responds to **every customer query individually** via email and telecall — remarkable for a company of its scale.

**Horlicks' CRM History (Best Practices):**

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 2005 | GSK released clinical study: children consuming Horlicks grew better; first brand to address kids directly instead of mothers |
| 2018 | Addressed dangerous exam pressure in advertising — emotionally resonant CRM |
| Various | Adopted both emotional AND combative advertising (head-to-head with Complan) |
| Various | First brand to release separate TV ads in regional languages |
| 80 years | J. Walter Thompson (JWT) worked exclusively on Horlicks in India |

**The 'Jug-Mug' Brand:** Horlicks always had a free jug or mug on promotion — mothers loved the brand for the product AND the free gift. This deep consumer connect was built decades before digital CRM existed.

> **Lesson:** Horlicks understood customer connect earlier and more deeply than most brands — the digital era simply provides new channels for the same principle.

## 🔍 Final Short Summary

Lecture 25 establishes that **CRM is a philosophy of customer-centricity** — not merely software — demonstrated by exemplars like the Taj Group and Apple. Marketing has evolved from mass marketing to personalised marketing, and customers span six distinct psychological personas requiring tailored strategies, governed by six core customer principles (Expectation Discovery, Performance Fulfilment, Portfolio Mix, Quality Pinnacle, Lifetime Value, and CRM). CRM itself evolved over 70 years from the Rolodex to integrated cloud-based systems, with the term coined in 1995. The Kimberly-Clark HBR case shows CRM in action via a five-stage cycle, and a structured framework separates routine issues from strategic signals. Ultimately, CRM must be **humanised** — combining data analytics with psychology, behavioural economics, and neuroscience — as no metric can substitute genuine human relationship building.
