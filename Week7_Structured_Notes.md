### 31.1 Brand Strategy and Plan — The Core of Business Development

> **Core idea:** Brand strategy and planning is the cornerstone of business development. Without it, a company cannot sustainably differentiate itself and grow.

A robust brand strategy requires three inputs:

```
Understand Market Needs  →  Understand the Competition  →  Position the Product and Brand
```

The brand planning process integrates these inputs into structured phases: **Diagnosis → Prognosis → Objectives → Strategies → Actions → Controls & Feedback**.

### 31.3 The Brand Planning Process

The pharma industry has developed one of the most systematic brand planning processes in Indian business. It is structured in **nine stages**:

**Phase I — Diagnosis:**
1. **Product & Market Definition** — Define the product and its target market (e.g., the endocrinology drug universe for a new anti-diabetic).
2. **Situation Analysis** — Macro analysis (PESTED+) and micro analysis.
3. **Market & Business Drivers Analysis** — What external and internal forces drive the market?
4. **Segmentation & Characterization** — Identify distinct segments.
5. **Customer/Brand Attribute Mapping** — Align customer needs with brand attributes.

**Phase II — Prognosis:**
6. **SWOT Analysis** — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats.
7. **Key Challenges & Critical Success Factors (CSFs).**
8. **Insights** — Interpretative reading of data leading to actionable conclusions.

**Phase III — Objectives, Strategy, and Execution:**
9. **Qualitative & Quantitative Objectives → Go-to-Market Brand Strategy → Marketing Mix Strategies → Tactics → KPIs → Budgets → Monitoring.**

### 31.4 Building Strong Brands — The Positioning Process

**Levels of Segmentation:**
```
Mass Market → Multiple Segments → Single Segment → Individual as Segment (Customization)
```
The level of segmentation chosen determines the brand's scope and positioning approach.

**Steps in the Positioning Process:**

1. Identify competitive products serving the target market.
2. Determine **determinant attributes** that define the "product space."
3. Map consumer perceptions of each brand on those attributes.
4. Identify the brand's current location (positioning) in product space.
5. Determine customers' preferred combination of attributes.
6. Assess the fit between market segments and current brand positions.
7. Develop the **value proposition** for marketing strategy.
8. Identify positions where new brands might be placed.

> **Key principle:** "A positioning statement must be an enduring statement of product and brand that has a fit with customer needs."

**Case Study — Bottled Water:**

| Player | Positioning Strategy |
|--------|---------------------|
| Coke's Dasani | Flavoured variant (Dasani Essence) |
| Pepsi's SoBe Lifewater | Natural sweetener (stevia) |
| Himalaya | Source differentiation (Himalayan origin) |
| Brita / PUR | Water filters — eco-friendly, home-use alternative |

> **Emerging trend:** ESG-linked brand differentiation — net positive water balance, groundwater replenishment, net carbon neutrality.

### 31.5 Software Tools for Positioning Decision-Making

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **Conjoint Analysis** | Identifies the combination of attributes consumers most prefer; narrows down product design options |
| **Factor Analysis** | Groups related attributes into underlying factors; constructs product space maps |
| **Discriminant Analysis** | Identifies perceptual dimensions based on attributes that best differentiate brands |
| **Multidimensional Scaling (MDS)** | Constructs geometrically spaced maps based on consumer judgments of brand similarity or preference |

### 31.7 Monitoring Competition — Three Shares

A brand's competitive health is tracked on three dimensions:

| Metric | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| **Market Share** | Competitor's share of target market (sales volume/value) |
| **Mind Share** | Top-of-mind recall among consumers |
| **Heart Share** | Consumer's resolve to buy the brand despite awareness of competitors |

> **Key insight:** Mind share and heart share are **leading indicators** of future market share. A brand that loses mind or heart share will lose market share subsequently.

### 31.9 BrandAsset® Valuator Model

This model (adapted from Young & Rubicam's BAV framework) evaluates brands on four pillars:

| Pillar | Category | Definition |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Differentiation** | Brand Strength (Leading Indicator) | The brand's point of difference in the market |
| **Relevance** | Brand Strength (Leading Indicator) | How appropriate the brand is for the customer |
| **Esteem** | Brand Stature (Current Indicator) | How helpful the brand is for the customer's personality and self-image |
| **Knowledge** | Brand Stature (Current Indicator) | Depth of consumer understanding of the brand |

**The Universe of Brand Performance (2×2 matrix):**

| | Low Brand Stature | High Brand Stature |
|-|------------------|-------------------|
| **High Brand Strength** | **Rising Stars** — Building energy and relevance but small audience; consumers showing curiosity | **Champions** — Combining high strength with high stature; highest earnings, margin power, and future value |
| **Low Brand Strength** | **Laggards** — Not well known; new entrants or brands that have "lost their way" | **Declining Icons** — High stature insufficient without strength; struggle to overcome consumer expectations |

> **Objective:** Every firm must aim to move to the top-right quadrant — **high brand strength AND high brand stature**.

### 31.10 Building Brand Equity — Four Value Stages

Strong brand equity requires building through four sequential value stages:
1. **Brand Identity** — Who are you? (Salience)
2. **Brand Meaning** — What are you? (Performance + Imagery)
3. **Brand Response** — What do I think/feel about you? (Judgments + Feelings)
4. **Brand Resonance** — What kind of relationship do I have with you? (Loyalty)

**Case Study — Fortune Edible Oil (Adani Wilmar):**

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| 2001 | Launch of Fortune brand |
| 2002 | Becomes segment leader |
| 2010 | Launch of Fortune Plus range |
| 2013 | Launch of Fortune Rice Bran Health Oil |
| 2014–21 | Extensions: Atta, GN Active, Soya Nuggets, Basmati, Besan, Pulses, Tetra packs, Sugar, Chana Sattu |

**Fortune Brand Audit — Key Contributions:**
- Accelerated shift from unorganised to organised edible oils sector.
- Graduated from generic recognition to celebrity endorsements.
- Transformed product-push approach (price discounting) to consumer-pull (premium pricing).
- Extended from edible oils into synergistic foods adjacencies.

### 32.1 Functional Organisation of an SBU

Every SBU (Strategic Business Unit) requires a functional structure:

```
SBU President
├── R&D
├── Production
├── Marketing
│   ├── Marketing Research
│   ├── Sales
│   ├── Advertising
│   └── Sales Promotion
├── Human Resources
└── Finance / Control
```

Marketing is the central function that connects all customer-facing activities.

---

### 32.2 Types of Marketing Organisation Structure

**Type 1 — Product Management Organisation:**
- Marketing Director oversees both sub-functions AND a Group Product Manager.
- Group Product Manager supervises individual Product Managers (one per product).
- **Use when:** Products differ significantly in requirements or volume.

**Type 2 — Market Management Organisation:**
- Marketing Director oversees sub-functions AND a Group Markets Manager.
- Group Markets Manager supervises Market Managers (one per market/geography).
- **Use when:** Growth must be driven through specific markets.

**Type 3 — Product/Brand Management Organisation (nuclear organisation):**
- Does NOT replace the functional organisation — it is an **integrating layer** on top.
- Marketing Head → Advertising/Sales Promotion Manager / Sales Manager / Marketing Research Manager / New Products Manager.
- Group Product Manager → Product Category Managers → Specific Product and Brand Managers.
- **Use when:** Product portfolio is large, diverse, and complex.

> **Key insight:** The product management organisation is sometimes called a **"nuclear organisation"** because the product manager is at the core, orbited by all supporting functions.

---

### 32.3 The Role of the Product Manager

> **Definition:** A product manager is like a **CEO for the product** — owning full responsibility and accountability for brand development and profitability.

The product manager interfaces with ALL of the following:
- Manufacturing & Distribution

- Legal
- Finance

**Project Management Responsibility:**

| Interface | Key Activity |
|-----------|-------------|

**Sun Pharma Example:** Sun Pharma's product management structure is a benchmark in the Indian pharma industry for its discipline and execution focus.

---

### 32.4 Three Types of Product Teams

| Team Type | Structure | Use Case |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Triangular** | Product Manager + Associate PM + Product Associate | Small product/early stage |
| **Vertical** | Product Manager + Brand Manager + Researcher/Planner/Communicator/Analyst | Medium complexity |
| **Horizontal** | Product Manager with Researcher, Planner, Communicator (cross-functional peers) | Large-scale, mature brand |

> The move from Triangular → Vertical → Horizontal happens as product scale and complexity grows.

---

### 32.5 Brand Profitability

Brand profitability is tracked through a P&L framework:

| Line Item | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Goods Sold (Units) | Volume |
| Sales Revenue | Net Sales |
| Less: Salesforce fixed costs, incentives, advertising, promotions, logistics, overheads | All direct marketing costs |
| = Brand Profit Contribution | The brand's real contribution to the business |

> **Key insight:** Product management's core financial responsibility is to maximise **brand profit contribution**, not just top-line revenue.

---

### 32.6 Marketing Metrics and ROI

Marketing must justify its investments with rigorous metrics at two levels:

**Level 1 — Sales Metrics:**
- Revenue, Volume, Market Share, Growth Rate, Distribution reach.

**Level 2 — Marketing Metrics (beyond sales):**
- Brand awareness, trial rate, satisfaction score, net promoter score (NPS), marketing ROI.

**Marketing ROI Formulas (examples):**
- Revenue/Marketing Spend
- Incremental Revenue/Incremental Marketing Spend
- Customer Lifetime Value / Customer Acquisition Cost

**Marketing Audit:**
- A periodic, independent review of a company's marketing environment, objectives, strategies, and activities.
- Conducted to identify areas of strength, weakness, and opportunity.

> **Key principle:** "The better the marketing organisation, the more it will rely on evidence-based decision-making rather than gut feel."

---

### 32.7 Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) in Marketing

CSR is not a peripheral activity — it is integral to a company's marketing legitimacy:
- Companies must demonstrate **genuine social commitment**, not tokenism.
- CSR enhances brand equity by aligning the brand with consumer values.
- The structural shift towards ESG-aware consumers makes CSR a **marketing imperative**.

### 33.1 The Standard Communication Process

```
Sender → [Encodes Message] → Media (with Noise) → [Decodes Message] → Receiver → Response (Feedback)
```

> **Key principle:** The well-designed communication process minimises noise and ensures the message received matches the message sent. In marketing, the response is a **purchase decision**.

---

### 33.2 Advertising Models — Three Stages

| Stage | Consumer State | Activities |
|-------|--------------|-----------|
| **Cognitive** | Thinking | Attention, Awareness, Knowledge, Comprehension |
| **Affective** | Feeling | Interest, Desire, Liking, Preference, Conviction |
| **Behavioural** | Acting | Evaluation, Trial, Order, Reorder |

> **Key insight:** Different advertising approaches are required at each stage. What works at the awareness stage (cognitive) will not work at the conviction or reorder stage (behavioural).

---

### 33.3 Steps in Developing Effective Communication

1. **Identify the target audience** — Who is being communicated to?
2. **Determine communication objectives** — What response is sought?
3. **Design the message** — Content, structure, format, source.
4. **Select channels** — Personal (sales force, KOLs) vs. non-personal (ATL/BTL/TTL).
5. **Establish the budget** — Affordable method, % of sales, competitive parity, objective & task.
6. **Decide on the media mix** — Allocate budget across channels.
7. **Measure results** — Impact on sales, awareness, attitude.
8. **Manage the process** — Integrated marketing communications (IMC).

---

### 33.4 ATL, BTL, and TTL Advertising

| Type | Full Form | Channels | Best For |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **ATL** | Above The Line | TV, Radio, Print (National Newspapers, Magazines), Cinema, Outdoor | Mass market awareness; broad reach |
| **BTL** | Below The Line | Direct marketing, Sales promotions, Point-of-sale, Events, Sponsorships, Loyalty programmes | Targeted engagement; measurable ROI |
| **TTL** | Through The Line | Integrated use of both ATL and BTL; Digital marketing bridges both | Holistic campaigns combining reach + engagement |

**Profiles of Major Media Types:**

| Medium | Advantages | Limitations |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Television | Mass reach, sight+sound+motion, emotional impact | High cost, fleeting exposure, cluttered |
| Radio | Local targeting, high frequency, low cost | Audio only, fragmented audience |
| Print (Newspaper) | Credibility, geographic flexibility, detailed copy | Short life, print quality varies |
| Magazine | High-quality reproduction, targeted readers | Long lead time, not immediate |
| Outdoor | Geographic flexibility, high repeat exposure | Limited creative, hard to target |
| Digital | Precise targeting, measurable, flexible, interactive | Ad-blocking, privacy concerns, fragmented |

### 34.1 What is Servitization?

> **Definition:** Servitization refers to adding services to existing product-based offerings to stimulate additional revenue and growth. It is the shift from selling products to selling **outcomes**.

**The famous Levitt quote:**  
> *"People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole!"*  
— Prof. Theodore Levitt, Harvard Business School

**Traditional Manufacturing vs. Servitization:**

| Dimension | Traditional Manufacturing | Servitization |
|-----------|--------------------------|--------------|
| Where value lies | Products & physical parts | Solutions |
| When value is realised | At point of sale | At point of consumption, repeatedly |
| Customer gets | Ownership of product | Access to outcomes |
| Revenue model | One-time sale | Recurring, usage-based fees |

**Servitization represents:**
- Service-dominant logic
- Access-based services and business model
- Product-Service Systems (PSS)
- Value co-creation with the customer
- Fractional ownership
- Sharing economy and circular economy

---

### 34.2 Value Matrix — Two Dimensions

| | Value in Products & Parts | Value in Solutions |
|-|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **Realised at Point of Sale** | Traditional (product-focused) | Advisory-focused |
| **Realised through Life** | Aftermarket-focused | Outcome-focused (pure servitization) |

> **Goal:** Move toward the **Outcome-Focused** quadrant — where value lies in solutions AND is realised repeatedly over the product's life.

---

### 34.3 Why Servitization? — Strategic Importance

1. Acts as a **growth engine** by opening new revenue streams from existing customers.
2. Creates **deeper, stickier customer relationships** (lock-in through value, not contracts).
3. Provides **recurrent revenue** — more predictable and less volatile than one-time product sales.
4. Enables **circular economy** — ownership retained by the manufacturer incentivises product longevity and reuse.
5. Drives **sustainability and decarbonization** — pay-per-use models incentivise energy efficiency.

---

### 34.4 Customer Journey Towards Servitization

| Stage | Service Level | Technology Enabler |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Basic Services** — Operation manuals, training, service, parts | None / minimal |
| 2 | **Intermediate Services** — Real-time product performance analysis and troubleshooting | ICT (remote troubleshooting) |
| 3 | **Customised Services** — ICT combined with telematics for expanded support | ICT + Telematics |
| 4 | **Advanced Services** — Total lifecycle management; revenues linked to utilisation | IoT, cloud, AI |
| 5 | **Outcome-based Services** — End-to-end solutions; automatic hassle-free upgrades | IoT + AI + Digital twin |

> **Typical products ideal for servitization:** Capital goods, consumer durables, high-cost products, high-technology products.

---

### 34.5 Pioneering Examples of Servitization

| Company / Sector | Traditional Model | Servitized Model |
|-----------------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Xerox** | Sold photocopiers | Pay-per-copy |
| **SunEdison** | Sold solar panels | Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) — buy solar energy, not panels |
| **Signify (Philips)** | Sold lighting equipment | Light-as-a-Service (implemented at Amsterdam's Schiphol Airport) |
| **Rolls-Royce (Jet Engines)** | Sold engines | "Power by the Hour" — sold thrust/flight hours; real-time engine telemetry |
| **Medical Imaging (PET/CT/MRI)** | Sold scanners to hospitals | Continuous technology upgrades as a service — OEM-hospital techno-economic partnership |

### 34.8 Servitization and Innovation

Servitization drives a **virtuous innovation cycle**:
- Pay-per-use model incentivises manufacturers to optimise equipment performance continuously.
- Operating efficiency drives first innovation, then subsequent waves of innovation.
- Aligns interests of buyer (user) and equipment supplier (vendor) — both benefit from improved performance.
- Maximises adoption of technologies and best practices in equipment operation.

### 34.9 Product-as-a-Service (PaaS) and the Circular Economy

```

                                                 ↓

                                                 ↓
                                    End of Product → Return of EoP → Circular reuse
```

> Keeping ownership of the equipment with the manufacturer **incentivises modular design** — components can be upgraded or recycled rather than discarded. This is the foundation of a circular economy.

### 35.1 Cost Build-Up and the Price Ladder

Price is NOT just a function of cost — it is often a function of **managerial leadership judgment**.

**Price Ladder:**
```
Material Cost
    + Inbound Logistics Cost
    = Factory Input Cost
    + Factory Conversion Cost
    + Overheads (allocated)
    = Fully Accounted Factory Cost
    + Outbound Logistics Cost
    + Company Margin
    = Price to Distributor (PTD)
    + Distributor Margin (+ Wholesaler Margin if applicable)
    = Price to Retailer (PTR)
    + Retailer Margin
    = Maximum Retail Price (MRP) / Price to Consumer
```

> **Key insight:** In India, the actual factory cost + margin can be ₹100, but the MRP can be ₹300. **Two-thirds of the consumer price can go to distribution and retail margins.** This makes D2C models highly attractive.

**Competitiveness of customer pricing depends on:**
1. **Delayering** the value chain (reducing intermediary steps).
2. **Cost compression** at each layer.

---

### 35.2 Direct-to-Consumer (D2C) Strategy

**D2C removes intermediaries**, enabling:

| Benefit | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Removes distributor and retail margins | Cost advantage of 30–50% of retail price |
| Brings company closer to consumers | Direct relationship and feedback loop |
| Strengthens relationships | Converts transactions into experiences |
| Enables customisation | Tailored products and personalised offers |

**Indian D2C Market:**
- Projected to reach **$100 billion by 2025**.
- As of 2021, India had **800+ D2C startups**.
- FMCG leaders — HUL, ITC, Emami, Nestlé India — have launched D2C portals.

**Case Study — Dabur D2C:**
- Positioned as one-stop shop for entire Dabur product range.
- Leveraging portal to scale digital-first brands (baby care, apple cider vinegar, ghee, peanut butter, honey, cold-pressed mustard oil).
- Target: ₹100 crore from digital-first brands portfolio by end-2022.

**Case Study — Marico D2C:**
- Key acquisitions: Beardo (men's grooming, ₹100 Cr+ at FY22 exit), Just Herbs (Ayurvedic), True Elements (healthy snacks).
- Target: ₹500 Cr from D2C/digital-first brands by FY24.
- Online share of revenue: moving from 9% to 15%.
- Homegrown D2C brands: CocoSoul, PureSense, Fittify.

> **Trend:** Seven FMCG companies (including Emami, ITC, Tata Consumer) have invested in D2C startups. D2C bets are high across the sector.

---

### 35.3 Steps in Setting a Pricing Policy

1. **Select the pricing objective** — Survival, maximum current profit, maximum market share, maximum market skimming, product-quality leadership.
2. **Determine demand** — Price sensitivity, demand curves, elasticity.
3. **Estimate costs** — Variable costs, fixed costs, total cost, learning curve effects.
4. **Analyse competitors' costs, prices, and offers.**
5. **Select a pricing method** — Cost-plus, target-return, perceived-value, going-rate, auction-type, value pricing.
6. **Select the final price** — Psychological, promotional, risk-sharing, international pricing considerations.

---

### 35.4 Entry Deterrent Pricing

> **Entry Deterrent Price (Prof. Michael Porter):** Set the price at a level that makes entry unattractive for potential competitors — low enough that the economics don't support new entrants, but high enough to be profitable for the incumbent.

This requires:
- Knowledge of competitors' cost structures.
- Understanding of the minimum viable scale for a new entrant.
- Willingness to sacrifice short-term margin for long-term market protection.

---

### 35.5 Pricing Strategies — Macro and Micro

**Macroeconomic Pricing Strategies:**
- Market penetration pricing (low price for rapid adoption)
- Market skimming pricing (high price for early adopters)
- Competitive pricing (matching the market rate)

**Microeconomic Pricing Strategies:**
- Premium pricing (for differentiated products)
- Economy pricing (cost leadership)
- Bundle pricing
- Psychological pricing (₹99 instead of ₹100)
- Geographic pricing

---

### 35.6 Factors That Reduce Price Sensitivity

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Unique value effect** | Product has no direct substitute |
| **Substitute awareness effect** | Consumers unaware of alternatives |
| **Difficult comparison effect** | Hard to compare with competitors |
| **Total expenditure effect** | Product is a small share of total spending |
| **End-benefit effect** | Product is critical input to a larger solution |
| **Sunk cost effect** | Customer has already invested in the ecosystem |
| **Price-quality effect** | Higher price signals better quality |
| **Inventory effect** | Customer cannot stockpile the product |

---

### 35.7 Cost Analysis for Pricing

**Key cost concepts:**

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Fixed Costs** | Do not vary with volume (rent, salaries, depreciation) |
| **Variable Costs** | Vary directly with volume (materials, packaging) |
| **Total Cost** | Fixed + Variable |
| **Average Cost** | Total Cost / Volume |
| **Learning / Experience Curve** | Accumulated production reduces unit cost; costs fall by a fixed % for every doubling of cumulative production |

**Break-Even Analysis:**
```
Break-Even Volume = Fixed Costs / (Selling Price − Variable Cost per Unit)
```

> **COVID-19 lesson:** When revenues collapse suddenly (as in 2020), companies that had not done break-even analysis properly found themselves unable to survive. Understanding the break-even point is a survival tool, not just a planning tool.

---

### 35.8 Fair Price-Setting Model

A fair pricing model balances:
1. **Cost-based floor** (must cover all costs + minimum margin).
2. **Value-based ceiling** (maximum the customer will pay based on perceived value).
3. **Competition-based anchor** (what the market will bear given competitor pricing).
4. **Strategic intent** (skimming, penetration, or neutral).

---

### 35.9 Price Discounts, Allowances, and Promotions

**Common types:**
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Cash discounts | Early payment incentives |
| Quantity discounts | Volume-based rebates |
| Trade discounts | Functional discounts to channel partners |
| Promotional allowances | Support for dealer advertising |

> **Warning:** Excessive discounting erodes brand equity and establishes price expectations. *"Just because we meddle with the prices, there will be higher demand"* — this is often a short-term fallacy; structural discounting trains customers to wait for promotions.

### 35.10 Four Market Types and Pricing Dynamics

| Market Type | Characteristics | Pricing Implication |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Secured Market** | Loyal, locked-in customers | Price premium sustainable |
| **Contested Market** | Active competition for customers | Price is a tactical weapon |
| **Commodity Market** | Undifferentiated products | Price is the primary differentiator |
| **Emerging Market** | New segment being created | Penetration pricing to build share |

### 35.11 Customer Value Management

Customer value management is essential because pricing must reflect **perceived value**, not just cost:

1. Understand customer value perceptions.
2. Identify what drives value for different segments.
3. Create differentiated value propositions.
4. Price to the value delivered, not to the cost incurred.

**Dimensions of Product Quality (relevant to pricing):**
- Performance, Features, Reliability, Conformance, Durability, Serviceability, Aesthetics, Perceived Quality.

**Service Quality Dimensions (SERVQUAL):**
| Dimension | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Reliability** | Ability to perform the promised service dependably |
| **Responsiveness** | Willingness to help and provide prompt service |
| **Assurance** | Knowledge and courtesy; inspires trust |
| **Empathy** | Personalised care and attention |
| **Tangibles** | Physical facilities, equipment, appearance |

> Reliability is ranked **most important** across industries (airlines, banking, hospitals, telecom).

**The Perception-Value Chain Gaps:**
- Gap 1: Customer expectation vs. management perception.
- Gap 2: Management perception vs. service quality specification.
- Gap 3: Specification vs. actual service delivery.
- Gap 4: Service delivery vs. external communication.
- Gap 5: Expected service vs. perceived service.

---

### 35.12 Costing Systems

> **Principle:** "Stringent costing is important, but flexible pricing is also required."

- **Stringent costing** = discipline in tracking and controlling all cost elements.
- **Flexible pricing** = adapting price to market conditions, segment value, and competitive dynamics.

A robust costing system must:
- Capture all costs accurately.
- Be contextually relevant to the product's life cycle stage.
- Enable cost analysis and capture across the full product life cycle.

### 35.13 Case Study — Apple iPhone 13 Pricing

**Apple's Strategic Pricing Approach:**

| Model | List Price (₹) | Discount | Cashback | Exchange Value | Exchange Bonus | Net Effective Price | Reduction |

| iPhone 13 Mini | 69,990 | 3,000 | 5,000 | 3,000 | — | 58,990 | 16% |

### 35.14 Case Study — BS IV to BS VI Truck Pricing (Regulatory-Driven Pricing)

|-----------|------------|------------|
| GVW | 37 MT | 32 MT |

### 35.15 Cost Leadership vs. Price Leadership

| | Cost Leadership | Price Leadership |
|-|----------------|-----------------|
| **Definition** | Achieving the lowest cost of production in the industry | Ability to secure the highest possible price without losing volumes |
| **Who is responsible** | Company-wide — every function | Primarily the CMO / Marketing team |
| **How achieved** | Operational excellence, scale, process innovation | Brand equity, differentiation, customer advocacy, micro-segmentation |
| **Risk** | Benefits frittered away by low pricing | Lost if brand loses differentiation |

> **The ideal position for any firm** is the synergy of:
> - **High Market Share** (scale and volume)
> - **Low Cost** (cost leadership)
> - **High Price** (price leadership)
>
> Achieving all three simultaneously delivers **long-term sustainable value**.
