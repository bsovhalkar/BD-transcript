## 🧠 Main Points

- Customers use **multiple sequential filters** before arriving at a final purchase decision — from need awareness to post-purchase behaviour
- Forming strong customer bonds is a **cross-functional, organisation-wide responsibility**, not just a marketing function
- Companies must invest in **personalised loyalty and club marketing programs** (Lufthansa, Taj, HDFC, Amazon Prime, etc.) for emotional and financial bonding
- Value creation in customer engagement follows four pillars: **Socialising, Engaging, Recognising, Integrating**
- **Customer Performance Scorecards** go far beyond gross sales — they measure satisfaction, repurchase intent, brand recall, positioning accuracy, and relative quality perception
- Customer engagement must yield results across **four metric verticals**: Customer Metrics, Unit Metrics, Cash-Flow Metrics, and Brand Metrics
- **Consumer behaviour** is shaped by both company-controlled marketing inputs and external macro-environmental factors
- **Consumer Search Dynamics** reduce the total product set through awareness → consideration → choice → decision funnel
- The **Expectancy Model** allows companies to score competing products on multiple attributes to predict consumer preference
- **Unanticipated situational factors and attitudes of self** can derail a purchase decision even after alternatives are evaluated
- The **Use-and-Reuse mindset** is central to the circular economy — responsible consumption, trade-in, and reuse are marketing responsibilities
- The **Mutual Awareness Framework** (2×2 grid) reveals four product-market play zones; start-ups and innovators thrive by exploring "open spaces"
- **Neuromarketing** uses brain scanning and physiological tracking to decode consumer motivations, but raises privacy and ethical concerns
- **Individual customer bonds** are budget/lifestyle-driven and transactional-emotional; **Business customer bonds** are strategy-driven and techno-commercial
- **Air India** post-privatisation exemplifies that rebuilding bonds is harder — but more rewarding — than building from scratch
- **Emotional connectivity** is twice as valuable as mere customer satisfaction (HBR research)
- The **Customer Loyalty Ladder** captures progressive loyalty behaviour: endorsing → feedback → resisting switch → paying premiums → co-creating
- **Business buyers** can be classified into four types based on firm investment vs. customer loyalty: Commodity Buyers, Underperformers, Valued Customers, Valued Partners

## ⭐ Important Points

1. **Gross sales figures alone are insufficient** to measure customer performance — companies must track a full scorecard including brand recall, repurchase intent, positioning accuracy, and relative quality perception.
2. The **customer is never to be taken for granted** — even loyal, repeat customers can switch at any point in the purchase-decision funnel.
3. **HBR research finding**: Emotionally connected customers are **more than twice as valuable** as merely highly satisfied customers.
4. The **Mutual Awareness Framework** explains why established companies falter (blinkered play or blind alley) while innovative start-ups shine (open space / Apple way).
5. **Expectancy Model**: Consumers evaluate and score competing brands on multiple weighted attributes; companies should model this and project the attributes most favourable to their products.
6. **Business customer bonds** are defined by techno-commercial competitiveness and organisational synergy — once validated, they remain as preferred partner status for multiple iterations.
7. Customer loyalty is the **biggest defence in recession** and the **biggest driver in growth** — it must be treated as a strategic vertical.
8. The **Value Creation Framework** (Socialise → Engage → Recognise → Integrate) is a systematic programme, not a one-off event.
9. **Neuromarketing** is scientifically feasible but **dystopian** in the context of consumer privacy — limited deployment for hypothesis generation is the recommended path.
10. The **Use-and-Reuse mindset** in the circular economy is an emerging consumer characteristic that marketers must incorporate into product life-cycle and disposal strategies.
11. In the **Business Buyers matrix**, achieving "Valued Partnership" (high firm investment + high customer loyalty) represents the pinnacle of B2B relationship building.
12. **Britannia's Homepreneur Programme** is a best-practice example of using CSR-oriented customer bonding — it integrates social responsibility with product packaging to deepen customer connect.
13. **C.K. Prahalad's Bottom of the Pyramid (BoP)** concept — 5 billion unserved/underserved people represent both a social obligation and a market innovation opportunity.
14. Rebuilding customer bonds (e.g., **Air India**) requires not only correcting past negatives but constructing entirely new brand magic — even harder than building bonds originally.
15. Every **touchpoint** (in-store, media, digital, sales/service) is an opportunity for emotional connection — well-trained salesforce and empathetic corporate culture are the key drivers.

## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. Customers' Multiple Filters — The Purchase Decision Funnel

Before arriving at a final purchase decision, customers pass through a **sequential set of filters**. Companies must understand and support each stage.

| Stage | Filter | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Target Product Search** | Awareness of need + awareness of the target product category |
| 2 | **Awareness of Products and Brands** | Knowledge of alternatives; experience-based or store/digital visit |
| 3 | **Open to Trying Out** | Physical feel, reviews, exploration interest (e.g., test-driving a car) |
| 4 | **Switching Cost Analysis** | Benefits must outweigh the cost of switching from a current brand |
| 5 | **Use Experience** | Post-purchase satisfaction/dissatisfaction governs future patronage |

> **Key Insight:** At any of these stages, a customer can switch brands. Companies must actively support migration and retention throughout this funnel.

### 2. Forming Strong Customer Bonds

Bond formation is a **complex, cross-departmental process** — not just a marketing function. Six foundational practices:

1. **Create superior products, services, and experiences** — Packaging itself signals quality; poor labelling or leakage damages bonds even for great products.
2. **Cross-departmental participation** in customer satisfaction and retention — from R&D to labelling to logistics.
3. **Voice of the Customer (VoC)** — Capture both stated and unstated needs; communicate them to top management, not just internal metrics.
4. **Customer database** — Maintain accessible records of individual needs, preferences, purchase frequency, preferred retailers, and interactions.
5. **Customer-company interaction forums** — Enabling customers to express needs, perceptions, and complaints builds mutual understanding.
6. **Loyalty and club marketing programmes** — Reward long-standing customers, outstanding employees, distributors, and retailers.

> "Nobody takes that product loyalty, once established, will remain on its own forever — everybody strives to keep this loyalty intact."

### 4. Value Creation Practices — The SERI Framework

Customer engagement must enhance value for both customers and company stakeholders. Four pillars:

#### 4.1 SOCIALISING
- **Welcoming** — Make new customers feel like long-valued customers from day one
- **Empathizing** — Understand the customer's experience with the product (design thinking in continuous practice)
- **Governing** — Build a community feeling among customers

#### 4.2 ENGAGING
- **Communicating** — Share product/service features completely, consistently, and with relevance
- **Supporting** — Provide samples, trial products, AMCs, lifetime support, trade-in programmes, and warranties

#### 4.3 RECOGNISING
- **Tiering** — Acknowledge both homogeneity and heterogeneity through meaningful loyalty tiers (e.g., Blue/Silver/Gold in Taj InnerCircle)
- **Celebrating** — Mark personal milestones (birthdays, anniversaries, career achievements) — social profile tracking may enable this in future
- **Badging** — Convert milestones into symbols and mementos (e.g., a New Year card or a feel-good certificate)
- **Documenting** — Record customer journeys and experiences for role-modelling and co-sharing

#### 4.4 INTEGRATING
- **Guiding** — Provide complete product documentation and guidance (both minimalist quick-guides and comprehensive manuals)
- **Customising** — Develop product variants that meet specific customer needs
- **Diffusing** — Use brand power to spread inclusivity and positivity, including via socially responsible activities

> **Corporate and product brands are important tools for creating a social community of customers and stakeholders.**

### 5. Britannia Homepreneur Programme — CSR-Linked Customer Bonding

A **best-practice case study** in integrating corporate social responsibility with customer bonding:

- Britannia recognised the entrepreneurship potential of **women from Tamil Nadu towns and villages**
- Launched a **yearly startup contest**: women submit proposals, independent panels select winners
- **₹10 lakh** funding per selected start-up
- **5 winners featured on Marigold biscuit packs** — consumer can scan the QR code to read their story and **fund their ventures directly**
- 30 women supported so far through this initiative

**Why this works:**
- Deepens emotional connection between consumer and brand
- Turns a product pack into a **social impact touchpoint**
- Creates community, purpose, and pride for the customer — not just a loyalty point

### 6. Finding Gold at the Bottom of the Pyramid (C.K. Prahalad)

- **C.K. Prahalad** (Wharton/Harvard theorist) estimated **5 billion unserved and underserved people** make up the "Bottom of the Pyramid" (BoP)
- Believed much innovation can emerge from **emerging markets** like India and China
- Companies such as **Aravind Eye Care, Narayana Health, Nestlé, and Harman** have created models serving BoP customers
- **Reverse innovation** — solutions developed for BoP markets often travel back to developed countries (Vijay Govindarajan & Chris Trimble concept)
- Customer bonding with BoP consumers requires **affordability, accessibility, and adaptability**

### 7. Customer Performance Scorecard

Most companies confine themselves to gross sales figures — this is **inadequate**. A complete Customer Performance Scorecard includes:

| Metric | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| % new customers / total customers | Customer acquisition rate |
| % lost customers / total customers | Attrition / defection rate |
| % repeat customers / total customers | Loyalty / retention rate |
| Customer satisfaction levels | Likert/scale-based satisfaction index |
| % who would repurchase | Repurchase intent |
| % who would recommend | Net Promoter Score proxy |
| % with brand awareness / recall | Market-level brand health |
| % who rank product as most preferred | Category dominance |
| % who correctly identify brand positioning | Positioning accuracy |
| Average product quality perception vs. competitor | Relative quality index |
| Average service quality perception vs. competitor | Relative service index |

> **Warning:** High gross sales do not always mean strong customer bonds — attrition, poor positioning, or credit-driven sales can mask structural weakness.

### 8. The Economic Aspects of Customer Engagement — Four Metrics Verticals

Customer engagement must deliver **both emotional and economic fulfilment**. Metrics must be analysed on a standalone and integrated basis:

| Vertical | Key Metrics |
|---|---|
| **Customer Metrics** | Repeat purchases, new product acceptance, satisfaction levels |
| **Unit Metrics** | Product/category sales, marketing cost per unit, per capita sales productivity |
| **Cash-Flow Metrics** | Product profitability, credit period in distribution, Return on Marketing Investment (ROMI) |
| **Brand Metrics** | Brand awareness, attribute awareness, loyalty level |

> **Integration matters:** High per-capita sales productivity with low product profitability sends conflicting signals. High sales with long credit periods indicate brand is not selling on its own merit. All four must be analysed together.

### 9. Model of Consumer Behaviour

Consumer behaviour is shaped by multiple inputs:

```
Marketing Inputs          Macro Factors
(Product, Price,    +     (Economic, Tech,     →  Consumer Psychology  →  Purchase Process  →  Decision
 Distribution,             Political, Cultural,    (Motivation,              (Need → Search →     (Product, Brand,
 Communication)            Social)                  Perception,              Evaluation →          Channel, Amount,
                                                     Learning, Memory)        Decision →            Timing, Payment)
                                                                               Post-purchase)
```

- **Marketing inputs** and **macro factors** both influence consumer psychology
- **Consumer characteristics** (cultural, social, demographic, personal) shape individual variation
- Companies must model the entire consumer behaviour system to understand sensitivity to different stimuli
- Example: Mutual fund companies adapt messaging to economic cycles (bull market → hybrid funds; bear market → SIPs; infrastructure cycle → thematic funds)

### 10. Consumer Search Dynamics — The Funnel Filtration

When buying a laptop, a consumer narrows down from:

```
Total Set               Awareness Set          Consideration Set      Choice Set         Decision
─────────────────────   ─────────────────────  ─────────────────────  ─────────────────  ──────────
Apple, Dell, HP,        Apple, Dell, HP,       Apple, Dell, HP,       Apple, HP,         ?
Toshiba, Lenovo,        Lenovo, Acer,          Microsoft              Microsoft
Asus, Acer, NEC,        Microsoft
Samsung, Microsoft
```

- **Total Set**: All brands that exist in the market
- **Awareness Set**: Brands the consumer has heard of / seen (well-advertised, present in working environment)
- **Consideration Set**: Brands shortlisted based on features, image, and price
- **Choice Set**: Final shortlist after deeper evaluation
- **Decision**: Triggered by store visit, digital review, or peer recommendation

**Filtration levels:**
1. **Corporate level** — "Apple is a great company"
2. **Product brand level** — "MacBook is well known; HP Envy has a strong reputation"
3. **Product level** — RAM, display quality, weight, battery life

> The transparent company that provides complete standalone and comparative information gains in the long term.

### 11. Consumer Brand Beliefs — The Expectancy Model

Consumers mentally score brands on multiple attributes. Companies can model this:

**Sample: Brand Beliefs about Laptops (0–10 scale; Price reverse-indexed)**

| Attribute | Laptop A | Laptop B | Laptop C | Laptop D |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Memory Capacity | 8 | 7 | 10 | 5 |
| Graphics Capacity | 9 | 7 | 4 | 3 |
| Size and Weight | 6 | 7 | 3 | 8 |
| Price | 9 | 7 | 2 | 5 |
| Processor Capacity | 5 | 2 | 7 | 3 |
| Camera and AV | 5 | 6 | 3 | 8 |
| Battery Life | 7 | 5 | 6 | 4 |
| Display Quality | 3 | 5 | 9 | 7 |
| Elegance | 7 | 8 | 5 | 6 |
| **Total Rating** | **59** | **54** | **49** | **49** |

- **Laptop A wins** with a total score of 59
- Companies should identify the attributes where their product scores highest and **project those to the target segment**
- **Test marketing** of own + competitor products on key attributes → build expectancy models → choose right marketing strategy

### 12. Steps Between Evaluation of Alternatives and Purchase Decision

Even after evaluating alternatives, the decision is not final:

```
Evaluation of Alternatives
         ↓
   Purchase Intention
         ↓
   Two intervening factors:
   ① Attitudes of Self (how greeted in store, product display, availability)
   ② Ecosystem Factors (purchasing power that day, competitive promotions, urgency)
         ↓
   Final Purchase Decision
```

- **Attitudes of Self**: Salesperson behaviour, product display, availability
- **Ecosystem Factors**: Purchasing power, competitive promotions, urgency of need, psychographic state on that day
- Understanding consumer **rigidities and sensitivities** helps companies predict purchase conversion rates

### 13. Use-and-Reuse Consumer Mindset (Circular Economy)

The rapid pace of technological change creates **large inventories of old products**. The circular economy requires marketers to adopt a new mindset:

**Options when retaining a product:**
- Use it for original purpose (old smartphone for basic calling)
- Convert to new purpose (add external HDD to old laptop)
- Store it idly (suboptimal but common)

**Options when disposing of a product:**
- Monetise the ownership (rent out)
- Lease or lend
- Trade-in for a new product
- Sell (direct-to-consumer via OLX/eBay, through middleman, or to middleman)
- Give away / donate
- Throw away (least preferred)

> **Critical Shift:** Earlier disparaging views about second-hand markets must be replaced by responsible consumption and trade-in frameworks. **Producer responsibility** (extended producer responsibility/EPR) includes taking back products, reclaiming packages, and recycling.

### 14. The Mutual Awareness Framework (2×2 Grid)

A powerful framework for understanding where companies and consumers stand in terms of awareness:

|  | **Customer Aware** | **Customer Unaware** |
|---|---|---|
| **Company Aware** | **Traditional Product-Market Play** (Specialised Way) — Comfort zone; both know the need | **Developmental Product-Market Play** (Diversity Way) — Company knows the need, customer doesn't; company must educate |
| **Company Unaware** | **Blinkered Product-Market Play** (Blind Alley) — Customer knows the need, company misses it; leads to disruption | **Open Space for Start-up Play** (Apple Way) — Neither knows; driven by intuition, innovation, creativity |

- **The Blind Alley**: Company overconfident in current products; eventual disruption
- **The Open Space**: Where truly innovative start-ups shine — requires intuition + design thinking
- **Apple Way**: Rarer — where the company discovers needs that neither the company nor the consumer was explicitly aware of
- Companies should move from traditional play → developmental play → open space play

### 15. Consumer Neuroscience and Neuromarketing

| Dimension | Details |
|---|---|
| **Definition** | Measurement of physiological and neural signals to gain insight into consumer motivations, preferences, and decisions |
| **Common Methods** | Brain scanning (neural activity); physiological tracking (eye movement, face tracking, proxy sensors); AI-based analysis |
| **Insights Possible** | Consumer motivations, preferences, decisions, engagement levels, openness to change, emotional responses, brand recall |
| **Potential Uses** | Price setting, brand improvement, ad campaign design, hypothesis generation |
| **Pros** | Scientifically links behavioural responses to internal information processing; systematises marketing mix to predictable influences |
| **Cons** | Expensive equipment; controlled conditions needed (not scalable to Kirana stores); **intrudes on consumer privacy**; risks thought and behaviour manipulation |
| **Recommended Path** | Limited use of scanning for hypothesis development on target groups; extensive use of sensing/tracking/AI to improve consumer experience, not just sales |

> "Consumer neuroscience and neuromarketing are scientifically feasible but **dystopian** in the face of consumer privacy."

### 18. Building Customer Bonds — Air India Case Study

**Context:** Tata Group took over Air India under the Government of India's privatisation drive.

**Initiatives to rebuild bonds:**

| Area | Actions |
|---|---|
| **Scheduling Systems** | Alerts and proactive schedule corrections |
| **Flight Service** | Better menu, better in-flight service, grooming guidelines |
| **Passenger Comfort** | Refurbished interiors, enhanced on-board experience |
| **Check-in & Baggage** | Improved ground handling processes |
| **Strategic (Long-term)** | Direct flights to preferred US/Europe destinations; wide-body aircraft fleet; consolidation of all Tata airlines under one entity |

**Key Lesson:**
- Rebuilding bonds is **more challenging** than building from scratch — past negative experiences must be actively corrected
- Rebuilding requires constructing **new brand magic** while leveraging the residual heritage brand value (Tata brand + national airline legacy)
- Air India demonstrates that brand revival is **feasible and viable** with committed strategic investment

### 19. Customer Bonds through Emotional Connect

**HBR Research finding:** Emotionally connected customers are **more than twice as valuable** as highly satisfied customers.

**Why emotional connectivity matters:**
1. Emotional connection creates **active participants** who co-create fulfilling experiences with the firm
2. Investment in customer experiences leads to **better emotional connectivity**
3. Every touchpoint is an opportunity — not just the sale moment

**Four touchpoint categories:**

| Touchpoint | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Personal in-store** | Salesperson interaction, product display, ambience |
| **Media touchpoints** | Advertisements, brand stories, press coverage |
| **Digital store touchpoints** | App experience, website, e-commerce UX |
| **Sales and service touchpoints** | Post-sale service, warranty support, helpline |

> **Key Enablers:** Well-trained salesforce + empathetic corporate culture

### 20. The Customer Loyalty Ladder

A loyal customer progressively demonstrates these behaviours (bottom to top):

```
Level 5: COLLABORATES — co-creates new products and experiences with the firm
Level 4: PREMIUM PAYER — willing to wait or pay premiums for loyal brands
Level 3: RESISTANT — resists competitors' inducements to switch
Level 2: FEEDBACK PROVIDER — wants to grow the relationship through feedback
Level 1: ENDORSER — remembers the brand and endorses products to others
```

> **Example:** Apple customers wait months for Apple's annual launch cycle even when Samsung releases phones in February — this is loyalty embedded in an ecosystem.

> Customer loyalty is the **biggest defence in recession** and the **biggest driver in fast-growth markets**.

---

### 21. Business Buyers Matrix — 2×2 Classification

**Axes:** Firm's investment in customers (Low/High) × Customer's position on loyalty ladder (Low/High)

| | **Low Customer Loyalty** | **High Customer Loyalty** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Firm Investment** | **Underperformers** — Over-invested but cannot move customers to high loyalty | **Valued Partners** — Highest correlation; pinnacle of B2B relationship |
| **Low Firm Investment** | **Commodity Buyers** — Pure price-based purchases; no differentiation (e.g., sand, clinker, pigments) | **Valued Customers** — Customer remains loyal despite low firm investment; positive brand perception |

**Progressive Journey:** Commodity Buyers → Valued Customers → Valued Partners

> The correlation between firm's investments and customer's loyalty is **most profound in Valued Partnership**.

## 📊 Summary

Lecture 24 opens by establishing that customers navigate a multi-stage filter process — from need awareness through trial to post-purchase behaviour — and companies must actively support every stage to prevent brand switching. Forming strong customer bonds is a cross-departmental responsibility that requires building superior products, integrating the Voice of the Customer, maintaining customer databases, and running loyalty and club programmes. The SERI framework (Socialising, Engaging, Recognising, Integrating) offers a comprehensive model for systematically deepening customer engagement. Real-world loyalty programmes — from Lufthansa's Miles and More to Britannia's Homepreneur initiative — demonstrate the spectrum of financial and emotional bonding tools available to companies of all sizes. Customer performance must be measured through a full scorecard (not just gross sales) and tracked across four economic verticals: Customer Metrics, Unit Metrics, Cash-Flow Metrics, and Brand Metrics. The Consumer Behaviour Model contextualises purchasing within both company-controlled inputs and macro-environmental factors, while the Expectancy Model and Consumer Search Dynamics funnel reveal how consumers cognitively evaluate alternatives. The Mutual Awareness Framework provides a powerful 2×2 lens for strategic positioning — companies that play in the "Open Space" (Apple Way) or "Developmental" zones are best positioned for long-term growth. Individual bonds are transactional and emotional; business bonds are strategic and techno-commercial — each requiring tailored investment. The Air India case shows that rebuilding damaged bonds is even harder than building them, but yields high returns. Emotional connectivity — backed by HBR research showing it delivers more than twice the value of mere satisfaction — and the Customer Loyalty Ladder together make the case that loyalty is not just an outcome but a strategic vertical that companies must invest in patiently and continuously.

## 🔍 Final Short Summary

Lecture 24 establishes that customer bonding is a **strategic imperative** requiring cross-functional commitment, not a standalone marketing activity. Customers navigate multiple decision filters, and companies must actively support every stage while measuring performance through a comprehensive scorecard — not just gross sales. The SERI framework (Socialise, Engage, Recognise, Integrate) provides a structured approach to building emotional and economic connections. The Mutual Awareness Framework reveals why innovative companies (especially start-ups) outperform incumbents by exploring unknown customer-company space. Ultimately, emotionally connected customers — who endorse, resist switching, pay premiums, and co-create — are a company's most powerful competitive asset and must be cultivated through patient, purposeful investment.
