## 🧠 Main Points

- **Customers vs. Consumers**: Customers purchase goods/services; consumers use them for personal needs. Dealers are customers; end-users are consumers.
- **Individual vs. Business Markets**: Consumer markets are large, dynamic, and elastic; business markets are fewer, structured, and inelastic — each requiring distinct strategies.
- **Customer-Oriented Organisation**: Traditional pyramid keeps customer connect at the frontline only; a circular/contemporary model connects every function with customers.
- **CEO-Level Customer Connect**: Exceptional leaders like Azim Premji (Wipro) and Mike Ball (Hospira) set benchmarks for top-level customer engagement.
- **Types of Customer Needs**: Functional, esteem, augmentation, replacement, seasonal, individual, family, social, and business needs.
- **Maslow's Need Hierarchy**: Five levels — physiological, safety, social, esteem, self-actualization — applicable to both individuals and institutional markets.
- **Limitations of Maslow in Marketing**: Income-group segmentation produces parallel need hierarchies, not strictly sequential ones.
- **Six Key Customer Influencers**: Demographic, socio-cultural, economic, lifestyle, technological, and natural factors — often overlapping.
- **Vertical vs. Horizontal Customer Engagement**: Horizontal (product specialisation + scale) should precede vertical (full range for one customer cluster) to avoid strategic traps.
- **Customer Value Perception**: Total perceived value = total benefits (usage, ownership, image, resale) minus total costs (purchase, operating, service, image/esteem loss).
- **Traditional Reactive Customer Model**: Firm is the provider; customer is a passive receiver responding to the firm's value proposition.
- **Digital Economy & Customer Experience**: Internet economy enables instant search, multi-channel purchase, mutual experience sharing, and online product optimisation.
- **DAWN Framework (Original Framework)**: Customers are driven by **D**esires, **A**spirations, **W**ants, and **N**eeds — a more practical extension of Maslow.
- **DAWN Hierarchy**: Needs are prime movers; wants motivate diversification; desires drive higher-end activity; aspirations are the hallmark of a developed economy.
- **Multiple Customer Personalities**: Customers act as individuals, family members, friends, and organisational members — each layer shaping DAWNs differently.
- **Customer Ecosystem (Individual)**: Asian Paints example — from core painting → interior design → fitments → home furnishings → lifestyle architecture.
- **Business Customer Ecosystem**: Hierarchical from basic operations → viable → competitive → sustainable → innovative operations; BD must align to the customer's growth stage.
- **Variety as Strategy**: Restaurant typology illustrates how BD must address diverse segments with tailored propositions.
- **BD Creating New Models**: Foodplex concept — collaborative, network-based fulfilment transcending the one-restaurant-one-cuisine limitation.

## ⭐ Important Points

1. **The Interchangeability Caveat**: Though "customer" and "consumer" are used interchangeably in practice, the strategic distinction matters — the *consumer* drives demand, the *customer* executes the purchase.
2. **Consumer vs. Business Markets — 10-Factor Difference Table**: A foundational framework covering buyers, products, access, branding, packaging, promotion, demand elasticity, buying process, decision-making, code of conduct, and contract extent. Frequently tested.
3. **Circular Organisation Concept**: The contemporary customer-centric model places **all functions** (R&D, production, finance, IT, marketing) in equal contact with customers — not just the sales/frontline team.
4. **Listening Tour (Hospira)**: Mike Ball's first act as CEO was a "listening tour" — understanding how the company serves its customers before making any strategic moves. A classic case of customer-first leadership.
5. **Maslow Applied to Vehicles**: Scooter (low income) → i20 (lower middle) → Maruti Ciaz (upper middle) → Audi Q7 (high income) → Lamborghini (super high income). Multiple products in the same category serve **different Maslow levels**.
6. **Maslow's Limitation in Practice**: A higher-income individual does *not* abandon lower-level products. Need hierarchies coexist; they are not strictly sequential in consumer markets.
7. **Scope before Scale = Strategic Trap**: Attempting vertical customer engagement (meeting all needs of all income groups) before achieving horizontal scale is a strategic error.
8. **Customer Perceived Value Formula**: Value = (Usage benefit + Ownership benefit + Image benefit + Resale value) − (Purchase cost + Operating cost + Service & parts cost + Image/esteem loss)
9. **Image & Esteem Loss**: A product that once conveyed status can become a liability over time (e.g., carrying an outdated smartphone in a tech-forward workplace). Firms must manage this perception.
10. **Software as Optimisation**: Post-sale OS updates on smartphones are a live example of ongoing product optimisation — a new dimension of customer value delivery.
11. **DAWN is an Original Framework**: Prof. Rao's DAWN framework (Desires, Aspirations, Wants, Needs) is introduced as an **original course framework** — expect direct exam questions on it.
12. **DAWNs and Economic Development**: As an economy grows, aspirations of the past become today's needs; new aspirations emerge. Firms that track this trajectory gain first-mover advantage.
13. **Asian Paints as Customer Ecosystem Case**: Evolved from surface protection → surface attraction → interior design → total home lifestyle — a textbook example of expanding a customer ecosystem.
14. **Business Need Hierarchy**: Basic → growing → viable → competitive → sustainable → innovative business operations. BD strategy must be calibrated to the customer's current phase.
15. **Foodplex Concept**: A BD innovation where collaborative restaurant networks allow customers to choose any food from any restaurant at any outlet — eliminates the pre-decide constraint.

## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. Customers and Consumers — The Key Distinction

In business parlance, *customer* and *consumer* are used interchangeably, but a strategic distinction exists:

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Customer** | Individual or entity that **purchases** a product/service | Administrative manager buying office supplies |
| **Consumer** | Individual or entity that **uses** a product/service for personal need | Employees using those supplies |

> "Trying to segregate customers and consumers in a vast market is like searching for needles in a haystack."

**Why it matters strategically**: The *consumer* (end-user) shapes demand. A dealer is the customer of an automobile firm, but it is the ultimate consumer's preferences that determine what models sell. Firms must understand the consumer to decode the true demand signal.

---

### 2. Individual Consumer Markets vs. Business Markets

Strategic marketing begins with choosing whether to operate in consumer markets, business markets, or both.

| Factor | **Consumer Markets** | **Business Markets** |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Buyers** | Huge in numbers (lakhs to millions) | Relatively fewer |
| **Products/Services** | Common, standardised | Common and specific (e.g., adhesives for different joints) |
| **Access** | Relatively easy (kirana to supermarkets) | Hard; needs appointments, negotiations |
| **Branding** | Strong influence on perception | Low impact; buyers test independently |
| **Packaging** | Designed to attract and display | Primarily for product protection |
| **Promotion** | Influences purchase decisions | May be prohibited (ethical restrictions) |
| **Demand** | Relatively **elastic** (price-sensitive) | Relatively **inelastic** (capability-driven) |
| **Buying Process** | Direct — in-store or online | Procedure-bound; may use intermediaries |
| **Decision-Making** | Individual; sometimes influenced by family | **Multi-tier** with levels of authority |
| **Code of Conduct** | General buyer-seller principles | Formal ethical code; consequences for breach |
| **Extent of Contract** | Limited to individual purchase; manufacturer warranty | Project-specific, long-term; strong warranty + AMC |

**Example**: Edible oil companies sell consumer packs (pouches, pet bottles) AND tins for the hotel-restaurant-café (HoReCa) business market. The same product but different packaging, pricing, and relationship models.

### 3. Customer-Oriented Organisation

#### 3.1 The Traditional Pyramid Model

```
         ┌──────────────┐
         │  Top Mgmt    │   ← Least customer contact
         ├──────────────┤
         │ Middle Mgmt  │   ← Moderate customer contact
         ├──────────────┤
         │  Frontline   │   ← Primary customer contact
         └──────────────┘
```

The weakness: Customer connect *decreases* as seniority increases. The real pulse of the market is siloed at the bottom.

#### 3.2 The Contemporary Circular Model

The Prof. proposes a **circular (ring) model** where customers encircle the organisation, with every function — R&D, Production, Marketing, Finance, IT, Others — in continuous, equal contact with customers.

> The IT leader, for example, should not merely build architecture; they should visit distributors, retailers, and consumers to understand how real-time data can optimise the supply chain for customer fulfilment.

**This is the true definition of a customer-centric organisation.**

#### 3.3 Leadership Examples in Customer Connect

| Leader | Company | Action | Outcome |
|--------|---------|--------|---------|
| **Azim Premji** | Wipro | Personally visited all clients (small and large), sought feedback, shared views on technology | Positioned Wipro as a reliable, customer-centric partner; strengthened customer bonding |
| **Mike Ball** | Hospira | Conducted a **"Listening Tour"** as his first act as CEO — visited every geography where Hospira operated | Laid the foundation for company transformation based on direct customer insight |

> "CMOs must set the tone at the top — meetings should be spontaneous, genuine, and goal-directed, not arranged and performative."

### 4. Who are Customers? — Types of Needs

A customer judges every purchase through two filters:
1. **Need fulfilment** — Does the product solve the problem?
2. **Price affordability** — Is the value worth the cost?

**Types of Customer Needs:**

| Need Type | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Functional** | Utility for undertaking an activity | Buying a car to commute |
| **Esteem** | Social acceptability and recognition | Buying a premium brand pen |
| **Augmentation** | Enhancing existing capability | Buying a second laptop for redundancy |
| **Replacement** | Product has exhausted its utility | Replacing an old refrigerator |
| **Seasonal** | Festival or weather-driven needs | Winter woollens, Diwali sweets |
| **Individual** | Personal requirements | A hobby kit |
| **Family** | Household requirements | A washing machine |
| **Social** | Community or peer-group needs | Social media subscriptions |
| **Business** | Organisational requirements | Office ERP software |

> Even a "one-time" product like a lift creates recurring needs — daily cleaning, periodic maintenance, annual service contracts.

---

### 5. Maslow's Need Hierarchy — Application in Marketing

| Level | Need Type | Description | Marketing Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **5** | Self-Actualisation | Self-development, realisation | Premium education, spiritual retreats |
| **4** | Esteem | Recognition, status, self-worth | Luxury cars, branded watches |
| **3** | Social | Belonging, love, peer acceptance | Social platforms, family cars |
| **2** | Safety | Security, protection, stability | Insurance, home security systems |
| **1** | Physiological | Food, water, shelter | FMCG, real estate, utilities |

**Key Insight**: A single product can satisfy multiple Maslow levels. An automobile satisfies Level 1 (transportation/shelter from elements), Level 2 (safety features), and Level 4 (brand prestige).

#### 5.1 Limitations of Maslow in Consumer Marketing

Maslow assumes sequential fulfilment — satisfy Level 1 before Level 2, and so on. **This does not hold in practice**:

| Income Group | Product Preferred | Maslow Level Satisfied |
|-------------|------------------|----------------------|
| Low Income | Scooter | Level 1–2 |
| Lower Middle | i20 Car | Level 1–3 |
| Upper Middle | Maruti Ciaz | Level 2–4 |
| High Income | Audi Q7 | Level 3–5 |
| Super High | Lamborghini | Level 4–5 |

> A higher-income consumer does **not** stop using lower-category products. They develop **parallel need hierarchies**, not strictly sequential ones. A person with an Audi can still ride a scooter in certain contexts.

### 6. Key Customer Characteristics (Six Influencer Factors)

Customers bring diverse attributes shaped by multiple, overlapping factors:

| Influencer | Components | Marketing Implication |
|------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Demographic** | Age, income, gender, ethnicity | Different price points, product features |
| **Socio-Cultural** | Social values, religion, relationships, history | Cultural marketing, festival campaigns |
| **Economic** | GDP, per capita income, savings, price indices, taxes | Premium vs. value positioning |
| **Lifestyle** | Quality-of-life aspirations, benchmarks, comparators | Aspirational branding |
| **Technological** | Tech-savviness, productivity tools, digital adoption | Digital-first products, app ecosystems |
| **Natural** | Climate, seasonal availability, environmental concerns | Seasonal product portfolios |

> **Overlap is the norm**: Two customers at the same income level can have completely different need profiles based on their socio-cultural background. An upper-middle-income professional might prefer a roadside dhaba over a five-star restaurant.

### 7. Vertical vs. Horizontal Customer Engagement

#### 7.1 The Strategic Framework

| Dimension | **Vertical Engagement** | **Horizontal Engagement** |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Scope** | All products for one cluster of customers | One class of products for multiple customer clusters |
| **Brand** | Corporate brand dominates | Product brand dominates |
| **Focus** | Multiple needs of a single customer | Singular product need of a large cross-section |
| **Risk** | Scope before scale → strategic trap | Scale-first → efficiency and reach |
| **Advantage** | Scope advantage when done at the right time | Scale efficiency and geographic diversification |

#### 7.2 The Strategic Prescription

```
Step 1: Build HORIZONTAL scale first
         → Product specialisation + customer & geographic diversification
         → Achieve scale efficiency

Step 2: Then migrate VERTICALLY
         → Meet diverse needs of specific customer groups
         → Gain scope advantage
```

> "It would be somewhat foolhardy for a company at the very initial stages to aim at providing a product for every customer in the total customer universe."

**Vertical engagement too early** = scope placed ahead of scale = manufacturing complexity + cost burden.

### 8. Customer Value Perception

Customer perceived value is **not objective** — it is a mental calculation influenced by communication, experience, and comparison.

#### 8.1 The Value Formula

$$\text{Customer Perceived Value} = \text{Total Customer Benefit} - \text{Total Customer Cost}$$

| **Total Customer Benefit** | **Total Customer Cost** |
|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Usage Benefit | Purchase Cost |
| Ownership Benefit | Operating Cost |
| Image Benefit | Service & Parts Cost |
| Resale Value | Image & Esteem Loss |

#### 8.2 Three Dimensions of Value Delivery

| Dimension | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Product** | Designed for a specific price-performance point; product mix suits a customer mix | Cadbury from single candy bar to family pack |
| **Service** | Uptime support, parts availability, warranty, nationwide service network | Kendriya Vidyalaya's portability model |
| **Optimisation** | Post-sale improvements via software/OS updates | Smartphone OS updates improving performance over time |

**Image & Esteem Loss** is a real cost: an outdated smartphone in a technology-progressive workplace imposes a social penalty. Firms must manage **technological obsolescence** as part of value proposition.

### 9. The Traditional Reactive Customer Model

In the **transaction economy** model:
- Firm creates value proposition → Products provide value → Consumers represent demand
- Firm researches needs → Firm innovates → Firm builds value and supply chains
- **Customer = passive receiver; Firm = sole provider**

The accent is on tangible attributes: **Look & Feel → Functionality → Utility → Lifecycle Cost**

> This is the foundational model of most 20th-century businesses — adequate in a supply-constrained world, but insufficient in the information-abundant digital economy.

---

### 10. Customer Experience in the New Internet Economy

The digital revolution has made customers **active, informed, and multi-channel**:

```
Universal Digital Market
        ↓
Instant Filtered Search → Product Description
        ↓
Multiple Options → Product Stores (Brand Sites)
        ↓
Mutual Experiences & Insights (Reviews)
        ↓
Purchase Transaction → Product Delivery
        ↓
Customer Support (Manufacturer) or Return/Trade-in (Platform)
```

**Purchase modalities (2×2 matrix):**

| | **Buy Physical** | **Buy Digital** |
|--|----------------|----------------|
| **View Physical** | Traditional retail | E-commerce |
| **View Digital** | Showrooming | Pure digital commerce |

> The digital marketplace is no longer a channel — it is the *primary* arena for customer search and fulfilment experience.

### 11. The DAWN Framework (Original Course Framework)

Prof. Rao introduces DAWN as a **practical, philosophical extension of Maslow** — a framework that better explains what drives modern customers.

| Component | Definition | Strategic Meaning for Firms |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------------|
| **D — Desires** | A strong wish to have or do something | Drive higher-end product categories; upgrade paths |
| **A — Aspirations** | A strong requirement for something not yet easily achievable | Fuel for technological innovation; long-term product roadmaps |
| **W — Wants** | Routine requirements; regular diversification needs | Core portfolio; subscription and recurring revenue models |
| **N — Needs** | Essential, non-negotiable requirements | Base business; must be fulfilled efficiently and economically |

**Illustrative Application**:
- **Need**: Study to stay professionally relevant (e.g., taking one NPTEL course)
- **Want**: Regularly upgrading knowledge across domains
- **Desire**: Becoming top scorer in NPTEL; pursuing multiple certifications
- **Aspiration**: Linking all learning to become a serial entrepreneur — a long-range, systemically difficult goal

#### 11.1 DAWN and Economic Development

| DAWN Level | Economic Significance | Firm's Role |
|-----------|----------------------|------------|
| **Need** | Prime mover of all economic activity | Fulfil efficiently and economically |
| **Want** | Driver of economic diversification | Expand portfolio; offer variants |
| **Desire** | Catalyst for higher-end economic activity | Convert desires into accessible products |
| **Aspiration** | Benchmark of developed-economy status | Invest in innovation to realise aspirations |

> "As an economy grows, new needs and wants get developed even as the aspirations of the past get fulfilled." — Smartphones in India are a perfect example: once an aspiration, now a universal need.

---

### 12. Customers Have Multiple Personalities

Customers do not act in isolation. They occupy multiple roles simultaneously:

```
        Individual
           ↕
        Family ←→ Organisation
           ↕
         Friends
```

- **Individual**: Personal motivations — may want to spend
- **Family**: Shared goals — may prioritise saving
- **Friends**: Social benchmarking — may drive upward consumption
- **Organisation**: Professional identity — may require status-consistent purchases

Each group **pursues its own DAWNs**, is **constrained by its purchasing capability**, and is **influenced by others' purchasing capability**.

> "Customers buy not only for themselves but also for the related ecosystem influencers."

---

### 13. The Well-Aligned Customer Ecosystem

**Asian Paints** is the canonical example of building a customer ecosystem:

```
Customer Goal Hierarchy:
  Have a Home → Furnish a Home → Lifestyle in a Home → Social & Professional Recognition

Asian Paints' Business Evolution:
  Core Painting → Interior Design → Interior Fitments → Home Furnishing → Design Architecture
```

This is **ecosystem expansion in action** — the company moves from a product to a solution to a lifestyle partner. Even if a customer later chooses a competitor's paint, the ecosystem relationship endures.

> Companies are opening new business frontiers to meet customer needs **holistically**, not just transactionally.

---

### 14. Business Customer Ecosystem

Business customers have their own need hierarchy — mirroring Maslow but for organisational health:

| Level | Business State | BD Strategy Required |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|
| **1** | Basic Operations | Supply components; ensure reliability |
| **2** | Growing Operations | Scale-up support; capacity solutions |
| **3** | Viable Operations | Cost-efficient solutions |
| **4** | Competitive Operations | Differentiated, competitive products |
| **5** | Sustainable Operations | Long-term partnerships; AMC |
| **6** | Innovative Operations | Co-development; R&D collaboration |

> **BD strategy must match the business customer's current growth phase.** Offering innovative co-development to a company in basic operations is misaligned — and vice versa.

---

### 15. Variety as a BD Strategy — The Restaurant Typology

| Restaurant Type | Target Customer | Key Value Proposition |
|----------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **High-End Special** | Luxury-minded, high income | Ambience + Service + Signature Food |
| **High-End Popular** | Middle & High income families | Ambience + Quality Food |
| **Quick-Dine** | Youngsters, Millennials | Standardised menu + Speed + Value |
| **Native Ethnic** | All demographic groups | Tradition + Nativity + Home taste |
| **Thematic Eateries** | Niche (e.g., chocolate lovers) | Theme + Experiential |
| **Street Food Joints** | Local community | Personal + Authentic + Affordable |

> "This model can be applied to any product or service. Only thoughtfulness and customer observation are required."

---

### 16. Business Development Creating New Models — The Foodplex

Three models of food delivery evolution, illustrating BD innovation:

| Model | Description | Customer Experience |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **One Restaurant, One Food Type** | A restaurant serves only its cuisine | Customer must pre-decide restaurant AND food type |
| **Food Delivery** | Aggregator delivers multiple cuisines to doorstep | Choice of food; **no ambience** |
| **Foodplex** | Restaurants collaborate; any cuisine available at any outlet | Choice of food **AND** ambience; maximum customer freedom |

**Foodplex in practice** (Taj Coromandel example):
- Southern Spice (South Indian) + Anise (Multi-cuisine) + Golden Dragon (Chinese) + Italian Restaurant
- Customer enters any restaurant but can order food from all four

> The **network effect** in a Foodplex can fulfil customer needs far better than any single-restaurant model — and this principle applies to any product or service ecosystem.

## 📊 Summary

Lecture 21 opens Week 5 ("Connecting with Customers") by establishing the foundational understanding of who customers are and what drives them. The lecture begins by differentiating *customers* (buyers) from *consumers* (users), then elaborates 10 structural differences between consumer markets and business markets — spanning buyer scale, demand elasticity, branding impact, and contract complexity. The traditional pyramid organisation, which confines customer contact to frontline staff, is critiqued and replaced conceptually with a circular model where every function engages customers continuously. Two leadership archetypes — Azim Premji's client visits and Mike Ball's Listening Tour — illustrate how exceptional leaders operationalise this philosophy. The lecture then explores the spectrum of customer needs (functional, esteem, augmentation, replacement, seasonal) before applying Maslow's hierarchy to product markets, noting that income stratification creates parallel, not sequential, need hierarchies. Six overlapping customer influencer factors (demographic, socio-cultural, economic, lifestyle, technological, natural) are examined to show why customer characteristics resist simple classification. A critical strategic point emerges: firms should build horizontal scale first (product specialisation across geographies) before attempting vertical engagement (full range for one customer cluster), as scope-before-scale is a strategic trap. Customer value is redefined as total perceived benefit minus total perceived cost — with post-sale optimisation (software updates, service networks) now constituting a third delivery dimension alongside product and service. The lecture introduces the **DAWN framework** — an original model that categorises customer drivers as Desires, Aspirations, Wants, and Needs — and ties it to economic development: as an economy matures, yesterday's aspirations become today's needs, and new aspirations emerge. Customers are shown to have multiple simultaneous personalities (individual, family, friends, organisation), each shaping DAWNs differently. The restaurant typology demonstrates how BD strategy must cater to diverse value propositions simultaneously, and the **Foodplex concept** is presented as an original BD model that uses collaboration and network effects to transcend single-category fulfilment.

## 🔍 Final Short Summary

Lecture 21 establishes the customer as the central organising principle of business development. It systematically builds from the basic customer-consumer distinction through a 10-factor B2C vs. B2B comparison framework, and proposes a circular (not pyramidal) organisation model for true customer-centricity. Customer needs are mapped through both Maslow's hierarchy (with its practical limitations) and Prof. Rao's original DAWN framework — Desires, Aspirations, Wants, Needs — which connects individual motivation to economic development trajectories. Customer value is quantified as total benefits minus total costs (including the often-overlooked image and esteem loss), delivered across product, service, and optimisation dimensions. The lecture closes with BD strategy implications: achieve horizontal scale before vertical scope, understand the customer's ecosystem (individual or business), and innovate new models like the Foodplex to transcend transactional limitations.
