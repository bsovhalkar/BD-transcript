## 🧠 Main Points

- Market research is a **continuous institutional process** that provides timely, accurate, and actionable information about consumers, customers, competitors, and brands.
- The **6-step market research process**: Define problem → Develop research plan → Collect information → Analyse information → Present findings → Make decision.
- Types of enquiry are classified into **open-ended** (completely unstructured, word association, sentence completion, story completion, picture/TAT) and **closed-ended** (dichotomous, multiple-choice, Likert scale, semantic differential, importance scale, rating scale, intention-to-buy scale).
- **Seven characteristics of good marketing research**: scientific method, research creativity, multiple methods, models and data, value vs cost, evaluative and objective, ethical marketing.
- **Creativity in marketing research** should move from question-pad-and-ticking to digital inclusivity using visual explainers, participative thinking, emotional pivots, digital simulation, reflection, and self-evaluation.
- **Design thinking** is a human-centred, 5-step iterative process: **Empathise → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test**.
- Design thinking's unique advantages (per Prof. Jeanne Liedtka): lowers risks/costs, ensures employee and customer buy-in, and acts as **social technology**.
- The five dimensions of design thinking as customer engagement: counteracting human biases, emphasising the human element, commitment to change, structure for innovation, social technology.
- **Mistakes in empathising**: using design filters, asking incorrect questions, inability to convert words to feelings, ignoring body language, and failing to differentiate core from peripheral insights.
- **MR space vs DT space**: expressed thoughts and visible behaviour belong to MR; unknown and unexpressed thoughts belong to design thinking.
- Holistic design thinking engagements involve consumer research, technology forecasting, Gemba walks, solution sketching, pressure testing, design freezing, initial manufacturing, viability analysis, and pathway to prototyping.
- **Market research examples** (FMCG, automobiles, consumer durables, smartphones, spectacles): MR improves products incrementally but cannot disrupt them.
- **Design thinking examples** for the same five product categories: DT reimagines products fundamentally — personalised nutrition packs, solar-powered flying cars, 100% washer-driers, AI-driven smartphones, and smart spectacles.
- **Comparative table**: MR vs DT differs across customer communication format, outcome, sponsor, key lever, time with customer, prototyping approach, testing method, commercialisation focus, and post-commercialisation strategy.
- Design thinking is the ideal format for **Industry 4.0** customer experience co-creation.

## ⭐ Important Points

1. Market research provides **tactical (short-run)** and **strategic (long-run)** decision support — but cannot replace strategic judgment or entrepreneurial passion.
2. The questionnaire is the **core component** of market research; question construction, flow, and administration method all affect authenticity of responses.
3. Open-ended questions are ideal for **exploratory research**; closed-ended questions are easier to interpret and tabulate.
4. The **Likert scale** measures degree of agreement/disagreement; the **semantic differential** uses bipolar adjectives; the **intention-to-buy scale** measures purchase likelihood.
5. **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)**: a picture is presented; respondents create a story about it — a projective technique for uncovering deep feelings.
6. Market research must be **both organic (internal) and inorganic (external)** because no small internal team can cover the entire market.
7. The **value vs cost** characteristic: every MR assignment is expensive; the information value must justify the cost of collection and analysis.
8. **Ethical marketing** in MR: privacy standards must be met; personal details are optional for respondents; competitor products must not be disparaged.
9. Design thinking's five stages are **not necessarily sequential** — they are highly iterative until the best solution emerges.
10. Prof. **Jeanne Liedtka** (HBR, Sept–Oct 2018) characterises design thinking as a "**revolution of social technology**" in the research domain.
11. Customer discovery in design thinking requires **immersion, sense-making, and alignment**; idea generation requires **emergence, articulation, and prioritisation**; testing requires **pre-experience, learning in action, and validation**.
12. The **DT space** covers unknown thoughts (consumer is unaware of desires) and unexpressed thoughts (consumer has desires but is unwilling to express); the **MR space** covers expressed thoughts and visible behaviour.
13. MR can calibrate, benchmark, and improve products; it **cannot produce disruptive, transformative product innovation** — that requires DT + strategic/entrepreneurial thinking.
14. Design thinking's **Three Proofs**: Proof of Concept, Proof of Product, and Proof of Business — all three must be validated.
15. The key DT vs MR contrast: DT is **customer-centric** and **experience-driven**; MR is **product-centric** and **Q&A-driven**.

## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. The Market Research Process

Market research links the **consumer, customer, and public** with the firm. It generates insights about marketplace stakeholders and helps the firm offer what customers truly need.

**Definition:** Market research is a function that helps marketers take appropriate **tactical decisions (short run)** and **strategic decisions (long run)**. It makes available **timely, accurate, and actionable** information about consumers, customers, competitors, and brands.

**Institutional characteristics:**
- It is a **continuous** institutional process linked to the strategic planning process.
- It requires a **central department** excelling in data collection, analytics, experimental design, and interpretation.
- It must be both **organic** (internal expertise) and **inorganic** (external expertise) — the market is too vast for an internal team alone.

**The 6-Step Market Research Process:**

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1. Define problem & objectives | Identify what needs to be researched |
| 2. Develop research plan | Decide the methodology, tools, and scope |
| 3. Collect information | Gather data from respondents/sources |
| 4. Analyse information | Use statistical and qualitative methods |
| 5. Present findings | Report insights to decision-makers |
| 6. Make the decision | Apply insights to marketing/business strategy |

**Sample questions MR can answer:**
- Why is my product not selling as well as competitors'?
- What features do consumers prioritise?
- How price-elastic is my product?
- What incentive schemes help retailers?
- What is the role of loyalty/brand connect?
- How can we reposition the brand for better profitability?

### 2. Types of Enquiries

#### 2A. Open-Ended Questions
Used in **exploratory research** with relatively open boundaries. No pre-set answer choices — respondents can answer in unlimited ways.

| Technique | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Completely Unstructured** | No pre-set questions; answer in any way | Open conversation with customer |
| **Word Association** | A word is presented; respondent says the first word that comes to mind | "Gold Winner" → "Pure" / "Healthy" |
| **Sentence Completion** | Incomplete sentence to complete | "I like Brand X because ___" |
| **Story Completion** | Incomplete story presented; respondent completes it | "When I opened my ice cream parlour, customers came all day — then only daytime customers came. Why?" |
| **Picture (Cartoon)** | Two characters shown; respondent fills in the empty speech balloon | Identify with one character; fill the dialogue |
| **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)** | A picture presented; respondent makes up a full story | Picture of an ice cream parlour → predict footfall patterns |

#### 2B. Closed-Ended Questions
All possible answers are specified. Easier to interpret and tabulate. Still require careful structuring with statistical tools for weighting and ranking.

| Scale / Type | Description | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| **Dichotomous** | Two possible answers | Yes / No |
| **Multiple Choice** | Three or more options | A / B / C / D |
| **Likert Scale** | Degree of agreement/disagreement (5 points) | "My company treats employees respectfully" → Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree |
| **Semantic Differential** | Two bipolar adjectives; respondent marks a point between them | Modern ←————→ Old-fashioned |
| **Importance Scale** | Rates the importance of an attribute | "Creaminess in dairy is: Very Important / Somewhat / Not Important" |
| **Rating Scale** | Rates attribute from poor to excellent | Service quality: 1 (Poor) to 5 (Excellent) |
| **Intention-to-Buy Scale** | Measures likelihood of purchase | "Definitely will buy / Probably / Might / Probably not / Definitely not" |

> **Note:** Besides quantitative analysis, **qualitative research** using word association, projective techniques, and visualisation is also deployed. Food delivery apps use minimal surveys and comment solicitation as real-world examples.

### 3. Seven Characteristics of Good Marketing Research

| # | Characteristic | Description |
|---|----------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Scientific Method** | Careful observation, proper hypothesis formulation, iterative testing |
| 2 | **Research Creativity** | Use of creative prompts and comparators to read the customer mind |
| 3 | **Multiple Methods** | Using more than one technique (quantitative + qualitative; open + closed) |
| 4 | **Models and Data** | Realistic data and statistically meaningful models |
| 5 | **Value and Cost** | Value of information must justify the cost of collection and analysis |
| 6 | **Evaluative and Objective** | Overcome legacy biases; data-based but contextualised |
| 7 | **Ethical Marketing** | Respect privacy, make personal details optional, avoid disparaging competitors |

> Market research is also called **marketing research** — the terms are used interchangeably.

### 4. Creativity in Marketing Research

Marketing research must not be mechanical or routine. It should be **exciting and inclusive** to respondents.

**Shift from:** Question Pad and Ticking → **Shift to:** Digital Inclusivity

| Creative Method | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Visual Explainers & Comparators** | Show images of two products; ask which form factor / colour scheme / design the respondent prefers |
| **Participative Thinking** | Treat the process as collaborative; let customers think and engage, not just respond |
| **Emotional Pivots** | Ask questions that evoke emotional connections: "What makes this product connect with your family?" |
| **Digital Simulation** | Use AR, VR, MR, interactive digital gamification to simulate real usage scenarios |
| **Reflection** | Design questions that prompt thoughtful reflection, not knee-jerk reactions |
| **Self-evaluation** | Both the questioner and respondent reflect on their own biases and responses |

By using digital technologies, market research can be **significantly stimulating and holistically comprehending**.

### 5. Design Thinking — Overview

**Definition:** Design thinking is a **human-centred, 5-step iterative process** that designers, marketers, and problem solvers use to discover true customer needs and issues, and find solutions collaboratively.

**The Five Stages:**

| Stage | Done With/For | Key Activity |
|-------|---------------|--------------|
| **Empathise** | Users | Deep immersion in user experience; observe, listen, engage |
| **Define** | Users' needs, problems, solutions | Synthesise empathy data; define the core problem statement |
| **Ideate** | Integrating Define-phase inputs | Challenge assumptions; seek divergent ideas and creative solutions |
| **Prototype** | Chosen ideas | Convert ideas into workable, tangible prototypes |
| **Test** | Prototypes | Validate solutions against specifications; assess viability |

> The five stages are **NOT necessarily sequential** — they are **highly iterative**; teams may loop back to any earlier stage based on findings.

---

### 6. Unique Advantages of Design Thinking (Jeanne Liedtka Framework)

Source: *"Why Design Thinking Works"* — Prof. Jeanne Liedtka, HBR, September–October 2018.

Design thinking offers a **seamless flow of ideas into actionable work — from research to rollout**.

**Three outcomes successful innovation requires:**
1. **Customer Discovery** → Immersion, Sense Making, Alignment
2. **Idea Generation** → Emergence, Articulation, Prioritisation
3. **Testing Experience** → Pre-experience, Learning in Action, Validation

### 7. Design Thinking as a Tool of Customer Engagement

Immersion in customer experience produces **data → insights → design criteria → brainstormed solutions**.

Five key dimensions of design thinking as customer engagement:

| Dimension | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Counteracting Human Biases** | DT processes are open-ended; biases cancel each other out; creativity is unleashed; superior solutions emerge at lower cost and risk |
| **Emphasising the Human Element** | Recognises customers and organisations as human; emphasises engagement, dialogue, and learning |
| **Commitment to Change** | Customers become stakeholders in problem definition and solution development; broad commitment to change is secured |
| **Structure for Innovation** | Provides a structural process for innovators (whether customers or company reps) to collaborate and agree on essentials at every phase — a Gemba innovation |
| **Social Technology** | Company co-experiences the problem and solution with the customer; representatives are open, transparent, and socially networked — this is social technology at work |

> **Contrast with traditional approach:** When a customer reports a product problem, a company typically first checks warranty status and investigates misuse. Design thinking removes this investigative posture entirely — representatives experience the problem alongside the customer.

### 8. Mistakes in Empathising

Empathising is the "foremost interpersonal and emotional skill" — it requires special training. Common mistakes:

1. **Viewing customer experience through design/functional filters** — judging how the product *should* be used instead of how it *is* used.
2. **Asking incorrect questions** — leading to incorrect answers; also interpreting even correct answers through biases.
3. **Inability to interpret words into feelings** — language and expression vary; body language is often ignored.
4. **Inadequate observation of engagement and listening** — if only one customer is articulate in a group of five, the activity is failing.
5. **Inability to differentiate core from peripheral insights** — in an open discussion, many ideas emerge; the key is identifying which insight drives the primary product development imperative.

**Remedy:** Acquire skills to be an empathetic **observer, engager, listener, and analyser**.

### 9. MR Space vs DT Space

A consumer's thought process can be mapped across four layers:

| Consumer Thought Layer | Description | Research Space |
|------------------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Unknown Thoughts** | Desires about potential needs/wants the consumer is not even aware of | **DT Space** |
| **Unexpressed Thoughts** | Thoughts the consumer has but is unwilling to express (personal care, symbolic products) | **DT Space** |
| **Expressed Thoughts** | Thoughts the consumer is willing to share | **MR Space** |
| **Visible Behaviour** | Behavioural actions the consumer is willing to reveal externally | **MR Space** |

> **Key insight:** DT can extend into the MR space, but MR typically cannot reach the DT space. DT is **entrepreneurial and start-up oriented**; MR is **structured and systematic**. Today, both established companies and startups are recognising DT's importance for every new product development.

### 10. Holistic Engagements in Design Thinking

Design thinking engagements must produce holistic solutions through an iterative cycle:

**Create → Refine → Repeat**

Components of the holistic engagement:

| Component | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Consumer Research | Understand the problem |
| Technology Forecasting | Identify solution platforms |
| Gemba Walks | Be in the field; observe actual usage |
| Solution Sketching | Draw initial solution ideas |
| Challenging / Pressure Testing | Stress-test assumptions |
| Freezing Design Solution | Finalise a design |
| Initial Manufacturing Solution | Translate design to producible form |
| Viability Analysis | Assess feasibility for company and customer |
| Cost-Price Analysis | Ensure economic viability |
| Pathway to Prototyping (Product Plan) | Bridge to commercial prototype |

> Customer involvement is greatest during **ideation, prototyping, and testing**; less so in manufacturing viability analysis.

### 11. Market Research vs Design Thinking — Five Product Examples

#### FMCG (Ready-Mix Batter)

| Aspect | Market Research | Design Thinking |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Focus areas | Packaging, product consistency, ease of mixing/cooking, shelf life | Expectations beyond taste; personalised nutritional additives (protein, immunity, memory, insulin) for each family member |
| Outcome | Calibrate supply, benchmark, improve product | Personalised batter + customisable additive packs (diabetic batter, child-nutrition batter, immunity batter) |
| Limitation | Cannot create disruptive product | Combines tech innovation with customer fulfilment |

#### Automobile

| Aspect | Market Research | Design Thinking |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Focus areas | Look/feel, performance, navigation, interior, safety | Zero-traffic, zero-operating-cost mobility; solar-powered flying cars |
| Outcome | Calibrate supply, benchmark, improve/next-gen model | Combines aerospace + automobile tech; new generation mobility (electric tramways, hovercrafts) |
| Key insight | MR improved cars but **did not** address emissions or bring electrification — those came from **regulators and entrepreneurial passion** | DT reimagines mobility fundamentally |

#### Consumer Durables (Washer-Drier)

| Aspect | Market Research | Design Thinking |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Focus areas | Form factor, washing performance, reliability, durability, safety | 100% integrated washer-drier with twin compartments + robotic transfer + super-heater |
| Outcome | Better supply/product/next-gen improvement | Dirt sensing, AI-based cloth mixing warnings, calibrated detergent/water/power use; reduced carbon footprint |
| Key insight | Even Dyson abandoned twin-drum innovation; MR cannot break the 85% dryness mold | DT creates genuinely disruptive consumer durables |

#### Smartphone

| Aspect | Market Research | Design Thinking |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Focus areas | Form factor, processor/RAM/camera, OS, connectivity, safety (Gorilla Glass) | AI-driven human companion; digital twin; speech-to-text lifestyle integration; supercomputer-level processing |
| Outcome | Incremental improvement (candy-bar → bezel-less → foldable) | Physical + digital + biological convergence; Industry 4.0 exemplar |
| Key insight | MR improved smartphones but did not disrupt them — disruption was **company-led** | DT envisions a stress-free, AI-enabled, joyful life |

#### Spectacles

| Aspect | Market Research | Design Thinking |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Focus areas | Intended use (prescription/protection/stylistic), look/feel, performance (clarity, UV), reliability, durability | Smart spectacle personalised for students, professionals, homemakers, retirees; inner + outer vision |
| Outcome | Improved spectacles, not disrupted | AI-driven information companion; streaming personalised information to the Glass; Industry 4.0 product |
| Key insight | MR has not changed the basic constitution of spectacles | DT: "desired information at a blink" |

### 12. Market Research vs Design Thinking — Comparative Framework

| Factor | Market Research (MR) | Design Thinking (DT) |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Customer Communication** | Q&A format; within boundaries | Experience format; unconstrained |
| **Outcome** | Improved product | New customer experience |
| **Sponsor** | Company | Customer |
| **Key Lever** | Product development | Technological innovation |
| **Time with Customer** | A few minutes | Days and even weeks |
| **Prototyping** | Post-research in company labs | Simultaneous with co-creation of experience |
| **Testing & Validation** | Product performance | **Three Proofs**: Concept, Product, Business |
| **Commercialisation** | Product-centric | Customer-centric |
| **Post-commercialisation** | Product lifecycle management (+ more MR) | Customer experience development (+ more DT) |

> Design thinking is the ideal format for discovering and **co-creating new-generation customer experiences** as we move toward Industry 4.0.

## 📊 Summary

Lecture 23 covers two major, complementary tools for understanding customers: **Market Research (MR)** and **Design Thinking (DT)**. Market research is defined as a continuous institutional process following six steps — from problem definition to decision-making — that delivers timely and actionable information to support both tactical and strategic choices. The lecture categorises research enquiry techniques into open-ended formats (word association, sentence completion, TAT, story completion) and closed-ended formats (Likert scales, semantic differentials, dichotomous questions, rating scales), noting that the questionnaire remains the core instrument. Seven characteristics define good marketing research: scientific rigour, creativity, multiple methods, realistic data, cost-value balance, objectivity, and ethical practice. The professor emphasises that creative techniques — visual explainers, participative thinking, emotional pivots, digital simulation — must replace the mechanical question-pad-and-ticking approach. Design thinking is introduced as a superior, human-centred five-stage iterative process (Empathise–Define–Ideate–Prototype–Test) that addresses the two thought layers MR cannot reach: **unknown thoughts** and **unexpressed thoughts**. Prof. Jeanne Liedtka's framework positions DT as social technology that counteracts bias, emphasises human engagement, commits all stakeholders to change, structures innovation, and enables co-creation. Five real-world product examples (FMCG batter, automobile, washer-drier, smartphone, spectacles) systematically show how MR improves products **incrementally** while DT reimagines them **disruptively** — from personalised nutritional batters to solar-powered flying cars. The lecture concludes with a nine-factor comparative table distinguishing MR and DT and positions design thinking as the ideal methodology for co-creating new-generation customer experiences in the Industry 4.0 era.

## 🔍 Final Short Summary

Lecture 23 establishes that market research and design thinking are complementary but distinct tools for customer understanding. Market research — through structured open/closed enquiry techniques, seven quality characteristics, and creative digital methods — effectively improves products incrementally by addressing what customers express and reveal. Design thinking goes deeper, accessing unknown and unexpressed customer needs through empathy-driven immersion (Empathise–Define–Ideate–Prototype–Test), producing disruptive rather than incremental innovations, as illustrated across five product categories (FMCG, automobile, consumer durables, smartphone, spectacles). The fundamental distinction: MR is product-centric, company-sponsored, and Q&A-driven; DT is customer-centric, customer-sponsored, experience-driven, and validated through three proofs — making DT the essential methodology for co-creating next-generation solutions in the Industry 4.0 era.
