## 2. Key Explanations

### 2.1 The Competitor Landscape

Firms often narrow their view of competition to players in the same league. This is a strategic blind spot. Using the Indian IT industry as an example, the sector is multi-tiered — from giants like TCS (₹1,52,497 Cr revenue) and Infosys down to emerging firms like Happiest Minds — and any tier can threaten another. In the global IT services brand rankings (2021), India holds 29% of brand value (second to the USA's 47%) and has 6 of the top 25 brands. Indian IT leaders like TCS and Infosys are rated **AAA−**, reflecting world-class competitive capability.

> **Core Insight:** A firm must maintain a wide and dynamic view of its competitive landscape — not just who the competitors are today, but who they could become tomorrow.

---

### 2.2 The Analytical Framework for Competitor Analysis

A firm must analyse competitors across **four dimensions**:

| Dimension | Key Questions |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Future Goals** | At all levels and in all domains of the organisation |
| **Current Strategy** | How the business is currently competing |
| **Assumptions** | What the competitor believes about itself and the industry |
| **Capabilities** | Both strengths and weaknesses |

**Competitor Response Profile** asks:
- Does the competitor want to improve its market share?
- What strategies will it adopt?
- Where is it strong or weak?
- What could trigger strong retaliation?
- Are competitors acting singly or collectively?

> **Key Insight:** The same quantitative framework used to analyse your own company applies equally to analysing competitors. Smugness or alarm must be replaced by **systematic analysis**.

---

### 2.3 Categories of Competitors (Multi-Focus Analysis)

Five types of competitors must be tracked — direct and potential:

1. **Direct competitors** — firms in the same industry (e.g., Metropolis vs. another medical diagnostics firm)
2. **Firms that can overcome entry barriers easily** — e.g., a four-wheeler company entering the two-wheeler market; Eicher moving from tractors to commercial vehicles
3. **Firms with obvious synergy** — e.g., an oncology company leveraging sterile injectable capability to enter adjacent pharmaceutical markets
4. **Firms whose corporate strategy drives entry** — when a company's existing core business is saturated, it moves into your space (e.g., Bharat Forge entering defence products)
5. **Backward/forward integrating customers or suppliers** — yesterday's partner becomes tomorrow's rival

> **Illustration:** A medical diagnostics firm faces competition from: (a) Metropolis (direct), (b) Practo (medical services), (c) Apollo Hospitals (end-to-end healthcare), (d) Lupin (pharma pivoting into diagnostics), and (e) Angstrom Biotech (home test-kit technology).

> **Key Insight:** "Forecasting direct competitors is not easy; forecasting potential competitors is even more difficult." The competitive horizon must extend well beyond the visible industry boundary.

---

### 2.4 Competitor Goals Hierarchy

Competitors maintain a structured hierarchy of goals:

| Goal Category | Examples |
|--------------|---------|
| **Physical & Financial** | Production, capacity utilisation, breakeven, revenue, ROI, EPS, market cap |
| **Strategic** | Generic strategies, portfolio, diversification, globalisation, IP generation |
| **Governance** | Compliance, ethics, ESG, risk management, business continuity |
| **Structural** | Board composition, CEO & CXO profiles, corporate and organisational structure |
| **People** | CEO pedigree, CXO profiles, LTIP, non-financial recognition |
| **National** | Alignment with Atmanirbhar Bharat, Make in India, MSME support, start-up development |

**Analysing Competitor Goals — a three-step process:**
1. Identify goals and achievements (of both the company and competitors)
2. Conduct gap analysis between goals and achievements
3. Determine sustainable competitive advantage from those gaps

> **Key Insight:** A narrow gap between goals and achievements signals superior competitive advantage. The firm should use this analysis to identify actions for sustainable competitive advantage.

---

### 2.5 Assumptions and the Assumptions-Achievement Framework

Competitors — and your own firm — hold assumptions about future capabilities, the industry, and rivals. These assumptions are in five tiers:

- **Technical:** Cost, quality, technology, processes, facilities
- **Pipeline:** Product pipeline, regulatory approvals, capacity, markets, finances
- **Reputation:** Brand value, corporate reputation, ESG, OSH ratings
- **Identification:** Historical identification, emotional identification, belief in future environment and industry
- **Competitive beliefs:** Belief about future competition intensity

**Framework for Analysing Assumptions vs. Achievements:**

|  | **Achievements worse than competitor** | **Achievements better than competitor** |
|---|---|---|
| **Assumptions worse than competitor** | Endemic Competitive Disadvantage | Likely Competitive Success |
| **Assumptions better than competitor** | Likely Competitive Failure | **Likely Sustainable Competitive Advantage** |

> **Key Insight:** "Firms tend to have beliefs about the future while taking quantitative facts somewhat less seriously." A balanced view — grounded in current realities but informed by future projections — is essential.

**Methodology for assessing assumptions:**
1. Collect all company and competitor assumptions
2. Filter against the history of past realities (identify blind spots)
3. Assess managerial competencies of both sides
4. Assess support factors (board, auditors, advisors)
5. Decide on the probability of success vis-à-vis the competitor

---

### 2.6 Generic Strategy and Competitive Rivalry

| Strategy Combination | Rivalry Outcome |
|---------------------|----------------|
| Cost Leadership vs. Cost Leadership | Intense Rivalry |
| Differentiation vs. Differentiation | Intense Rivalry |
| Differentiation vs. Cost Leadership | Co-existent Rivalry |
| Differentiation or Cost Leadership vs. Focus/Niche | Co-existent Rivalry |

> **Key Insight:** A firm should choose a competitive strategy that is *somewhat unique* to its position. Overlapping strategies with rivals intensify competition and erode margins.

---

### 2.7 Functional and Organisational Capabilities

**Functional Capabilities (Strengths and Weaknesses):**

| Function | Key Capabilities |
|----------|----------------|
| **R&D** | Product pipeline, patents, in-house capability, R&D facilities, scientists |
| **Operations** | Capacity & BEP, process efficiency, economies of scale and scope, lean manufacturing |
| **Marketing** | Channel relationships, salesforce skills, 4Ps strengths, digital marketing, design thinking |
| **Finance** | Cash flow, debt/equity headroom, borrowing capacity, financial governance |

**General Organisational Capabilities:**

| Dimension | Key Aspects |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Structure** | Alignment to strategy, efficiency, flexibility to reconfigure |
| **Processes** | Clarity of purpose, process integrity, code of conduct, compliance |
| **Leadership** | CEO/CXO quality, succession strength, flexibility and adaptability |
| **Talent** | Competencies, cultural moorings, unity of values, collaboration |

---

### 2.8 Offensive and Defensive Moves

**Offensive moves** ask: How aggressive is the competitor? How will they reach an improved position? How strong and persistent will those moves be?

**Defensive moves** ask: How vulnerable is the competitor to our actions? How will the competitor retaliate? How strong will we be in the face of retaliation?

> **Key Insight:** A firm must understand the *degrees of freedom available to both sides* in the competitive game. For example, a non-lowest-cost producer should never compete on price alone.

---

### 2.9 Types of Retaliators

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Operational Retaliators** | Match the competitor's move (e.g., match a price cut) to maintain balance |
| **Strategic Retaliators** | Match in part and plot a broader, long-term strategic response |
| **Non-Retaliators** | Do not respond; either succumb to or outlast the pressure over time |

> **Key Insight:** Firms with **sustainable brand equity** can ride out competitive storms without retaliating. Firms lacking this must find ways to build a defensive capability or risk succumbing to competitive pressure.

---

### 2.10 Battlegrounds for Competition

| Area of Competitive Action | Best Suited For |
|---------------------------|----------------|
| **Price** | Works best for already-differentiated firms (offer differentiation at a better price) |
| **Features** | Works best for cost leaders (add differentiation to a generic position) |
| **Service** | Works well for both cost leaders and differentiators |
| **Combination of above** | Can diffuse impact; any abrupt competitor response could undermine the strategy |

> **Key Insight:** "When features and price are further supported by service, a firm gains competitive advantage on a **holistic basis**." The right battleground depends on who the customers are and how well the firm understands and connects with them.

---

## 3. Important Insights (Lecture 20 Summary)

1. **Broaden the competitive lens** — Competition is multi-tiered and can arise from unexpected strategic adjacencies (e.g., Reliance Financial Services emerging as a direct competitor to Paytm).
2. **Systematic, not emotional, analysis** — Smugness or panic must be replaced by structured frameworks for competitor evaluation.
3. **Original Assumptions-Achievement Matrix** — This framework (presented as an original model in the course) maps where a firm stands relative to its competitor on both assumptions (foresight) and achievements (execution). Sustainable competitive advantage requires superiority on **both** dimensions.
4. **Competitor statements must be read between the lines** — Bold announcements often signal deeper strategic intentions (e.g., Adani's 5G private network bid hints at future ambitions beyond the stated scope).
5. **Capabilities determine realisation of strategy** — Vision statements are empty without functional and organisational capabilities to back them. Every firm must assess both its own and its competitors' capabilities across R&D, operations, marketing, finance, structure, processes, leadership, and talent.

## 2. Key Explanations

### 2.1 Customers vs. Consumers

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Customer** | An individual or entity that *purchases* a product or service | An administrative manager buying software for the firm |
| **Consumer** | An individual or entity that *uses* the product or service for personal needs | The employees who actually use the software |

- A **dealer** is a customer for an automobile firm; the dealer's end-buyers are the consumers.
- The company must understand both — the dealer (customer) and the end-user (consumer) — because **demand at the consumer level drives the entire marketing value chain**.
- In practice, the two terms are used interchangeably in strategic and business development courses.

---

### 2.2 Individual Consumer Markets vs. Business Consumer Markets

| Factor | Consumer Markets | Business Markets |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Buyers** | Huge in number (lakhs to millions) | Relatively fewer |
| **Products/Services** | Common | Common and specific |
| **Access** | Relatively easy | Relatively harder |
| **Branding** | Strongly influences perception | Low branding impact |
| **Packaging** | Designed to attract | Primarily for protection |
| **Promotion** | Influences customers | Sometimes even prohibited |
| **Demand** | Relatively elastic | Relatively inelastic |
| **Buying Process** | Direct, in-store or online | Procedure-bound, through intermediaries |
| **Decision Making** | Individual (at times influenced by others) | Multi-tier with levels of authority |
| **Code of Conduct** | General buying-selling principles | Ethical code with legal consequences |
| **Extent of Contract** | Limited to individual purchase; warranty expected | Long-term, project-specific; strong AMC requirements |

> **Strategic Question:** Should the company focus on consumer markets, business markets, or both? (e.g., edible oil companies serve both individual consumer packs and bulk tins for HoReCa businesses)

---

### 2.3 Customer-Oriented Organisation

**Traditional Organisation:** A pyramid — top management at apex, frontline at the base. Only the frontline has strong customer connect.

**Contemporary Concept:** A circular organisation where **every function** (R&D, Production, Finance, Marketing, IT, Others) is encircled by and directly connects with customers.

**Leadership Examples:**
- **Azim Premji (Wipro):** Personally visited clients — large and small — and sought feedback during his tenure as full-time CEO.
- **Mike Ball (Hospira):** On taking over as CEO, his first action was a "listening tour" to understand how the company serves customers — laying the foundation for meaningful change.

> **Key Insight:** Chief Marketing Officers must set the tone at the top in customer interface by frequently meeting customers. Customer connect must not be left only to the sales team.

---

### 2.4 Types of Customer Needs

Customers have diverse and layered needs:

| Need Type | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Individual Needs** | Personal daily requirements |
| **Family Needs** | Needs of the family unit |
| **Social Needs** | Needs driven by relationships and community |
| **Business Needs** | Professional and organisational requirements |
| **Functional Needs** | Utility-based requirements |
| **Seasonal Needs** | Time-bound or seasonal requirements |
| **Esteem Needs** | Status and image-related needs |
| **Replacement Needs** | Needs arising from product obsolescence or wear |
| **Augmentation Needs** | Needs for upgrading or enhancing existing products/services |

---

### 2.5 Maslow Need Hierarchy and Its Limitations

**Maslow's Hierarchy (bottom to top):**
1. Physiological Needs (food, water, shelter)
2. Safety Needs (security, protection)
3. Social Needs (belonging, love)
4. Esteem Needs (recognition, status)
5. Self-Actualisation (self-development and realisation)

**Limitation in Practice:** Maslow postulates that once a lower-order need is fulfilled, the individual moves to the next. However, different products in the *same category* fulfil multiple need levels depending on the customer's income and background:

| Income Group | Product |
|---|---|
| Super High Income | Lamborghini |
| High Income | Audi Q7 |
| Upper Middle Income | Maruti Ciaz |
| Lower Middle Income | i20 |
| Low Income | Scooter |

> **Key Insight:** Customers of different income groups have their own specific need hierarchies *regardless* of what was fulfilled at the previous income level. **Demographic and socio-economic factors make the hierarchy non-linear.**

---

### 2.6 Key Customer Characteristics

Customers come with different attributes, backgrounds, and influences:

| Influencer | Key Factors |
|-----------|------------|
| **Demographic** | Age profile, income patterns, gender diversity, ethnicity |
| **Socio-Cultural** | Social values, mores, relationships, historical and religious influences |
| **Economic** | GDP, per capita income, savings, price indices, consumer spending, tax levels |
| **Lifestyle** | Quality of life, comparators and benchmarks |
| **Technological** | Use of technology for productivity, convenience, esteem; technology adoption in professional and personal life |
| **Natural** | Resource endowments, climatic conditions, nature's vagaries, environmental concerns |

> **Key Insight:** These factors have **overlapping influences**. Two customers with the same income level can have different need profiles based on socio-cultural background or technology-friendliness. Customer characteristics resist straight-jacketed classification.

---

### 2.7 Vertical vs. Horizontal Customer Engagement

| Type | Description | Risk/Reward |
|------|------------|------------|
| **Vertical Customer Engagement** | All products required by a single cluster of customers; meets multiple needs of a single customer across income/life levels | Requires a full product range from low-end to high-end. Placing *scope ahead of scale* can lead to a serious strategic trap (manufacturing complexity) |
| **Horizontal Customer Engagement** | Single class of products to multiple customer clusters across geographies; singular product need for a large customer cross-section | Requires product specialisation + geographic diversification = **scale efficiency** |

**Strategic Recommendation:** A firm should first build *scale* by meeting common customer needs globally (horizontal), and then move into vertical engagement with diverse needs of specialised groups. Timely vertical migration provides scope advantage.

---

### 2.8 Customer Value Perception

Customer value is a **perceived** value — the customer calculates it mentally based on:

**Total Customer Benefit:**
- Usage Benefit (functional value during use)
- Ownership Benefit (pride, practicality of ownership)
- Image Benefit (brand/esteem image)
- Resale Value (value at end of life cycle)

**Total Customer Cost:**
- Purchase Cost
- Operating Cost
- Service and Parts Cost
- Image and Esteem Loss (technological obsolescence eroding status, e.g., an old-generation smartphone in an evolved office environment)

**Three Routes to Value:**
1. **Product** — Designed for a price-performance point relevant to the customer (e.g., Cadbury from candy bar to family pack)
2. **Service** — Uptime during use, parts support, robust warranty, nationwide service network, interoperability
3. **Optimisation** — Continuous performance monitoring and ongoing upgrades (e.g., mobile OS updates)

> **Key Insight:** Companies are placing increasing efforts on service and optimisation verticals. Software has become a key tool for sustaining higher value perception **after** the product has been sold.

---

### 2.9 Traditional Customer Model vs. New Internet Economy

**Traditional Model (Transaction Economy):**
- Firm creates value proposition → Products/services provide value → Consumers represent demand
- Firm is the provider; customer is the receiver. Interaction is purely transactional.
- Focus on tangible attributes: look & feel, functionality, utility, lifecycle cost

**New Internet Economy:**
- Universal digital market → Instant filtered search → Product description → Multiple options → Purchase transaction → Product delivery → Customer support & service → Product return/trade-in
- Customer can now: **View Physical, Buy Physical** | **View Physical, Buy Digital** | **View Digital, Buy Physical** | **View Digital, Buy Digital**

> **Key Insight:** The digital marketplace has become an integral part of the customer search and fulfilment experience. The customer is no longer merely reactive — they arrive informed, compare across platforms, share reviews, and co-create brand reputation.

---

### 2.10 The DAWN Framework — A New Need Spectrum

Beyond Maslow, this course introduces an original practical framework: **DAWNs (Desires, Aspirations, Wants, Needs)**

| Level | Definition | Strategic Implication for Firms |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------------|
| **Need** | A requirement for something essential or very important; the prime mover for most economic activity | Firms must fulfil all consumer needs efficiently and economically |
| **Want** | A requirement as a matter of routine | Firms must fulfil wants along with needs to stay relevant |
| **Desire** | A strong wish to have or do something | Economic development should convert desires into needs; firms must be prepared to cater to desires |
| **Aspiration** | A strong requirement for something not easily achievable; gives the economy a "developed" qualification | Economic aspirations prime society to focus on technological innovation |

> **Key Insight:** Companies can *move customers up the DAWN ladder* — from need to want to desire to aspiration — through thoughtful products, upgrade offers, and trade-in programs (e.g., NPTEL self-paced programs fulfilling learning aspirations; real estate companies promising apartment upgrades along with career growth).

---

### 2.11 Customer Multiple Personalities and the Customer Ecosystem

Customers are influenced by multiple overlapping ecosystems:

| Layer | Intrinsic Motivation |
|-------|---------------------|
| **Individual** | Personal DAWNs and purchasing capability |
| **Family** | Family needs may diverge from individual needs (e.g., save vs. spend) |
| **Friends** | Peer influence on lifestyle and purchase decisions |
| **Organisation** | Professional needs governed by business goals and budgets |

**Well-aligned Customer Ecosystem (Individual example — Asian Paints):**
- Core need: Have a home → Furnish a home → Lifestyle in a home → Social and professional recognition
- Asian Paints moved from surface protection → surface attraction → interior design → interior fitments → home furnishings → holistic home design

**Business Customer Ecosystem (Hierarchy of Business Needs):**
1. Basic business operations
2. Viable business operations (cost-efficient)
3. Competitive business operations
4. Growing business operations (innovative)
5. Sustainable business operations

> **Key Insight:** Business development must align itself to the **phase of growth** of the business customer. The offering must evolve as the customer's business evolves.

---

### 2.12 Business Development and New Business Models

Using the food industry as an illustrative canvas:

| Model | Description | Customer Value |
|-------|------------|---------------|
| **One Restaurant, One Food Type** | Traditional, specialised, pre-decided choice | Limited to one cuisine |
| **Food Delivery** | Multiple cuisines from multiple restaurants to doorstep | Choice without ambience |
| **Foodplex** | Restaurants collaborate; customer can walk into any and order from any | Choice with ambience |

> **Key Insight:** Business development is about providing **personalised experiences to a wide spectrum of customers**. Companies must move beyond the transactional "provider-receiver" paradigm to understand the full DAWN hierarchies of individual customers, groups of customers, and businesses — and then design innovative models to fulfil them.

---

## 3. Important Insights (Lecture 21 Summary)

1. **Customers ≠ Consumers, but both matter.** A company must understand the full demand chain — from the purchasing customer (e.g., a dealer) to the end-consuming customer — to drive effective business development.
2. **Customer-orientation must be enterprise-wide.** The traditional pyramid limits customer insight to frontline teams. Circular, customer-encircled organisations — exemplified by leaders like Azim Premji and Mike Ball — create structural advantage.
3. **Maslow's hierarchy has real-world limitations.** Income alone does not determine need hierarchy; socio-cultural, technological, and lifestyle factors create non-linear customer journeys.
4. **The DAWN Framework is a richer toolkit than Maslow.** By distinguishing needs, wants, desires, and aspirations, firms can design offerings that *evolve with the customer's ambition*, not just current purchasing power.
5. **Vertical vs. Horizontal engagement is a key strategic decision.** Scale first (horizontal) → then scope (vertical) is the recommended sequence to avoid strategic traps.
6. **Customer value is perceived, not absolute.** The total value equation (benefits minus costs, including image and esteem) must be actively managed by the firm across the product, service, and optimisation dimensions.
7. **The internet economy has shifted power to the customer.** Firms can no longer be purely product-centric. The new paradigm demands customer co-creation, real-time feedback, and flexible multi-channel purchase journeys.
8. **Business models must innovate around customer need ecosystems.** As Asian Paints demonstrated, understanding the full customer ecosystem (from home ownership to lifestyle to recognition) unlocks entirely new revenue streams and deepens competitive differentiation.
