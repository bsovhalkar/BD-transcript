### 1.2 What is a Business?

> **Definition:** Business is the activity of an organisation to develop, manufacture, and/or market products and/or services to individuals or entities to earn revenues and profits and generate returns to the investors.

**Typology of Businesses:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Integrated Businesses | Combine R&D, manufacturing, and marketing |
| Manufacturing Businesses | Focus on production and output |
| Service Businesses | Deliver intangible value to customers |
| Marketing Businesses | Driven by branding and distribution |
| Product Businesses | Centered on tangible product development |
| R&D Businesses | Primarily knowledge and innovation driven |

---

### 1.3 What is Business Development?

> **Definition:** Business development involves anticipating, identifying, and developing opportunities for enhancing business.

BD operates along three complementary dimensions:

1. **Transactional** — By providing products and services to customers through commercial relationships.
2. **Reinforcing** — By developing collaborations, alliances, and partnerships with business entities.
3. **Futuristic** — By identifying opportunities as per the classic product-market grid, also spotting the mega-trends of the future.

**BD as a Process:**
The business development process is a conceptual and analytical process of fusing the company and customer value chains:

```
Introspect on Company Value Chain
        ↓
Identify Value Points and Value Gaps
        ↓
Identify Potential Partners with Complementary Value Points and Gaps
        ↓
Test Strategic Feasibility and Economic Viability
        ↓
Create a Combined Value Proposition
        ↓
Discuss the Potential Value to Reinforce Each Other's Value Chain
        ↓
Align Business Strategy and Business Model
        ↓
Structure Partnership Agreement
        ↓
Execute and Monitor the Partnership
```

---

### 1.4 Differences Between Sales, Marketing, and Business Development

This is one of the most critical distinctions in the course. The three functions are **not** synonymous — they differ in essence, orientation, scope, methodology, and metrics.

| Factor | Sales | Marketing | Business Development |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Essence** | Selling a product/service to a customer at a given point of time | Addressing the total market to develop and fulfil demand over a timeline | Creating partnerships between the company and stakeholders for developing business |
| **Orientation** | Transaction for mutual consideration | Customer experience for long-term association | Creating structures and relationships for long-term value creation |
| **Focus** | Company needs and targets | Customer needs and trends | Business synergy between company and customers |
| **Scope** | The individual sale | All activities including market research, pilot, sale, and service | Analysing mutual business needs and fulfilling them |
| **Methodology** | Induce customers to buy | Create a customer-centric experience | Develop a value proposition for strategic association |
| **Metrics** | Revenue and Profit | Brand value | Business growth |
| **Rule** | Caveat Emptor (buyer beware) | Caveat Vendor (seller beware) | Transparency |
| **Techniques** | 4 Ps (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) | 4 Vs (covered in Lecture 3) | 4 Cs (covered later) |
| **Objective** | Business growth | Sustainable business growth | Sustainable strategic business growth |

> **Key insight:** Sales is the narrowest in scope and most transactional. Marketing is broader and customer-centric. Business Development is the widest and most strategic, seeking to create lasting, mutually reinforcing relationships.

---

### 1.5 The Selling Process

The selling process is a fairly standardised, sequential process between a seller and a buyer in a marketplace:

```
Prospecting → Initial Contact → Qualifying Lead → Approaching Customer →
Product/Service Demonstration → Proposal and Clarification →
Sale Closing → Cash Collection → Repeat Contact
```

> **Judged by:** Its ability to generate sustainable revenues and profits from target groups.

---

### 1.6 The Strategic Marketing Process

A rigorous, customised, iterative process of discovery, development, and execution:

```
Research → Identify Trends → Define Market Needs → Segment the Market →
Create Product Concept → Prove the Market → Position the Company and the Product →
Align Business Strategy → Execute and Monitor the Marketing Plan
```

> **Key characteristic:** Internally and externally networked, aligned to business strategy for the firm's sustainable growth.

---

### 1.7 Role of Strategy in Business Development

> **Strategy (simply put):** A plan of action to achieve certain goals. Michael Porter popularised *competitive strategy* as the key concept — going beyond the sum of functional strategies.

**Strategy is a network of multiple plans:**
- Conglomerate strategy → Corporate strategy → Business strategy → Functional strategies (R&D, Marketing, Finance, HR, IT, Operations)

> **Important rule:** Business strategy leads to functional strategies — the functional strategies must not drive the overall corporate strategy. Strategy **drives** functions; functions do not define strategy.

---

### 1.8 The Domain of Business Development

BD integrates various strategies, processes, and tasks to drive a firm's business performance. It:
- Requires close interface with various other functions (strategy, marketing, sales).
- Is both **tactical** and **strategic** in nature.
- Operates along a Vision-Mission → Business Strategy → Functional Strategies hierarchy.
- Drives outcomes: Short-term performance → Long-term performance → Sustainable performance.

**Two Key Streams of Enquiry in BD:**

| Stream | Factors Involved |
|---|---|
| **Supply-side (Industry factors)** | Nature of industry, Competitors, Environmental factors, Firm factors, Product factors, Collaborators |
| **Demand-side (Customer factors)** | Demographics, Attitudes/Beliefs/Preferences, Behavioural response, Enablers |

---

### Important Concepts & Definitions

- **Business Development:** The anticipation, identification, and development of opportunities for enhancing business through transactions, collaborations, and future-spotting.
- **Selling Process:** Standardised transactional process aimed at generating revenue by completing individual sales.
- **Strategic Marketing Process:** A rigorous discovery-development-execution process aligned to business strategy.
- **Competitive Strategy (Porter):** Positioning a firm in its industry to achieve superior, sustainable competitive advantage.
- **Product-Market Grid (Ansoff):** A framework for identifying growth opportunities across existing/new products and markets.

### Key Insights and Takeaways

1. Business Development is **not** just selling or marketing — it is a strategic, partnership-building function.
2. The three allied functions — Sales, Marketing, and BD — must **work together** but each has a distinct role, scope, and tool kit.
3. Strategy is the **backbone** of BD. Every BD initiative must be traceable to a firm's vision, mission, and competitive strategy.
4. BD is both **reactive** (responding to mega-trends) and **proactive** (anticipating and shaping them).
5. The BD process is about fusing the **company's value chain** with the **customer's value chain** to create mutual, sustainable value.

### 2.1 What is Marketing?

> **Definition (AMA, 2017):** Marketing is the activity, set of institutions, and processes for creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners, and society at large.

**The Marketing Concept:**
- Planning and coordination of **all company activities** around the primary goal of satisfying customer needs.
- This is the most effective means to attain and **sustain competitive advantage**.

---

### 2.2 Market-Oriented Firms

A truly market-oriented firm exhibits four key characteristics:
1. **All departments and personnel** are focused on customer needs and competitive fulfilments.
2. **Develop and adapt** products and programmes to respond to changes in the environment.
3. **Conduct intense marketing research** prior to product development.
4. **Segment markets** and position appropriate products, with tailored programmes.

> **Example:** Samsung's Galaxy portfolio (S22 Series for premium, A Series for mid-range, M Series for value) exemplifies a market-oriented approach — different products for different segments, each tailored to needs.

---

### 2.3 Production Orientation vs. Marketing Orientation

| Business Activity | Production Orientation | Marketing Orientation |
|---|---|---|
| Product offering | Develop as per available competencies | Develop as per customer needs |
| Product range | Narrow | Broad |
| Pricing | Based on costs and margins | Based on perceived value |
| Market segments | Homogenous | Heterogenous |
| Product packaging | Product protection | Customer appeal + lifecycle value |
| Financing | Third party | Captive or networked |
| Promotion | Technical | Additionally, lifecycle value |

> **Insight:** Production orientation is **inside-out** (what we can make → sell it). Marketing orientation is **outside-in** (what customers need → build it).

---

### 2.4 Methods of Market Development

Based on the **Ansoff Growth Matrix**, businesses can deploy four growth modes:

| Market \ Product | Existing Products | New Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | Market Penetration (improve market share) | Product Development (expand through new products) |
| **New Markets** | Market Development (additional markets) | Diversification (new products in new markets) |

> Most businesses deploy a **hybrid** of the four modes. The balance among modes depends on the maturity of the business, competitive dynamics, and resource availability.

---

### 2.5 Categories of Marketing

Marketing can be categorised along two axes:

**Digital vs. Physical** and **Business-to-Consumer (B2C) vs. Business-to-Business (B2B):**

| Mode | B2C | B2B |
|---|---|---|
| **Digital** | Direct to Consumer (D2C) via company digital sites | Direct to Business (D2B) via customer portals, auction sites |
| **Physical** | Layered selling through wholesale and retail chains | Layered selling to businesses through intermediaries |

> Most modern businesses deploy a **hybrid** across these categories, especially post-pandemic, where digital touchpoints have proliferated.

---

### 2.6 Marketing Functionalities

Marketing is multi-faceted and supports business strategy through the following functionalities:
- **Market research** → feeds into product and brand strategy
- **Sales promotion** and **advertising** → directly drives revenues
- **Service and spare parts** → ensures customer retention
- **Operations-level marketing** (pricing, distribution) → ensures competitive access

Marketing links to **Sales** (direct revenue) and to **Business Strategy** (long-term positioning).

---

### 2.7 What is Business Development?

> **Definition:** Business Development is the activity, set of institutions, and processes for identifying medium and long-term business opportunities and creating relational frameworks that ensure **complementarity** and **mutual benefit** on a **sustainable basis**.

**Business Development Functionalities:**
1. Identify Macro-Markets (which country/segment is good for business)
2. Create Long-Term Networks (partnerships, alliances)
3. Create Joint Ventures
4. Manage Mergers and Acquisitions
5. Enhance Business through Alliances
6. Drives Revenues and Profits → aligned to Business Strategy

---

### 2.8 Roots and Role of Business Development Across Company Types

BD's role varies significantly by the type and maturity of the company:

**Established Single Business Companies:**
- Improve market share for current products in current markets → Strong sales and sales promotion capabilities
- Expand markets for current product line → Strong marketing capabilities
- Add new product variants → Strong sales and marketing capabilities
- Expand markets for new product variants → Strong strategic marketing capabilities

*Example:* ID Fresh Food — started with idli batter in Chennai/Bengaluru (2005), expanded to metro cities (2010), then to USA and Middle East (2021), adding new products along the way.

**Established Diversifying Business Companies:**
- Improve current product-market depth → Strong sales and marketing
- Create new product-market niches → Strong strategic marketing
- Spot emerging mega-trends → Business strategy + strategic marketing
- Innovate to create new mega-trends → Visionary, missionary, and strategic capabilities

*Example:* A major conglomerate diversifying from automobiles to electric vehicles, renewable power, and super-apps across decades.

**Start-ups in Initial Phase:**
- Launch test and pilot marketing → Strong technical selling skills
- Commercial launch and initial sale → Strong sales and marketing
- Achieve market breadth and depth → Strong sales and strategic marketing
- Establish sustainable competitive advantage → Strong strategic business skills

**Start-ups in Ramp-up Phase:**
- Improve the breadth and depth of the niche → Strong technical selling
- Create multiple product-market niches → Business strategy + strategic marketing
- Achieve market breadth and depth → Sales + sales promotion + strategic marketing
- Become a pan-market phenomenon → Strong strategic skills

*Example:* Zomato — from food reviews (2008), to food delivery (integrated payment and tracking), to pan-India delivery covering 500+ cities, aided by the COVID-19 pandemic.

---

### 2.9 Three Approaches to Business Development

| Approach | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Product Driven** | Product fulfils a novel need; product creates appeal for the customer | Digital Watch — the product itself creates demand |
| **Customer Driven** | Customer has choices; customer evaluates alternatives and drives demand | Automobiles — customer compares, chooses, and negotiates |
| **Context Driven** | A dynamic product-market match; adaptive BD in a changing environment | Mutual Funds — BD adapts to regulatory, economic, and investor sentiment contexts |

---

### 2.10 Synergy of Sales, Marketing, and Business Development

- **Sales:** Earns revenue in exchange for product or service.
- **Marketing:** Seeks customer clusters with homogeneity of needs.
- **Business Development:** Prospects and develops relationships with major customer groups for current or future products.

These three functions act as an **integrated system**: BD identifies and structures the opportunity, Marketing creates demand and awareness, and Sales converts the opportunity into revenue.

---

### Important Concepts & Definitions

- **AMA Definition of Marketing:** Activity and processes for creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging value for customers and society.
- **Marketing Concept:** Planning around satisfying customer needs as the most effective path to sustainable competitive advantage.
- **Ansoff Matrix:** A 2×2 framework for identifying business growth paths using existing/new products and markets.
- **BD Functionalities:** The five key activities of BD — identifying macro-markets, creating networks, forming JVs, managing M&As, and enhancing business through alliances.

### Key Insights and Takeaways

1. The shift from **production orientation to marketing orientation** is a cultural transformation, not just a process change.
2. BD is not just applicable to large firms — **start-ups** need BD as urgently as established multinationals, though the form of BD differs.
3. The **product-driven, customer-driven, and context-driven** approaches to BD reflect the importance of matching strategy to the nature of the market opportunity.
4. BD's role grows as a company moves from single-business to multi-business to diversified conglomerate — the **strategic stakes become higher**.
5. **Long-term networks** and **partnerships** are the ultimate output of effective business development.

### 3.1 Marketing Strategy

> **Definition:** Marketing strategy is the planning and execution of how a firm can exchange its products and services with individuals or organisations for a consideration. The consideration can be cash (for-profit firms) or goodwill/service motive (not-for-profit organisations).

**Everything can be marketed:** Goods, Services, Events, Experiences, Persons, Places, Properties, Entities, Information, Ideas.

The marketplace sits at the intersection of **The Firm (Supply)** and **The Customers (Demand)**, with **Fulfilment** as the function of the marketplace.

---

### 3.2 Marketplaces: Traditional and Digital

**Traditional Marketplaces:**
- Range from institutional platforms to self-entrepreneurial ones.
- Physical in nature — each format supplements, not eliminates, the earlier ones.
- Marketing makes the marketplace effective in fulfilling buyer-seller motivations.

**Digital Marketplaces:**
- Radical redefinition over the last two decades, aided by creative business models and digital technologies.
- Evolution: from 'aggregation and disintermediation' models → value-added solution models that enhance customer experience.
- Key insight: **Digital needs Physical.** Omnichannel integration is the current reality.

---

### 3.3 Types of Demand

A robust marketing strategy must identify and respond to different types of demand:

| Demand Type | Characteristics | Marketing Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Negative Demand** | Consumers dislike the product; may pay to avoid it | Reframe, educate, or redesign |
| **Non-existent Demand** | Consumers unaware of or disbelieving the need | Create awareness and relevance |
| **Latent Demand** | Consumers feel the need but cannot express it; think it can't be fulfilled | Innovate to unlock demand |
| **Declining Demand** | Consumers lose interest; the need becomes less relevant | Revitalize or pivot |
| **Irregular Demand** | Demand is seasonal, cyclical, or random | Smoothen demand through pricing, promotion |
| **Full Demand** | Consumers need and consume fully; encourage extensions | Maintain quality, innovate variants |
| **Overfull Demand** | More demand than supply; may mask mega-trends | Manage capacity, prioritise growth |
| **Deviant Demand** | Consumers chase incorrect prescriptions or 'sin products' | Ethical marketing, regulatory engagement |

> **Key insight:** There is no single type of demand. Effective marketers diagnose demand type before crafting strategy.

---

### 3.4 Core Marketing Concepts

Every marketer must have mastery over these fundamental concepts:
- Needs, Wants, and Demands
- Target Markets, Positioning, and Segmentation
- Offerings and Brands
- Marketing Channels
- Paid, Owned, and Earned Media
- Impressions and Engagement
- Value and Satisfaction
- Supply Chain
- Competition
- Cooperation and Collaboration
- Marketing Environment

---

### 3.5 Marketing Universe

Marketing universes are created based on exploration. They range from **local** to **global**, shaped by trade policies, supply chains, and competitive dynamics:

- Local companies serving local markets → National companies → Export-oriented companies → SEZ companies → Global companies with multidomestic emphasis → True global networking MNCs.
- Global supply chains based on outsourcing and offshoring have created new marketing universes.

> **Example:** The Fair Trade coffee supply chain — Ethiopian farmers → Fair Trade Cooperative → Alternative Trading Organisation → Retail channels in the developed world. This supply chain creates a global marketing universe for what was once a purely local agricultural product.

---

### 3.6 The Marketing Chain

In many product categories and markets, cooperatives and government intermediaries add layers to the supply chain. The actual supply chain is more complex than the basic seller-buyer model. **Resource sufficiency** and **conversion efficiency** determine the evolution of a supply chain.

---

### 3.7 Holistic Marketing

Holistic marketing recognises that marketing is a multi-dimensional, cross-functional enterprise:

```
                     HOLISTIC MARKETING
                           │
         ┌─────────────────┼─────────────────┐
   Internal Marketing   Integrated       Performance
   (all departments      Marketing       Marketing
    customer-centric)  (consistent       (revenue,
                        messaging)        ethics, 
                                         environment)
                                              │
                                    Relationship Marketing
                                   (customers, partners,
                                       employees)
```

> **Key insight:** The job of the marketing manager is most effective when *all employees* see marketing not as a narrow functional task, but as a company-wide ethos of customer centricity.

---

### 3.8 The 4 Ps of Marketing Mix

The foundational framework of marketing strategy, still highly relevant:

| P | Components |
|---|---|
| **Product** | Variety, Quality, Design, Features, Brand Name, Packaging, Sizes, Services, Warranties, Returns |
| **Price** | List Price, Discounts, Allowances, Payment Period, Credit Terms, Cashbacks, Loyalty Points |
| **Place** | Channels, Coverage, Assortments, Locations, Inventory, Transport |
| **Promotion** | Sales Promotion, Advertising, Sales Force, Public Relations, Direct Marketing |

---

### 3.9 The 4 Vs of New Age Marketing Mix

To supplement the traditional 4 Ps in the era of digital and sustainable commerce:

| 4 P | 4 V | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Product | **Validated Experience** | Customers need validated experience, not just functionality |
| Place | **Velocity** | Point of Sale is far more widespread than a dealer point; speed matters |
| Promotion | **Vendor's Responsibility** | In the circular economy, production and consumption must be responsible |
| Price | **Value over Lifetime** | Purchase cost + lifecycle operating cost − trade-in at NPV; total cost of ownership |

> **The 4 Vs are additions, not replacements.** Effective new-age marketing requires both the traditional 4 Ps and the 4 Vs.

---

### 3.10 Internal Marketing Environment

Internal environment is no longer purely internal — it is now a **networked ecosystem**:
- **The Firm:** Seeking growth, driving execution.
- **Financiers:** Return-seeking on business-related investments.
- **Suppliers:** Business-related supply-side partners.
- **Customers:** Need-driven; constitute the demographic environment.
- **Distributors and Dealers:** Multi-channel, alliance-based.

---

### 3.11 External Marketing Environment

External environment drives marketing opportunity and necessitates strategy adaptation:

| Factor | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Demographic** | Known, predictable; key driver of customer needs |
| **Economic** | Historical, aspirational, globally linked |
| **Politico-Legal** | Susceptible to change |
| **Socio-Cultural** | Native, national |
| **Technological** | Transformative |
| **Natural** | Resource-based |

> **Strategic marketing requires** thorough understanding of how the external environment will shape demand, and how the internal environment must be shaped to respond.

---

### 3.12 Conceptual Evolution of Marketing

Five phases of the evolution of marketing philosophy:

1. **The Production Concept** (Ford Model T era): Standardised products at the lowest costs through mass distribution. No market segmentation.
2. **Feature Diversification Concept** (GM Model): Diversify features and enhance quality. Beginning of market segmentation.
3. **The Selling Concept**: Firm needs to push itself to sell more to consumers. Enhanced sales will lead to better economics.
4. **The Marketing Concept**: Customer-oriented, builds loyalty to brand and company by serving the customer effectively.
5. **The Strategic Marketing Concept**: Predicts or influences mega-trends of the future; co-creates novel customer experiences; seamless strategic fit, internal and external.

> **The direction of evolution is toward greater customer centricity and strategic integration.**

---

### 3.13 Strategic Marketing

> **Definition:** Strategic marketing is the process of aligning the marketing function of an organisation with the long-term vision and mission of the organisation such that the firm secures competitive advantage in the short, medium, and long term.

| Time Horizon | Focus |
|---|---|
| **Short Term** | Current business; Revenue and Profit Maximisation |
| **Medium Term** | Emerging business; Market Share and Revenue Share |
| **Long Term** | Futuristic business; Value Creation |

Strategic Marketing must work alongside **Strategic Planning** to lead the company on a long-term sustainable growth path.

---

### 3.14 Culture of Customer Centricity

Customer centricity is not a marketing department initiative — it is an **organisational cultural anchor**. The inverted pyramid model puts **customers at the top**, followed by frontline people, middle management, and top management at the base.

Every department must exhibit customer-centric behaviour:
- **R&D:** Meet customers, benchmark competitors, seek feedback throughout development.
- **Manufacturing:** Visit customer plants, work toward zero-defect production, customise where possible.
- **Marketing:** Segment markets, measure satisfaction, continuously gather ideas for improvement.
- **Sales:** Directly fulfil customer needs, execute contracts, act as the translational arm of marketing.
- **Logistics:** Maintain high delivery standards, resolve problems promptly.
- **Finance:** Tailor financial packages to customers; make quick creditworthiness decisions.
- **Public Relations:** Send favorable news; act as internal customer advocate.

---

### Important Concepts & Definitions

- **Marketing Strategy:** Planning and execution of how a firm exchanges products/services for consideration.
- **Holistic Marketing:** A cross-functional, integrated approach treating marketing as a company-wide responsibility.
- **4 Ps:** The classical marketing mix: Product, Price, Place, Promotion.
- **4 Vs:** The new-age addition to 4 Ps: Validated Experience, Velocity, Vendor's Responsibility, Value over Lifetime.
- **Strategic Marketing:** Long-term alignment of marketing function with corporate vision and mission for competitive advantage.
- **Customer Centricity:** An organisational culture where every department views its actions through the lens of customer value.

### Key Insights and Takeaways

1. The marketplace is **both physical and digital** — and the two must be integrated, not siloed.
2. Understanding **demand types** is the first step in any effective marketing strategy; one approach does not fit all demand situations.
3. The 4 Ps remain foundational, but the **4 Vs** are critical additions in the era of digital, experiential, and sustainable commerce.
4. **Strategic marketing is future-oriented**: it is not just about managing current business but about predicting, shaping, and co-creating future markets.
5. **Customer centricity is an all-organisational ethos**, not just a marketing tagline — it must permeate every role, from R&D to Finance.

### 4.1 What is Strategy Formulation?

> **Strategy formulation** is a process that begins with setting the vision for the company, developing the strategy, and executing it as per a timeline.

**The three building blocks:**
- **Vision:** What the company aspires to become.
- **Strategy:** Where to compete, how to compete, what resources to deploy, and how to organise.
- **Execution:** Milestones, metrics, monitoring, and feedback.

> **Key principle:** Strategy is not one grand plan — it is a **network of multiple plans**. Conglomerate, corporate, business, and functional strategies must all align, while business strategy drives (not follows) functional strategies.

---

### 4.2 Strategy Hierarchy

```
Conglomerate Strategy
        │
   ┌────┴────┐
Company A  Company B  Company C
        │
  ┌─────┴─────┐
Business A1  Business A2
        │
  ┌────┼────┬────┬────┬────┐
 R&D  Ops  Mktg  Fin  HR  Others
```

In a diversified conglomerate, there are multiple layers of strategy. **Each layer has its own strategic mandate** while remaining aligned to the overall corporate or conglomerate strategy.

---

### 4.3 The Strategy Process

Strategy encompasses three interconnected and cyclical processes:

| Phase | Activities |
|---|---|
| **Planning** | Corporate planning, Division planning, Business planning, Product planning |
| **Implementing** | Organising, Resourcing, Implementing |
| **Controlling** | Measuring results, Evaluating results, Taking corrective action |

> **Important:** Strategic planning is usually **annual**, framed within a **five-year horizon**, with the first year forming the firm's budget. However, given dynamic environments, projects are evaluated continuously.

---

### 4.4 The Comprehensive Strategy Framework

```
EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT                    INTERNAL CAPABILITIES
(Society, Economy, Industry)            (Values, Culture, Structure, Resources)
         │                                          │
    Environmental Scanning ──────────────── Strategy Formulation
         │                                          │
    (Objectives + Strategies + Policies)            │
                      │                             │
              Strategy Implementation ──────────────┘
                      │
              Strategy Evaluation & Control
                      │
              Feedback/Learning: Make Corrections
```

**Key components of strategy:**
- **Vision and Mission:** The reason for existence; long-term purpose.
- **Objectives:** Results to accomplish.
- **Strategies:** Plans to achieve the mission and objectives.
- **Programmes, Tactics, and Budgets:** Detailed execution plans.
- **Policies and Procedures:** Decision-making guidelines.

---

### 4.5 Leaders Label Strategies

Leaders tend to view strategies as 'labelled process-outcomes.' Three broad categories:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Competitive Strategies** | Cost leadership, Differentiation, Niche positioning |
| **Corporate Strategies** | Integration, Diversification, Venturing, Merger, Acquisition, Globalisation |
| **Functional Strategies** | Market share building, Product portfolio development, Operational excellence |

> **Important caveat:** Whichever label is used, the **linkages with external environment and internal capabilities cannot be ignored.** Great leaders build internal capabilities that can proactively cope with environmental dynamics.

---

### 4.6 The Essential Business Platform

Every business — irrespective of type, scale, or industry — requires a robust platform of six interconnected elements:

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Vision** | A view of what the business/company should be over a 5–10 year frame |
| **Mission** | Why the company exists; its core purpose |
| **Values** | The ethical and cultural bedrock; great values stay for perpetuity |
| **Strategy** | A series of approaches and steps to convert vision and mission into reality |
| **Structure** | The organisational platform for executing the strategy |
| **Execution** | Undertaking various acts and activities to convert strategy into reality |

> **Important distinction:** Vision and mission stay for long (reviewed and reconfirmed periodically). Values stay for perpetuity. Strategy, structure, and execution constitute the **core triad** requiring continuous attention from leaders.

---

### 4.7 Vision and Mission Statements

**Examples from leading global pharma companies:**

| Company | Vision/Mission |
|---|---|
| **Pfizer** | "We apply science and our global resources to bring therapies to people that extend and significantly improve their lives." |
| **GlaxoSmithKline** | "To help people do more, feel better, live longer." |
| **Merck** | "We aspire to be the premier research-intensive biopharmaceutical company in the world." |
| **Novartis** | "To discover new ways to improve and extend people's lives." |
| **Amgen** | "To serve patients by transforming the promise of science and biotechnology into therapies that restore health or save lives." |
| **AstraZeneca** | "We push the boundaries of science to deliver life-changing medicines." |

**Observation:** Great vision and mission statements share common characteristics — they are **patient-centric**, **purpose-driven**, and **science-backed**. They inspire action at every level of the organisation.

---

### 4.8 Crafting a Mission Statement

A good mission statement has six major characteristics:

| Characteristic | Description |
|---|---|
| **Concise** | Limited goals, meaningful, reflects values, ensures integrity |
| **Values** | Embeds the firm's ethical commitments |
| **Clarity** | Defines the future, provides a timeline, guides the strategy |
| **Long Term** | Usually 5–10 years; sustainable |
| **Inspiring** | Motivates people, creates ownership |
| **Bridge** | Follows the vision, guides the strategy |

> **Historical examples:** Sony's "personal portable sound" vision led to the Walkman. Fred Smith's desire to deliver mail before 10:30 AM the next day led to FedEx. Apple's mission under Steve Jobs needs no introduction. *Strategic management gets significant inspirational guidance from a well-crafted mission.*

---

### 4.9 Strategy as a Set of Programmes — Hospira India Example

**Context:** Hospira India's strategy (2010–15) aimed to dominate the global sterile injectable market through an India-based manufacturing and R&D platform.

**Strategic Programmes Executed:**
1. Acquire Orchid's sterile antibiotics injectables business (Manufacturing and R&D).
2. Double acquired antibiotics Manufacturing and R&D capacity.
3. Establish biologics R&D facility.
4. Establish medical device R&D facility.
5. Establish non-antibiotics sterile injectables facility.
6. Acquire antibiotics API business (sterile and non-sterile).
7. Establish global centres of excellence in Medical Affairs and Regulatory Affairs.
8. Conduct product and technology transfers to Indian sites.
9. Rationalise global development and manufacturing footprint.
10. Enhance commitment to Oncology joint venture.

**Outcome:** Enhanced capacity, lower costs, and increased global business for Hospira.

> **Key lesson:** Strategy is implemented through a **programme portfolio**. Each programme must be tracked, and the portfolio must culminate in a competitive advantage. This requires disciplined **project management** aligned to strategic intent.

---

### 4.10 Structural Barriers

Structural barriers are raised by firms to create competitive moats. They can only be built through deliberate strategy and sustained execution:

| Barrier Type | How to Raise |
|---|---|
| Difficult-to-replicate products | Create patent estate proactively |
| Channel domination | Own/dominate channel access |
| Preferred supplier access | Ensure preferred supplier status |
| Purchase stickiness | Ensure customer lock-in |
| Scale and scope economies | Increase scale; develop scope |
| Brand equity | Build strong brand |
| Integration/diversification | Judiciously integrate or diversify |
| Facility sharing | Co-own, co-share facilities |
| EHS and ESG standards | Maintain hard-to-imitate standards |

---

### 4.11 Organisation Redesign for Transformation

Effective strategy execution often requires **organisational redesign**, especially during technology transitions. Six principles for redesign (EV example):

1. Review external and internal ecosystems; review roles and performance.
2. Identify new growth drivers at three levels: new departments, new hires, reskilling.
3. Determine current state and future state; prioritise transformation.
4. Determine new leadership requirements; on-board new leaders.
5. Create an organisation transformation team; make the CHRO the executor.
6. Roll out pilots in identified areas; extend organisation-wide after feedback.

**Three-Phase Execution (EV Transformation):**
- Phase 1: Acquire new core competencies.
- Phase 2: Develop new components and EVs.
- Phase 3: Full-scale commercial EV range.

---

### 4.12 Core Purpose Does Not Change

Even as operating models, technologies, and industry structures change dramatically, the **underlying core value — customer centricity — does not change.**

Examples of core purpose endurance across industry transformations:
- Digital personalisation in banking: core = financial empowerment.
- Autonomous/connected automobiles: core = better customer service.
- Aligning farm productivity and farmer security: core = farm modernisation.
- Pharmaceuticals: core = healthcare as a patient right.
- Renewables: core = concern for the planet.

> **The locus must change from firm-customer to product-customer experience.** Technology changes *how* you serve customers, but the fundamental obligation to serve them does not change.

---

### 4.13 International Organisation Structures

Global companies develop through stages:

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 | Domestic company |
| 2 | Domestic company with export division |
| 3 | Primarily domestic company with international division |
| 4 | Multinational corporation with multidomestic emphasis |
| 5 | Multinational corporation with global emphasis and networking |

---

### Important Concepts & Definitions

- **Strategy Formulation:** The process of setting vision, developing strategy, and planning for execution.
- **Strategy Hierarchy:** The cascading levels from conglomerate → corporate → business → functional.
- **Strategy Process:** The cyclical trilogy of planning → implementing → controlling.
- **Vision:** A long-term aspiration for what the company wants to become.
- **Mission:** The company's reason for existence and core purpose.
- **Structural Barriers:** Deliberate, firm-built moats that make it difficult for competitors to replicate success.
- **Competitive Strategy:** A plan to achieve superior, sustainable competitive advantage within an industry.

### Key Insights and Takeaways

1. **Strategy is a hierarchy, not a single plan.** Firms must align strategies at the conglomerate, corporate, business, and functional levels — but always led by the higher-order strategy.
2. **Vision and mission are not mere statements** — they are the northstar that guides every strategic decision and resource allocation.
3. **Execution is inseparable from strategy.** A brilliant strategy that is poorly executed is worthless. Strategy must be implemented through disciplined, measurable programmes.
4. **Structural barriers** are the most durable source of competitive advantage — they are hard to replicate and must be built proactively.
5. **Organisational redesign** is a strategic imperative, not an operational choice, during technology transitions. Companies that fail to redesign their organisations to match their new strategy will not succeed.

## Lecture 5: Business Development Cases

### Key Topics Covered
- A structured template for analysing BD journeys of companies
- BD case studies across diverse Indian industries and company types
- Common patterns and differentiators of BD success
- Lessons applicable across start-ups, private limited companies, and large listed conglomerates

---

### 5.1 A Structured Template for BD Analysis

Every company's business development journey can be understood through a **unified template** with the following components:

| Dimension | Components |
|---|---|
| **Product / Service** | Includes the underlying technology |
| **Place (Market)** | ToP (Top of Pyramid), MoP (Middle of Pyramid), BoP (Bottom of Pyramid) |
| **Customer** | Who the target customer is; income profile |
| **Business Core Strategies** | Key strategic choices |
| **Finance** | Ownership, organisational structure |
| **Operations** | Operational model |
| **Pricing** | Level vis-à-vis competition |
| **Promotion** | Means of communication and brand-building |

> **Key insight:** Each of these components varies based on the nature of the industry and the company's strategic position. The template allows for a structured comparison across diverse businesses.

---

### 5.2 Case Studies: BD Journeys of Indian Companies

#### Case 1: Nirma (Founded 1969, Karsanbhai Patel)
- **Product:** Detergent (indigenous product)
- **Customer:** Bottom of Pyramid (BoP) — price-sensitive Indian consumers
- **Pricing:** Highly affordable
- **Promotion:** Strong mass-media advertising
- **Strategy:** Competed against established MNCs through a mix of entrepreneurial passion, highly affordable pricing, and strong promotional campaigns.
- **Lesson:** A focused BoP strategy with the right product and price can disrupt established MNCs.

#### Case 2: Reliance Jio (Mission began 2007)
- **Product/Service:** 4G technology-based data and VoLTE services; Fibre to Home (broadband, TV, telephone)
- **Customer:** All segments (B2C + B2B)
- **Pricing:** Strategic market-capture pricing (initially free or near-free)
- **Promotion:** Intense, omnichannel
- **Strategy:** Completely integrated strategy — product, technology, infrastructure, people, service, and marketing driven by a singular vision of dominating Indian telecom.
- **Lesson:** Business development is driven by vision and mission. BD at this scale requires complete integration across all business dimensions.

#### Case 3: Zydus Wellness (Established 1994, Cadila Healthcare)
- **Product:** Sugar substitutes (Sugar Free Gold, Natura)
- **Customer:** Diabetics initially; health-conscious consumers broadly
- **Pricing:** Import-competitive
- **Promotion:** Advertising-driven
- **Strategy:** Strategic positioning as a Wellness FMCG company, with deep focus on an underserved niche (diabetics).
- **Lesson:** Category creation through niche focus can become a platform for broader wellness growth.

#### Case 4: Vini Cosmetics (Established 2010)
- **Product:** Deodorants (flagship: Fogg)
- **Customer:** BoP and mass market
- **Pricing:** Market-competitive
- **Promotion:** Visual media; celebrity-linked campaigns
- **Strategy:** Achieved 3× the market share of the closest MNC rival through aggressive BD and innovative advertising.
- **Lesson:** Strategic BD can help a new entrant achieve dominant market share against established MNC competitors.

#### Case 5: L&T Construction (Established 1946)
- **Product/Service:** Infrastructure construction for governments, PSUs, and large corporates
- **Customer:** Governments, public sector, private sector
- **Pricing:** Lower in price, higher in quality
- **Promotion:** Iconic landmark projects serve as the best promotion
- **Strategy:** BD through demonstrated excellence in large-scale project delivery; contracts won through competitive bidding supported by a reputation for execution.
- **Lesson:** In B2B and government markets, BD is built on **proven performance**, not advertising.

#### Case 6: The Happiest Minds (Established 2011, Ashok Soota)
- **Service:** IT services for global technology companies
- **Customer:** Big US technology companies
- **Pricing:** Similar to rivals, with better delivery as differentiator
- **Promotion:** Cross-selling and up-selling to existing relationships
- **Strategy:** BD driven by core relationships of the founding team, sustained and grown after transition from MindTree. Classic example of human capital-driven BD.
- **Lesson:** In professional services, **relationships are the strongest form of BD capital.** They transcend organisational changes.

#### Case 7: Paytm (Founded 2010)
- **Product:** Wallet payments, digital financial marketplace
- **Customer:** ToP, MoP, BoP — mass market
- **Pricing:** Presumably lower than fully-accounted cost (subsidised model for growth)
- **Promotion:** Intense, omnichannel
- **Strategy:** BD as a pioneer in India's fintech space; leveraged demonetisation as a mega-trend catalyst; built multiple verticals.
- **Lesson:** First-mover advantage in a rapidly growing market, if coupled with strong BD and investment backing, can create massive scale — but monetisation remains a challenge.

#### Case 8: Adani Green (Established 2015)
- **Product:** Renewable energy
- **Customer:** Government DISCOMS (distribution companies)
- **Pricing:** Set by governments and tenders
- **Promotion:** Contractual relationships with government agencies
- **Strategy:** Early mover in India's green energy transition; BD through securing government contracts and capacity building.
- **Lesson:** In infrastructure and energy, **government policy alignment is the core of BD strategy.** Regulatory relationships matter as much as technology.

#### Case 9: SBI Yono (Established 2017)
- **Product:** Super app for banking, investments, and e-commerce
- **Customer:** SBI's vast customer base (MoP and BoP)
- **Pricing:** Opaque due to captive customer base
- **Promotion:** SBI co-promotion
- **Strategy:** BD through digital channel development within an existing customer base; positioned as a youthful, digital-first sub-brand within a conservative PSU.
- **Lesson:** Established organisations can create new BD channels through **digital innovation**, even with a legacy customer base.

#### Case 10: Hyundai India (Established 1996)
- **Product:** Cars and Utility Vehicles
- **Pricing:** Competitive to Maruti
- **Promotion:** Celebrity-driven promotion
- **Strategy:** BD through strong product portfolio, competitive pricing, and aggressive marketing — achieved rapid brand-building in an unfamiliar market.
- **Lesson:** Sustained BD in a competitive market requires a **combination of product excellence, pricing intelligence, and effective promotion.**

#### Case 11: iD Fresh Food (Founded 2005)
- **Product:** All-natural, preservative-free batter; ready-to-cook food items
- **Customer:** MoP — urban middle class
- **Pricing:** Premium
- **Promotion:** PoS and advertisements
- **Strategy:** Category creation — identified the latent demand of busy urban professionals who want traditional food with convenience. BD through category marketing.
- **Lesson:** **Category creation** is one of the most powerful BD strategies. If you build the category, you own it.

#### Case 12: Mankind Pharma (Established 1995)
- **Product:** Multiple products in multiple therapies
- **Customer:** All channels — C&F, retail (ToP, MoP, BoP)
- **Pricing:** Low cost
- **Promotion:** Celebrity corporate brand building
- **Strategy:** Entered a fiercely competitive market, focused on domestic markets and multiple therapies, reached Top 10 status through aggressive BD, pricing, and promotion.
- **Lesson:** In a highly competitive industry, a **low-cost positioning** combined with wide therapeutic coverage and strong brand promotion can achieve significant scale.

#### Case 13: Apollo Hospitals (Founded 1983, Dr. Pratap Reddy)
- **Product/Service:** Multi-specialty healthcare with diagnostic and pharmacy services
- **Customer:** Patients (premium segment)
- **Pricing:** Premium
- **Promotion:** Word-of-mouth and outdoor advertising
- **Strategy:** BD through creating India's first corporate hospital, bringing Western healthcare practices, top-class doctors, and integrated services. Built a pan-India chain on the back of a trusted brand.
- **Lesson:** **Brand trust** is the most powerful BD tool in healthcare. Quality of service becomes self-reinforcing through word-of-mouth.

#### Case 14: Zomato (Founded 2008)
- **Service:** Online food ordering and delivery
- **Customer:** Individuals, families, and businesses
- **Pricing:** Discounted pricing
- **Promotion:** Digital, social media, and print
- **Strategy:** BD by developing a pan-India delivery force and forging relationships with restaurants and hotels; leveraged technology as USP. Evolved from food reviews to full-service food marketplace.
- **Lesson:** BD in platform businesses requires building **two-sided network effects** — acquiring both supply (restaurants) and demand (consumers) simultaneously and at scale.

### 5.3 Cross-Case Patterns and Lessons

| Pattern | Illustrative Examples |
|---|---|
| **Category Creation** | iD Fresh Food, Paytm, Zydus Wellness |
| **Low-Cost + Mass Distribution** | Nirma, Mankind Pharma |
| **Technology + Scale** | Reliance Jio, Zomato, SBI Yono |
| **Niche First, Then Expand** | Vini Cosmetics, Glenmark Pharma |
| **Relationship-Driven BD** | The Happiest Minds, L&T Construction |
| **Brand Trust as BD Capital** | Apollo Hospitals, GRT Jewellers, Hatsun |
| **Government/Policy Alignment** | Adani Green, L&T Construction |
| **First-Mover Advantage** | Paytm, Adani Green, iD Fresh |
| **Product-Driven BD** | Sudarshan Chemicals, Groz Engineering |

---

### Important Concepts & Definitions

- **BD Template:** A structured framework comprising product/service, market (ToP/MoP/BoP), customer, core strategies, finance, operations, pricing, and promotion.
- **ToP/MoP/BoP:** Top of Pyramid, Middle of Pyramid, Bottom of Pyramid — customer segmentation by income and purchasing power.
- **Category Creation:** A BD strategy where the firm creates an entirely new product-market category, giving it first-mover advantage and defining the competitive landscape.
- **Two-Sided Network Effects:** A value dynamic where a platform's value increases for each side (e.g., buyers and sellers) as the other side grows.

### Key Insights and Takeaways

1. **There is no single BD template** that works across all industries and company types — context determines strategy. However, the **structured framework** of product, market, customer, strategy, finance, operations, pricing, and promotion provides a powerful analytical lens.
2. **BD can be driven by product technology, customer insight, or context** — but the most successful companies are those that eventually master all three.
3. **Start-ups and established firms approach BD differently:** Start-ups focus on establishing a beachhead and achieving initial market breadth; established firms focus on deepening penetration, expanding product lines, and diversifying.
4. **Every BD strategy must eventually be accountable to financial outcomes** — revenues, profits, and market capitalisation.
5. **The Indian market offers a rich laboratory for BD insights**, spanning diverse industries, multiple market layers (ToP/MoP/BoP), and a variety of ownership structures (family businesses, start-ups, PSUs, MNC subsidiaries).
