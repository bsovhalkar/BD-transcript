### 1. Introduction
This lecture lays the conceptual foundation for understanding the relationship between a business and the industry it operates in. It clarifies how businesses are defined—both from product-oriented and market-oriented perspectives—and how industries evolve through specialization, diversification, and acquisition. It further illustrates how Indian companies, both legacy conglomerates and new-age startups, have built businesses from simple, focused origins into large diversified enterprises.

- Multiple ways of doing business within a single industry

### 3. Detailed Notes

#### 3.1 Definitions of Business and Industry

**Business** is the activity of developing, producing, and selling products or services to generate revenues and profits. A business spans the full value chain—from pre-design visualization to post-marketing support. Business can be conducted by a single person or a group of persons.

**Industry** is a group of companies conducting the same or similar businesses, characterized by the same or similar products, services, and activities. Similar industries grouped together form an **economic sector**. An economy comprises several sectors, each with multiple industries.

> *Example from the lecture:* In the transportation sector, road transport, commercial vehicle manufacture, passenger cars, and two-wheelers are distinct industrial segments but together form the transportation economic sector.

**Michael Porter's definition of industry:** "The group of firms producing products (including services) that are close substitutes of each other." The notion of substitutability is subject to practical interpretation.

#### 3.2 Product-Oriented vs. Market-Oriented Business Definitions

Philip Kotler pioneered the idea of defining business in market terms rather than product terms. In today's context, even broader market definitions are emerging.

| Company | Product Definition | Market Definition |
|---|---|---|
| Union Pacific Railroad | We run a railroad | We are a people-and-goods mover |
| Xerox | We make copy equipment | We help improve office productivity |
| Hess Corporation | We sell gasoline | We supply energy |
| Paramount Pictures | We make movies | We make entertainment |
| Encyclopaedia Britannica | We sell encyclopaedia online | We distribute information |
| Carrier | We make air-conditioners and furnaces | We provide climate control in the home |

**Key insight:** An automobile manufacturer viewing itself as a **mobility provider** rather than a vehicle manufacturer opens up infinite possibilities for market segmentation, product design, and business model construction. A broad market definition affords greater strategic flexibility.

#### 3.3 Multiple Ways of Doing Business in an Industry

Within any single industry, different entities operate in fundamentally different ways. Each represents a specialization, and specialization does not mean exclusivity.

**General conversion-industry value chain:**
- Raw Materials Manufacture → Materials Trading → Developing Products → Manufacturing Products → Marketing Products → Distributing Products → Retailing Products → Selling Products → Servicing Products

**Pharma industry example:** Multiple distinct business models coexist within one industry:
- Fully integrated global and India player
- API-focused global player
- Contract R&D for global clients
- Sterile eye drops for Russia & CIS
- Online pharmacy
- CDMO (Contract Development & Manufacturing Organization) for Big Pharma
- India pharmaceutical distribution
- R&D services for Indian pharma

#### 3.4 Businesses Built Over Simple Foundations

Most businesses begin from a single, specific activity—trading, broking, one product—and evolve organically:
- **Pharma:** Distributors evolved into manufacturers
- **Auto:** Component manufacturers diversified into multiple components
- **Chemicals:** Importers moved into import substitution
- **Finance:** Simple lenders became investment houses or commercial banks
- **Infrastructure:** Companies started with a single road-laying project and became majors

#### 3.7 Diversification Pathways

A 2×2 matrix illustrates the natural progression:

|  | One Product | Multiple Products |
|---|---|---|
| **One Customer** | Starting point | Product extension |
| **Multiple Customers** | Market expansion | Full diversification |

**Galaxy Surfactants Case:** Starting as a maker of a single toothpaste ingredient, through focused execution and co-creation with customers, the company moved to homecare, personal care, and global exports to marquee clients.

#### 3.8 Three Essential Proofs for the First Success

1. **Proof of Concept:** The product idea resonates with the market; co-creation with customers through design thinking
2. **Proof of Product:** The product is technically and operationally viable
3. **Proof of Business:** Revenues, profits, and sustainability are achieved

Innovation and management are the two critical drivers.

#### 3.9 The Business Development Pathway

A sequential, value-accretive pathway:
1. Choose and Prove a Product
2. Initiate the Sales Cycle
3. Generate Consistent Revenues and Profits → Create Cash Pool
4. Invest in New Markets & New Products
5. Prove New Products
6. Initiate Supplemental Sales Cycle
7. Enhance and Diversify Revenues and Profits

#### 3.10 Three Essential Requirements for Sustained Business Success

| Requirement | Components |
|---|---|
| **Core Competence** | Technology, Product, Management |
| **Niche** | Product niche, Market niche, Business niche |
| **Strategic Skill** | Vision, Focus, Passion |

Every industry requires characteristic investments and must earn above-expense profits to sustain. If returns fall below a benchmark rate, investments dry up. However, **returns are not the only factor**—strategic importance to the economy sustains investment even during below-average periods.

### 4. Important Insights & Takeaways
- A market-oriented definition of business is strategically superior to a product-oriented one—it opens up more opportunities and provides greater positioning flexibility.
- Specialization is the starting point, but openness and progressive diversification are the crux of sustained business development.
- Most great businesses—from conglomerates to startups—are built on a simple, single initial business driver. The lesson applies equally to new ventures entering new domains.
- Proof of Concept, Product, and Business are sequential, non-negotiable milestones before scaling.
- Acquisitions compress the timeline for business development: organic growth that takes years is delivered quickly through well-structured acquisitions.
- Asset-light or asset-heavy, businesses can be run independent of full ownership of the value chain.
- India's business development is becoming multifarious—with infrastructure-led conglomerates, BFSI players, digital-native firms, and manufacturing champions all coexisting.
- Variance in P/E ratios within the same industry underscores that firm-level competitive strategy, not just industry dynamics, determines financial performance.

### 5. Summary
Lecture 11 establishes the conceptual landscape of business and industry, demonstrating through Philip Kotler's framework that market-oriented definitions are more strategically powerful than product-oriented ones. It shows, through rich Indian examples spanning from Tata Steel to Zerodha, that most businesses grow from a single, focused origin into diversified enterprises through disciplined proof-of-concept, product, and business milestones. Acquisitions and collaborative strategies accelerate business development, while the variation in P/E ratios within an industry underscores that competitive strategy—not just sector membership—is the true determinant of firm-level returns.

## Lecture 12: Porter's Five Forces Theory
**Week 3 – Industry Structure and Company Analysis**
**Course:** Business Development: From Start to Scale (NPTEL – IIT Madras)
**Instructor:** Prof. C. Bhaktavatsala Rao, Department of Management Studies, IIT Madras

### 1. Introduction
This lecture presents Michael Porter's Five Forces Theory—one of the most elegant frameworks in management—as the lens through which industry structure and competitive dynamics are analyzed. The lecture goes beyond describing the framework to applying it rigorously across multiple Indian and global industries, including automobiles (established and new electric), IT, capital goods, FMCG, retail, semiconductors, oil, consumer electronics, and consumer durables. The theory is further connected to business development strategy.

### 2. Key Concepts
- Porter's Five Forces framework and its theoretical origins
- Primers for industry entry
- Entry barriers and Entry Deterrent Price
- Bargaining power of buyers
- Bargaining power of suppliers (sellers)
- Threat of substitute products (improvements, extensions, disruptions)
- Industry rivalry intensity
- Five Forces applied to: Established Auto, New (Electric) Auto, IT, Capital Goods, FMCG, Retail, Semiconductor, Oil, Consumer Electronics, Consumer Durables

### 3. Detailed Notes

#### 3.1 Theoretical Foundations of Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces Theory is drawn from **Structure-Conduct-Performance (SCP) theories** of industrial economics. It posits that five competitive forces operate in every industry, varying in intensity based on the nature of the industry and the state of the firm.

**The Five Forces:**
1. **Threat of New Entrants** — new competitors entering an attractive industry
2. **Bargaining Power of Buyers** — customers negotiating for lower prices, higher quality, or more features
3. **Threat of Substitute Products or Services** — alternative technologies/products making incumbents obsolete
4. **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** — vendors exerting power over the supply chain
5. **Intensity of Industry Rivalry** — the competitive dynamics among existing players

The firm exists within the industry, and the industry exists within a broader environment. All five forces act together to shape competitive intensity and profitability.

#### 3.2 Primers for Entry — Why Companies Enter Industries

Without periodic new entrants, any industry becomes staid, ossified, and technologically obsolete. Consumers lose access to the latest generation products and services. Key primers that trigger entry:

- **Growing economy and markets** — significant scope for upward growth
- **Inadequate capacity of incumbents** — demand-supply gap exists
- **Obsolete technology of incumbents** — late entrants can leverage technological leapfrogging
- **New proprietary technologies of entrants** — competitive edge (e.g., superior display or camera technology in smartphones)
- **Desire to diversify/grow** — existing firms entering new sub-sectors via subsidiaries or JVs
- **Government policies** — PLI schemes, investment incentives for new technologies
- **Overseas MNC and investor interest in India** — capital formation by multinationals seeking emerging market volumes

**Sequence:** Times of transformation → Bursts of new entrants → Responses from incumbents → Intense industry rivalry.

> *Example:* After Maruti Suzuki's early dominance in the Indian car market in the late 1980s, multiple new entrants followed, attracted by high returns and market growth.

Entry strategies are typically undertaken by **cash-rich or well-funded firms** (or PE-backed firms) to capture capacity and market presence. When entry is leveraged on superior technology, both the industry and consumers benefit.

#### 3.3 Entry Barriers

Entry barriers arise from structural and strategic factors, making it difficult or costly for new players to compete effectively. Incumbents can create even higher barriers singly or in combination.

| Industry | Primary Entry Barrier | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Automobiles | Economies of Scale | Minimum viable volume: 1,000 passenger cars; 50,000 utility vehicles; 2–3 lakh two-wheelers per annum |
| White Goods | Product Differentiation | First-time new features (e.g., Dyson's vacuum cleaner innovation) |
| Steel | Capital Requirements | High cost per unit of production; capital-intensive |
| FMCG | Switching Costs | Marketing expenditure percentage; brand loyalty; cost to switch a loyal user |
| Chemicals | Vendor/Channel Access | Incremental price % for incremental output; complex supply chains |
| IITs/IIMs | Established Unique Positions | Unique reasons—brand, historical reputation, societal trust |

**Capacity pre-emption** is a classic incumbent strategy to deter entry; new entrants counter it with superior technology, process, or business model.

#### 3.4 Entry Deterrent Price (Porter's Concept)

The **Entry Deterrent Price** is the exact price premium beyond which a customer will not entertain a new entrant's product. It is determined by the incumbent's competitive position vs. the new entrant's cost structure.

**Incumbent's price equation:**
- Old-generation technology (depreciated facility) → lower cost → competitive price + established market position advantage

**New entrant's price equation:**
- Superior technology + modern, costlier facility → higher cost → higher price, but customer's willingness to pay the premium is limited by the cost to switch a loyal user

Many new entrants have failed in India because they could not get the customer to pay the full price premium for their superior features over the incumbent's established position.

#### 3.5 Bargaining Power of Buyers

Buyers exert power when:
- Capacity exceeds demand (surplus supply)
- More products are available than the market requires
- Customers are price-sensitive or have specific, sophisticated preferences
- Buyers aggregate into institutional groups (procurement aggregation, bidding processes)
- Sellers are fragmented and reluctant to spend on promotion

**Conditions for a Buyers' Market:**
- Aggregation of buyers / institutional buyer groups
- Fragmented sellers
- Bidding processes (e.g., government procurement)
- Digital commerce enabling price comparison

**Business implication:** Higher bargaining power of buyers intensifies rivalry among sellers.

#### 3.6 Bargaining Power of Sellers (Suppliers)

Sellers/suppliers exert power when:
- Demand exceeds capacity (supply shortage)
- Severe shortages in key inputs exist
- Sellers have superior or unique products
- Purchasing power is unconstrained
- Buyers have no specific preferences or are learning to follow trends

**Conditions for a Sellers' Market:**
- Disaggregated buyers
- Scaling up of sellers
- Pre-launch expectations / order booking processes
- Strong brand or superior technology

**Business implication:** Higher bargaining power of sellers weakens buyers and can increase rivalry among buyers competing for scarce supply.

#### 3.7 Threat of Substitute Products

Industrial progress occurs through successive product innovations—when these are not just improvements but true substitutes, incumbents are threatened.

| Type | Example |
|---|---|
| **Improvements** | Mechanical watch replaced by quartz watch |
| **Extensions** | Solid soap replaced by liquid soap |
| **Disruptions** | Cameras by smartphones; regular watch by digital watch; sugar by sugar substitutes |

**Contemporary game-changing substitutes:**
- Human surgery → Robotic surgery
- ICE automobile → Electric automobile
- Thermal energy → Green energy
- Metal removal (machining) → Additive manufacturing
- Office work (in-person) → Google Meet / remote collaboration
- Currency coin → Crypto coin
- Bullet train → Hyperloop
- Tower telecom → Satellite telecom

#### 3.8 Business Development and the Five Forces (Combined Effects)

**Threat of New Entrants + Threat of Substitutes = Multiplied Rivalry**

> *Example:* The Indian automobile industry simultaneously faces new EV entrants (Ola, Ather, and 50+ others) AND substitute technologies (electric, hybrid, CNG, hydrogen). Together, these dramatically increase industry rivalry.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers + Bargaining Power of Buyers = Multiplied Network Pressure**

> *Example:* In automobiles, semiconductor shortages and commodity price escalations increase supplier power, while government transport undertakings and informed consumers increase buyer power.

#### 3.9 Five Forces Applied Across Industries

| Industry | New Entrants | Buyer Power | Substitute Threat | Supplier Power | Rivalry |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Established Auto (ICE)** | Low (EVs inevitable) | Moderate (EV option / postponement) | Low (emission norms stabilize ICE) | Very High (semiconductor shortage, logistics) | Moderate; driven by electrification investment |
| **New Auto (Electric)** | Very High (50+ players) | High (governments, tenders) | High (electric, hydrogen, fuel cells) | High (tech/quality leaders) | Very High |
| **IT/Infotech** | Moderate (mid-tier revival) | Very High (big client deal power) | High (AI, ML, AR/VR, metaverse specialisms) | High (niche tech specialists) | Very High (10 large + 20 mid + hundreds small) |
| **Capital Goods** | Low (capital-intensive) | Tech-driven (buyer picks technology leaders) | High (renewables shift) | Moderate → reducing with commoditization | Moderate; intensified by shift to renewables |
| **FMCG** | High (localized low-barrier) | Proportional to brand power | Moderate (supplementary in India) | Linked to brand power & sales investment | Very High (national + regional + local players) |
| **Retail** | High (e-commerce vs. brick-and-mortar) | Proportional to access/density | Low (e-commerce is enabler, not substitute) | Linked to store image & service | Disruptive (Covid-accelerated home delivery) |
| **Semiconductor** | Very High in India (Tata, Vedanta, global giants) | Limited (core to all products) | Low (SiC, GaN are new substitutes) | Very High (technology & capacity) | High concentration; high rivalry |
| **Oil / Energy** | Moderate (renewables entering) | Tech-supply-driven | Very High (ethanol, biodiesel, CNG, LNG, hydrogen, fuel cells, batteries) | Linked to tech and time-to-market | Intense; industry self-disrupting |
| **Consumer Electronics** | Low (Japan/Korea/China dominance) | Relatively low (growing markets) | High (IoT, AI transforming landscape) | Linked to IP and commercialization | High—digital tech as driver |
| **Consumer Durables** | Moderate (localized electro-mechanical entry) | Higher than electronics | Low (disruptive tech awaited) | Linked to trouble-free performance assurance | Moderate; functional orientation |

### 4. Important Insights & Takeaways
- Porter's Five Forces is not merely a diagnostic tool—it is a dynamic strategic planning framework that must be reapplied periodically as industry conditions evolve.
- New entrants benefit the consumer and the market even though they destabilize incumbents; thus, entry barriers, while protecting incumbents, can harm consumer welfare if excessive.
- The Entry Deterrent Price is a critical concept for business development managers and new entrant strategists—the customer's willingness to pay for newness is finite and calculable.
- Combined forces (entrants + substitutes; buyer power + supplier power) create far greater competitive pressure than individual forces acting in isolation.
- Industries under transformation (e.g., auto going electric) experience all five forces simultaneously and intensely—making dynamic competitive strategy indispensable.
- The bargaining power of buyers has structurally increased globally due to institutional bidding, digital commerce, and information asymmetry reduction.
- Business Development teams must scan the environment for nascent substitutes and nascent entrants long before they become material threats.

### 5. Summary
Porter's Five Forces Theory provides an elegant, industry-spanning analytical framework that explains why some industries are structurally more profitable than others, and why firms within the same industry perform differently. The lecture demonstrates through rich cross-industry examples that the theory is not static but must be dynamically applied—as shown in the nuanced contrast between the established ICE automobile industry and the emergent electric vehicle industry. Business Development has a pivotal role in translating Five Forces intelligence into actionable competitive strategies.

#### 3.5 Auto Component Industry

The Indian auto component industry has **emerged from the shadow of OEM industry**—it is now an industry in its own right, with export capability, and a hub of production for global automotive hubs.

**Current metrics:**
- 2.3% of India's GDP; USD 49 billion (FY20); 5 million jobs; 13% growth over 6 years

**Target:** USD 200 billion by 2026; exports to grow 5X in 10 years (to USD 80 billion by 2026)

**Transition challenge (ICE → EV):**
- ICE-specific components (cylinder heads, crankshafts, camshafts, valves) will see declining OEM demand
- If hydrogen dominates (trucks, buses), ICE architecture may be retained substantially
- Phase-out of established technologies and phase-in of new ones must be carefully planned with OEMs and technology developers

**Growth drivers:**
- Expanding R&D (currently 8% of India's R&D expenditure is in automotive)
- Becoming a global sourcing hub (ASEAN, Europe, Japan, Korea)
- Favourable trade policy (100% FDI, no import-export restrictions)
- PLI scheme (Rs 57,042 crore under Atmanirbhar Bharat 3.0)

**Export destinations:** USA, Germany, UK, Thailand, Italy

- Massive power capacity addition; nuclear expansion
- Key customers: SEBs, DISCOMs, Railways, Core Sector Companies

**BD imperatives for capital goods:** Understanding business transformations in B2B segments; going beyond the classical definition of capital goods to utilise new industrial opportunities (renewables, low-carbon, carbon capture).

#### 3.10 Business Development Imperatives by Industry (Summary)

| Industry | Key BD Imperative |
|---|---|
| Automobile | Retain ICE customers + network for EV infrastructure |
| Auto Components | New B2B marketing for ICE + EV; global B2C for ICE vehicle population |
| Tyres | Enhance global exports (insulated from ICE/EV transition) |
| Chemicals | Market Indian capacity to global sponsors migrating from China |
| Oil | Reorient marketing for diversification into multiple new energy domains |
| Capital Goods | Understand transformations in B2B segments; venture into new industrial domains |

#### 3.11 Case Study: Ather Energy vs. Ola Electric

**Ather Energy (Early Pioneer, IIT Madras incubated):**
- Founded Oct 2013 by IIT Madras alumni Tarun Mehta and Swapnil Jain
- Products: Ather 450X and 450 Plus (indigenously developed)
- Plant: Bengaluru; Hosur (from Jan 2021); 110,000 EV capacity; 120,000 Li-ion batteries
- 90% localised; LG Korea cells; South India vendors; 1–2 day stock system
- Funding: Tiger Global (USD 12 mn, 2015); Hero MotoCorp (Rs 205 crore, 2016); NIIF + Hero (USD 128 mn, 2022)
- Modest scale; first-generation venture; slow but deep developer

**Ola Electric (Late Follower, Unicorn):**
- Founded Feb 2017; aggressive entry from 2018
- Acquired Etergo (Dutch EV maker) in May 2020
- Products: Ola S1 and S1 Pro
- Plant: Krishnagiri, Tamil Nadu—500-acre site, 3,000+ robots; 10 million capacity (claimed world's largest scooter factory)
- Hypercharger Network: 4,000+ charging points by 2023; target 100,000 points across 400 cities
- Funding: Tiger Global, Matrix Partners, SoftBank (USD 250 mn); Rs 2,400 crore total investment plan
- April 2022: Recalled 1,441 units due to safety concerns (fire incidents)
- Plans for electric four-wheelers by 2023/24 and a global design hub in Bengaluru

**Strategy Comparison:**

| Dimension | Ather | Ola Electric |
|---|---|---|
| Scale | Modest (1 lakh units) | Ambitious (10 million units) |
| Generation | First-generation venture | Second-generation (late follower) |
| Development pace | Slow developer / Early pioneer | Fast developer / Late follower |
| Funding | Modest funding | Large founding rounds (Unicorn) |
| Key strength | Deep technology, quality | Scale, marketing power |
| Risk | Growth limited by funding | Unconventional scaling before market proof |

**Risks to EV Strategy (Ola case):**
1. **Greenfield + unconventional strategy:** Scaling before product proof
2. **Engineering and quality:** Harsh Indian conditions (weather, terrain)
3. **User education curve:** EVs require consumer education on charging, safety
4. **Talent conundrum:** Aggressive timelines cause talent churn
5. **Supply chain complexity:** Assembling is easy; sourcing parts at scale is challenging

**EV cost parity:** Expected by FY2026, when EV two-wheelers will achieve total cost of ownership equal to petrol equivalents—at that point, electric two-wheeler mix is projected to reach ~35%.

### 4. Important Insights & Takeaways
- India's industrial transformation is not incremental—it is structural, driven by technology shifts (EVs, renewables, digital) overlaid on the existing industrial base.
- The EV ecosystem is far larger than the vehicle itself—it includes batteries, charging infrastructure, swapping stations, new fuels, and eMaaS, each representing distinct business opportunities.
- The auto component industry is at a strategic inflection point: components for ICE engines face phase-out, but new components for EVs and the existing aftermarket population create simultaneous opportunities.
- The tyre industry, uniquely, is insulated from the ICE-to-EV transition—its BD imperative is export-led growth.
- BPCL's transformation from an oil marketer to a multi-energy company is a landmark example of a legacy company proactively disrupting its own business model.
- Ather's story demonstrates that patient, technology-first, quality-oriented building creates durable competitive advantage; Ola's story illustrates the power—and risk—of ambitious, capital-fuelled scaling.
- Business Development must link corporate and functional strategies to national missions, translating macro opportunities into firm-level product and market strategies.

### 5. Summary
Lecture 13 demonstrates, through data-rich analysis of six key Indian industries, that industrial transformation is the defining strategic context for business development in contemporary India. The automobile-to-EV, fossil fuel-to-renewable, and analog-to-digital transitions are not isolated events but systemic shifts requiring proactive, ecosystem-wide BD strategies. The Ather-Ola case study elegantly illustrates the contrast between a technology-first pioneer and an ambitious capital-fuelled unicorn—each instructive in different ways for strategy formulation in transforming industries.

## Lecture 14: Competitive Strategies
**Week 3 – Industry Structure and Company Analysis**
**Course:** Business Development: From Start to Scale (NPTEL – IIT Madras)
**Instructor:** Prof. C. Bhaktavatsala Rao, Department of Management Studies, IIT Madras

### 1. Introduction
Building on Porter's Five Forces framework introduced in Lecture 12, this lecture focuses on Porter's **Generic Competitive Strategies**—the second major contribution of Porter's theory of competitive strategy. It begins with a taxonomy of industry types from a strategic perspective, then examines how firms choose the industry to compete in, and ultimately presents the three generic strategies: **Cost Leadership**, **Differentiation**, and **Focus/Niche**. The lecture concludes with a comparison of these strategies on key competency and capability dimensions.

### 2. Key Concepts
- Types of industries from a strategic perspective (10 classifications)
- Central enquiry of strategy: choosing the industry to compete in
- Industry attractiveness factors (industry factors + company factors)
- The Essential Business Platform: Vision, Mission, Values, Strategy, Structure, Execution
- McKinsey's 7S Framework
- Competitive Advantage (Porter's definition)
- Cost Leadership strategy: components, mechanisms, and India examples
- Differentiation strategy: nature, examples, and interaction with Five Forces
- Focus / Niche strategy: dimensions and construction
- Comparison of the three generic strategies

### 3. Detailed Notes

#### 3.1 Types of Industries from a Strategic Perspective

While industries are generally classified by the nature of products or services, from a **strategic perspective**, industries are classified differently—by their value-creation trajectory and competitive dynamics.

| Industry Type | Characteristics | Indian Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Emerging** | Technologies evolving; boundless but distant potential | Biotechnology, Genetics, Regenerative Medicine, AI/ML/DL, AR/VR/MR, Robotics, Autonomy |
| **Growth** | Linked to demographic and population growth; quality of life improvement | Information Technology, Pharmaceuticals, Consumer Electronics, Renewables |
| **Mature** | Steady, consistent demand; essential products/services | Hotels & Hospitality, Retail, Movies, Automobiles, Oil & Gas |
| **Declining** | Technological obsolescence; superseded by new generation products | Printers & Copiers, Land Phones, Cable Programming, Tobacco |
| **Cyclical** | Move with economic cycles; sensitive to macro events | Steel, Commodities, Sugar, Agricultural Products |
| **National** | Confined to national boundaries; cannot self-transplant | Railways, Road Transportation, Utilities, Airports, Seaports |
| **Regional** | Linked to availability of basic feedstock | Iron ore; Steel and Engineering |
| **Fragmented** | Highly attractive → many entrants → highly fragmented | Retail, Food & Beverage, Local services |
| **Monopoly** | Early mover holds dominant, often 100%, market share | Rare earth extraction, mRNA vaccines, Google (search), Intel (early chips) |
| **Clustered** | Competitive advantage from geographic clustering | Chemicals, Aerospace, AI/ML startups |
| **Global** | Product technologies and marketing practices portable across nations | Consumer Goods, Financials, Consumer Electronics, Automobiles |

> **Key insight:** Industries evolve over time from one class to another. An emerging industry becomes a growth industry, then a mature industry, and—if not rejuvenated—a declining industry. Similarly, a highly attractive market may become fragmented as many players enter.

#### 3.2 Central Enquiry of Strategy: The Industry to Compete In

A firm's choice of **which industry to compete in** is the central strategic question. This choice is inextricably linked to:
- The breadth or narrowness of the industry definition
- The company's vision for the future
- Internal capabilities vs. industry attractiveness

**The Mobility Industry hierarchy (example):**
- **Commercial Vehicle Industry** (narrowest; manufacturing trucks/buses)
- **Road Transport Industry** (broader; business of transporting goods and passengers)
- **Transport Industry** (includes road, rail, waterways, shipping, airlines)
- **Mobility Industry** (broadest; any means of moving people and goods)

A commercial vehicle manufacturer that defines its industry narrowly (CV manufacturing) focuses on cost and quality of products. The same company defining itself as a **mobility company** sees opportunities in logistics platforms, fleet management, connected vehicles, multimodal logistics, and transport-as-a-service.

> *Example:* During COVID-19, road transport reconfigured itself to transport oxygen cylinders from manufacturers to hospitals—a powerful demonstration of broad industry definition enabling business agility.

#### 3.3 Industry Attractiveness

Industry attractiveness is determined by both external and internal factors:

**Industry Factors (External):**
1. Market size and growth rate
2. Pricing potential
3. Number of competitors
4. Entry barriers
5. Exit barriers
6. Mobility barriers
7. Technological obsolescence
8. Availability of inputs
9. Availability of distribution & sales channels
10. Availability of people skills

**Company Factors (Internal):**
1. Product line-up
2. Cost structure
3. Competitive advantage
4. Economies of scale and scope
5. Product-Market segmentation
6. Product lifecycle characteristics
7. Technological innovation
8. Access to suppliers
9. Access to distribution & sales channels
10. People competencies

#### 3.4 The Essential Business Platform

Every business and corporation requires a combination of:
- **Vision, Mission, and Values** — long-term orientation (stable, reviewed periodically; values endure permanently)
- **Strategy** — a series of approaches and steps to convert vision and mission into reality
- **Structure** — the organisational platform to execute strategy
- **Execution** — acts and activities through the structure to convert strategy into reality

**McKinsey's 7S Framework** complements this by adding: Staff, Skills, Style, and Shared Values as interlinked elements that must all be aligned for effective strategy execution.

#### 3.5 Competitive Advantage

**Porter's definition:** Competitive advantage is the leverage a company has over rivals; specifically, the ability to develop, produce, and deliver products or services with **lower cost** and **higher quality** than competitors. Competitive advantage must be **sustainable over time**.

Porter's three generic competitive strategies:
1. **Cost Leadership** — achieving lowest cost position in the industry
2. **Differentiation** — creating something unique, perceived as such industry-wide
3. **Focus / Niche** — concentrating on a particular target segment

#### 3.6 Cost Leadership Strategy

A low-cost position generally yields **above-average industry returns** even when multiple low-cost producers exist. However, if all incumbents are equally cost-efficient, cost leadership as a standalone strategy fails.

**Key mechanisms of cost leadership:**
| Dimension | Approach |
|---|---|
| Products | Lean design; value engineering |
| Production | Scale-efficient; standardised processes |
| Expenses | Tight cost controls; low overheads |
| Customers | Elimination of marginal accounts; low promotion |
| Quality | Fit-for-purpose; self-inspection |
| HR | Low fixed pay; performance-linked variable pay |

**Cost leadership reduces the minimum viable scale**, enabling market expansion.

**Industry transformation through cost leadership — Pharmaceutical Generics:**
- Generic entry between 2015–2017 drove median generic-to-brand price ratios down sharply as the number of generic producers increased
- Cost advantage from generics + insurer pressure to reduce reimbursements fuelled a huge increase in generic prescriptions in the US

**Business Development for Generic Products (India vs. USA):**

| Factor | US Innovator Product | US Generic Product | India Branded Generic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Product | Innovator Branded | Innovator's Brand, generic name | Generic Player's Brand Name |
| Winning Proposition | Proven clinical efficacy | Multi-year therapeutic experience | Reference to innovator's experience |
| Driver of Promotion | Innovator brand | Generic manufacturer's reputation | Generic manufacturer's reputation + brand |
| USP | Innovator science | Innovator reputation; low cost | Extremely low cost; packaging + company rep |
| End Game | Transition to generic | Spinoff to generic company | Continue brand as long as viable |

**Unique lesson:** India's pharma industry has created the **branded generic** model—a unique competitive proposition where Indian companies blend generic cost advantages with brand-building, creating a third category that doesn't exist in the US market.

#### 3.7 Differentiation Strategy

Differentiation is **the creation of something unique in the product or service that is perceived industry-wide and in the marketplace as unique**. A differentiated firm earns above-average returns by creating a defensible position against all five competitive forces.

**Classic differentiators:**
- Mercedes-Benz — premium reputation in automobiles
- The Hindu — trusted for factual reporting
- Bose — synonym for acoustic purity
- Pfizer — reputation for breakthrough innovations

**How Differentiation Addresses Each of the Five Forces:**

| Force | How Differentiation Helps |
|---|---|
| **Rivalry** | Brand loyalty + higher prices/margins despite competition |
| **New Entrants** | Differentiated position + customer loyalty = stronger entry barrier |
| **Substitutes** | Differentiation-driven loyalty dilutes the impact of substitutes |
| **Buyer Power** | Buyers find differentiated products inescapable; even price-sensitive buyers comply |
| **Supplier Power** | Differentiated products attract supplier association; higher margins absorb supplier demands |

#### 3.8 Thin Line Between Innovation and Differentiation

Many firms embark on the same development path simultaneously. The **first to get a patent and achieve first commercialization** is the innovator; others become differentiators.

| Domain | Innovator | Differentiators |
|---|---|---|
| Pharma | Merck's Sitagliptin (diabetes; DPP-IV inhibitor) | Multiple 'gliptin' DPP-IV drugs from other innovators |
| Electric Two-Wheeler | Ather Energy | Multiple manufacturers including Ola |
| Chips | Intel | AMD and others |

- Differentiators can **emerge as innovators** in their own right over time (e.g., Samsung)
- Scale supports differentiation as an entry barrier (e.g., Microsoft OS)
- Business Development must strike a balance between market share benefits and financial returns in differentiation play

**Differentiation × Price matrix (strategic options):**

| | **Low Differentiation** | **High Differentiation** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Price** | Tough to defend (need intense BD) | Best possible market share (BD tailwinds) |
| **Low Price** | Stuck in the middle; neither differentiator nor cost leader | Vulnerable to declining market share (existential question) |

#### 3.9 Focus / Niche Strategy

The Focus strategy limits the pursuit of strategic advantage to a **specific target segment only**. It can be internally driven (firm's capabilities) or externally oriented (market opportunity).

**Dimensions of focus:**
- **Product Line** — e.g., Aston Martin focusing exclusively on luxury sports cars
- **Buyer Group** — e.g., public utilities
- **Geographic Market** — e.g., UK as the sole market
- **Business Offering** — e.g., contract delivery
- **Service Line** — e.g., dedicated service line

**Strategic target framework:**

| | **Uniqueness Perceived by Customer** | **Low-Cost Position** |
|---|---|---|
| **Industry-wide** | Differentiation | Cost Leadership |
| **Particular Segment Only** | **Focus (Differentiation-based)** | **Focus (Cost-based)** |

**Driving Focus / Niche:**
A highly differentiated line-up with a low-cost position creates a **sustainable niche**—a strategy that achieves differentiated cost leadership (or cost-competitive differentiation) with greater resilience. Focus strategy conserves resources while earning above-average returns in the chosen segment.

#### 3.10 Comparison of the Three Generic Strategies

| Factor | Cost Leadership | Differentiation | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scale Requirements** | Cheap and plentiful capital for expansion | Scale less important than scope/features | Combination of scale and scope |
| **Core Competence** | Operational excellence | Functional creativity (design, mfg.) | Combination of product and price superiority |
| **Value Chain Mgmt.** | End-to-end low-cost approach | Networked, end-to-end | Functional specialization |
| **Management Approach** | Tight cost control | Creative flair | Best for chosen segment |
| **Role of Technology** | Limited to improvements | Basic research for first-time features | Functional specialization |
| **Talent Management** | High efficiency + performance incentives | Premium talent (attract, develop, retain) | Balanced optimal mix |

### 4. Important Insights & Takeaways
- The choice of industry to compete in is the most fundamental strategic decision—it determines the entire product-market-technology-organizational trajectory of the firm.
- Emerging industries offer boundless potential but are not immediately monetizable; growth industries offer the best near-to-medium-term return on investment.
- Cost leadership is not merely a pricing strategy—it is a systemic, organization-wide discipline spanning design, production, expense management, customer selection, and HR.
- India's branded generics model is a uniquely Indian competitive innovation—it combines the cost advantages of generics with brand-building, creating a third strategic archetype not found in Western markets.
- Differentiation is naturally defensive against all five competitive forces—brand loyalty counters rivalry, insulates against substitutes, reduces buyer power, attracts supplier partnership, and raises entry barriers.
- The "stuck in the middle" trap is deadly—companies that neither differentiate nor achieve cost leadership lose market share and face an existential threat.
- Focus strategy is appropriate when a firm cannot compete industry-wide on differentiation or cost, but can be decisively superior in a chosen segment—the key is selecting the right segment and executing flawlessly.

### 5. Summary
Lecture 14 completes the Porter strategic framework by presenting the three Generic Competitive Strategies—Cost Leadership, Differentiation, and Focus—as the action-oriented responses to the competitive forces analyzed in Lecture 12. Beginning with a rich taxonomy of industry types, the lecture demonstrates how the choice of industry definition shapes the entire strategic landscape. The pharma generics example for cost leadership and the branded generics model for differentiation are particularly instructive for Indian business strategists. The comparison table succinctly captures the organizational competencies required for each strategy, providing a practical tool for competitive strategy formulation.

## Lecture 15: Company Analysis
**Week 3 – Industry Structure and Company Analysis**

### 1. Introduction
This lecture shifts the lens from industry-level analysis to **firm-level analysis**, presenting a comprehensive framework for analyzing a company—and its competitors—across four major dimensions: Physical & Financial Performance, Vision/Mission/Strategy/Execution, Resources/Assets/Networks/Goodwill, and Leadership/Management/Competency. #### 3.7 Case Study: Sudarshan Chemicals — A Basic Company Analysis

**Company Introduction:**
- Leading colour and pigment manufacturer with 60+ years of experience
- Global, world-class colour and chemical solutions provider
- Mission: Value creation for stakeholders with a performance-driven, vibrant environment

**Financial Performance (FY 2021):**
- Total Sales: Rs 1,864 crore
- PBIT: Rs 190 crore
- Market Capitalisation: Rs 3,592.2 crore

**Strategic Position:**
- World's **4th largest producer of pigments**
- Widened base from classical pigments to high-performance pigments and cosmetics
- Exporting to Germany (where BASF and Clariant are headquartered)—established as significant global player by volume (Global 3rd)

**Products:**
- **Organic Pigments** — high-value, high-performance segment
- **Inorganic Pigments** — established, mature segment
- **Effect Pigments** — specialty; cosmetics and special effects
- **Nano Pigments** — emerging, high-growth segment

**BCG Matrix Analysis of Product Portfolio:**

| Quadrant | Product | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Star** | Organic Pigments | High growth, strong competitive position |
| **Cash Cow** | Inorganic Pigments | Established, stable revenue generator |
| **Question Mark** | Nano Pigments | High potential, developing position |
| **Underdog** | Effect Pigments | Emerging; requires investment |

**Porter's Five Forces Analysis of Sudarshan:**

| Force | Assessment | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Bargaining Power of Buyers** | High | Many players compete with local (often substandard) manufacturers in low-cost segment; international players available for high-value items |
| **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** | Moderate | Sudarshan's downward integration (making own raw materials) reduces supplier impact |
| **Threat of New Entrants** | Moderate | Established industry with wide network; tough environmental regulatory approvals deter casual entry |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | Moderate | Colour is integral to all products; Sudarshan active in all four pigment segments reduces substitution risk |
| **Industry Rivalry** | Very High | Entry into global markets (Germany); can buy out smaller global players with cash-rich status |

**Strategic Focus for Growth:**
- **Core focus:** Pigments — vertical expansion across all pigment segments
- **One product → multiple customers → global** business development model
- **Competitive advantages:** Product consistency & process control; 800+ tests; 4000+ specifications; NABL-certified labs; supply chain operational excellence; safety standards

**Investing for Growth (Strategic Capex):**
- Commissioning capex projects of ~Rs 600 crore — crucial for growth journey
- Building world-class operations with integrated transformation (productivity, quality, safety)
- Investment in solar power plant (19.495 MWh; cost savings ~Rs 201 lakh)

**Pathway Toward USD Billion Turnover:**
- **Indian Economy:** Opened up in 1990s; consistent growth; globalising rapidly
- **Domestic Market:** Per capita consumption of paints, inks very low vs. global peers — major growth potential
- **Global Market:** Expansion into global markets only in the last decade — major opportunity
- **Technology:** Currently a low risk-taker on new technology; reliance on overseas partners — area requiring strategic upgrade

#### 3.8 The Advanced Analysis Process

Once a preliminary analysis provides an overall appreciation of the company, a **detailed multi-factor analysis** across all four dimensions is conducted:
1. Physical and Financial Performance Metrics (multi-year, standalone + relative)
2. Strategic and Tactical Positioning Indicators
3. Strength and Resilience Factors (company + network)
4. Leadership and Competency Strengths

This produces a **continuously updated strategic intelligence database** that drives competitive strategy decisions.

### 4. Important Insights & Takeaways
- Company analysis and competitor analysis are not different exercises—they must be conducted on the same analytical platform to enable meaningful benchmarking.
- The four-dimension framework (Performance, Strategy/Execution, Resources/Goodwill, Leadership/Competency) provides a holistic view that goes far beyond the traditional financial analysis.
- Network strengths—vendor relationships, dealer relationships, government relationships, strategic alliances—are increasingly as important as internal company strengths in the post-COVID strategic environment.
- C.K. Prahalad's concept of core competencies is the foundation of leadership-driven competitive advantage—companies must systematically develop and protect their core competencies.
- Sudarshan Chemicals illustrates that a focused, specialty manufacturer can build a global competitive position starting from a single product category through disciplined quality, technology, and market expansion.
- The BCG matrix and Porter's Five Forces, when applied simultaneously to the same company, provide complementary insights: the BCG matrix reveals the portfolio dynamics while the Five Forces reveals the competitive pressure landscape.
- The path to the USD Billion Turnover for Sudarshan requires both market development (domestic under-penetration + global expansion) and technology development (reduced reliance on overseas partners).

### 5. Summary
Lecture 15 provides a rigorous, multi-dimensional framework for company and competitor analysis that is indispensable for business development practitioners. By decomposing analysis into performance, strategy, resources, and leadership dimensions—each with 10 specific indicators—the framework operationalizes what might otherwise be an intuitive or anecdotal assessment. The Sudarshan Chemicals case study demonstrates how this framework applies in practice: a specialty chemical company's BCG portfolio analysis, Five Forces competitive profile, strategic focus, and growth roadmap are synthesized into an integrated picture that informs specific BD and investment decisions. The lecture thus concludes Week 3 by anchoring industry-level analysis directly to firm-level strategic intelligence.
