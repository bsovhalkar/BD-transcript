## 3. Detailed Notes

### 3.1 Markets and Industries — The Fundamental Distinction

**Market (Definition):**  
An aggregation of individuals or organisations who are *interested in and willing to buy* a product or service to obtain benefits that fulfil a particular want or need, **and who have the resources to buy**.

> *"There are three-four underlying concepts — interest, willingness, a purpose (benefits), and resources. All four must be present for a market to exist."* — Prof. Rao

**Industry (Definition):**  
A group of companies that conduct the same or similar businesses, characterized by same or similar products, services, and activities (e.g., paint industry, cement industry, renewable energy industry).

**Key Principle:**  
- One market ↔ served by **multiple industries** (e.g., college students market served by pen, textbook, apparel, accessories industries)
- One industry ↔ serves **multiple markets** (e.g., power industry serves manufacturing, services, infrastructure, and residential sectors)

> *"Good strategists understand these differences to optimize the industry-market matrix for aggressive business development."*

---

### 3.2 Mobility Markets and Buyer Clusters

Markets fulfilling a single purpose are made up of **several buyer clusters with varied needs**. Within the macro "Mobility" market:
- **Personal Mobility** (2-wheelers, passenger cars)
- **Family Mobility** (3-wheelers, small buses)
- **Business Mobility** (staff transport vehicles)
- **Group Mobility** (large buses)
- **Off-Road Mobility** (4×4, construction vehicles)
- **Standby Mobility** (reserved fleet vehicles)

An industry develops **groups of products** to serve these multiple buyer clusters — it does not serve all clusters with a single product type.

---

### 3.3 The Seven Domains of Attractive Opportunities

*Source: John Mullins — "The New Business Road Test" (2010)*

This framework ensures markets are analysed by more than just size. It has **three domain types**:

| Domain Type | Macro Level | Micro Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Market Domains** | Market Attractiveness | Target Segment Benefits & Attractiveness |
| **Industry Domains** | Industry Attractiveness | Sustainable Advantage |
| **Team Domains** | Missions, Aspirations, Propensity for Risk | Ability to Execute on CSFs; Connectedness up, down & across Value Chain |

> **Key Insight:** The attractiveness of an industry must be assessed based on multiple factors — size alone is insufficient. A business opportunity is truly attractive only when market, industry, and team domains all align positively.

---

### 3.4 Key Market Influencers

Six macro environmental variables shape market breadth, depth, and evolution:

| Environment | Key Factors |
|---|---|
| **Demographic** | Age profile, income patterns, gender diversity, ethnicity |
| **Socio-Cultural** | Social values, mores, historical/religious influences, CSR |
| **Economic** | GDP, per capita income, savings, price indices, consumer spending, tax levels |
| **Regulatory** | Open/closed markets, duties & taxes, incentives, negative lists |
| **Technological** | Local technology competitiveness, dependence on external tech |
| **Natural** | Resource endowments, climatic conditions, environmental concerns |

> *"Nations with harsh cold climates have high demand for protective clothing and heating equipment. Those with hot humid environments have high demand for air conditioners."* — an example of Natural Environment shaping markets.

---

### 3.5 Industry Forces and Market Evolution — Indian Telecom Case Study

**Five Forces Analysis of Indian Telecom Industry (~2020):**

| Force | Score | Market Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Industry Rivalry** | Very High | Low tariffs; customer migration to cheapest provider |
| **Threat of New Entrants** | High | Many small players (1990s-2000s) + Reliance Jio (2010s) disrupted incumbents |
| **Supplier Power** | Very High | Government controls spectrum via auction; few telecom gear manufacturers |
| **Buyer Power** | High | Huge price elasticity; BoP customers adopted mobile phones en masse |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | Medium-High | 2G → 4G leap; non-adapters (e.g., Reliance Comm.) became uncompetitive |

**Lesson:** The Indian telecom model demonstrates how competitive forces shape industry structure and sustainability — aggressive pricing, massive capital requirements, and technological leaps combined to reduce the field to 2-3 major players.

---

### 3.6 Market Opportunity Analysis — Five Dimensions

1. **Need in the Market** — Progress and quality of life create demand for new products/services
2. **Industry Products & Customer Benefits** — What solutions provide benefits others do not?
3. **Growth Potential** — Target segment likely to grow, offering scope for same or similar products
4. **Adjacencies** — Currently served segment offers pathways into related segments (e.g., cement → readymix → construction)
5. **Readjustments** — New technologies will eventually transform market-industry configurations (e.g., 3D printing in construction or biomedical)

### 3.7 Understanding the Market — Four-Layer Model

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              POTENTIAL MARKET                       │
│   (Interest exists, but limited access / income)   │
│  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │
│  │           AVAILABLE MARKET                   │ │
│  │   (Compelling interest + access + income)    │ │
│  │  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ │
│  │  │         TARGET MARKET                  │ │ │
│  │  │  (Subset of Available Market the       │ │ │
│  │  │   company decides to pursue)           │ │ │
│  │  │  ┌────────────────────────────────┐   │ │ │
│  │  │  │     SECURED MARKET            │   │ │ │
│  │  │  │  (Actual buying customers)    │   │ │ │
│  │  │  └────────────────────────────────┘   │ │ │
│  │  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘ │ │
│  └───────────────────────────────────────────────┘ │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

> *"Reaching and penetrating the potential market fully is well-nigh impossible for any company, regardless of its resources."*

**Case — Hospira (Global Generic Pharma):**
- **Secured Market**: USA — 100+ generic drug filings, ~USD 3 Bn revenue
- **Target Market**: ~150 countries (EU, ME, Latin America, Canada, ANZ) — 1000+ dossiers filed
- **Available Market**: 150+ countries allowing importation with purchasing power
- **Potential Market**: All 195 countries worldwide

**Outcome:** Expanding market reach grew turnover from USD 3 Bn → USD 4 Bn (33% increase) simply by redefining market layers.

### 3.8 Structural Typology of Markets

**Two major types:**

**I. Government Markets:**
- Defence, Paramilitary, Police — *budgeted funds, bids, field trials, multi-tier approvals, guarantees*
- Public Sector Enterprises — *economic considerations tempered by public policy, regulated purchase preferences*
- Government Agencies/Institutions — *auctions, bids, Swiss challenges*

**II. Civilian Markets:**
- Private Sector Enterprises — *profit/service maximisation, procurement bids, cost savings*
- Social Organisations — *MSME support, green products, social motto, low margins*
- Individuals & Families — *lifestyle needs, discretionary/mandatory spend, emotional/esteem factors*

> *"You cannot sell soap in the same manner in individual markets as in governmental markets. Different markets require fundamentally different business development approaches."*

### 3.9 Pyramid of Markets

| Level | Unit Price | Unit Volume | Market Power Index | Strategic Importance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Top of Pyramid (ToP)** | High | Low | Low | Niche; prestige but limited mass impact |
| **Middle of Pyramid (MoP)** | Mid | Mid | Mid | Backbone of viable business |
| **Bottom of Pyramid (BoP)** | Low | High | High | Drives national economic strength |

> *"India reaching the top-5 of the global economic order reflects the index of market power at the BoP and MoP levels."*  
> The BoP is not a charity play — it is a genuine market opportunity requiring quality products at affordable prices.

### 3.10 Specificity of Markets

**Physical Markets:** Traditional markets, from *mandis* to modern malls (centuries-old evolution)  
**Virtual Markets:** Internet-based (Amazon, Flipkart) — grew exponentially over last two decades  
**Hybrid Markets:** Future physical-virtual markets powered by AI, AR/VR, MR

> *"There can be no virtual markets without the support of physical infrastructure — the supply and delivery chain interconnects both."*

**Specialized Market Types:**

| Market Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Financial | Stock markets (Zerodha), Bond markets, Forex, Crypto (Coinbase) |
| Commodity | Bullion, Metals, Energy, Agri |
| Housing | New homes, REITs (Brookfield), Foreclosed house markets |
| Auction | Livestock, Antiquities (Christie's), Used goods |
| Shadow | Semi-formal, unregulated, less regulated informal markets |

### 3.12 Three Marketing Approaches

| Factor | Transaction Marketing | Opportunity Marketing | Strategic Marketing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Approach** | Every sales call/click must generate sale | Every market opportunity must maximise sales | Create brand value; achieve sustainable customer mindshare |
| **Levers** | Salesforce & product promotion | Comparative promotion; competitor customer migration | Consumer feedback → better products/services |
| **CEO Involvement** | Monitor revenues/profits | Gaining over competitors | Positioning for long-term sustainability |
| **Other Functions** | Routine fulfilment | Offensive/opportunity support | Value chain optimisation |
| **Linkage with Strategy** | Sales budget | Market share strategy | Business & corporate strategy |
| **Measurement Metric** | Per capita sale/person/month | Growth rate; market share | Return on Investment |

> *"All three types of marketing are required by a company — they must coexist. Transaction + Opportunity + Strategic marketing together lead to Sustainable Competitive Advantage."*

## 5. Important Insights & Takeaways

- **One market ≠ one industry**: The one-to-many (and many-to-one) relationship between industries and markets is fundamental to strategy.
- **Market layers are expandable**: Companies grow by deliberately moving from secured → target → available market, as Hospira did (3→4 Bn USD).
- **BoP is not small**: High unit volumes compensate for low unit price. India's global rise is BoP-powered.
- **Virtual markets need physical infrastructure**: No e-commerce works without logistics, warehousing, and last-mile delivery.
- **Strategies must coexist**: A firm that only practices strategic marketing will starve today; one that only transacts will have no brand tomorrow.
- **Five Forces in telecom**: Technology-driven industries with high sunk costs and aggressive new entrants tend toward consolidation — a key lesson for business strategists.

## 3. Detailed Notes

### 3.1 Introduction to Specialty Chemicals

**Definition:** Specialty chemicals are used based on their **performance and function** — also called *effect chemicals* or *formulation chemicals*. They are customized, high-value, and require significant R&D.

**Indian Market Overview:**
- ~22% of total chemicals and petrochemicals industry in India
- Forecast CAGR: ~16% over coming years
- India ranks 14th in exports and 8th in imports of chemicals worldwide
- China is the current global leader in manufacturing and exports

**End-user Industries:** Food processing, textile, automotive, paints and coatings, real estate, agriculture, home and personal care

---

### 3.2 Specialty Chemicals vs. Commodity Chemicals — Six Differentiators

| Factor | Base/Commodity Chemicals | Specialty Chemicals |
|---|---|---|
| **Product Variety** | Standardised products | Customised products |
| **Base for Differentiation** | Pricing | R&D capability + Quality consistency |
| **Market Structure** | Large number of players | Fewer players |
| **Switching Cost** | Low (low-cost preference) | Moderate (long approval process) |
| **Core Growth Driver** | Cost leadership | Innovation-driven high R&D |
| **Economy of Scale** | Very significant | Limited (high variety, low volume) |

> *"This differentiation applies equally between generic pharma and innovative pharma, between a Skoda and a Mercedes-Benz. The principles are universal."* — Prof. Rao

**Pharmaceutical Analogy:**
- Innovative (patented) pharma ↔ Specialty chemicals: fewer players, high R&D, high switching cost, no generic substitution
- Generic pharma ↔ Commodity chemicals: many players, cost-driven, easy switching, high economies of scale

### 3.6 PEST Analysis of Indian Specialty Chemicals

**Political:**
- Make in India program attracting domestic and foreign investors
- "Housing for All by 2022" scheme driving construction chemical demand
- REIT and REMF introduction shifting construction to formal/regulated channels
- **China Plus One** strategy — India as alternative to Chinese chemical supply

**Economic:**
- 7th largest economy by nominal GDP; 3rd by PPP
- Low labour cost encourages foreign investments
- Construction sector = 8% of GDP; significant expected growth
- Rising disposable incomes, low interest rates, increasing urbanisation

**Social:**
- Rising urbanisation → demand for new infrastructure
- Increasing penetration of ready-mix concrete (currently only 2% of cement production used in ready-mix — huge scope)

**Technological:**
- Low-grade technology still used in construction → opportunity for specialty chemicals enabling better construction methods
- Growing popularity of ready-mix concrete → requires chemical admixtures

### 3.8 Competitive Strategies for the Indian Specialty Chemicals Industry

Four strategic options:

1. **Cost + Focus:** Indian market is nascent at 3% global share; low investment cost; focus on single product/cluster to achieve cost+focus advantage
2. **Differentiation + Focus:** One core product → penetrate market deeply → create awareness/knowledge (*SRF is the archetype — leader in refrigerant chemistry*)
3. **Differentiation:** Blue Ocean strategy — co-create with customers; innovation and R&D-driven growth
4. **Cost + Differentiation:** Become a global player; leverage low-cost manufacturing; scale up; provide differentiated solutions globally

### 3.10 Product-Market Development — Four Pillars

1. **Technology Adaptation:** Invest in new technology for cutting-edge products (better cohesive strength, flexibility, thermal stability, multi-usage capability)
2. **Sustainability, Society & Innovation:** Moisture-curable systems; environmental resistance (UV, corrosion, salt water); product innovation toward green chemistry
3. **Going Global:** Enter international markets through partnerships/alliances; understand global technological trends; eventually build standalone presence
4. **Strategic Acquisitions:** Reduce time-to-market; capture vacuums created by China+1 strategy; access technology transfers and best practices

### 3.14 Six Key Growth Factors + Constraints

3. Digitalization and Industry 4.0
4. Increasing M&A and investment activity
5. **China Plus One** strategy — India as preferred alternative

### 3.2 Case Study 1: Off-Highway Brakes Industry

Mobility

# Lecture 19: Demand Forecasting

## 1. Overview

This lecture addresses one of the most critical yet complex tasks in business development — **estimating and forecasting demand**. Prof. Rao begins with the sobering observation that even sophisticated economic models can fail to predict demand patterns, then systematically walks through 90 types of demand measurement, four demand archetypes, two organisational approaches (top-down vs. bottom-up), six forecasting techniques, the adoption process, fast-forward adoption examples, and finally the cautions, caveats, and keys to good forecasting practice. The lecture emphasises that demand forecasting is not a one-time budgeting exercise but a continuous, DNA-level organisational process.

## 2. Key Concepts

- **90 Types of Demand Measurement** (6 product levels × 5 space levels × 3 time periods)
- **Four Demand Types**: Surge, Cascading, Snowballing, Disrupted
- **Demand Estimation Approaches**: Macroeconomic → Industry → Company (cascading model)
- **Two Organisational Approaches**: Top-Down and Bottom-Up (pros and cons)
- **Six Demand Forecasting Techniques**: Statistical, Observation, Survey/Focus Groups, Analogy, Judgment, Test Marketing
- **Adoption Process** and five types of adopters
- **Fast-Forward Adoption** — technology mandates and regulatory push
- **Cautions and Caveats** in forecasting
- **Keys to Good Demand Forecasting** — Dos and Don'ts framework

---

## 3. Detailed Notes

### 3.1 The Complexity of Demand — 90 Types of Measurement

Companies can prepare up to **90 different types of demand estimates**:

| Dimension | Levels |
|---|---|
| **Product Level** (6) | Product Item → Product Form → Product Line → Company Sales → Industry Sales → All Sales |
| **Space Level** (5) | Customer → Territory → Region → Country (USA/India) → World |
| **Time Level** (3) | Short Run → Medium Run → Long Run |

**6 × 5 × 3 = 90 demand measurement types**

> *"The more global a company is and the more diverse its product range, the greater the number of market measurements required. And all of this must still contend with further customer characteristic-based segmentation."*

---

### 3.2 Demand Estimation — The Core Challenge

**The Problem:** Even the best models cannot always predict demand unravelling.

**Four Real-World Examples of Unpredictable Demand:**

| Example | Demand Type |
|---|---|
| **Ola Electric** — 1 lakh scooter orders in 24 hours at launch | Surge Demand |
| **Semiconductor Shortage** — millions of products (cars to white goods) affected for 2.5+ years | Cascading Demand |
| **China Power Crisis** — steep power cuts disrupting industrial production | Disrupted Demand |
| **India Demat Account Surge** — 14.1 million new accounts in 2020-21 (3× previous year) | Surge/Snowballing Demand |

> *"Availability of statistics is no assurance that there would be predictability from data. There is a need to integrate quantitative forecasting, qualitative forecasting, and scenario planning to arrive at proper demand estimation."*

**Four Demand Archetypes:**
1. **Surge Demand:** Sudden, sharp spike in demand (e.g., Ola Electric)
2. **Cascading Demand:** Ripple effects across supply chains (e.g., semiconductor shortage affecting auto to white goods)
3. **Snowballing Demand:** Growing demand momentum over time (e.g., Rakia product with 1-year waiting list)
4. **Disrupted Demand:** Sudden collapse or redirection of demand due to external shocks

---

### 3.3 Demand Estimation Approach — Three Sequential Layers

```
Layer 1: MACROECONOMIC FORECAST
    Variables: GDP, Inflation, Employment, Savings, Consumer Spending, Foreign Trade,
    Interest Rates, Business Investments, Govt. Expenditures, FDI, Sectoral Outputs
         ↓
Layer 2: INDUSTRY FORECAST
    Variables: Growth of specific industry, Related industries, Vendor industries,
    Dealer/Channel industries, Supply chain factors
         ↓
Layer 3: COMPANY FORECAST
    Variables: Past sales, Buyers' intentions, Buyers' purchasing power, Sales force
    forecasts, Expert opinions, Market research, Market tests
```

> *"Industry forecasts and company sales forecasts must be customised forecasts — not just lifted from macro data. Many agencies (government, multilateral, rating agencies) provide economic forecasts; firms should subscribe to or develop their own."*

---

### 3.4 Two Organisational Approaches to Demand Forecasting

#### Top-Down Approach

A central department (e.g., market research cell reporting to CMO) takes ownership.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Centrally driven → responsive to management | Bias due to proximity to top management |
| Access to macro-level data from multiple sources | Not sharp for segmental/sub-segmental estimation |
| Facilitates modelling; appropriate for gross demand | Risk of autocorrelation and false data correlations |

> *"Smaller companies should subscribe to syndicated macro studies; larger companies should build in-house strengths in economic and industrial interpretation."*

#### Bottom-Up Approach

Ownership is diffused to operating levels — sales force develops the first building blocks; data bubbles up through area → regional → general → national sales management hierarchy.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Organisationally inclusive; greater ownership | Biased by day-to-day happenings |
| Proximity to ground-level customers → more accurate for segments | Cannot perceive multiple data points and emerging trends |
| Effective for sub-segmental demand projection | Favours incrementalism over breakthrough thinking |

> *"Bottom-up forecasters tend to react too strongly to current disruptions and too exuberantly to current excitement — neither is good for forecasting."*

**The ideal approach:** A **combination** of top-down (strategic view) and bottom-up (operational granularity), triangulating to arrive at a balanced demand estimate.

---

### 3.5 Six Demand Forecasting Techniques

#### Technique 1: Statistical Methods

- Use past history, multiple regression, or time series analysis
- Best when demand triggers (positive or negative) are well-known

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Data-driven; objective | Cannot capture future triggers, especially disruptors |
| Reliable when assumptions are valid | Quality of output = quality of inputs + assumptions |

**Key:** Choosing the **right number and type of independent variables** to predict the dependent variable.

**Example — Ethanol Demand Forecasting Model:**
- *Dependent Variable:* Demand for ethanol from car industry
- *Independent Variables:* Ethanol production capacity, number of cars on road, average passenger km/car, flexi-fuel dispensing stations, petrol pricing, expected savings per passenger km, technology levels of different engines

---

#### Technique 2: Observation Method

Build forecasts from **real-time consumer behaviour** in the product-market space.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Based on actual behaviour (not stated preferences) | Behaviour can be random, structured, or misleading |
| AI can extend to every customer in a store | Not applicable to newly introduced products |
| Faster and cheaper than surveys | Needs well-defined product-market space |

**Examples of Observation:** Movie trailers, product samples to doctors, clinical trials, retail purchasing behaviour, trade exhibitions, targeted internet leaks, process observations (queuing), build-your-own design platforms, pre-orders

> *"Observation is going to be the trend of the future in demand estimation — very powerful as AI and sensors integrate with data analytics to understand even customer thoughts and intent."*

---

#### Technique 3: Surveys and Focus Groups

Select a representative *microcosm* of the target market and gather structured feedback.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Appropriate when product concept is well-defined | Chasm between what people say and what they do |
| Can elicit salesforce opinions | Respondents may not be knowledgeable on new concepts |
| Enhances customer connection as a side benefit | Firm may fail to deliver on concept customers voted for |

**Example — Mahindra KUV 100 Focus Survey:**
- Conducted at a Chennai mall; executives interacted with prospective buyers
- Caution: Focus group surveys can inadvertently **signal market strategy to competitors**

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Select the product-market space
2. Select focus group venue (high relevant customer flow)
3. Interact and seek feedback
4. Check representativeness through flow analysis

---

#### Technique 4: Analogy

Gain insights from **similar products or competitor experiences** — using past launches as proxies for future demand.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Useful when product prototypes aren't available | New product is never an exact clone of the analogue |
| Successes AND failures both offer learning | Could be misleading — past ≠ future |
| Good for conceptual thematic demand profiling | Judgmental and subjective |

**Examples of Analogy Working Well:**

| New Product | Analogy Used |
|---|---|
| **Spotify** (music streaming) | Apple had Napster; Spotify had Apple as proof-of-concept |
| **Sleeper Buses** | Aircraft seat ergonomics → Railway sleeper design |
| **Autonomous Mobility** | Guided factory floor transport → Fly-by-wire aircraft |
| **Danone Greek Yogurt variants** | Positive response to initial flavour variants → confidence to expand range |
| **COVID Oral Pill** | Tamiflu for influenza; other oral anti-retrovirals |
| **Ashok Leyland Bada Dost** | Success of "Dost" LCV + Tata Ace phenomenon as analogies |

> *"Conceptual analogy is useful in estimating demand profile for a new product making a similar thematic impact. However, launches may not be accompanied by identical conditions — the analogy cannot be taken at face value."*

---

#### Technique 5: Judgment

Expert opinion-based qualitative assessment. Particularly relevant when quantitative data is unavailable or unreliable. Prof. Rao notes this must be used with circumspection — it is valuable but not infallible.

---

#### Technique 6: Test Marketing (Experimental)

**Testing a fully finished product** to assess demand and fine-tune positioning before full launch.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Comes closest to actual demand determination | Too late for major demand-based redevelopment |
| Eliminates unanticipated bugs and special usage requirements | Costly and time-consuming |
| Fine-tunes product to market needs | Open to competitor stalking and counter-positioning |

**With Digital Marketing/AR/VR:** Test marketing activities can now be conducted much earlier and at much lower cost.

**Examples of Test Marketing:**
- Floor shift → Paddle shift (automotive steering)
- Bottle shampoo → Sachet shampoo
- Vials & syringes → Pre-filled syringes
- Bottle spirit → Alcohol swabs
- Straw punch → Flexi spout pouch
- SpaceX civilian flight (test for commercial space travel)
- Stand-up pouches vs. pillow pouches (FMCG packaging)

### 3.6 The Adoption Process

**Why It Matters:** Demand existing is not the same as demand being realised. **People must accept and adopt the product.**

**Five Types of Adopters (Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations):**

| Adopter Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Innovators** | Embrace the concept immediately; tech geeks; first movers |
| **Early Adopters** | Adopt after little research; opinion leaders |
| **Early Majority** | Join after seeing innovators and early adopters succeed |
| **Late Majority** | Skeptical; price-conscious; need proof of value |
| **Laggards** | Not interested in new stream; last or never adopt |

> *"Strategic marketing must aim at shortening the time span as much as possible. The shorter the adoption process, the faster demand is realised."*

**Role of Floor Promoters:** Companies post dedicated promoters in multi-brand retail stores to accelerate demand conversion — the promoter's job is to translate latent interest into actual purchase.

### 3.7 Fast-Forward Adoption Examples

| Product/Technology | Fast-Forward Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Touch Phones vs. QWERTY phones** | Design and UX superiority; rapid consumer migration |
| **Ubiquitous Sensors (IoT)** | Fastest adoption rate of any industrial/electronic product category |
| **Microsoft Duo 2 (Android OS)** | Intended fast-forward; but Samsung Fold was adopted faster — brand trust differential |
| **BS VI Emission Standards** | Regulatory mandate; direct jump from BS IV; ripple effect on biodiesel, ethanol, clean fuels |
| **Norway EV Mandate** | All passenger cars by 2025 to be zero-emission; government policy as adoption accelerator |
| **Renewable Energy in India** | Adani and Ambani leading; PSU refiners and energy companies following |

> *"Mandated withdrawal of older-generation technologies fast-forwards adoption and hence demand buildup. The rate of adoption is a function of the tangible, experienceable, real net benefits that a new product offers with ease and assurance."*

### 3.8 Cautions and Caveats in Demand Forecasting

| Bias / Error Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Delusional Optimism / Misplaced Pessimism** | Excitement or disappointment about a product skews forecasts |
| **Anchoring Bias** | Previous events of deep impact (failure) or recent halo (success) distort judgment |
| **Demand Misinterpretation** | Confusion between supply constraints (semiconductor shortage), channel stuffing, income effects, financing changes |
| **Personal/Professional Concerns** | Forecasters over-invest or under-invest based on fear of accountability |
| **Process Failures** | Gaps in communication in qualitative methods; wrong model assumptions in quantitative |

---

### 3.9 Keys to Good Demand Forecasting — Dos and Don'ts

| Factor | Don't | Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Use as one-time extended budgeting tool; confuse targets with forecasts | Recognise targets ≠ forecasts; use dynamically |
| **Time** | Do forecasts without adequate data collection or fieldwork | Revise forecasts continuously as more data becomes available |
| **Models** | Rely on just one methodology | Triangulate using multiple quantitative and qualitative methodologies |
| **Measurement** | Accept random expert opinions as ground truth | Focus on reducing forecast errors to continuously refine accuracy |
| **Risk** | Ignore probability, risk, and sensitivity | Simulate scenarios when forecasts are challenging |
| **Process** | Treat forecasting as optional or periodic | Integrate forecasting as a DNA strand of the company's continuous process |

> *"Prediction of the future with a fair degree of plausibility is not easy — even with tools and techniques. While taking demand forecasts as guideposts, firms can shape their products and services through strategic marketing to shape demand itself."*

## 5. Important Insights & Takeaways

- **Demand is never simple:** The 90-type matrix and four demand archetypes show that demand is multi-dimensional and context-specific.
- **Statistics ≠ predictability:** The Ola Electric, semiconductor shortage, and Demat surge examples prove that even world-class statistical models can fail to anticipate real-world demand.
- **Combine approaches:** No single technique suffices. Top-down + bottom-up + multiple forecasting techniques = most reliable estimate.
- **Adoption gap is real:** Demand existing on paper and demand being realised are different — the adoption process is the bridge, and companies must actively manage it.
- **Fast-forward adoption = regulatory or technology mandate:** When left to natural diffusion, adoption is slow. When mandated (BS VI, Norway EV), it accelerates dramatically.
- **Forecasting is a process, not an event:** It must be embedded in the organisational DNA — revised continuously, not prepared annually for the budget.
- **Scenario planning over single-point forecasting:** In volatile environments (geopolitical, monetary policy, pandemic), scenario planning is more valuable than one forecast number.

### 3.3 The Strategic Foundation for Competitor Analysis

**The Central Argument:**
- Best market analysis + best demand forecasting + best market segmentation + best consumer analysis = **NAUGHT** if competitors are not analysed properly

**Why?**
- Strategic marketing = a subset of competitive strategy
- It involves positioning such that capabilities distinguishing the firm from competitors create maximum value
- Sustainable competitive advantage is achieved on a strategic plane

```
Aspiration → [distinguish capabilities from competitors]
       ↓
Competencies → [build sustainable competitive advantage strategically]
       ↓
Execution → [realise revenues, profits, market share operationally]
```

> *"Smugness or alarm about competition must be replaced by systematic analysis."*

### 3.4 The Analytical Framework — Four Dimensions

| Dimension | What It Captures | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Future Goals** | What the competitor wants to achieve — at all levels of the organisation | Does the competitor want to improve share? What growth ambitions? |
| **Current Strategy** | How the business is currently competing | What is the evolution of the competitor's business? Where are they focusing? |
| **Capabilities** | Both strengths AND weaknesses | Where is the competitor strong or weak? |
| **Assumptions** | What the competitor believes about itself and the industry | What assumptions might create blind spots? |

**Competitor Response Profile — Five Key Questions:**
1. Does the competitor want to improve its market share?
2. What strategies will the competitor adopt?
3. Where is the competitor strong or weak?
4. What could trigger strong retaliation?
5. Are competitors acting singly or collectively?

> *"If competitors are heterogeneous in ownership, capabilities, and strategy, you must act on multiple fronts. If they are homogeneous, a singular strategy addressing the entire competitive block suffices."*

### 3.5 Multi-Focus Analysis of Competition — Five Categories

One of the most important conceptual contributions of this lecture:

| Category | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Category 1** | Firms that are **direct competitors** in the same industry | Metropolis vs. another diagnostics firm |
| **Category 2** | Firms **not in the industry** but who could overcome entry barriers easily | Four-wheeler company entering two-wheeler; Eicher (tractors) entering commercial vehicles |
| **Category 3** | Firms for whom there is **obvious synergy** from being in the industry | Oncology injectable company entering other sterile injectables |
| **Category 4** | Firms for whom competing is an **extension of corporate strategy** | Core business saturated → strategic adjacency into new industry |
| **Category 5** | **Customers or suppliers** who integrate backward or forward | Bharat Forge (auto components) → forward integration into defence products/artillery |

> *"Forecasting direct competitors is not easy. Forecasting potential competitors is even more difficult."*

**Illustrative Case — Medical Diagnostics Firm:**

| Competitor | Category | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Metropolis** | 1 — Direct | Same diagnostics industry |
| **Practo** | 1/2 — Direct/Near | Medical services company that could enter diagnostics |
| **Apollo Hospitals** | 2/3 — Near | End-to-end healthcare chain; diagnostics is a natural internal capability |
| **Lupin** (pharma) | 4 — Strategic Extension | Decided to venture into diagnostics as a corporate strategy extension |
| **Angstrom Biotech** | 5 — Tech Disruption | Biotech test kit company; lab-on-chip technology threatens traditional diagnostics |

### 3.6 Competitor Goals Hierarchy

Competitors have a hierarchy of goals tied to corporate and business structures:

| Goal Category | Key Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Physical & Financial** | Production, sales, capacity utilisation, breakeven point, growth rate, revenue, profit, RoI, EPS, D/E ratio, market cap |
| **Strategic** | Generic strategy (cost/differentiation/focus), portfolio, specialisation, diversification, integration, globalisation, IP generation, technological innovation |
| **Governance** | Compliance, ethics, values, corporate governance, risk management, EHS, ESG, business continuity |
| **Structural** | Board composition, committees, CEO & senior leadership team, corporate/business/organisation structure |
| **People** | CEO profile, CXO profiles, alignment, rewards, LTIPs, non-financial recognitions |
| **National** | Alignment with national goals (PLI, Atmanirbhar, Make in India), MSME support, startup development, privatisation |

### 3.7 Analysing Competitor Goals — Three-Step Process

**Step 1 — Identification:** Identify competitor goals AND achievements (not just goals)  
**Step 2 — Gap Analysis:** Measure the gap between competitor's goals and achievements vs. company's own gap  
**Step 3 — Sustainable Competitive Advantage:** Determine if the gap differential favours the company

> *"If the gap between competitors' goals and achievements is very wide compared to the narrow gap that you have, then you have a superior competitive advantage relative to the competition."*

**Useful Metrics:**
- Mean time between product introductions (product launch velocity)
- Goals accomplished vs. stated timelines
- Market share gain rate
- Revenue/profit growth vs. stated targets

---

### 3.8 Assumptions Framework

Competitors (and the firm itself) make assumptions about themselves and others. These assumptions can be about:

**Technical Assumptions:**
- Cost, Quality, Technology, Processes, Facilities

**Pipeline Assumptions:**
- Product pipeline, Regulatory approvals, Capacity, Markets, Finances

**Brand/Reputation Assumptions:**
- Brand value, Corporate reputation, ESG reputation, OSH reputation, Credit ratings

**Identification Assumptions:**
- Historical identification (years in market)
- Emotional identification (trust and relationship with society)
- Belief in future environment
- Belief in future of the industry
- Belief in future competition level

> *"Interestingly, firms tend to have beliefs about the future while taking quantitative present-day facts somewhat less seriously. Getting fully occupied with current realities but ignoring future evolutions is equally problematic — balance is required."*

---

### 3.9 Assessing Assumptions — Methodology

1. Collect all assumptions made by the company and competitors
2. **Filter against history of past realities** — Does the competitor make pompous announcements without delivery?
3. **Look for blind spots** — Where is the competitor (and the company) not seeing reality clearly?
4. Assess managerial competencies of both companies
5. Assess support factors (board quality, auditors, advisors, consultants)
6. Decide on probability of company's success vis-à-vis competitors

**Who is best positioned?** Marketing and business development functions — customer-connected and market-facing — are best positioned to provide inputs to senior leadership for assumption analysis.

### 3.10 Assumptions vs. Achievements Matrix (Original Framework by Prof. Rao)

```
                    Company's ACHIEVEMENTS vs. Competitor's
                      Worse              Better
                  ┌─────────────────┬──────────────────┐
Company's     W   │   Likely        │  Likely          │
ASSUMPTIONS   o   │   Endemic       │  Competitive     │
vs.           r   │   Competitive   │  Success         │
Competitor's  s   │   Disadvantage  │                  │
              e   ├─────────────────┼──────────────────┤
              B   │   Likely        │  Likely          │
              e   │   Competitive   │  SUSTAINABLE     │
              t   │   Failure       │  COMPETITIVE     │
              t   │                 │  ADVANTAGE       │
              e   └─────────────────┴──────────────────┘
              r
```

| Assumptions | Achievements | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Worse than competitor | Worse than competitor | **Endemic Competitive Disadvantage** |
| Better than competitor | Worse than competitor | **Likely Competitive Failure** |
| Worse than competitor | Better than competitor | **Likely Competitive Success** |
| Better than competitor | Better than competitor | **Likely Sustainable Competitive Advantage** |

> *"The central tendency would be that assumptions and achievements are the same between the company and the competitor — that means the entire industry is in an equilibrium state."*

### 3.11 Current Strategy and Rivalry

**How Generic Strategies Determine Competition Intensity:**

| Company Strategy | Competitor Strategy | Rivalry Type |
|---|---|---|
| Cost Leadership | Cost Leadership | **Intense Rivalry** — same positioning, direct head-on |
| Differentiation | Differentiation | **Intense Rivalry** — equally pitched capabilities |
| Differentiation | Cost Leadership | **Co-existent Rivalry** — operating in different spaces, can coexist |
| Differentiation or Cost Leadership | Focus/Niche | **Co-existent Rivalry** — different segments, coexistence feasible |

**Current Strategy Formula:**
```
Goals + Capabilities + Assumptions → STRATEGY → Achievements
```

> *"If the same generic strategy is adopted by both firms, the intensity of competition will be much higher than when different strategies are chosen — which is why you try to choose competitive strategies that are somewhat unique to your position."*

### 3.12 Reading Competitor Statements

Competitor announcements can be bold, provocative, and preemptive — they should not always be taken at face value.

**Example — Indian Energy Sector:**

| Company | Announcement | Strategic Reading |
|---|---|---|
| **Adani Group** (Gautam Adani) | USD 30 Bn investment across energy sector; thrust on renewables and hydrogen | Mega-scale commitment to energy infrastructure dominance |
| **Reliance Industries** (Mukesh Ambani) | USD 20 Bn in new energy (giga factories + partnerships) | Transition from petrochemicals to green energy leadership |
| **Ola Electric** (Bhavish Aggarwal) | Electric scooter plant at 10 million units/year | Aggressive scale for mass market EV dominance |
| **TVS Motor** (Sudarshan Venu) | Rs 1,000 Cr investment in 2-wheeler plant at 1,00,000 units/year | Selective, measured EV entry — strategic contrast to Ola |

**Also:** When Adani buys cement companies, it signals infrastructure ambitions. When Adani bids for 5G only for private networks, there may be more behind it than meets the eye. **Competitive statements must be read in their totality and between the lines.**

### 3.13 Functional Capabilities of Firm and Competitors

### 3.14 General Organisational Capabilities

Beyond functional capabilities, organisational capabilities distinguish firms:

| Capability Area | Key Indicators |
|---|---|
| **Structure** | Aligned to strategy; promotes efficiency & effectiveness; clear roles & responsibilities; flexibility to reconfigure |
| **Processes** | Clarity of purpose; process efficiency; coordination; resilience; integrity; code of conduct; compliance standards |
| **Drive (Leadership)** | CEO/CXO leadership qualities; ability to drive business; succession strength; depth of management; flexibility and adaptability |
| **Talent** | Skill/competency set; cultural moorings; unity of values; organisational age; learning & development; collaboration and networking |

> *"A company scoring very high on cultural integrity is likely to be a better performer than one that does not subscribe to good cultural anchors."*

### 3.15 Two Types of Competitive Moves

**Offensive Moves — Questions to Ask About Competitor:**
- How aggressive is the competitor to improve position?
- How would the competitor try to reach an improved position?
- How strong and persistent will the moves be?

**Defensive Moves — Questions to Ask:**
- How vulnerable is the competitor to the firm's actions?
- How would the competitor retaliate, and how strong will the retaliation be?
- How strong will the firm be in the face of retaliation?

> *"Every firm should understand the degrees of freedom available to both sides in the competitive game. For every competitive move, there will be a counter-competitive move."*

**Key Principle:** Do not make competitive moves that exceed your degrees of freedom. If you are not the lowest-cost producer, do not try to win a price war. If you have differentiation capability, use offensive moves in differentiation.

---

### 3.16 Three Types of Retaliators

| Type | Behaviour | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Operational Retaliators** | Match the move — reduce price if competitor reduces price | Competitive balance maintained; both companies stay level |
| **Strategic Retaliators** | Beyond operational matching; plot a broader strategic response | Potentially outmanoeuvre through superior strategy |
| **Non-Retaliators** | Do not respond; wait it out | May succeed (if brand equity is strong) or succumb (if no differentiation) |

> *"It is not necessary that firms should retaliate against every competitive move. Companies with sustainable brand equity can write out the storm. Those lacking differentiated capability that do not retaliate are likely to succumb to competitive pressures over time."*

---

### 3.17 Battlegrounds for Competition

| Battleground | Best Suited For | Logic |
|---|---|---|
| **Price** | Differentiated firms | Already have quality advantage; price makes them even more attractive |
| **Features** | Cost leaders | Adding features transforms commodity perception into differentiated positioning |
| **Service** | Both cost leaders and differentiators | Service network, spare parts availability, trained mechanics — works universally |
| **Combination** | Risk of dilution | Could diffuse impact; competitor cannot easily determine your positioning; but also makes firm vulnerable to random retaliation |

> *"When features and price are further supported by service, a firm gains competitive advantage on a holistic basis."*

**Critical Reminder:** The battleground chosen MUST be **strategically aligned with the company's overall generic strategy**. Do not choose features as a battleground if you don't have R&D capability to sustain it.

## 5. Important Insights & Takeaways

- **Competition is wider than you think:** The five-category model shows that suppliers, customers, companies from adjacent industries, and companies with strategic reasons — not just direct competitors — can threaten your business.
- **Assumptions are as important as capabilities:** The 2×2 matrix shows that wrong assumptions with right capabilities lead to competitive failure; right assumptions with right achievements = sustainable competitive advantage.
- **Brand equity absorbs competitive shocks:** Companies with strong brand equity can choose not to retaliate and still survive — this is a key strategic choice.
- **Goals come in hierarchies:** Understanding competitor goals across six dimensions (financial, strategic, governance, structural, people, national) gives a 360-degree view of competitive intent.
- **Generic strategy determines rivalry intensity:** Choosing a different strategy from the dominant competitor is a rational competitive move — it changes the nature of rivalry from intense to co-existent.
- **Read competitor statements carefully:** Announcements are strategic signals — they can be preemptive, directional, or deliberately ambiguous. Always read between the lines.
- **Competitor analysis and self-analysis use the same framework:** The standard by which you judge competitors should be the same standard you apply to yourself — intellectual honesty is a competitive virtue.

## 7. Definitions & Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Competitor Landscape** | The complete set of competitors including direct, indirect, potential, and strategic entrants |
| **Sustainable Competitive Advantage** | A unique position a firm maintains over competitors over a long period, based on capabilities competitors cannot easily replicate |
| **Competitive Response Profile** | A framework predicting how a competitor will respond to competitive moves based on goals, strategy, capabilities, and assumptions |
| **Endemic Competitive Disadvantage** | Innate weakness where both assumptions and achievements are worse than competitors |
| **Competitive Failure** | Scenario where assumptions are better than competitors but actual achievements are worse |
| **Non-Retaliator** | A competitor who does not respond to competitive moves, either by choice (brand equity strength) or inability |
| **Operational Retaliator** | Competitor who matches each competitive move symmetrically (e.g., price cut for price cut) |
| **Strategic Retaliator** | Competitor who responds to moves with a broader strategic repositioning |
| **Battleground** | The specific competitive dimension (price, features, service) on which a firm chooses to compete |
| **Co-existent Rivalry** | Competitors operating in different strategic positions, able to coexist without direct head-on competition |
| **Intense Rivalry** | Competitors using the same generic strategy, directly fighting for the same positioning and customers |
| **Forward Integration** | A supplier or component maker moving into the customer's business space (e.g., Bharat Forge → defence) |
| **Backward Integration** | A customer moving into the supplier's business space |
| **China+1 Strategy** | Global supply chain diversification away from China to alternative suppliers |
| **ESG** | Environmental, Social, and Governance — dimensions of responsible corporate conduct increasingly used for competitive assessment |
