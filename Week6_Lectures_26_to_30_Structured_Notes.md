# Lecture 26: Market and Market Descriptors

## 1. Overview
This lecture introduces the concept of **descriptors** as identifiers of markets, products, and customers, and builds the foundation for systematic market segmentation. It explores the STP (Segmentation–Targeting–Positioning) framework, types of market segmentation, the role of psychographics, behavioural influences, and the marketing funnel.

---

## 2. What is a Descriptor?
A **descriptor** is a characteristic of any entity — market, product, institution, or person — that uniquely identifies it and makes transactions and associations easier.

| Entity | Example Descriptor |
|--------|-------------------|
| Nation (India) | High-GDP, diversified economy |
| Society (India) | Multi-ethnic and multi-linguistic |
| Industry | Specialised industry verticals |
| Company | Business verticals of the company |
| Product | Product lines (e.g., high-end motorbikes) |
| Customer | Various demographic/psychographic segments |

**Key Insight:** Companies that segment customers, products, and markets based on descriptors achieve sharper focus, better product-market fit, and stronger brand identity.

---

## 3. Major Decisions in Marketing — Macro Market Selection

**Segmentation** is the sub-domain of marketing that translates the macro selection of a market into a target segment achieving the best fit with customer needs and fulfilling the growth requirements of the firm.

### Decision Hierarchy:
1. **Which Macro Market to Enter?**
   - National → Pan-Indian or Regional
   - International → Developed Markets or Regional Markets
2. **Which Segments within Each Market?**
   - Based on customer characteristics, product attributes, experience match, and mutual viability.

> Each market segment should have its own: **Marketing Strategy → Marketing Plan → Execution Programme → Monitoring Framework**

---

## 4. National vs. International Markets

| Feature | National Market | International Market |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Definition | Firm has all or substantial operations here | Firm does not have substantial operational presence |
| Also called | Home/Domestic market | Export market |
| Characteristics | Comprises several sub-markets (state/regional) | Each export market has distinct characteristics |

**Trend:** The distinction between national and international markets is blurring with increasing globalisation (e.g., the EU).

---

## 5. Indian Company Experience

### Developed Country Companies (typical path):
- Strongly established in the native market first → then aggressive international expansion.
- Example: Japanese auto companies dominated the Japanese market until the 1970s, then moved to the US and Europe.

### Indian Companies (typical path):
- Started in the native market at low scale (license raj era pre-1990) → only tentative export market entry.
- Indian companies rarely achieve >10% revenue from exports, unlike global peers who achieve 50%+.

### Exception — Export-Oriented Indian Industries from Inception:
| Industry | Characteristic |
|----------|---------------|
| **IT** | Served global clients from the 1980s; domestic market is only a small fraction of revenue |
| **Pharmaceuticals** | Some started as 100% Export Oriented Units (EOUs) despite availability of local markets |
| **Apparel** | Built businesses on contract manufacturing for global supply chains |
| **Jewellery** | Companies like Rajesh Exports built businesses wholly on jewellery exports |
| **Spices** | Natural home of spices; many companies are internationally oriented |
| **CDMO** | Companies like Suven focused 100% on contract development & manufacture for global clients |

---

## 6. Case Study: India's Gem & Jewellery (G&J) Sector Growth Strategy

- G&J contributed ~10% of India's export target in 2021–22.
- Trade agreements (India–UAE CEPA; India–Australia ECTA) are expected to unlock billions in additional trade.
- India–Australia bilateral G&J trade: USD 950 million; CEPA targets $10 billion+ in bullion and G&J trade.
- Investment: ₹200 crore in SEEPZ, Mumbai; ₹70 crore in a mega common facility centre for G&J.
- Enablers: AI, lab-grown diamonds, hybrid retail, new e-commerce policy for direct-to-consumer worldwide.
- **Vision:** India becoming the "Jeweller to the World," just as it is the "Pharmaceutical Capital of the World."

---

## 7. Logic for Global Segmentation

Three key drivers attract global companies to specific markets:

| Driver | India's Advantage |
|--------|------------------|
| **Skills** | Large, cost-competitive skill base beyond production/consumption potential |
| **Resources** | Abundant natural resources with further discovery potential |
| **Pricing** | Ability to be cost-competitive while also addressing a highly technology- and price-sensitive market |

**Framework:** Industry Structure → Business Strategy → Market Structure → Marketing Strategy

---

## 8. STP of Marketing

**STP** = Segmentation → Targeting → Positioning

| Element | Purpose | Outcome |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Segmentation** | Groups customers by homogeneous needs | Customer homogeneity → better product fit |
| **Targeting** | Selects the most appropriate segments | Sharper fulfilment of customer requirements |
| **Positioning** | Differentiates the brand for target customers | Ensures customer connect; builds brand loyalty |

Together, STP helps grow products into brands over time with customer loyalty and resilience against competition.

---

## 9. Segmenting Consumer Markets

A market segment = a group of customers sharing similar needs and wants.

> "No single factor can define a market segment completely. There will be many overlaps. What works for one product may not work for another. This makes market segmentation both challenging and exciting."

### Four Dimensions of Segmentation:

| Dimension | Examples |
|-----------|---------|
| **Geographic** | Nation, region, state, city, urban, rural |
| **Demographic** | Age, lifecycle, education, gender, income, generation, race, culture, religion |
| **Psychographic** | Personality traits, lifestyle, values |
| **Behavioural** | Knowledge and attitude towards products; purchase behaviour |

---

## 10. Indian Demography: Rural–Urban Markets

### Rural Demand Reality:
- Rural population: ~60%; Urban: ~40% (and rising).
- **Rural demand outpaced urban demand** during COVID (Q3 2020: rural +10.6% vs rest of India +4.4%).
- In FMCG, rural demand growth rate has been **twice** that of urban in certain quarters.
- Companies currently sell only 20–30% of their products in rural markets despite 60%+ rural population — **huge untapped potential**.
- Small towns accept **higher prices** due to limited competition and information imperfections.
- Electronics products show **higher growth and better pricing** in rural areas post-COVID.

### Two Imperatives for Marketers:
1. Ability to **penetrate rural markets** and cater to unmet rural demand.
2. Ability to **meet demand from increased urbanisation**.

> "Business development has to be responsive to all segments and responsible too."

### Urban Middle-Class Boom:
- 63 biggest cities contribute >25% of India's middle class and 29% of disposable income.
- Urban growth expected to contribute 73% of total population increase by 2036.
- 9 metros, 16 boom towns, 38 niche cities — each driving demand differently.
- Mumbai leads in super-rich households (173k → 269k); Surat shows the highest growth rate.

## 11. Psychography: The VALS Segmentation System

**VALS** = Values and Lifestyle Survey (developed 1978, Arnold Mitchell, SRI International)

Two main dimensions:
- **Primary Motivation** (horizontal): ideals, achievement, self-expression
- **Consumer Resources** (vertical): energy, self-confidence, innovativeness, etc.

| Cluster | Motivation | Description |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Thinkers & Believers** | Ideals | Knowledge-driven, principled |
| **Achievers & Strivers** | Achievement | Demonstrate success |
| **Experiencers & Makers** | Self-Expression | Social/physical activity |
| **Innovators** (top) | All | Leading edge of change, high resources |
| **Survivors** (bottom) | Status quo | Do not seek change; lowest resources |

## 12. Six Types of Customer Personas

| Persona | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Analytical** | Logical, data-based decisions; evaluates and validates |
| **Experimental** | Tuned to new and innovative; driven by high expectations |
| **Impulsive** | Influenced by instincts and appeals; not about need fulfillment |
| **Dynamic** | Always looking for something new; quick to switch |
| **Demanding** | Seeks high quality at low prices; cost leadership with differentiation |
| **Loyal** | Conformist; goes by past associations; passive decision-making |

> **Key Insight:** Even with a single product platform (e.g., a smartphone), different customer personas require different marketing approaches.

---

## 13. Behavioural Influencers

Actual consumer behaviour may deviate from an individual's geographic, demographic, and psychographic profile due to:

| Influencer | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| **Word of Mouth & User Reviews** | Individual decisions overwhelmed by influencers |
| **Herd Behaviour** | Intense channelling of social information for/against a decision |
| **Disruptive Trends** | Emergencies (COVID) or breakthrough technologies hold or accelerate decisions |
| **Waiting for Next Gen** | Prior knowledge of upcoming products delays purchase decisions |
| **Situational Factors** | Communities under stress due to natural calamities, crop losses, etc. |
| **Regulatory Directions** | Government directives on consumption patterns |

---

## 14. Digital Segmentation

In today's digitally savvy world, market segments express themselves through digital channels:
- **Radio, Television, Mobile, Movies, Virtual Events, Live Events, Social Media, Podcasts, Apps**

People carry 40+ apps on average; digital advertising ranges from explicit to stealth. All market segments find their descriptors in varying degrees through digital means.

## 15. Special Benefits Segmentation

Another dimension: segments defined by the **special benefits** sought:

| Segment Type | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **SME-Sector Products** | Consistent with entrepreneurial development (e.g., mini-ECG, mobile lab, metrology equipment) |
| **Socially Relevant Products** | Encourage social entrepreneurship (recyclable products, water filtration) |
| **Native and Ethnic Products** | Help rural and self-help groups (traditional sweets, carpets) |
| **Imported/Branded Products** | Pursuit of self-esteem; quality equated with high price (luxury/vanity goods) |
| **Low-Cost Functional Products** | Consistent with economic affordability; critical for BOP consumers |
| **Net Carbon-Neutral Products** | Contribute to climate change control; aligned with ESG goals |

## 16. Awareness–Usage Hierarchy

Any product's journey within the target market follows a cascade:

```
Target Market
   └── Unaware  |  Aware
                     └── Not Tried  |  Tried
                     (Neg/Neutral/Fav)   └── Rejected | Not Yet Repeated | Repeated
                                                             └── Light User → Regular User → Heavy User
                                                                 (Loyal to another brand / Switcher / Loyal to brand)
```

**Goal of marketing:** Move as much of the target market as possible towards **Heavy User / Brand Loyal** status.

---

## 17. Singular Descriptors and Multiple Segments

For sharper focus, markets can be defined by a single umbrella descriptor:

| Singular Descriptor | Sub-categories |
|--------------------|---------------|
| **Millennials** | Working professionals / Non-working dependents |
| **Working Class** | Public sector / Government / Private sector |
| **Students** | School/College / University/Higher education |
| **Homemakers** | Working / Non-working |
| **Entrepreneurs** | Start-ups / SMEs |
| **Senior Citizens** | Pension-dependent / Family-dependent |

**Matrix Approach:** Superimposing multiple segments (gender, income, urban/rural, global, linguistic) on singular descriptors creates a focused product development and marketing framework.

---

## 18. Marketing Funnel and the Sales & Marketing Task

The six-stage marketing funnel:

| Stage | Action | Type |
|-------|--------|------|
| **A – Awareness** | Create awareness among potential users | Initial Breakthrough |
| **B – User Trial** | Prompt potential users to try the product | Initial Breakthrough |
| **C – Initial Sale** | Achieve the first sale to a satisfied trial user | Initial Breakthrough |
| **D – Occasional User** | Make the initial buyer keep purchasing sporadically | Sustainable Strength |
| **E – Regular User** | Convert sporadic users to regular purchasers | Sustainable Strength |
| **F – Most Often Buyer** | Bring the user into the most-frequent buyer group | Sustainable Strength |

### Example — Brand Comparison (per 100 potential users):

| Stage | Brand 1 | Brand 2 |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Awareness | 90 (90%) | 80 (80%) |
| User Trial | 63 (70% of A) | 64 (80% of A) |
| Initial Sale | 50 (80% of B) | 51 (80% of B) |
| Occasional User | 40 (80% of C) | 43 (85% of C) |
| Regular User | 32 (80% of D) | 39 (90% of D) |
| Most Often Buyer | **26 (80% of E)** | **37 (95% of E)** |

**Key Lesson:** Brand 2 achieves higher final sales despite lower initial awareness — **conversion efficiency at every stage matters more than initial reach**.

---

## 19. Key Takeaways — Lecture 26

1. Descriptors are the building blocks of market identification and segmentation.
2. STP (Segmentation–Targeting–Positioning) is the core framework of focused marketing.
3. Rural India is a massive under-penetrated market with significant demand potential.
4. No single factor completely defines a segment — multiple overlapping criteria are needed.
5. The marketing funnel is a tool for continuous customer courting, not a one-time campaign.
6. Psychographic and behavioural dimensions are the most powerful yet hardest to capture.

# Lecture 27: Market and Product Segmentation

## 1. Overview
This lecture examines the important distinction between **product segmentation** and **market segmentation**, explores segmentation across income levels, applies Porter's Five Forces to market segments, and provides comprehensive coverage of demographic, geographic, psychographic, and behavioural descriptors.

---

## 2. Confusing Product Segmentation with Market Segmentation

A common pitfall: product attributes are used as proxies for market segments without understanding the underlying usage and application.

### Commercial Vehicle Example:

| Product Classification (GVW) | Product Segment Name | True Market Segment |
|-----------------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 3–4 Tonne | SCV (Small Commercial Vehicle) | **Last-mile delivery** |
| 5–8 Tonne | LCV (Light Commercial Vehicle) | **Intra-city, short-haul** |
| 9–12 Tonne | ICV (Intermediate Commercial Vehicle) | **City–suburb, medium-haul** |
| 14–28 Tonne | MCV (Medium Commercial Vehicle) | **Inter-city, long-haul** |
| 30–45 Tonne | HCV (Heavy Commercial Vehicle) | **National haul** |
| Tippers, Dumpers, Trailers | Product nature descriptor | **Construction, coal, mining** |

> **Key Insight:** Products serve as *surrogates* of market segments, but the **underlying application and usage** must never be lost sight of. A company achieving the lowest unladen weight can move a 3-4T truck into the LCV market segment — a source of competitive advantage.

---

## 3. Market Segmentation and Business Strategy

### Airlines Case Study:
- Traditional segmentation: **First Class** (super-rich/CEOs) → **Business Class** (corporates) → **Economy Class** (everyone else).
- COVID-19 disruption: First-class travellers' desire for exclusivity (door-to-door) + adversity of COVID created demand for **private/chartered jets**.
- **Business strategy**, not just marketing strategy, had to be invoked to serve segment customers.

> **Principle:** Market segmentation must be reviewed periodically from the perspective of **business strategy**, not just marketing strategy.

---

## 4. Segmenting by Income Level

### 4a. Low-Income Market Segments — Frugal Innovation
C.K. Prahalad's *The Fortune at the Bottom of the Pyramid* opened eyes to the aggregate purchasing power of those earning <$2/day.

| Innovation | Pioneer |
|-----------|--------|
| **Velvette Shampoo Sachet** (smallest pack) | S.A. Rajkumar (later Cavinkare) |
| **Air Deccan** (low-cost airline) | Capt. C.R. Gopinath |
| **Mittikool** (clay fridge for areas without electricity) | Rural start-up |
| **Corosure** (low-cost RT-PCR kit, developed in 3 months) | IIT Delhi |
| **Jaipur Foot** (low-cost, versatile prosthetic) | Dr. P.K. Sethi |
| **Affordable ECG device** | GE India team |

> **Strategic Imperative:** Marketing must explicitly address low-income categories with **frugal innovation**.

### 4b. Middle-Income Segments — Techno-Social Revolution
- Shashi Tharoor argues the middle class is "more sociological than logical" — it represents a **new mindset** and spending pattern (less on food/housing, more on education, healthcare, gadgets).
- Characteristics: Heterogeneous needs, homogeneous drive for benefits.
- **Strategy:** Micro-segmentation — Indian businesses have taken this to a new level (e.g., 100 ml vs. 1000 ml hair oil).

### 4c. High-Income Segments — Lavish Spin on Tradition
- HNIs and uber-rich are unabashed brand-conscious spenders: destination weddings, bespoke brands (Dior, Versace, Hermès), luxury automobiles (Rolls-Royce), HNI investing, millionaire homes.
- Capital productivity of HNI spending is unfavourable in resource-scarce economies but has a top-down trickle-down effect.

---

## 5. Challenge of Defining Market Segments

Three core tasks:
1. **Identify Homogeneity within Heterogeneity** — Find relatively homogeneous clusters among heterogeneous customers.
2. **Specify Segment-Defining Criteria** — Clear profiling of customers' wants and needs.
3. **Determine Potential** — Current and prospective size of the market segment.

> Understanding a market segment is akin to understanding an industry — **Porter's Five Forces** applies.

---

## 6. Five-Forces Model for Market Segments

| Force | Market Segment Application |
|-------|--------------------------|
| **Threat of New Entrants** | High attractiveness invites new players |
| **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** | Reduces attractiveness if suppliers have high power |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | Substitutes reduce segment attractiveness |
| **Bargaining Power of Buyers** | High buyer power compresses margins |
| **Intensity of Rivalry** | Excessive entrants/substitutes + strong buyer/supplier power intensifies rivalry |

### Practical Implication (Pharmaceutical MR Example):
- A medical representative must cover both hospital pharmacies AND local chemists near patients' homes.
- The customer location is not just where the prescription is written — it is where the product is ultimately purchased.
- Solution: **Saturation coverage** — cover both the source (hospital catchment area) and the effect area (patient home).

## 7. Demographic Descriptors

| Factor | Segmentation Implication |
|--------|-------------------------|
| **Age** | Youngsters buy for own needs; elders buy for themselves and families |
| **Gender** | Clothes, accessories, grooming products are highly gender-specific; white goods involve mutual influence |
| **Income** | Higher income = more differentiated products (e.g., HNIs use Taj's Qmin; others use Zomato/Swiggy) |
| **Occupation** | Jeans/sneakers for students/millennials; business casuals for career employees; WFH attire for remote workers |
| **Education** | Strong positive correlation with travel, books, magazines, smart devices, consumer electronics, stock market services |
| **Social Background** | Impacts category, brand, and product preferences differently |
| **Ethnic Origin** | Ethnicity drives preferences in food, grooming, and services |
| **Personal Values/Religion** | Strong association or disassociation with certain products |

**Case: Licious** — Religious practices reduce non-vegetarian consumption for ~4 months/year. Strategy: introduce **plant-based meat** products for vegetarian festivals.

## 9. Psychographic Descriptors

Psychological characteristics and traits of an individual customer that influence buying:

| Descriptor | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Values** | "I want to provide the best conveniences to my family." |
| **Desires** | "I want to possess the best-looking smartphone." |
| **Goals** | "I want to be economically sufficient and socially responsible." |
| **Interests** | Fine arts, cricket, photography, volunteering |
| **Lifestyle** | "I believe in a good work-life balance." |

When consumers are identified by psychographic descriptors, the base for **segment crystallisation** is developed.

## 10. Behavioural Descriptors

The most varied and hardest to capture accurately.

**Consumer Behaviour Process:**
Data-seeking → Visualization → Analytical matching → Decision-making → Usage analysis

> **Key Formula:**  
> Geography **supports**  
> Demography **enables**  
> Psychography **shapes**  
> Behaviour **determines** the buyer persona

The behavioural profile has an **amplification effect** on the combination of the other three profiles. Understanding it is the most important task for strategic marketing.

## 13. Seven Steps in the Segmentation Process

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1. **Need-Based Segmentation** | Group customers by similar needs; identify the consumption problem being solved |
| 2. **Identify the Segment** | Determine demographics, lifestyles, and usage behaviours that make the segment distinct |
| 3. **Assess Attractiveness** | Use criteria: market growth, competitive intensity, market access, profitability |
| 4. **Determine Profitability** | Evaluate segment-level profitability (may include multiple products) |
| 5. **Positioning Strategy** | Create value proposition and price positioning strategy for the segment |
| 6. **Acid Test (Storyboard)** | Test attractiveness of each segment's positioning strategy |
| 7. **Develop Marketing Mix** | Expand positioning to full 4P strategy (Product, Price, Promotion, Place) |

## 15. Automobile Industry — When Industries Transform, Segments Boom

With electrification, segment count doubles:
- **ICE Passenger Cars:** Hatchback (~1000cc), Mid-Sedan (~1300–1500cc), Large Sedan (~1700–2200cc)
- **Electric Passenger Cars:** Hatchback (15–20 kWh), Mid-Sedan (30–40 kWh), Large Sedan (50–60 kWh)

## 16. Two Principles of Product-Market Segmentation

1. **The number of segments served by a product is proportional to the number of individual performance units integrated into the product.**  
   (More features → more segments addressed)

2. **The number of products that can serve a segment is proportional to the spectrum of functionalities enjoyed by the segment.**  
   (More diverse needs → more products required)

**Implication:** Proper segmentation can make the total market larger than the sum of its parts. Poor segmentation leads to cannibalisation.

---

## 17. Qualifying Criteria for a Stable Market Segment

A sustainable market segment must satisfy **6 criteria**:

| Criterion | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| **Differentiated** | Different need profile from other segments |
| **Identifiable** | Customers identifiable by descriptor variables |
| **Accessible** | Can be reached through targeted communications |
| **Appropriate Size** | Sufficient scale for the company's strategy |
| **Measurable** | Quantifiable in volumes, revenues, and profits |
| **Stable** | Customers remain in the segment for a sustained period |

---

## 18. IndiGo Airlines — Segment Characteristics Assessment

IndiGo succeeded via a unique combination:
- **Low fares** (primary segment position — cost advantage)
- **On-time performance** (brand image builder)
- **Clean cabins** (brand differentiator)

These three together created a **sustainable market segment** that supported growth.

---

## 19. Key Takeaways — Lecture 27

1. Product categories are surrogates for market segments — never lose sight of the underlying application.
2. Low, middle, and high income are distinct markets requiring very different product and marketing strategies.
3. The Five-Forces model applies to individual market segments, not just industries.
4. Behaviour is the most powerful but least easily captured dimension — it amplifies all other descriptors.
5. Localisation is not optional in global markets — it is a strategic necessity.
6. The six qualifying criteria (DIAMS: Differentiated, Identifiable, Accessible, Measurable, Stable + appropriate size) define a robust market segment.

# Lecture 28: Product-Market Segmentation

## 1. Overview
This lecture explores the strategic frameworks for product-market expansion, targeting strategies, and the deep link between strategic marketing and corporate strategy. It uses case studies from Marico, the clean mobility segment, real estate, and the pharmaceutical/automobile industries to illustrate dynamic and flexible segmentation.

---

## 2. Product-Market Expansion Routes

A company can pursue long-range product-market expansion via four routes:

| Route | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Route 1** | Stay with one market; prove the product; expand the market; then add products |
| **Route 2** | Stay with one product; add markets |
| **Route 3** | Start with multiple products and multiple markets (requires deep pockets) |
| **Route 4** | One core product → adjunct products → multiple core products; simultaneously one core market → adjunct markets → multiple core markets |

### Case Study — Edible Oil Industry:
- **Gold Winner (Kaleesuwari Refinery):** Started with sunflower oil as core product in Tamil Nadu (75% of turnover) → added Karnataka, Kerala, AP, Telangana, Maharashtra, Odisha as adjunct markets.
- **Freedom (Gemini Edible Oils):** Started with sunflower oil in AP/Telangana → added palm oil, rice bran oil as adjunct products.

> **Key Principle:** Every successful company starts with **one core product and one core market**. Resources and talent determine the pace of expansion.

---

## 3. Beyond Market Segmentation — Full STP Application

Segmentation is only the **first step**. The STP cycle involves increasing resource commitment:

| Phase | Action | Resource Level |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| **Segmentation** | Identify relatively homogeneous customer groups | Lower (research-oriented) |
| **Targeting** | Dedicate resources to cultivate prioritised segments; assess segment attractiveness | Medium |
| **Positioning** | Allocate resources for intense product-market promotion; develop positioning strategies | Higher |

**Example:** Consumer electronics music systems
- Segmentation: Identify youngsters, college students, home residents as customers
- Targeting: Focus on millennials needing high-power speaker systems for weekend parties
- Positioning: "Best for millennial weekend gatherings and business events"

---

## 4. Three Targeting Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Niche Market Strategy** | Serve one/two segments requiring specialised features and benefits | 7-seater off-road vehicles; low glycemic index rice; unpolished rice |
| **Mass Market Strategy** | Appeal to the widest possible market with product variants around a common base | Multi-flavour coffee (pure to 60% chicory blends); covers the entire coffee market |
| **Growth Market Strategy** | Evaluate segments based on growth potential, not current status | Blended edible oils (sunflower + olive, + rice bran); nutrient-enhanced oils for health |

> Targeting strategies cannot be structured in isolation — they need a **robust product and operations strategy** to back them up.

---

## 5. Case Study: Marico — Strategic Marketing to Corporate Strategy

Marico's journey exemplifies how market segmentation links with corporate strategy:

| Phase | Category | Products |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Core** (early) | Hair Nourishment | Parachute coconut oil (once 70% of turnover) |
| **Expansion** | Hair Nourishment | Plain coconut oil → Value-added hair oils → Premium hair nourishment tonics |
| **Adjacency** | Family Foods | Healthy foods, nature cure products |
| **New Vertical** | Male Grooming | New category entirely (added by acquisition) |

Later expansions: Skincare → Grooming → Healthy Foods → Hair Nourishment → Hygiene

## 8. Segment Migration

### Tata Nano — Cautionary Tale of Failed Segment Migration:

# Lecture 29: Segmentation Deep Dive

## 1. Overview
This lecture takes a deeper analytical look at segmentation through the Samsung vs. Apple contrast, explores the pros and cons of intense product and market segmentation, examines restraints from R&D and operational perspectives, identifies when intensive segmentation works and when it fails, and links market segmentation to the product life cycle.

---

## 2. Samsung vs. Apple — Contrasting Segmentation Strategies

| Dimension | Samsung (Galaxy) | Apple (iPhone) |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Product Range** | Galaxy S (high-end) + S-FE + Z Flip + Z Fold + Galaxy A/F/M | iPhone 14 series (4 models only) |
| **Total SKU Count** | ~45 models | ~12 models |
| **Approach** | Intense product segmentation: high-end specs at various price points | Minimalist: one backbone platform per series |
| **Market Segmentation** | Every price point; every purchase frequency (from 8 months to 4 years) | Selective; premium positioning |
| **B2C Suitability** | Very high | High |

> **Key Contrast:** Samsung covers the full market canvas (intense product + intense market segmentation). Apple powers growth through **minimalist strategy** with maximum platform standardisation.

---

## 3. Pros and Cons of Intense Product Segmentation

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Very high customer personalisation | High customer indecision (choice overload) |
| Higher customer satisfaction | Exponential R&D and manufacturing complexity |
| More focused customer targeting | Higher per-unit development and manufacturing cost |
| Well-suited to B2C | **Not** suitable for B2B |

---

## 4. R&D and Operations Implications of Intense Market Segmentation

### Pros:
- Covers every price point
- Caters to all demographic needs
- May lead to higher overall volumes
- Suited for products where minor tweaks create new micro-segments

### Cons:
- Huge SKU complexity
- Low switching costs → customers jump between models easily
- Must fight multiple players simultaneously
- May mislead customers (too many similar options)

---

## 5. Restraints on Intense Product Segmentation

Four key technological and operational constraints:

| Constraint | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Technology in Bands** | Technology of 2030 is not available today; components evolve in time bands |
| **Mix-and-Match is Suboptimal** | E.g., the latest Sony camera sensor cannot be meaningfully combined with an LCD display screen |
| **Irrational Combinations Reduce Performance** | Not every antibiotic + potentiator combination is therapeutic; some are toxic |
| **Supply Chain Disruption** | Multi-generational components disrupt volume-optimised supply chains |
| **Economies of Scale Adversely Affected** | Excess SKU multiplicity destroys per-variant economics; economies of scope rarely compensate |

---

## 6. Restraints on Intense Market Segmentation

| Constraint | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Customer Heterogeneity Never Fully Understood** | 100 segments ≠ complete customer understanding; markets can only be read in broad bands |
| **Closer Price Bands → Easy Inter-Brand Migration** | Market volatility becomes the norm; purchasing power shifts with inflation |
| **Price Elasticity vs. Segmentation** | Discretionary/non-discretionary nature of expenditure is more accurate than price-point segmentation (e.g., COVID: essentials soared; luxury cars stalled) |
| **Frequency of Purchase** | Requires historical analysis: staple demand vs. surge demand; festival demand; seasonal demand |
| **Brand Power Transcends Segmentation** | Brand attachment overrides all other factors — companies may gain more from brand development than from segmenting |

---

## 7. When Intense Product Segmentation Works Well

| Category | Reason |
|---------|--------|
| **Yogurt / taste-driven products** | Any fruit combination creates new taste profiles |
| **Fragrances / cosmetics** | Natural scents are simulatable in infinite combinations; individual sensory preferences vary widely |
| **Music and Painting** | Creation is eternally novel; every piece is unique |
| **Colour (cosmetics, paints)** | Nature's infinite range of colours keeps generating new products |
| **Products where preferences are not predetermined** | Craftsmanship products (jewellery) can create new preferences on display |
| **Clothing and Apparel** | Innumerable designs and hues can be applied to basic garments |

> "As products begin mimicking nature's infinite creativity, intense product segmentation occurs naturally."

---

## 8. When Intense Market Segmentation Works Well

### Hair Oils Example (same product category, intensely segmented by end purpose):

| Product | Segment Targeted |
|---------|----------------|
| Kesh King Ayurvedic Oil | Healthy and strong hair |
| Navratna Hair Oil | Cool scalp |
| Dabur Vatika | Supple and shiny hair |
| Induleka | Hair growth |
| Patanjali Kesh Kanti Amla | Prevent hair loss |
| Parachute Ayurvedic | Prevent dandruff; healthy scalp |

> All ayurvedic hair oils may share similar base constituents, but different mixes + different positioning = distinct market segments.

---

## 9. Product Life Cycle and Market Segmentation

| PLC Stage | Duration | Segmentation Activity |
|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| **Introduction** | 6 months – 1 year | 1–2 core segments only |
| **Growth** | 1–2 years | Many viable segments added to power growth |
| **Maturity** | 3–6 years | Segment viability stagnates; scale and scope economics plateau |
| **Decline** | Several years | Segments become progressively unviable |

> **Market segmentation and industry sales are positively correlated in the growth phase.**

**Apple vs. Samsung insight:** Apple's minimalist strategy preserves cost economics across the entire PLC. Samsung's intense segmentation powers the growth phase but creates complexity in maturity.

---

## 10. Pitfalls in Segmentation

| Pitfall | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Facile Segmentation** | Revenue-focused; slicing market without deep analysis leads to lopsided strategies |
| **Excessive Clutter** | Too many overlapping products → high brand noise and thin spread of resources |
| **High Fixed Cost** | More segments = more fixed costs; variability mistakenly assumed to be incremental |
| **Losing Long-Term Relationships** | Segmentation drives short-term viability but may not build durable customer relationships |

**Optimal Approach:**
- Choose strategic segments
- Invest for dominance in those segments
- Keep others on a bundled approach
- Calibrate efforts strategically

**Example:** Sun Pharma achieved dominance in **chronic therapy** first; Ranbaxy achieved dominance in **acute therapy** first — focused segmentation, not scatter-shot coverage.

---

## 11. Customer Groups vs. Market Segments

| Feature | Customer Groups | Market Segments |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| **Definition** | Individuals with some common factors (income, location) | Groups with common approach to specific needs and products |
| **Example** | All residents of a gated community (similar income, lifestyle) | Within the community: yoga enthusiasts / home gardeners / social workers |
| **Scope** | Geographic/demographic commonality | Need/want commonality — may span geographies |

> A large customer group in a geographic segment is a **fundamental first advantage** for starting segmentation — but it is not the end of segmentation.

---

## 12. Collateral Segment Effect

Users are not always purchasers, and customers in one segment cross-impact others.

### Book Purchase Ecosystem:
```
Publishers
   → Faculty (recommend to students + librarians)
   → Librarians (prioritise, negotiate, purchase for library)
   → Students (read, provide feedback → drives next purchase cycle)
```

**Lesson:** The **purchasing segment** (librarian) is not the **usage segment** (students), who are not the **recommendation segment** (faculty). All three must be addressed.

> **Even collateral segments that do not purchase the product are important for a company's growth strategy.**

---

## 13. Methodological Approach to Segmentation

A systematic 6-question decision flow:

1. Are **customer need profiles** understood? → If No: Conduct Market Research
2. Are **customer perceptions** of products understood? → If No: Conduct Market Research
3. Are there **differences** among customers in need profiles? → If No: Conduct Market Research
4. Are there **significant differences** among groups? → If No: Continue
5. Are the groups at **appropriate scale and size**? → If No: More Market Research
6. → Arrive at **end segments**

> This process is continuous and iterative — competitors' new offerings require periodic re-segmentation.

---

## 14. Quantitative Approach — Cluster Analysis

Step-by-step process:
1. Develop many statements/variables about customer needs.
2. Develop questions and variables that identify customers.
3. Administer statements and questions to a **random sample** of current and potential customers.
4. **Cluster analysis** on customer needs responses.
5. Choose the number of clusters that form the best groupings.
6. Examine each cluster for identifying characteristics (e.g., frequency of grocery purchase, digital vs. physical buying, preference for organic vs. conventional).
7. **Map results to economic, demographic, and geographic profiles** of the customer.

> The effectiveness depends on the **relevance and sharpness of variables chosen** and the types of alternatives presented to customers.

---

## 15. Constructing the Market Attractiveness–Competitive Position Matrix

Five-step process:
1. **Choose criteria** to measure market attractiveness (scale, revenue, profitability) and competitive position (manufacturing capacity, cost competitiveness, sales & marketing infrastructure).
2. **Weight factors** by relative importance (e.g., macro trends: 0.5; segment growth: 0.3; unmet needs: 0.2 for EVs).
3. **Assess current position** of each potential target market on each factor.
4. **Predict future position** based on environmental, customer, and competitive trends.
5. **Evaluate implications** and conduct sensitivity analysis (e.g., Deepavali season car sales fluctuation year-over-year).

---

## 16. Five-Step Process to Score Business Strengths

1. **Identify factors** for each market segment — the capabilities any player must possess.
2. **Weigh factors** — allocate 100 points based on importance for success.
3. **Score** current capabilities relative to competitors.
4. **Project** future capabilities and constraints.
5. **Calculate weighted scores** and plot on the matrix.

---

## 17. Key Takeaways — Lecture 29

1. Samsung's intense product segmentation vs. Apple's minimalist approach represent two valid but very different strategies — each with its own cost, complexity, and lifecycle implications.
2. Intense segmentation works when technology, product, or market allows infinite variety (taste, scent, music, colour); it fails when technological constraints, SKU complexity, and fixed costs outweigh benefits.
3. Segmentation pitfalls arise from over-segmentation — companies must choose strategic segments and dominate them.
4. Customer groups ≠ market segments — move from geographic clustering to need-based clustering.
5. The marketing funnel and cluster analysis are the quantitative backbone of market segmentation decisions.
6. Brand power ultimately transcends segmentation — investment in brand may yield more than investment in more segments.

## 3. Business Strength × Market Segment Attractiveness Matrix

A 3×3 matrix combining business strength (Low/Medium/High) and market segment attractiveness (Low/Medium/High):

| Business Strength | Market Attractiveness: Low | Medium | High |
|------------------|---------------------------|--------|------|
| **Low** | **Avoid** | Avoid / Harvest | Specialize & Pivot (use tailwind) |
| **Medium** | Protect + Minimize Investment | Manage for Earnings | Invest & Build |
| **High** | Protect & Refocus | Build Selectively | **Protect & Grow** ✅ |

**Strategies by Quadrant:**
- **Avoid (Low-Low):** Divest; maximize cash value; cut costs to avoid further investment.
- **Harvest (Medium-Low):** Limited expansion; look for ways to grow without high risk.
- **Specialize (Low-High):** Pivot around existing strengths; seek ways to overcome weaknesses; withdraw if sustainable growth is lacking.
- **Manage for Earnings (Medium-Medium):** Take cash out; invest only in low-risk improvements; defend position.
- **Invest & Build (High-Medium or Medium-High):** Strengthen position; use market attractiveness for higher capability.
- **Protect & Grow (High-High):** Invest at maximum digestible rate; concentrate on maintaining strength.

| **Technological** | Add innovative nutrients that retain capability at high heating temperatures |
| **Process innovation** | Convert deep-frying oils into shallow-frying oils; improve clarity and colour |
| **Premium Niche** | Enter olive oil (higher WISE values), ghee, or highly health-beneficial categories adapted for Indian cooking |
| **Cost Leadership** | Improve quality of crude procurement; reduce process losses |
| **Technical Scoring** | Compete on a technical scorecard (shelf life, moisture, iodine, MUFA/PUFA, peroxide level, refractive index, etc.) |
| **Advertising** | TV-based brand recall (family situations + health messaging) — but this alone is insufficient |

**Key Technical Parameters for Quality Assessment:**
Moisture levels | Insoluble impurity | Saponification value | Acid value | Iodine value | Unsaponifiable matter | Refractive index | Flash point | Specific gravity | Peroxide level | Saturated fatty acids | MUFA | PUFA

> **Differentiation in a commodity marketplace is NOT just advertising — it is a technical job.**

## 11. Growth Planning — Market Attractiveness Matrix in Practice

For an edible oil company, the MA-CP matrix helps prioritise:

- **Which oil segments to protect and grow?** (High attractiveness + strong competitive strength → white cells)
- **Which to avoid?** (Low attractiveness + low strength → exit/divest)
- **Which to build?** (High attractiveness + medium strength → invest and build)

**Strategic Options for Expansion:**
1. Other edible oils: soybean, mustard (high growth), rice bran, gingelly
2. FMCG adjacencies: energy bars, kitchen essentials, ghee, dairy products, ready-to-eat foods, flours, dals

**Routes to Expansion:**
- **Organic:** Segment by segment, or end-to-end
- **Inorganic:** Acquisition of brands; outsourcing manufacturing capabilities

## 12. Key Takeaways — Lecture 30

1. Market attractiveness scoring is a disciplined, multi-factor exercise — not a gut feel exercise. Weighted scores tell you **whether to enter or avoid** a segment.
2. The Business Strength × Market Attractiveness matrix provides a 9-cell strategic prescription for any combination of positions.
3. Building business strength is **more within a company's control** than influencing segment attractiveness. Focus on what you can control.
4. Titan's journey is the gold standard of matching business strength building with segment attractiveness — from standard watches to smartwatches, always staying ahead of the market.
5. The edible oil industry case study demonstrates that even a commodity FMCG market has deep segmentation, strategic differentiation opportunities, and clear growth trajectories.
6. Differentiation in a commodity-like market requires **technical excellence**, not just advertising.
7. India's growth environment — population, urbanisation, digital adoption, and significant under-penetration of rural markets — creates structural tailwinds for FMCG categories including edible oils.
