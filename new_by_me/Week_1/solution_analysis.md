### Q1. Strategic marketing looks at:
**Options:** a) Market segmentation | b) Cash collection | c) Point of Sales development | d) Price discounts for the day

> ✅ **Correct Answer: a) Market segmentation**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Strategic marketing is a long-term, analytical process that focuses on understanding and developing the marketplace — not on day-to-day transaction activities. Its foundation is **market segmentation**: dividing the total heterogeneous market into homogeneous groups (segments) based on needs, behaviour, or demographics so that the company can develop targeted strategies for each.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **b) Cash collection** – This is a transactional, operational finance activity, part of a salesperson's responsibility, not strategic marketing.
- **c) Point of Sales development** – POS is a tactical sales/retail execution activity.
- **d) Price discounts for the day** – Daily price discounts are short-term sales promotions, not strategic marketing.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Strategic Marketing: creating a marketing thrust for the medium and long term.
- Market Segmentation: grouping customers with similar needs for targeted strategy.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Confusing "marketing" (brand, long-term) with "sales" (transactional, short-term).
- Picking "Price discounts" because marketing seems to involve pricing — but strategic marketing ≠ promotional pricing.

**Memory Aid:** *Strategic marketing = SEGMENT first, then target and position (STP).*

### Q2. Which of the statements is true?
**Options:** a) Only conglomerates can be in multiple businesses | b) Only global companies can be in multiple businesses | c) Only proprietary companies can be in multiple businesses | d) Any company can be in multiple businesses, based on its business strategy

> ✅ **Correct Answer: d) Any company can be in multiple businesses, based on its business strategy**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Business strategy determines the scope and direction of a company. There is no rule restricting diversification to conglomerates, global firms, or proprietary companies. Even a small privately-owned firm can choose to diversify into multiple businesses if its strategy supports it. The course emphasises that "any company can be in multiple businesses, based on its business strategy."

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a), b), c)** — All three impose artificial restrictions based on size, ownership, or geography. These are false constraints; business strategy is the only real determinant.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Business Strategy: defines the scope of activities a company pursues.
- Diversification: entering multiple business segments.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Assuming that only large conglomerates (like Tata or GE) can diversify — any company can, if the strategy permits.

**Memory Aid:** *Business strategy = the master key that unlocks any number of businesses.*

### Q3. For successful business development between partners, there must be a clear:
**Options:** a) Competition | b) Lack of resources | c) Value proposition | d) Survival instinct

> ✅ **Correct Answer: c) Value proposition**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Business development is fundamentally a **partnership-centric process**. For two partners to align and collaborate effectively, there must be a well-defined **value proposition** — a statement of the mutual benefit each partner derives. The value proposition acts as "the bridge" connecting Partner A and Partner B in the BD process.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Competition** — Competition is an obstacle to partnership, not a prerequisite.
- **b) Lack of resources** — While resource gaps can motivate partnerships, a lack of resources alone does not ensure success.
- **d) Survival instinct** — This is reactive and short-term; BD requires strategic, long-term reasoning.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Value Proposition: a clear statement of the value two partners create together.
- Business Development Process: the bridge between partners is the value proposition.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Thinking "resources" is the answer because BD involves leveraging resources — but the critical element is the shared value proposition, not just resource availability.

**Memory Aid:** *BD Bridge = Value Proposition (VP connects Partners A and B).*

### Q4. A good salesperson must be primarily responsible, along with sales, for:
**Options:** a) Price discounts | b) Product development | c) Customer behaviour | d) Cash collection

> ✅ **Correct Answer: d) Cash collection**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
In the selling process, a salesperson is not only responsible for closing the sale but also for ensuring **cash collection** — i.e., that the money from the sale actually flows back to the company. The sales cycle is considered incomplete until payment is received. This makes cash collection a core responsibility alongside the sale itself.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Price discounts** — Salespeople may negotiate discounts, but determining them is typically a management decision.
- **b) Product development** — That belongs to R&D/marketing, not the salesperson.
- **c) Customer behaviour** — The salesperson observes and responds to customer behaviour but cannot be "responsible" for it.

**Concepts Involved:**
- The Selling Process: Prospecting → Qualifying → Demonstration → Closing → **Cash Collection** → Repeat Contact.
- Metrics of sales: Revenue and Profit — both require cash to be collected.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Choosing "product development" because salespeople provide customer feedback to R&D — but that is not their primary responsibility.

**Memory Aid:** *Selling = making the sale AND collecting the cash. No cash = incomplete sale.*

### Q5. Business development seeks to achieve:
**Options:** a) Long-term partnerships between companies and between the company and its customers | b) Immediate sales to customers | c) Sales training for people | d) Development of leadership

> ✅ **Correct Answer: a) Long-term partnerships between companies and between the company and its customers**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Business development is defined as "the process of creating strategic partnerships between a company and other stakeholders for long-term value creation." BD looks beyond the immediate transaction (sales) and beyond individual campaigns (marketing) to build **enduring alliances** — both B2B partnerships and long-term customer relationships.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **b) Immediate sales** — That is the goal of *selling*, not BD. BD is long-term and strategic.
- **c) Sales training** — Training is an HR/L&D function, not BD's primary objective.
- **d) Development of leadership** — Leadership development supports BD but is not BD's goal.

**Concepts Involved:**
- BD Definition: strategic partnerships, long-term value creation.
- Distinction between Sales (transactional), Marketing (market-centric), BD (partnership-centric).

**Common Mistakes:**
- Confusing BD with sales because both involve revenue generation — BD is fundamentally about partnerships, not individual transactions.

**Memory Aid:** *BD = Long-term, Strategic, Partnerships. NOT individual sales.*

### Q6. Which of the following is an important component of marketing effectiveness?
**Options:** a) Company history | b) Capacity utilization | c) Advertising and communication | d) None of the above

> ✅ **Correct Answer: c) Advertising and communication**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Marketing effectiveness is measured by how well a company reaches, informs, and converts its target market. **Advertising and communication** is a central lever — it creates awareness, stimulates interest, and drives purchase behaviour. It is one of the 4 Ps of marketing (Promotion) and a direct driver of marketing outcomes.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Company history** — History is context, not a driver of marketing effectiveness.
- **b) Capacity utilization** — This is an operations/manufacturing metric, not a marketing effectiveness component.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Marketing Mix (4 Ps): Product, Price, Place, **Promotion** (advertising & communication).
- Marketing Effectiveness: metrics like brand value, market share, communication reach.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Choosing "capacity utilization" because it affects supply — but marketing effectiveness is about demand creation, not supply.

**Memory Aid:** *4 Ps → Promotion = Advertising and Communication = key to marketing effectiveness.*

### Q7. Strategic marketing involves the creation of a marketing thrust for the:
**Options:** a) Short term (less than a year) | b) Medium and long term (2 to 3 years) | c) Old products | d) None of the above

> ✅ **Correct Answer: b) Medium and long term (2 to 3 years)**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
The word "strategic" is the key differentiator. Strategic activities are long-horizon, multi-year initiatives — as opposed to tactical/operational activities which are short-term. Strategic marketing creates a marketing thrust for **2–3 years**, aligning with business strategy cycles. It involves product positioning, market development, and brand building over time.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Short term** — Short-term marketing is tactical/operational marketing, not strategic marketing.
- **c) Old products** — Strategic marketing applies to all products (new and old); "old products" is not the defining criterion.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Strategic vs. Tactical Marketing.
- Time horizon of strategic planning: typically 2–5 years.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Thinking strategic marketing means "annual marketing plan" — strategic = multi-year horizon.

**Memory Aid:** *Strategic = 2–3 year horizon. Tactical = day-to-day or quarterly.*

### Q8. When consumers dislike and avoid a product, it is called:
**Options:** a) Deviant demand | b) Negative demand | c) Seasonal demand | d) Latent demand

> ✅ **Correct Answer: b) Negative demand**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
The course defines **8 States of Demand**. **Negative demand** specifically refers to a situation where consumers dislike the product so strongly that they actively avoid it and may even pay to avoid exposure (e.g., vaccinations, dental procedures, insurance). The company's challenge is to convert this negative attitude into at least neutral or positive demand.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Deviant demand** — Deviant demand is when consumers demand socially undesirable products (e.g., tobacco, illegal substances).
- **c) Seasonal demand** — Demand that is cyclical or periodic (e.g., umbrellas in monsoon).
- **d) Latent demand** — Consumers feel a strong need but no product exists to satisfy it (unexpressed need).

**Concepts Involved:**
- 8 States of Demand: Negative, Non-existent, Latent, Declining, Irregular, Full, Overfull, Deviant.
- Marketing task for each demand state.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Confusing **Negative demand** (active dislike/avoidance) with **Non-existent demand** (consumers simply unaware/indifferent).
- Mixing up **Deviant** (socially harmful products desired) with **Negative** (products disliked).

**Memory Aid — 8 Demand States (NNLDIFOD):**
*Never Neglect Latent Demand, It Fills Our Drive*
(Negative, Non-existent, Latent, Declining, Irregular, Full, Overfull, Deviant)

### Q9. Strategy once set is rolled out as a set of:
**Options:** a) Plans and projects | b) Concepts | c) Targets | d) Goals

> ✅ **Correct Answer: a) Plans and projects**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Once a business strategy is formulated (the "what" and "why"), its execution is structured through **Plans and Projects** — specific, time-bound, resource-allocated initiatives that translate strategy into operational reality. Plans define the roadmap; projects are the execution vehicles.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **b) Concepts** — Concepts are inputs to strategy formulation, not outputs.
- **c) Targets** — Targets are measurable goals but do not capture the full execution vehicle (projects, budgets, milestones).
- **d) Goals** — Goals are strategic intentions; they feed into plans and projects but are not the execution mechanism itself.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Strategy Cascade: Strategy → Plans → Projects → Execution → Monitoring.
- Strategy Implementation: converting strategic intent into operational action.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Choosing "targets" because it sounds execution-focused — but targets alone don't describe HOW strategy is implemented (plans and projects do).

**Memory Aid:** *Strategy → rolled out as Plans & Projects → monitored against Targets.*

### Q10. Ford's earliest Model T car concept involved:
**Options:** a) Product diversity | b) Product standardisation | c) Geographic diversity | d) Globalisation

> ✅ **Correct Answer: b) Product standardisation**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Henry Ford's Model T (1908–1927) is the classic example of **Product Standardisation**. Ford famously said customers could have the car in "any colour they like, as long as it's black." The entire production system (Ford's assembly line) was designed around ONE standardised product to maximise efficiency and minimise cost — the Production-Oriented Firm model at its most extreme.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Product diversity** — The exact opposite: Ford offered ONE model, not many.
- **c) Geographic diversity** — Not relevant; Ford's innovation was in manufacturing philosophy.
- **d) Globalisation** — While Ford later globalised, the Model T concept was about standardisation, not global market reach.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Production Orientation: internal efficiency over customer variety.
- Product Standardisation: one product for all, maximising scale.
- Contrast with modern Customer Orientation (product variety, customisation).

**Common Mistakes:**
- Confusing Ford's Model T concept (standardisation) with modern Ford (diverse global product portfolio).

**Memory Aid:** *Model T = "Any colour as long as it's black" = Standardisation.*

## 📝 Concise Weekly Summary

Week 1 establishes the foundational vocabulary of the course. The central distinction is:
- **Sales** = transactional, short-term, revenue/profit metrics
- **Marketing** = market-centric, medium-term, brand/market share metrics
- **Business Development** = partnership-centric, long-term, strategic growth metrics

Business strategy is the master plan that governs all three. Any company — regardless of size or structure — can operate in multiple businesses if its strategy supports it. The **value proposition** is the critical element of any BD partnership, serving as the bridge between collaborators.

Strategic marketing differs from operational marketing through its **multi-year horizon (2–3 years)** and analytical approach to market segmentation and positioning. Understanding the **8 states of demand** is critical — markets are rarely in a "full demand" state, and different states require different BD/marketing responses.

The Ford Model T case illustrates extreme **product standardisation** (production orientation) — a useful historical contrast with modern customer-oriented, segmented approaches.

**Exam hot spots:** Value Proposition definition, BD process steps, 8 Demand States, BD vs. Sales vs. Marketing distinctions.
