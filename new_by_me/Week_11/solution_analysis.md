### Q1. The skill and control over the design and manufacturing capabilities determine how companies can achieve strategic fit with their:
**Options:** a) Analysts | b) Shareholders | c) Strategic customers | d) Internal departments

> ✅ **Correct Answer: c) Strategic customers**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
**Strategic customers** are key accounts whose requirements shape a company's design and manufacturing direction. When a company masters the design and manufacturing capabilities that align perfectly with a strategic customer's specifications, it achieves **strategic fit** — becoming indispensable to that customer. This is the foundation of long-term B2B BD relationships: technical capability alignment with what the strategic customer needs.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Analysts** — Analysts observe performance but don't require design/manufacturing alignment.
- **b) Shareholders** — Shareholders care about returns; they don't drive design/manufacturing capability decisions directly.
- **d) Internal departments** — Capability development serves external customers, not just internal functions.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Strategic customer management: key account alignment.
- Design-to-customer: manufacturing and design capabilities tailored to key client needs.
- BD strategy: winning and retaining strategic customers.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Choosing "shareholders" because they seem most strategic — but shareholders want financial results, not design/manufacturing alignment specifically.

**Memory Aid:** *Design + Manufacturing capability → Strategic Customer fit. You build what your best customer needs.*

### Q2. A company will be less vulnerable to new entrants and new technology-driven competition if the company:
**Options:** a) Has only financial strength | b) Is large, with a high market share | c) Is small, with a low market share | d) Continues on the path of continuous product and market improvement

> ✅ **Correct Answer: d) Continues on the path of continuous product and market improvement**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
**Continuous improvement** — both in products and market engagement — is the most sustainable defence against competitive threats from new entrants and disruptive technologies. Financial strength and market share alone do not protect against disruption (Kodak had both but failed). A company committed to constant product evolution and market responsiveness builds a dynamic moat that is harder for competitors to breach.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Only financial strength** — Financial strength alone (Kodak, Nokia) didn't prevent disruption.
- **b) Large with high market share** — Market share can be eroded rapidly by technology disruption (Blackberry).
- **c) Small with low market share** — No inherent protection in being small.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Dynamic capabilities: continuous adaptation as competitive defence.
- Disruption vulnerability: static strengths (size, money) vs. dynamic ones (innovation, adaptation).
- Kaizen principle: continuous improvement as BD strategy.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Choosing "large with high market share" because scale seems protective — incumbency doesn't protect against disruptive innovation; continuous improvement does.

**Memory Aid:** *Kodak was large with high share = still failed. Continuous innovation = the only durable defence.*

### Q3. Which of the following is true?
**Options:** a) Innovation can occur only in respect of product | b) Innovation can occur in any and all parts of a company's value chain | c) Innovation can occur only in start-ups | d) Innovation cannot occur in marketing

> ✅ **Correct Answer: b) Innovation can occur in any and all parts of a company's value chain**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
**Innovation** is not limited to product R&D — it can and does occur across every activity in the value chain:
- **Operations**: process innovation (Toyota Production System).
- **Logistics**: supply chain innovation (Amazon's fulfillment).
- **Marketing**: digital marketing innovation.
- **Service**: customer experience innovation.
- **HR/Organisation**: management innovation (Holacracy, agile).
The broadest and most powerful BD leaders recognise that any activity can be a site of differentiation through innovation.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Only product** — Narrows innovation to R&D only; misses process, business model, and service innovation.
- **c) Only start-ups** — Established companies (Toyota, Apple) innovate continuously.
- **d) Cannot occur in marketing** — Marketing innovation is common (e.g., digital marketing, content marketing, influencer marketing).

**Concepts Involved:**
- Types of innovation: product, process, business model, marketing, organisational.
- Value chain innovation: every activity as a potential differentiator.
- BD strategy: innovation as a pervasive, not departmental, activity.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Limiting innovation to product/R&D — the course emphasises innovation across the entire value chain.

**Memory Aid:** *Innovation = EVERYWHERE in the value chain. Not just R&D lab — everywhere from procurement to after-sales service.*

### Q4. One of the more recent improvements in lighting, which is also backed by Nobel Prize-winning science and technology, has been:
**Options:** a) LED lighting | b) LCD lighting | c) Fluorescent lighting | d) Incandescent lighting

> ✅ **Correct Answer: a) LED lighting**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
**LED (Light Emitting Diode) lighting** technology was recognised with the **Nobel Prize in Physics 2014**, awarded to Isamu Akasaki, Hiroshi Amano, and Shuji Nakamura for the invention of efficient blue LEDs (which enabled white LEDs and LED lighting). LEDs represent a profound improvement: 80–90% more energy-efficient than incandescent bulbs, vastly longer lifespan, and enabling new form factors and applications. This is a classic BD case study of technology innovation disrupting an established lighting market.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **b) LCD** — LCD is display technology, not lighting technology per se; no Nobel Prize for LCD.
- **c) Fluorescent** — An older technology; not Nobel Prize-winning.
- **d) Incandescent** — The oldest technology (Edison's bulb); being replaced by LED.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Technology disruption in lighting: LED replacing incandescent and fluorescent.
- Nobel Prize as innovation validation.
- BD opportunity in energy-efficient lighting.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Confusing LED (lighting) with LCD (display) — both are modern technologies but serve different functions; LED lighting won the Nobel Prize.

**Memory Aid:** *Nobel Prize in Physics 2014 = Blue LED → White LED → LED lighting revolution. L.E.D. = Lighting's Evolutionary Disruption.*

### Q5. Corning is a great example of global business growth based largely on one product category. Corning's core competence is in:
**Options:** a) Processing of cobalt | b) Processing of glass | c) Processing of lithium | d) Processing of ion

> ✅ **Correct Answer: b) Processing of glass**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
**Corning Incorporated** (NYSE: GLW) is a world leader in **specialty glass and ceramics**. Its core competence in glass processing enabled it to build an extraordinary portfolio: optical fibre (communications), LCD glass substrates (displays), Gorilla Glass (smartphone screens), pharmaceutical glass (drug vials), and automotive glass. Starting from a single competence (glass), Corning achieved global scale by finding new BD applications across industries.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Cobalt** — Corning doesn't process cobalt; cobalt is used in EV batteries (different companies).
- **c) Lithium** — Lithium processing is the domain of battery/chemical companies.
- **d) Ion** — "Ion processing" is not a meaningful product category description.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Core competence (Hamel & Prahalad): deep capability in one technology platform.
- BD growth from core competence: applying one competence to multiple markets.
- Corning: single-competence, multi-market BD success story.

**Common Mistakes:**
- None usually; Corning = glass is a well-known association. Watch for "Gorilla Glass" as the most famous product link.

**Memory Aid:** *Corning = Glass King. From optical fibre to Gorilla Glass = all glass, multiple industries.*

### Q6. Which of the following integrates hardware and software of a device?
**Options:** a) Timeliness objective | b) Market objective | c) Operating system | d) R&D objective

> ✅ **Correct Answer: c) Operating system**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
An **Operating System (OS)** is the fundamental system software that integrates and manages a device's hardware (processor, memory, display, sensors) and provides a platform for software applications to run. iOS, Android, Windows, macOS — all operating systems bridge hardware capabilities and software functionality, enabling both to work together seamlessly. In BD terms, companies that control the OS control the platform — a powerful competitive moat (Apple, Microsoft, Google).

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Timeliness objective** — A performance metric, not a technology integrator.
- **b) Market objective** — A strategic goal, not a technology layer.
- **d) R&D objective** — R&D creates the OS; the OS itself is the integration layer.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Platform strategy: OS as the bridge between hardware and software.
- BD in technology: OS control = platform control = ecosystem lock-in.
- Technology value chain: hardware → OS → applications.

**Common Mistakes:**
- None expected; OS is clearly the technology that integrates hardware and software.

**Memory Aid:** *OS = Operating System = the universal translator between hardware silicon and software apps.*

### Q7. 3D printing is also called:
**Options:** a) Rapid printing | b) Deductive manufacturing | c) 360-degree printing | d) Additive manufacturing

> ✅ **Correct Answer: d) Additive manufacturing**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
**3D printing** is technically known as **Additive Manufacturing** — because it builds objects by **adding** material layer by layer, as opposed to subtractive manufacturing (CNC machining, which removes material from a block). Additive manufacturing enables complex geometries, rapid prototyping, and customised production without expensive tooling — a significant BD opportunity for manufacturing and healthcare sectors.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Rapid printing** — Not the technical term.
- **b) Deductive manufacturing** — Not a real manufacturing category (deductive is a logical reasoning term).
- **c) 360-degree printing** — Not the technical term for 3D printing.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Additive manufacturing: layer-by-layer material deposition.
- 3D printing applications: prototyping, healthcare implants, aerospace, automotive.
- Manufacturing innovation in BD.

**Common Mistakes:**
- None; additive manufacturing is the standard technical term for 3D printing.

**Memory Aid:** *3D Printing = ADD layers one by one = ADDitive manufacturing. CNC = SUBTRACT material = subtractive.*

### Q9. Firms in the fragmented industry must understand the following and plan their strategies accordingly:
**Options:** a) Market | b) Firm | c) Finance | d) Structural limitations which include the above and more

> ✅ **Correct Answer: d) Structural limitations which include the above and more**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
A **fragmented industry** (e.g., restaurants, dry cleaning, small retail) is characterised by many small players with no dominant firm. The fragmentation exists because of **structural limitations** — regulatory barriers to scale, geographic constraints, low technological barriers, high customisation requirements, or lack of scale economies. These structural factors INCLUDE market characteristics, firm-level factors, and financial constraints — but extend beyond them. BD strategy in fragmented industries must first understand WHY the industry is fragmented (structural reasons) before attempting to consolidate or operate within it.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Market** — Part of the picture but not the complete picture.
- **b) Firm** — Firm-level factors matter but don't explain industry fragmentation fully.
- **c) Finance** — Financial factors are one component.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Industry structure: fragmented vs. concentrated.
- Structural analysis: why fragmentation exists.
- Porter's structural analysis: barriers to consolidation.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Choosing "market" because market analysis sounds comprehensive — but structural limitations encompass market + firm + financial factors and more.

**Memory Aid:** *Fragmented Industry = Structural barriers PREVENT consolidation. Understand the structure FIRST, then plan.*
