### 📖 Course Notes Summary

Value Chain Competencies Introduction: Unlocking Your Company's Superpowers Every business, from a local coffee shop to a global giant like Apple, operates on a value chain—a sequence of activities that transforms raw materials into a final product or service for the customer. For a Business Development executive, this isn't just a process flow diagram; it's a map of strategic levers. Understanding your company's value chain—its strengths, weaknesses, and interconnected parts—is the key to positioning products, creating new business models, and building powerful relationships that drive growth and competitiveness. This lesson dissects the value chain, revealing how its components can be configured, outsourced, and globalized to win in the modern market. Core Concepts Term Definition Significance Primary Value Chain The core sequence of business functions: R&D, Procurement & Inbound Logistics, Manufacturing, Warehousing & Outbound Logistics, Sales & Marketing, and After-Sales Service. This is the fundamental backbone of any product- or service-based company. Each element is a potential source of competitive advantage or a candidate for outsourcing. Supportive Value Chains Corporate functions that support the primary chain, such as Finance, Human Resources, IT, Legal, and Planning. Also called "Shared Services." These functions, while not customer-facing, are critical for efficiency. Expertise in these areas can be spun off into new business ventures (e.g., Tata's strategic consulting group). Outsourcing & Offshoring Outsourcing: Contracting a business function to a third party. Offshoring: Moving a business function to another country. These can be These strategies are central to creating asset-light, agile business models. They allow companies to access specialized talent, reduce

Term Definition Significance combined (offshore outsourcing) or separate (in- house offshore facility). costs, and focus on core competencies. ESG Standards A framework that evaluates a company's performance on  Environmental, Social, and corporate Governance issues. Beyond pure efficiency, strong ESG performance is a powerful lever for differentiation. It allows business development teams to position a company as responsible and sustainable. Detailed Analysis: From Local Production to Global Networks The modern value chain has evolved from a fully integrated, in-house model to a disaggregated, global network. Understanding these strategic options is crucial for business development. The Evolution of Manufacturing Strategy: Maximum In-house Manufacture: The traditional model where a company does everything itself. Captive Ancillary Manufacture: Outsourcing to dedicated, dependent suppliers. Make-or-Buy Decisions: A strategic choice to produce a component in-house or source it externally based on cost and quality. National Outsourcing: Sourcing from specialized domestic firms. Global Offshoring: The modern standard, involving sourcing or producing from anywhere in the world to maximize competitiveness. •  ◦  ◦  ◦  ◦  ◦

A Framework for Globalization Models: Companies combine production/sourcing strategies with their market focus to create distinct business models. For the Local Market: Traditional Domestic Model: On-shore production for the local market. (e.g., Early Indian industries). Local Make-or-Buy Model: On-shore outsourcing to enhance competitiveness in the local market. Manufacturing Competitiveness Model: Offshore production (at your own facility) to create cost-competitive products for your home market (e.g., An MNC's export-only unit in India). Strategic Sourcing Model: Offshore outsourcing to bring in cheaper components/products for the local market. For the Global Market: Traditional Globalization Model: On-shore production for export. Enhanced Globalization Model: On-shore production for export, enhanced by on-shore outsourcing for key components to improve scale and cost. Regional Globalization Model: Offshore production in key regions to serve those global markets directly (e.g., Toyota, Airbus). Strategic Globalization Model: Offshore outsourcing from a global network of suppliers to serve global markets (e.g., Apple). The Impact of Currency Fluctuations: Global operations are highly sensitive to foreign exchange (Forex) rates. A devaluation of  a  country's  currency  (e.g.,  the  Indian  Rupee  weakening against the US Dollar) has mixed effects: Winners: Domestic firms selling locally: Their products become cheaper relative to imports. Domestic firms exporting globally: Their products become more price-competitive on the world stage. •  ◦  ▪  ▪  ▪  ▪  ◦  ▪  ▪  ▪  ▪  •  ◦  ▪  ▪

Losers: Global firms selling locally (with high import content): Their costs increase, making their products less competitive. Key Takeaways Checklist [x] Know Your Levers: A business development executive must have a deep understanding of their company's entire value chain—primary, supportive, and sub-elements—to identify strengths and opportunities. [x] Turn Functions into Features: A company’s core competence in a value chain element (like R&D, cyber security, or project management) can be marketed as a service itself, creating new revenue streams. [x] Agility is the Goal: The global trend is moving away from heavy, in- house operations toward asset-light, agile models that leverage outsourcing and offshoring. [x] Strategy Dictates Sourcing: The choice to outsource, offshore, or keep a function in-house is a strategic trade-off between cost, control, risk, and reward. [x] India as a Global Hub: Global giants like Mercedes-Benz, Pfizer, Intel, and Amazon leverage India's talent pool for critical R&D, design, and shared service centers, proving the country's depth in high-value activities. [x] The World is Your Factory (and Office): As shown by Airbus and Apple, modern supply chains are incredibly diffused global networks, with components and services sourced from dozens of countries to build a single product. Real-World Application This isn't just theory. Apple personifies the strategic globalization model, designing in the US but outsourcing manufacturing to a complex global network to achieve unparalleled scale and competitiveness.  Airbus operates a  regional globalization model, with major components built collaboratively across Europe and by partners worldwide  (including  many  in  India,  from  Tata  to  Wipro),  creating  a  truly multinational enterprise. Closer to home, the decision by MNCs to establish R&D, design, and service centers in  India  demonstrates  a  strategy  to  leverage  local  knowledge  talent  for  global competitiveness.  By  understanding  these  value  chain  dynamics,  you  can  better analyze a company's strategy, identify its competitive advantages, and propose ◦  ▪  •  •  •  •  •  •

business  development  initiatives  that  align  with  the  global  flow  of  production, knowledge, and talent.

### 47.1 Purpose and Hierarchy of R&D

> **Definition:** R&D (also called Product Development/Design & Development) is the engine of growth for companies, and especially for firms seeking self-reliance.

**R&D Functions:**
- Develops new products and services to fulfil customer needs
- Sometimes develops products *ahead* of customer needs
- Sets product standards and supports manufacture
- Spans the full hierarchy: End-product → Systems → Components → Materials

### 48.1 What is Business Negotiation?

> **Definition:** Negotiation is the process of conferring with someone to come to terms or reach an agreement. It is an essential plank of business development.

**Stages of High-Level Business Negotiation:**
1. Agreeing to meet and discuss potential collaboration
2. Agreeing for mutual due diligence of the parties
3. Agreeing on the key terms of a possible contract
4. Agreeing on the final terms of the contract

> *Real example from the professor:* When seeking a Toyota partnership for an auto company, the professor attended a conference knowing a Toyota senior executive would be there, introduced himself, and convinced Toyota to add the company to their India consideration list. *Even agreeing to meet is itself a negotiation.*

### 49.1 Why Culture Matters for Business Development

> **Key tension:** Business is global while culture is local. BD requires partnering with multiple entities of different nationalities, building long-term partnerships, and institutionalizing collaboration to withstand environmental vicissitudes.

**What is Culture?**
Culture is the characteristics and knowledge of a particular group of people, encompassing language, way of life, religion, cuisine, social habits, music, and arts.

**Three Pricing Approaches:**

1. **Cost Plus** — Price based on cost + margin
2. **Market-bearing Price** — What the market will accept
3. **Future Value** — NPV of future net cash flows from the transaction

