### Q1. In technical collaboration, the following is involved:
**Options:** a) Licensing of technology without any expectation | b) Franchising of technology | c) Licensing of a technology for a fee and/or royalty | d) Acquisition of technology and product

> ✅ **Correct Answer: c) Licensing of a technology for a fee and/or royalty**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
A **technical collaboration** is a formal arrangement where one party (the licensor) grants another party (the licensee) the right to use a specific technology. This right is granted in exchange for a **fee** (upfront payment) and/or **royalty** (ongoing percentage of sales). The licensee gains access to proven technology without having to develop it independently; the licensor earns income from its IP without directly entering new markets.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Without any expectation** — No commercial arrangement exists without consideration; technology licensing is a commercial transaction.
- **b) Franchising** — Franchising involves brand, business model, and operational systems (McDonald's model), not purely technology transfer.
- **d) Acquisition** — Acquisition involves ownership transfer of the technology and often the company; technical collaboration is a usage license, not ownership.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Technology licensing: fee + royalty structure.
- BD structures: licensing as a partnership form.
- IP commercialisation: turning technology into revenue.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Confusing "licensing" (usage rights) with "acquisition" (ownership transfer) — licensing is temporary access; acquisition is permanent ownership.

**Memory Aid:** *Technical Collaboration = Rent the technology, don't buy it. Pay fee upfront + royalty ongoing.*

### Q2. Successful technical collaborations may evolve into:
**Options:** a) Continued dependence on the licensor for supply of components | b) Supply of indigenised components by the licensee to the licensor | c) Premature terminations | d) None of the above

> ✅ **Correct Answer: b) Supply of indigenised components by the licensee to the licensor**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
The ideal outcome of a successful technical collaboration is **technology indigenisation** by the licensee — the licensee learns the technology, internalises it, and builds local manufacturing capability. In the most successful cases, the licensee eventually manufactures components at lower cost (due to local labour and scale) and may even **supply those components back to the original licensor** — a remarkable reversal that demonstrates true capability absorption. This happened in India-Japan auto and electronics collaborations.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Continued dependence** — This represents failure to indigenise; successful collaboration reduces, not maintains, dependence.
- **c) Premature terminations** — This is an outcome of failed collaboration; successful ones reach full term and evolve further.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Technology indigenisation: absorbing and building on licensed technology.
- Reverse flow: licensee becoming a supplier to the licensor.
- BD evolution: from dependency to partnership.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Thinking successful collaboration means continued dependence — the goal is always to build indigenous capability.

**Memory Aid:** *Best outcome of tech collaboration: student becomes teacher. Licensee supplies components BACK to licensor.*

### Q3. The global pharmaceutical corporation that runs an Innovation Mall for open-sourcing of solutions in drug development is:
**Options:** a) BMS | b) Merck | c) Pfizer | d) Eli Lilly

> ✅ **Correct Answer: d) Eli Lilly**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
**Eli Lilly** (US pharmaceutical company) pioneered the concept of **open innovation** in drug development through its **InnoCentive platform** (also known as the Innovation Mall or Open Innovation Drug Discovery). This platform posts scientific challenges — including drug development problems — to a global crowd of researchers, offering prizes for solutions. It's a BD model that leverages external expertise to accelerate innovation at lower cost.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) BMS (Bristol-Myers Squibb)** — Known for oncology innovation but doesn't run the Innovation Mall.
- **b) Merck** — Major pharma, but not the Innovation Mall operator.
- **c) Pfizer** — Known for vaccines and blockbuster drugs, not the Innovation Mall.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Open innovation: sourcing solutions from external parties.
- InnoCentive/Innovation Mall: crowd-sourced drug development.
- BD in pharma: open collaboration as an innovation strategy.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Confusing Eli Lilly with Pfizer — both are US pharma giants, but Eli Lilly is specifically associated with the Innovation Mall/InnoCentive.

**Memory Aid:** *Eli Lilly = L for Lilly = L for Let others innovate. Innovation Mall = Lilly's open innovation marketplace.*

### Q4. Technical collaborations are limited time arrangements that envisage:
**Options:** a) Continued dependence on technology even after the term | b) Conversion into joint ventures automatically | c) Indigenisation of technology during the term of the agreement | d) Import of completely built products

> ✅ **Correct Answer: c) Indigenisation of technology during the term of the agreement**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Technical collaborations are designed to be **time-limited** — the licensee has a defined period (typically 5–10 years) to absorb and indigenise the technology. The goal is for the licensee to build sufficient capability during the collaboration period so that when the agreement expires, it can operate independently without continued reliance on the licensor. **Indigenisation** (adapting and manufacturing technology locally) is the intended outcome during the term.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Continued dependence** — This contradicts the purpose; the collaboration is meant to build independence.
- **b) Automatic conversion to JV** — Not the standard outcome; conversion requires new negotiations.
- **d) Import of completely built products** — This is a distribution/import arrangement, not a technical collaboration.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Technology transfer with indigenisation goal.
- Time-bound collaborations: licence term + capability building.
- BD structure design: building self-sufficiency.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Thinking technical collaborations are meant to create permanent dependence — they are explicitly designed to transfer capability and end the dependence.

**Memory Aid:** *Tech Collaboration = School with an end date. Graduate by indigenising technology before the term expires.*

### Q5. For an alliance to succeed which combination is the most important?
**Options:** a) Strategic fit and Competency fit | b) Competency fit and Organisational fit | c) Strategic fit, Competency fit, Organisational fit and Cultural fit | d) Strategic fit and Cultural fit

> ✅ **Correct Answer: c) Strategic fit, Competency fit, Organisational fit and Cultural fit**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Alliance success requires alignment across **all four dimensions simultaneously**:
1. **Strategic fit**: Both partners pursue compatible strategic goals; the alliance serves both strategies.
2. **Competency fit**: Partners have complementary capabilities that together create a stronger whole.
3. **Organisational fit**: Compatible structures, processes, and governance models enable collaboration.
4. **Cultural fit**: Shared values, work styles, and relationship norms prevent friction.
Missing any one dimension causes alliance failure — even the best strategic rationale fails without cultural alignment, and great cultural compatibility cannot compensate for strategic misalignment.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a), b), d)** — Each option omits one or two critical fit dimensions. All four are necessary.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Alliance success factors: four-fit model.
- Partnership design in BD: comprehensive alignment check.
- Common alliance failure modes: culture/organisation misalignment.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Thinking strategic fit is sufficient for alliance success — many alliances fail despite strategic logic because of cultural and organisational misalignment.

**Memory Aid:** *Alliance = SCOC: Strategic + Competency + Organisational + Cultural fit. All four or failure.*

### Q6. A contract development and manufacturing organisation that supports multiple business partners typically adopts which BD structure?
**Options:** a) Licensing with each partner | b) Strategic alliance with fee for service for each partner | c) Joint venture with each partner | d) Franchising with each partner

> ✅ **Correct Answer: b) Strategic alliance with fee for service for each partner**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
A **CDMO (Contract Development and Manufacturing Organisation)** — such as Lonza, Wuxi Biologics, or Syngene in India — provides development and manufacturing services to multiple pharma/biotech companies simultaneously. This requires a **strategic alliance with fee-for-service** model: the CDMO is a strategic partner (committed, long-term) but charges a service fee rather than taking equity or revenue share. This structure allows multiple partnerships without conflicts of interest.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) Licensing** — CDMOs don't license their capabilities; they provide services.
- **c) Joint venture** — JVs involve equity stakes; CDMOs can't form equity JVs with all partners (conflict of interest).
- **d) Franchising** — Franchising replicates a business model; not applicable for contract manufacturing services.

**Concepts Involved:**
- CDMO business model.
- Fee-for-service BD structure.
- Strategic alliance vs. JV: equity vs. service model.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Thinking CDMOs form JVs with each partner — JVs require equity, which creates conflicts when serving multiple competing clients.

**Memory Aid:** *CDMO = Strategic partner for hire. Fee-for-service = you pay, we deliver, no equity complications.*

### Q7. Joint ventures tend to experience turbulent and stressful phases when:
**Options:** a) They integrate well | b) Disablers overpower enablers | c) Enablers overpower disablers | d) There exist no enablers or disablers

> ✅ **Correct Answer: b) Disablers overpower enablers**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
Joint ventures have **enablers** (factors that facilitate collaboration: shared goals, trust, compatible cultures, clear governance) and **disablers** (factors that create friction: conflicting interests, power imbalances, cultural clashes, market changes). When **disablers overpower enablers**, the JV enters turbulent, stressful phases — characterised by governance disputes, distrust, and strategic misalignment that can ultimately lead to dissolution or renegotiation.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **a) They integrate well** — Good integration leads to harmony, not turbulence.
- **c) Enablers overpower disablers** — This leads to success, not stress.
- **d) No enablers or disablers** — Not a realistic scenario; all JVs have both forces in play.

**Concepts Involved:**
- JV dynamics: enablers vs. disablers framework.
- Alliance management: maintaining balance of forces.
- JV lifecycle: formation → growth → stress/turbulence → resolution.

**Common Mistakes:**
- None expected; the question is logically structured — disablers causing problems is the intuitive answer.

**Memory Aid:** *JV health = Enablers > Disablers. Stress phase = Disablers win. Like a seesaw — who's heavier?*

### Q8. When an MNC enters a country to secure resources for itself, it is called:
**Options:** a) Security alliance | b) Growth alliance | c) Complementarity alliance | d) Value alliance

> ✅ **Correct Answer: a) Security alliance**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
A **security alliance** is a BD structure where a company (typically an MNC) enters a market or forms a partnership primarily to **secure access to critical resources** — natural resources, raw materials, strategic components, or supply chains. The MNC's primary objective is risk mitigation and supply security, not market growth or value creation. Resource-securing alliances in oil, rare earths, and critical minerals are classic examples.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **b) Growth alliance** — Growth alliances aim to enter new markets or grow revenue.
- **c) Complementarity alliance** — Complementarity alliances are formed to access capabilities the company lacks.
- **d) Value alliance** — Value alliances focus on creating shared value in the market.

**Concepts Involved:**
- Types of alliances in BD: security, growth, complementarity, value.
- Resource-seeking FDI: securing upstream supply.
- MNC strategy: resource-based internationalisation.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Choosing "complementarity alliance" because securing resources could be seen as filling a gap — but the course specifically uses "security alliance" for resource-securing arrangements.

**Memory Aid:** *Security Alliance = "We enter to make our supply SECURE, not to grow." Resource = Security.*

### Q9. The interesting aspect of Hero Honda JV and the separation of the JV partners has been that after the separation, Hero and Honda:
**Options:** a) Sold more vehicles and more models in the aggregate than likely to have been sold by the JV | b) Sold, in the aggregate, less vehicles than sold by the JV | c) Sold, in the aggregate, the same number of vehicles than sold by the JV | d) Stuck to the JV product line and did not diversify

> ✅ **Correct Answer: a) Sold more vehicles and more models in the aggregate than likely to have been sold by the JV**

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
When the **Hero Honda JV** separated in 2010–2011, both partners independently launched broader, more diverse product ranges than they could have within the JV's strategic constraints:
- **Hero MotoCorp** (formerly Hero Honda India) rapidly expanded with new models and retained dominant India market share.
- **Honda** (Honda Motorcycle & Scooter India) launched premium and diverse models including the Activa, CB series, etc.
Together, their combined sales exceeded what the JV would likely have sold — demonstrating that sometimes **separation enables greater value creation** than continued partnership.

**Why other options are wrong:**
- **b) Less vehicles** — Incorrect; both companies grew significantly post-separation.
- **c) Same number** — Combined output clearly exceeded the JV's trajectory.
- **d) Stuck to JV product line** — Both companies diversified extensively post-separation.

**Concepts Involved:**
- JV dissolution and value creation.
- Post-separation BD: both partners thriving independently.
- India motorcycle market dynamics.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Assuming JV dissolution always destroys value — the Hero Honda case is the course's example of dissolution creating MORE value for both parties.

**Memory Aid:** *Hero + Honda = 1+1 > 2. Separation created MORE value, not less. The sum of parts exceeded the whole.*

**Concepts Involved:**
- Pharma BD strategies: JVs vs. acquisitions for capability building.
- Oncology as a capital-intensive, partnership-driven therapeutic area.
- Indian pharma BD case studies.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Confusing Zydus Cadila with Sun Pharma — Sun Pharma grew through acquisitions (most notably Ranbaxy); Zydus used JV partnerships for oncology.

- **Types of alliances** serve different strategic purposes: security (resource access), growth (market expansion), complementarity (capability gaps), and value (joint value creation).
- **Hero Honda post-separation** is the course's evidence that sometimes dissolution creates MORE value than continued partnership.

## ✍️ Additional Practice Questions – Week 9

**PQ1.** What distinguishes a technical collaboration from a joint venture?
- A) A technical collaboration involves equity sharing; a JV does not
- B) A technical collaboration is time-limited with a royalty/fee; a JV involves shared equity and governance
- C) Technical collaborations only occur in manufacturing sectors
- D) Joint ventures only occur in service industries
> **Answer: B** | Tech collab = usage rights + fee/royalty + time-limited. JV = equity + shared governance.

**PQ2.** An alliance where Company A gains access to Company B's distribution network while Company B gains access to Company A's technology is best described as:
- A) Security alliance
- B) Value alliance
- C) Complementarity alliance
- D) Growth alliance
> **Answer: C** | Complementarity = filling each other's gaps (distribution + technology are complementary resources).

**PQ3.** The InnoCentive platform launched by Eli Lilly was revolutionary because:
- A) It allowed competitors to merge operations
- B) It opened drug development challenges to external global solvers for prizes
- C) It licensed entire drug formulations for free
- D) It created a closed innovation lab with 500 internal scientists
> **Answer: B** | InnoCentive = open innovation platform posting drug development challenges to global crowd of solvers.

**PQ4.** Which of the following is a key enabler of JV success?
- A) One partner having complete operational control
- B) Trust, shared goals, and compatible organisational cultures
- C) Minimum financial investment from both parties
- D) Secrecy about the JV's operations
> **Answer: B** | Trust + shared goals + cultural compatibility = core JV enablers.

**PQ5.** In the context of pharma BD, what is the key advantage of a CDMO model over a fully owned manufacturing facility?
- A) CDMOs provide equity in the drugs they manufacture
- B) CDMOs offer flexible, scalable manufacturing without capital investment by the drug developer
- C) CDMOs always produce drugs cheaper than in-house manufacturing
- D) CDMOs guarantee regulatory approval
> **Answer: B** | CDMO = outsourced manufacturing = no capital investment for drug developers + flexible scale.
