### 📖 Course Notes Summary

Customer Characteristics Introduction Why does a company's purchasing manager buy office supplies based on budget and efficiency, while an individual buys a laptop based on brand image and user reviews? Understanding the "who" and "why" behind a purchase is the foundation of all successful business development. This lesson moves beyond the simple idea of a "customer" to explore the complex characteristics, motivations, and ecosystems that define different buyer types. Mastering these concepts is crucial for creating products and strategies that don't just sell, but truly connect. Core Concepts Term Definition Significance Customer The individual or entity that  purchases a product or service. (e.g., a company's administrative manager, a dealer for an auto firm). The target for the sales transaction. They are the gatekeepers of the purchase, focused on the "buy" decision. Consumer The individual or entity that uses the product or service. (e.g., the employees using the software, the person driving the car). The target for marketing and product experience. Their satisfaction creates the underlying demand that drives the entire value chain. Consumer Market Consists of individuals buying products for personal use. Characterized by a huge number of buyers, dynamic needs, and emotional decision-making. Strategy focuses on branding, mass-market promotion, and accessibility. Demand is often price- elastic. Business Market Consists of organizations buying products for use in their own operations or production (B2B). Characterized by fewer buyers, Strategy focuses on relationship-building, technical specifications, and ethical conduct. Brand has

Consumer Markets vs. Business Markets: A Strategic Divide While the terms "customer" and "consumer" are often used interchangeably, the markets they operate in require fundamentally different approaches. Market Structure & Access Consumer: Millions of buyers, easily accessible through retail, e- commerce, and direct-to-consumer (D2C) channels. Business: Fewer, more concentrated buyers. Access is difficult and requires formal processes like appointments, negotiations, and winning orders. Decision-Making & Influence Consumer: Decisions are often individual, impulsive, and heavily influenced by branding, packaging, promotion, and social circles. Business: Decisions are multi-tiered, procedure-bound, and based on objective parameters, testing, and long-term value. Explicit codes of conduct are common. Product & Demand Consumer: Products are standardized (e.g., toothpaste). Demand is highly elastic—lower prices can significantly increase volume. Business: Products can be standard or highly specific (e.g., industrial adhesives). Demand is inelastic; once a product is proven, price is a secondary factor to reliability. 2. Evolving the Organization: From Pyramid to Circle A company's structure reveals its true customer focus. Traditional (Pyramid) Model: Top management is at the apex, farthest from the customer. •  ◦  ◦  •  ◦  ◦  •  ◦  ◦  •  ◦

Frontline staff have the most customer interaction. Customer connection decreases as you move up the hierarchy. Flaw: Creates silos where functions like R&D, finance, or IT are disconnected from the end-user's reality. Modern (Circular) Model: Customers are at the center, surrounded by all company functions (R&D, Marketing, IT, Finance, etc.). Principle: Everyone, regardless of role, must connect with customers. An IT leader must understand the sales process to build better tools; a CEO must conduct "listening tours" to grasp market sentiment (e.g., Mike Ball at Hospira). This is the true definition of a customer-centric organization. 3. Strategic Engagement: Horizontal vs. Vertical Growth A company must strategically decide how to engage with the customer universe. Horizontal Engagement (Build Scale First): Focus: Product specialization. Create a single class of products that serves multiple customer clusters across geographies. Goal: Achieve scale and efficiency by meeting a common need globally. Example: A company focuses on making one type of highly efficient engine component for car manufacturers all over the world. Vertical Engagement (Build Scope Later): Focus: Customer specialization. Create a full range of products required by a single customer cluster. Goal: Meet the multiple needs of a single customer, building a strong corporate brand association. Example: After mastering one component, the company expands to provide the entire engine system to its key clients. Strategic Advice: Start with horizontal engagement to build a strong, scalable foundation before attempting to cover the entire spectrum of needs for one group (vertical), which can be complex and costly. ◦  ◦  ◦  •  ◦  ◦  ◦  •  ◦  ◦  ◦  •  ◦  ◦  ◦  ◦

Key Takeaways [ ] Distinguish the Buyer from the User: The 'customer' who pays is not always the 'consumer' who uses. Your strategy must address both. [ ] Needs are Multilayered: Go beyond functional needs. Customers buy for esteem, augmentation (upgrading), replacement, and social acceptance. A single product, like a car, can fulfill all these needs simultaneously. [ ] Embrace the DAWN Hierarchy: Customer motivation is a spectrum. Understand if you are fulfilling a basic Need, a routine Want, a strong  Aspiration, or a powerful Desire. Great companies help move customers up this hierarchy. [ ] Value is a Perception: Customer Value = (Total Benefits [use, ownership, image, disposal value]) - (Total Costs [purchase, operating, service, image loss]). Your job is to maximize the perceived benefits while managing perceived costs. [ ] The Customer Ecosystem is Key: Individuals are influenced by their family, friends, and organization. A company like Asian Paints succeeded by evolving from selling paint (a product) to offering complete home design solutions (an ecosystem). [ ] Rethink Your Business Model: Don't be constrained by existing models. The "foodplex" concept—where a customer in one restaurant can order food from another in a collaborative network—is an example of innovating to fulfill the customer's desire for variety and choice. Conclusion: From Transaction to Transformation The modern marketplace is no longer a simple transaction where a firm provides and a customer receives. Fueled by the internet economy, today's customers are informed, active participants who co-create value. By moving from a firm-centric pyramid to a customer-centric circle and applying deeper frameworks like the DAWN hierarchy, businesses can transform their offerings. The goal is to design alternative paradigms  that  satisfy  the  complex,  evolving,  and  interconnected  needs  of customers, turning a one-time purchase into a lifelong relationship. •  •  •  •  •  •

### 3. Customer-Oriented Organisation

#### 3.1 The Traditional Pyramid Model

```
         ┌──────────────┐
         │  Top Mgmt    │   ← Least customer contact
         ├──────────────┤
         │ Middle Mgmt  │   ← Moderate customer contact
         ├──────────────┤
         │  Frontline   │   ← Primary customer contact
         └──────────────┘
```

The weakness: Customer connect *decreases* as seniority increases. The real pulse of the market is siloed at the bottom.


### 3.2 The Contemporary Circular Model

The Prof. proposes a **circular (ring) model** where customers encircle the organisation, with every function — R&D, Production, Marketing, Finance, IT, Others — in continuous, equal contact with customers.

> The IT leader, for example, should not merely build architecture; they should visit distributors, retailers, and consumers to understand how real-time data can optimise the supply chain for customer fulfilment.

**This is the true definition of a customer-centric organisation.**
