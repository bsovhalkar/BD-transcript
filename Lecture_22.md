## 🧠 Main Points

- **Strategic Customer Mix (SCM)** is the foundation of customer typology — it reflects a price-feature mix that customers support, enabling companies to develop a matching Strategic Product Mix.
- **Price-Feature Matrix** yields four customer groups: High Price–High Features, Low Price–Low Features, Low Price–High Features, and High Price–Low Features.
- **Income-Feature Pyramid** typology shows that customer demand is governed by income levels combined with desired product features — multiple cross-pyramid combinations are possible and common.
- **Samsung Galaxy** and **Taj Group of Hotels** are case studies illustrating how companies serve multiple customer groups simultaneously through differentiated product/service lines.
- There are **three structured ways to understand customers**: (1) Experiential & Intuitive, (2) Market Research, and (3) Design Thinking — plus Apple's unique fourth way: Seasoned Visionary Intuition.
- **Steve Jobs' philosophy of Device Socialism** broke conventional segmentation by offering one standardized high-technology, high-value product to all income strata — creating universal appeal.
- Customers are typified by **Demographics** (age, gender, education, income), **Geography** (state → global), **Behaviour** (emotional, analytical, thoughtful, compulsive), **Culture** (social, religious, nativity, ethnicity), and **Psychography** (personality, values, attitudes, interests).
- **Basic Customer Types**: Neutral, Questioning, New, and Loyal — each requiring a distinct communication strategy.
- **Business Customer (B2B) Typologies**: Visionaries, Explorers, Adventurers, Seekers, Analysers, and Protectors — each with distinct risk-reward orientations and partnership styles.
- A customer's persona is typically a **composite mix** of multiple typology attributes, not a single type.
- The **Business Development–Customer Interface** reveals a paradox: every BD executive is also a customer, and every customer is also a BD person (e.g., licensor-licensee relationship).
- The **BD Typology Interaction Matrix** maps customer personalities against BD executive personalities to predict deal outcomes — from "Mega Futuristic Ventures" to "Risk-Averse Poor Results."
- Not all same-type pairings are optimal; complementary personalities often produce better outcomes than identical ones.

## ⭐ Important Points

1. **SCM (Strategic Customer Mix)** is the starting point for any strategic product mix; the company first decides what customer mix it wants to serve, then builds products around it.
2. The **Low Price–High Features** group arises specifically when **capacity outpaces demand** (buyer's market); it is the most demanding customer group.
3. The **High Price–Low Features** group arises when **demand outpaces supply** (seller's market / capacity shortfall) — unlikely but real.
4. The **Low Price–Low Features** group (Bottom of the Pyramid, per C. K. Prahalad) is essential for social equity and represents a partner in economic progress, not just a market segment.
5. The **Income-Feature Pyramid** cross-mapping is not always diagonal — a high-income customer may buy basic products and a low-income customer may aspire to high-feature products, creating many viable non-obvious combinations.
6. **Samsung Galaxy's four tiers** — S Series (High-High), SE Series (High Price–Low Features), M/A Series (Low Price–High Features), and M/F Series (Low-Low) — demonstrate real-world SCM implementation.
7. **Taj Hotels' four tiers** — Lake Palace (Palatial: High Price–Low Service), Vivanta (Luxury: High-High), Gateway (Business: Low Price–High Features), and Ginger (Budget: Low-Low) — show the same SCM logic in services.
8. **Steve Jobs' "Device Socialism"** is a unique counter-segmentation philosophy: one standardised high-performance product for all — breaking the pre-Steve norm of utilitarian products for the poor and luxury products for the rich.
9. Apple's **iCloud, Apple Stores, and the digital ecosystem** represent the integration of retailing, technology, and customer experience — the Apple Store was conceived not as a retail outlet but as a *customer experience hub*.
10. **Psychography** encompasses personality, values, attitudes, and interests — it is the basis for ESG-aligned business development, as customers with strong values may refuse products from companies with poor ESG practices.
11. **Loyal customers** must be rewarded — through loyalty programs, customised products, or exclusive features — because brand connect is marketing's core goal.
12. Among B2B typologies, **Protectors** (risk-avoiders) are most likely to miss great opportunities; **Adventurers** are bold but often impatient; **Visionaries** create mega-relationships.
13. In the BD Interaction Matrix, **Protector × Protector** = worst outcome (Risk-Averse, Poor Result); **Visionary × Visionary** = best potential (Mega Futuristic Ventures) but may still need some personality complementarity.
14. **Self-awareness** in one's own BD personality type is as important as understanding the customer's type — convergence or divergence determines the nature and quality of deals.
15. The **licensor-licensee paradox**: the licensor needs the licensee's money and loyalty (so the licensee is a customer), and the licensee needs the licensor's product (so the licensor is also doing BD) — both parties are simultaneously customers and BD executives.

## 📘 Detailed Notes

### 1. The Concept of Strategic Customer Mix (SCM)

One of management's key challenges is **reducing complexity to simplicity**. In marketing, where millions of heterogeneous customers exist, the solution lies in grouping customers into meaningful, manageable segments. The **Strategic Customer Mix (SCM)** is the framework that does exactly this.

> *SCM reflects a price-feature mix that customers support — the company first identifies what customer groups it wants, then designs the Strategic Product Mix (SPM) to serve them.*

The SCM is not about forcing customers into boxes, but about *stimulating customer thinking* to opt for one or another value proposition.

---

### 2. Customer Typology 1 — The Price-Feature Matrix

The first typology maps customers on two axes: **Price** (low to high) and **Features** (low to high), yielding four quadrants:

| Quadrant | Customer Group | When It Arises | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High Price – High Features** | Premium Seekers | Developed economies; customers willing to pay for differentiation | iPhone Pro, BMW |
| **Low Price – Low Features** | Bottom of Pyramid | Emerging economies; basic needs; social equity | Entry-level phones, budget transport |
| **Low Price – High Features** | Demanding Buyers | **Buyer's market** — supply > demand; customers leverage competition | ICE cars vs. EVs: buyers demand better features from ICE makers |
| **High Price – Low Features** | Constrained Buyers | **Seller's market** — demand > supply; capacity shortfall | Scarce goods during a shortage |

**Key insight from the transcript:** When EVs arrive in the market, a customer may approach an ICE vehicle manufacturer and say, *"I will buy from you, but only if you give me better features"* — this is the Low Price–High Features dynamic driven by competitive market forces.

As an economy evolves from emerging to developed, the **High Price–High Features** customer group grows in share. The **Low Price–Low Features** group remains essential for social equity and, as C. K. Prahalad argued, represents *partners in progress* — both for the company and the broader economy.

---

### 3. Customer Typology 2 — The Income-Feature Pyramid

This typology overlays the **income pyramid** with the **feature pyramid** of products:

| Income Level | Typical Feature Preference |
|---|---|
| **High Income** | High+ Features |
| **Upper Middle** | High− Features |
| **Lower Middle** | Basic+ Features |
| **Low Income** | Basic Features |

**Critical nuance:** The pairing is *not always diagonal*. A high-income customer may deliberately choose a basic product (e.g., a minimalist lifestyle), and a lower-income customer may aspire to and acquire a high-feature product (e.g., a flagship phone bought on EMI). The matrix of combinations is wide, and identifying the **viable, commercially plausible combinations** is where true marketing skill lies. Market research plays a crucial role here.

---

### 4. Case Study: Samsung Galaxy — SCM in Mobile Phones

Samsung's Galaxy product line demonstrates SCM applied to consumer electronics:

| Product Series | Price | Features | Customer Group |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S Series** | High | High | Premium buyers — fully differentiated flagship experience |
| **SE Series** | High | Low/Medium | Aspirational buyers — S-series image at reduced specs |
| **M/A Series** | Low | High | Budget-conscious buyers seeking superior experience |
| **M/F Series** | Low | Low | Online-first, entry-level, value shoppers |

> *"Not every CEO can be intuitively and technologically futuristic on what can inspire customers at different value and price points. Most companies will need to follow structured methods."*

### 5. Case Study: Taj Group of Hotels — SCM in Hospitality

Taj Group maps its hotel brands across the same price-feature (price-service) matrix:

| Hotel Brand | Service Level | Price Level | Customer Group |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lake Palace** | Low (vast palatial — cannot have 1:1 service) | High | Palatial ambience seekers; heritage experience |
| **Vivanta** | High | High | Luxury travelers (business + leisure) |
| **Gateway** | High | Medium-Low | Business travelers seeking features at value price |
| **Ginger** | Low (functional) | Low | Budget travelers; basic but clean functional rooms |

**Note on labelling:** Calling "Lake Palace" low on service does not mean it has the same service level as Ginger. It means that *given its palatial scale and pricing*, the service is commensurate but comparatively less personalised than a luxury hotel. Context-sensitive labelling is essential.

This classification applies to **both B2B customers** (corporate travel desks booking rooms) and **B2C customers** (individual leisure travelers).

### 6. Three Ways to Understand Customers

| Method | Nature | Who Uses It |
|---|---|---|
| **Experiential & Intuitive** | Seasoned, visionary process; born of long customer interaction | Trade channel players, experienced marketers |
| **Market Research** | Established structured process; data-driven; repeatable | Professional market research firms, FMCG companies |
| **Design Thinking** | New, empathy-first approach to discovering latent customer needs | Start-ups, innovation-led companies |
| **Apple's Way** *(Bonus 4th)* | Seasoned visionary intuition at the CEO/CMO level; near-impossible to replicate | Apple under Steve Jobs |

Apple's approach is categorised separately because it is an **intrinsic natural competency** embedded in the personality of the CEO and CMO — it cannot be institutionalised into a process like market research can.

### 7. Steve Jobs — Life, Soul, and Philosophy of Technology

#### 7a. Who Was Steve Jobs?
- CEO of Apple, Inc. (1995–2011)
- Led Apple to become the **first company to cross USD 2 trillion in market capitalisation**
- Defined in practical terms what *creativity, innovation, and connectivity* mean
- Introduced the **first iPhone** on January 9, 2007, at the Macworld Conference & Expo, San Francisco
  - Described it as: *"A widescreen iPod with touch controls + a revolutionary mobile phone + a breakthrough internet communicator"*

#### 7b. Device Socialism

| Conventional Wisdom (Pre-Steve) | Steve's Paradigm |
|---|---|
| Offer utilitarian (low-level) products to lower strata | One standardised, high-technology, high-value product for **all** sections of society |
| Offer luxury (high-level) products to upper strata | No income-based fragmentation |
| Market skimming with hyped value and high pricing OR fragmentation with broken tech and flexible pricing | Universal appeal — same product for billionaires and commoners |

**Device Socialism** = the socialistic philosophy that product aspirations are common across income segments. In the first years of iPod, iPhone, and iPad, Apple offered **only one model per product** for all customer segments. It was common to see a cab driver and the MD of a company using the identical iPhone.

> *"Segmentation, as defined for decades by marketing gurus, had no relevance for Apple products and customers."*

#### 7c. Digital Ecosystem
Steve Jobs extended the product experience beyond the device:
- **Apple Stores**: Not mere retail, but *customer experience hubs* — challenging the norm that manufacturing and retailing are separate. Within 3 years: 320 stores globally (237 in USA).
- **iCloud (2011)**: Seamlessly stores music, photos, movies, documents, apps, mails, calendars — and wirelessly pushes to all Apple devices. The cloud became a **digital home** for Apple customers.
- **Strategies of integration, outsourcing, and collaboration** converged under a single vision: the Apple digital ecosystem.

### 8. Customer Typology by Demographics

Demographics are among the most foundational customer typology dimensions:

| Demographic Factor | Customer Persona Influence | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Gender** | Very specific for certain products; influential for others; neutral for others | Women's wear (specific); kitchen items (influential); men's casual wear (broad) |
| **Age** | Products very specific to age cohorts vs. age-neutral products | Elderly need specific devices/lifestyle products; millennials seek different experiences |
| **Education** | Shapes analytical vs. impulsive decision-making | Highly educated buyers tend to be **analytical and discriminating** |
| **Income** | Drives spending power and buying persona | Governs which tier of the Price-Feature matrix the customer occupies |

> *"Marketing and Business Development must be demographically relevant to be customer-centric and effective."*

---

### 9. Customer Typology by Geography

Geography is a multi-layered typology dimension:

| Geographic Level | Characteristics | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **State** | Unique food habits, ethnicity, lifestyles | Gujarat — distinct food culture, specific product needs |
| **Multi-State / Region** | Shared cultural and economic homogeneity | South India, North India — regional product mix planning |
| **Continental** | Cross-national commonalities (legal, trade, cultural) | European Union — harmonised regulations and preferences |
| **Global / Multi-Continental** | Universal products transcending all geography | iPhone, Coca-Cola, luxury cars |

As **diaspora populations** grow, even geographic typologies must account for cross-geography cultural needs within a single geography (e.g., Indian diaspora in USA maintaining Indian consumption patterns).

### 10. Customer Typology by Behaviour

| Behavioural Trait | Description | Example Product |
|---|---|---|
| **Emotional** | Purchase driven by sentiment, heritage, family tradition | Personal care products (soap used since childhood) |
| **Analytical** | Research-heavy decision-making; specs, data, comparison | Passenger car purchase — checking wheelbase, clearance, power |
| **Thoughtful** | Deliberate, considered purchase with meaningful reflection | Fitness products — lifestyle impact is weighed carefully |
| **Compulsive** | Impulsive, habit-driven, trend-following purchase | Smartphones — especially among younger demographics |

### 11. Customer Typology by Culture

| Cultural Facet | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Social** | Products driven by social standing and status signalling | Premium apartment type, independent villa vs. apartment |
| **Religious** | Products mandated or preferred by religious practice | Lamp oil (Hindu puja rooms), candles (Christian practice) |
| **Nativity** | Products tied to geographic or traditional heritage | Ayurvedic / herbal medicine (India), Traditional Chinese Medicine (China) |
| **Ethnicity** | Products tied to ethnic identity and tradition | Sarees in India; Chinos in Japan |

> *"Product Development, Marketing and Business Development for customers have to be culturally sensitive."*

### 12. Customer Typology by Psychography

Psychography captures the *inner identity* of the customer — deeper than demographics or behaviour:

| Psychographic Dimension | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Personality** | Fundamental self-expression through product choices | Signature dresses, branded accessories — statement of self |
| **Attitudes** | Lifestyle orientation influencing product tier choice | Preferring a humble apartment vs. a luxurious villa |
| **Interests** | Specific hobbies, causes, or pursuits guiding purchases | Interest in organic farming → demand for organic products |
| **Values** | Core ethical/moral principles shaping consumption | No plastic packaging → refusal of food delivery apps |

Psychography is the key driver for **ESG-aligned business development**. Understanding customer values helps companies develop and market **socially responsible, environmentally conscious** products.

### 13. Basic Customer Types

| Type | Description | BD/Marketing Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Neutral** | Unaffected by brand, ads, or past associations; buys only on genuine need | Communicate genuine utility; build awareness |
| **Questioning** | Never fully satisfied; challenges product utility; switches frequently | Provide comprehensive answers; address every objection proactively |
| **New** | Exploring a product/category for the first time | Encourage trial; build attachment and positive experience |
| **Loyal** | Deeply attached to brand; resistant to switching | Reward loyalty through exclusive programs, customised features, personalised service |

**Classic example of loyalty:** The professor mentions using Cinthol soap for over 50 years — demonstrating how deep brand connect survives decades of competitive alternatives.

### 14. Business Customer (B2B) Typologies

Business customers have personalities distinct from individual consumers. Six primary B2B personality types:

| Type | Characteristics | BD Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Visionary** | Shares the BD vision; focused on transformative outcomes | Ideal for *mega relationships*; long-term strategic alliances |
| **Explorer** | Constantly seeks new development vistas; multi-option thinker | Can get bogged down by too many choices; needs focused facilitation |
| **Adventurer** | Bold risk-taker; anticipates high rewards | Impatient and non-persistent; suited for high-risk, high-reward ventures |
| **Seeker** | Ever-hungry for new options; takes intuitive optimal risk-reward bets | Good for steady, systematic partnerships with innovative products |
| **Analyser** | Data-hungry; rigorous decision-making | Can only do limited partnerships; analysis can become paralysis |
| **Protector** | Risk-averse; seeks multiple assurances | Likely to miss great opportunities; slowest to commit |

**GE's entry into financial services** is cited as an example of Adventurer-type behaviour — bold, initially successful, but ultimately divested to private equity.

---

### 15. Desirable Customer Typology (Composite Persona)

A customer's persona is **never monolithic** — it is a blend of multiple typology attributes. The ideal (desired) business customer persona for a BD executive to target would lean towards:

- **Dominant**: Visionary + Explorer + Seeker
- **Secondary**: Adventurer
- **Minimal**: Analyser + Protector

This is not a standardised prescription — the ideal mix varies by industry, product, and context. The value lies in **knowing the mix** of your actual customer base and calibrating your BD strategy accordingly.

---

### 16. BD–Customer Interface: The Paradox of Roles

Using a **Licensor–Licensee** deal as the model:

| Party | What They Have | What They Need |
|---|---|---|
| **Licensor** | The product/technology | Money and market reach (monetisation) |
| **Licensee** | Money and market access | The product/technology |

**Paradox:** The licensor is doing BD (monetising the product), but *from the licensor's perspective, the licensee is a customer*. Simultaneously, the licensee is a customer acquiring a product, but *from the licensee's perspective, they are developing their business*. Hence:

> *"Every business developer is a customer. Every customer is a business developer."*

This duality means **self-awareness** (knowing your own BD personality) is as critical as awareness of the customer's personality. Convergence or divergence in personalities determines the *nature, quality, and sustainability* of business deals.

---

### 17. BD Typology Interaction Matrix

The matrix maps **Customer Personality** (rows) against **BD Executive Personality** (columns) to predict deal outcomes:

| Customer ↓ / BD → | **Visionary** | **Explorer** | **Adventurer** | **Seeker** | **Analyser** | **Protector** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Visionary** | Mega Futuristic Ventures | All Over with Little Results | Hi-Risk Futuristic Bets | Calibrated Futuristic Bets | Well-balanced Futuristic Ventures | Vision sub-optimised by conservatism |
| **Explorer** | All Over with Little Results | Hi-Potential Low Result | Moderation of high-risk bets | Conflict with Some Resolution | — | — |
| **Adventurer** | Hi-Risk Big Bets | Moderation of high-risk bets | Conflict with Some Resolution | Conflict with No Resolution | — | — |
| **Seeker** | Calibrated Futuristic Bets | Aggressive Outreach with Optimal Results | Conflict with Some Resolution | Steady, Systematic Bets | Eliminating Many Options | — |
| **Analyser** | Well-balanced Futuristic Ventures | Conflict with Low Results | Conflict with No Resolution | Joint Work with Good Result | High Analysis, Low Result | Eliminating Many Options |
| **Protector** | Vision sub-optimised by conservatism | — | — | — | Eliminating Many Options | Risk-Averse, Poor Result |

**Key insights from the matrix:**
- **Best outcomes**: Visionary × Visionary → Mega Futuristic Ventures; Seeker × Explorer → Aggressive Outreach with Optimal Results
- **Worst outcomes**: Protector × Protector → Risk-Averse, Poor Result; Analyser × Analyser → High Analysis, Low Result
- **Same type ≠ always best**: Explorer × Explorer leads to Hi-Potential but Low Result (both exploring, neither committing)
- **Cross-type can be optimal**: Seeker (BD) with Analyser (Customer) → Joint Work with Good Result

## 🧾 Key Concepts / Frameworks / Formulae

## 📊 Summary

Lecture 22 opens by establishing the central challenge of marketing: reducing the complexity of heterogeneous customer populations into manageable, strategically useful typologies. The **Strategic Customer Mix (SCM)** framework is introduced as the starting point — companies should first define the customer mix they wish to serve and then develop the product mix accordingly. The **Price-Feature Matrix** yields four customer quadrants (High-High, Low-Low, Low Price–High Features, High Price–Low Features), each arising under distinct market and economic conditions. The **Income-Feature Pyramid** adds a second dimension, showing that income level and feature aspiration do not always move together, creating a rich variety of viable customer-product combinations.

Two detailed case studies — **Samsung Galaxy** and **Taj Group of Hotels** — illustrate how SCM plays out in practice, with each brand creating a portfolio of sub-brands or product tiers to serve distinct customer groups simultaneously. Three formal methods of understanding customers are discussed — Experiential/Intuitive, Market Research, and Design Thinking — with Apple's approach recognised as a rare fourth: Seasoned Visionary Intuition. The lecture then takes a deliberate detour through **Steve Jobs' philosophy**, demonstrating that the highest form of customer understanding is achieving a **universal product** that transcends income-based segmentation — what Jobs called *device socialism* and the *digital ecosystem*. 

The lecture then maps customer typologies across five dimensions: **Demographics, Geography, Behaviour, Culture, and Psychography**, each with a four-sub-category framework and illustrative examples. **Four basic customer types** (Neutral, Questioning, New, Loyal) are explained with distinct BD/marketing implications. Shifting to B2B, **six business customer personality types** (Visionary, Explorer, Adventurer, Seeker, Analyser, Protector) are introduced, each with distinct risk orientations and partnership styles. The critical insight is that a customer's persona is a **composite mix** of these types, not a single label. Finally, the **BD Typology Interaction Matrix** maps all possible pairings of customer and BD personalities to predict deal outcomes, from Mega Futuristic Ventures to Risk-Averse Poor Results — establishing that **self-awareness in personality** is as essential as customer understanding for successful business development.

## 🔍 Final Short Summary

Lecture 22 introduces **Customer Typologies** as the cornerstone of strategic marketing and business development. The **Strategic Customer Mix (SCM)** based on a price-feature matrix forms the foundational framework, illustrated through Samsung Galaxy and Taj Hotels. Customers are typified across five dimensions — demographics, geography, behaviour, culture, and psychography — and classified into four basic types (Neutral, Questioning, New, Loyal). In B2B contexts, six personality types (Visionary to Protector) govern partnership styles, and their interaction with BD executive personalities determines deal outcomes, as captured in the **BD Typology Interaction Matrix**. The overarching lesson: understand customers deeply enough to see the commonalities within their heterogeneity — as Steve Jobs did by transcending segmentation entirely with his philosophy of **device socialism**.
